Shock: The Complete Resident Guide

Added June 18, 2026 • Critical Care • ~14 min read

A high-yield ICU guide to hypovolemic, distributive, cardiogenic, and obstructive shock, including hemodynamics, cardiac output, SVR, wedge pressure, mixed venous oxygen saturation, diagnosis, and treatment.

Overview

Shock is a state of inadequate tissue perfusion resulting in cellular dysfunction and organ failure.

Almost every ICU attending will organize shock into four major categories:

Type Examples
Hypovolemic Hemorrhage, dehydration, GI losses
Distributive Septic shock, anaphylaxis, neurogenic shock
Cardiogenic MI, severe heart failure, cardiomyopathy
Obstructive Massive PE, cardiac tamponade, tension pneumothorax
Neurogenic shock and anaphylactic shock are considered forms of distributive shock.

Key Hemodynamic Equations

Understanding these equations makes shock physiology much easier.

MAP = Cardiac Output × SVR
Cardiac Output = Stroke Volume × Heart Rate
Stroke Volume = Preload + Contractility
Most shock states can be understood simply by identifying which variable is primarily abnormal.

Hypovolemic Shock

Common Causes

  • Hemorrhage
  • Vomiting
  • Diarrhea
  • Poor oral intake
  • Severe dehydration
  • Burns

Primary Problem

Loss of intravascular volume causes reduced preload.

Variable Change
Preload ↓↓
Cardiac Output ↓
SVR ↑
Mixed Venous O₂ ↓
The hallmark of hypovolemic shock is markedly reduced preload.

Cardiogenic Shock

Common Causes

  • Acute MI
  • Severe HFrEF
  • Myocarditis
  • End-stage cardiomyopathy
  • Mechanical complications of MI

Primary Problem

The heart cannot generate adequate forward flow.

Variable Change
Preload ↑
Cardiac Output ↓↓
SVR ↑
Mixed Venous O₂ ↓
The hallmark of cardiogenic shock is severely reduced cardiac output caused by impaired contractility.

Distributive Shock

Common Causes

  • Septic shock
  • Anaphylactic shock
  • Neurogenic shock

Primary Problem

Loss of vascular tone causes profound vasodilation.

Variable Change
Preload ↓
Cardiac Output ↑
SVR ↓↓
Mixed Venous O₂ ↑
Distributive shock is the only major shock state that increases mixed venous oxygen saturation.

Obstructive Shock

Common Causes

  • Massive pulmonary embolism
  • Cardiac tamponade
  • Tension pneumothorax

Primary Problem

Mechanical obstruction prevents normal cardiac filling or forward blood flow.

Variable Change
Preload ↑
Cardiac Output ↓
SVR ↑
Mixed Venous O₂ ↓
Think of obstructive shock as blood backing up because something is physically blocking flow.

Mixed Venous Oxygen Saturation (SvO₂)

Mixed venous oxygen saturation reflects the amount of oxygen remaining after tissues have extracted what they need.

Why Is SvO₂ High in Septic Shock?

In distributive shock, blood moves rapidly through dilated vessels and tissues cannot extract oxygen efficiently.

Think of distributive shock as a waterfall. Blood moves so quickly that tissues cannot "drink" enough oxygen from it.

As a result, oxygen-rich blood returns to the heart, causing elevated SvO₂.

Why Is SvO₂ Low in Other Shock States?

In cardiogenic, hypovolemic, and obstructive shock, blood flow is slow and tissues have more time to extract oxygen.

Therefore the returning venous blood contains less oxygen.

Normal Hemodynamic Values

Parameter Normal Range
CVP / RAP 2–8 mmHg
PCWP ~10 mmHg
Cardiac Index > 2.2 L/min/m²
SVR 800–1200
Mixed Venous O₂ Saturation 70–75%
A cardiac index below 2.2 is one of the defining features of cardiogenic shock.

Board-Style Examples

Case 1

  • CVP = 1
  • Cardiac Index = 2.4
  • SVR = 1500
  • SvO₂ = 50%

Diagnosis: Hypovolemic shock.

Case 2

  • CVP = 12
  • Cardiac Index = 1.8
  • SVR = 1500
  • SvO₂ = 50%

Diagnosis: Cardiogenic shock.

Case 3

  • CVP = 3
  • Cardiac Index = 3.6
  • SVR = 500
  • SvO₂ = 90%

Diagnosis: Distributive shock.

Treatment Principles

Shock Type Primary Treatment
Hypovolemic IV fluids ± blood products
Cardiogenic Inotropes and mechanical support
Distributive Pressors ± fluids
Obstructive Relieve obstruction

Cardiogenic Shock Inotropes

Agent Pearl
Dobutamine Rapid onset, easy titration
Milrinone Useful in RV failure but renally cleared

Mechanical Support Options

  • Impella
  • Intra-aortic balloon pump
  • LVAD
  • ECMO (selected cases)

Obstructive Shock Interventions

Cause Treatment
Massive PE Thrombolysis or thrombectomy
Cardiac tamponade Pericardiocentesis
Tension pneumothorax Needle decompression / chest tube

Pulse Pressure Clues

Wide Pulse Pressure

80 / 30

Suggests distributive shock.

Narrow Pulse Pressure

90 / 75

Suggests cardiogenic shock.

Wide pulse pressure = think vasodilation. Narrow pulse pressure = think pump failure.

High-Yield ICU Pearls

  • Hypovolemic shock = low preload.
  • Cardiogenic shock = low cardiac output.
  • Distributive shock = low SVR.
  • Obstructive shock = blocked blood flow.
  • Only distributive shock increases mixed venous oxygen saturation.
  • Cardiac index < 2.2 strongly suggests cardiogenic shock.
  • Wide pulse pressure suggests distributive shock.
  • Narrow pulse pressure suggests cardiogenic shock.
  • MAP = Cardiac Output × SVR.
  • Cardiac Output = Stroke Volume × Heart Rate.
  • Dobutamine and milrinone are the classic ICU inotropes.
  • Always treat the underlying cause of shock.

Tuberculosis: The Complete Advanced Resident Guide

Added July 14, 2026 • Infectious Disease • ~52 min read

Tuberculosis ranges from asymptomatic latent infection to transmissible cavitary pulmonary disease, disseminated infection, meningitis, pericarditis, lymphadenitis, and multidrug-resistant disease. Management requires rapid airborne isolation, appropriate respiratory sampling, nucleic-acid testing, culture and susceptibility testing, multidrug therapy, toxicity monitoring, adherence support, public-health coordination, and careful management of HIV and other immunocompromising conditions.

Clinical Overview

Tuberculosis is caused by organisms within the Mycobacterium tuberculosis complex. Infection most commonly begins after inhalation of airborne droplet nuclei containing viable bacilli.

The clinical spectrum includes:

  • Latent tuberculosis infection
  • Subclinical tuberculosis disease
  • Active pulmonary tuberculosis
  • Extrapulmonary tuberculosis
  • Disseminated or miliary tuberculosis
  • Drug-resistant tuberculosis
Suspected Pulmonary TB → Airborne Isolation + Respiratory NAAT, Smear, Culture and Susceptibility Testing

The Major Clinical Questions

  1. Does the patient have latent infection or active disease?
  2. Could the patient currently transmit tuberculosis?
  3. Which specimens should be collected before treatment?
  4. Is drug resistance plausible?
  5. Is extrapulmonary or disseminated disease present?
  6. Does the patient have HIV or another major immune defect?
  7. Which treatment regimen is appropriate?
  8. Can medication toxicity, interaction, and adherence be monitored safely?
  9. Has the local health department been notified?
Tuberculosis is simultaneously a clinical disease, a microbiologic diagnosis, and a public-health emergency. Treatment planning should involve the local tuberculosis program early.

Time-Critical TB Situations

  • Suspected infectious pulmonary or laryngeal TB without airborne isolation
  • TB meningitis
  • Pericardial tamponade
  • Spinal TB with cord compression
  • Miliary TB with respiratory failure
  • Adrenal crisis from disseminated disease
  • Rifampin resistance detected by rapid molecular testing
  • Severe drug toxicity threatening continuation of therapy

Microbiology

Mycobacterium tuberculosis

  • Acid-fast bacillus
  • Slow-growing aerobic organism
  • Intracellular survival within macrophages
  • Cell wall rich in mycolic acids
  • Long culture incubation period
  • Natural propensity to develop drug resistance when treated inadequately

Mycobacterium tuberculosis Complex

The complex includes:

  • M. tuberculosis
  • M. bovis
  • M. africanum
  • Other closely related organisms

M. bovis Pearl

M. bovis may be associated with:

  • Unpasteurized dairy exposure
  • Animal exposure
  • Intravesical bacillus Calmette–Guérin therapy
M. bovis is intrinsically resistant to pyrazinamide. Species identification can therefore change the treatment regimen and duration.

Why Multiple Drugs Are Required

A pulmonary cavity can contain a large bacillary population with naturally occurring resistant subpopulations.

Active TB Must Never Be Treated With a Single Drug

Multidrug treatment:

  • Rapidly reduces organism burden
  • Prevents emergence of resistance
  • Treats bacilli in different metabolic states
  • Reduces relapse risk

Transmission

How TB Spreads

Pulmonary or laryngeal TB spreads through airborne droplet nuclei generated when a person:

  • Coughs
  • Speaks
  • Sings
  • Sneezes
  • Undergoes an aerosol-generating procedure

These small particles may remain suspended in poorly ventilated indoor air.

Factors Increasing Infectiousness

  • Cavitary pulmonary disease
  • Positive sputum AFB smear
  • Frequent productive cough
  • Laryngeal involvement
  • Extensive pulmonary disease
  • Inadequate treatment
  • Poor ventilation
  • Prolonged close indoor exposure

TB Is Not Usually Spread By:

  • Touching
  • Sharing food or utensils
  • Toilet seats
  • Surface contact alone
  • Extrapulmonary disease without respiratory involvement

Extrapulmonary Disease and Infectiousness

Most isolated extrapulmonary TB is not infectious. Important exceptions include:

  • Concurrent pulmonary disease
  • Laryngeal TB
  • Open draining lesions with heavy organism burden
  • Aerosol-generating manipulation of infected tissue

Latent Infection vs Active TB Disease

Feature Latent TB Infection Active TB Disease
Symptoms Absent May include cough, fever, weight loss, sweats or organ-specific symptoms
Transmission Not infectious Pulmonary or laryngeal disease may be infectious
IGRA or TST Usually positive May be positive, negative or indeterminate
Chest imaging Normal or shows old healed disease May show infiltrates, nodules, cavities, adenopathy or miliary disease
Respiratory testing Negative when no active disease NAAT, smear or culture may be positive
Treatment Preventive regimen with one or two drugs Multidrug active-disease regimen
An IGRA or tuberculin skin test cannot distinguish latent infection from active tuberculosis disease.

Subclinical TB

Some patients have microbiologically or radiographically active disease without classic symptoms. This is particularly important in:

  • HIV
  • Older adults
  • Pretransplant evaluation
  • Close-contact investigations
  • High-incidence settings

Risk Factors

Risk of Exposure or Infection

  • Birth or prolonged residence in a high-incidence country
  • Close contact with infectious pulmonary TB
  • Homeless shelter exposure
  • Correctional-facility exposure
  • Long-term care or congregate living
  • Healthcare or laboratory exposure
  • Travel with prolonged high-risk exposure

Risk of Progression From Infection to Disease

  • HIV infection
  • Recent infection, especially within two years
  • Young age
  • Solid-organ or stem-cell transplantation
  • TNF-alpha inhibitor therapy
  • Prolonged corticosteroid therapy
  • Other immunosuppressive biologic therapy
  • Silicosis
  • Chronic kidney disease and dialysis
  • Diabetes mellitus
  • Low body weight or malnutrition
  • Head and neck cancer
  • Gastrectomy or intestinal bypass
  • Smoking
  • Substance-use disorder

Highest-Risk Groups

HIV infection, recent close exposure, organ transplantation, TNF-alpha inhibition and severe immune suppression create particularly high progression risk.

Clinical Presentation

Constitutional Symptoms

  • Fever
  • Night sweats
  • Weight loss
  • Fatigue
  • Anorexia

Pulmonary Symptoms

  • Persistent cough
  • Sputum production
  • Hemoptysis
  • Pleuritic pain
  • Dyspnea

Important Nuances

  • Symptoms may evolve gradually over weeks or months.
  • Fever may be absent.
  • Older adults may present with functional decline.
  • Advanced HIV may produce minimal pulmonary symptoms.
  • Extrapulmonary disease may occur without pulmonary symptoms.
Do not exclude TB because the patient lacks fever, hemoptysis, cavitation or a strongly positive immune-based test.

Pulmonary Tuberculosis

Typical Reactivation Pattern

  • Upper-lobe infiltrates
  • Apical or posterior-segment disease
  • Cavitation
  • Fibrotic change
  • Endobronchial spread with tree-in-bud nodules

Primary TB Pattern

  • Middle- or lower-lung infiltrate
  • Hilar or mediastinal lymphadenopathy
  • Pleural effusion
  • Ghon focus or complex

Advanced HIV Pattern

  • Diffuse or lower-lobe infiltrates
  • Adenopathy
  • Miliary pattern
  • Minimal cavitation
  • Normal chest radiograph in selected patients

Endobronchial TB

May cause:

  • Persistent cough
  • Wheezing
  • Airway narrowing
  • Lobar collapse
  • Bronchial stenosis after treatment

Laryngeal TB

  • Hoarseness
  • Odynophagia
  • Chronic cough
  • Very high infectiousness

Extrapulmonary Tuberculosis

Common Sites

  • Lymph nodes
  • Pleura
  • Central nervous system
  • Bone and joints
  • Genitourinary tract
  • Pericardium
  • Peritoneum
  • Gastrointestinal tract
  • Adrenal glands
  • Skin

Clinical Clues

Diagnostic Principle

Sample the Most Accessible Active Site for Histology, NAAT and Mycobacterial Culture

Initial Approach to Suspected Active TB

Step 1: Isolate

Place the patient in airborne infection isolation when pulmonary or laryngeal TB is meaningfully possible.

Step 2: Notify

  • Hospital infection prevention
  • Local or state health department
  • Microbiology laboratory

Step 3: Obtain Microbiology

  • Respiratory AFB smears
  • Mycobacterial cultures
  • Rapid NAAT
  • Rapid molecular resistance testing when indicated
  • Blood cultures for mycobacteria in selected advanced HIV or disseminated disease
  • Tissue or fluid sampling for extrapulmonary disease

Step 4: Assess Severity and Dissemination

  • Hemodynamics
  • Oxygenation
  • Neurologic examination
  • HIV testing
  • Liver and renal function
  • Pregnancy status when relevant
  • Extrapulmonary symptoms

Step 5: Start Treatment When Indicated

Do not wait for culture confirmation when clinical, radiographic and epidemiologic evidence strongly supports active TB.

Step 6: Preserve Diagnostic Yield

Whenever feasible, collect respiratory and tissue specimens before the first treatment dose.

Airborne Isolation

Hospital Precautions

  • Airborne infection-isolation room with negative pressure
  • Fit-tested N95 or higher-level respirator for personnel
  • Door kept closed
  • Limit transport
  • Patient wears a surgical mask during necessary transport
  • Use appropriate precautions during aerosol-generating procedures

Procedures With Increased Transmission Risk

  • Sputum induction
  • Bronchoscopy
  • Intubation
  • Extubation
  • Open airway suctioning
  • Autopsy
  • High-flow aerosol-generating procedures depending on facility policy

Discontinuing Airborne Precautions in Suspected TB

Precautions may generally be discontinued when infectious TB is considered unlikely and:

  • An alternative diagnosis explains the syndrome, or
  • Three appropriately collected sputum AFB smears are negative

Specimens are traditionally collected:

  • Eight to 24 hours apart
  • With at least one early-morning specimen

Confirmed Pulmonary TB

Removal from isolation requires a combination of:

  • Effective therapy
  • Clinical improvement
  • Evidence that resistance is unlikely or appropriately treated
  • Microbiologic improvement according to institutional and public-health policy
“Two weeks of treatment” alone does not automatically establish noninfectiousness. Organism burden, adherence, clinical response, smear status and drug susceptibility matter.

NAAT and Isolation

Rapid NAAT may help infection-control decisions, especially when smears are positive, but it does not replace clinical judgment, culture or local policy.

Respiratory Specimen Collection

Preferred Initial Strategy

  • Collect three respiratory specimens.
  • Space them approximately eight to 24 hours apart.
  • Include at least one early-morning specimen.
  • Send AFB smear and mycobacterial culture on each.
  • Perform NAAT on at least one respiratory specimen.

Spontaneous Sputum

Obtain a deep expectorated specimen rather than saliva.

Induced Sputum

Induced sputum is often preferred before bronchoscopy when the patient cannot produce sputum because it:

  • May have high diagnostic yield
  • Is less invasive
  • Can reduce need for bronchoscopy

Bronchoscopy

Consider bronchoscopy when:

  • Induced sputum cannot be obtained
  • Alternative diagnoses must be evaluated
  • Airway obstruction or endobronchial disease is suspected
  • The patient is immunocompromised with a broad pulmonary differential
Sputum induction and bronchoscopy are aerosol-generating procedures and require appropriate engineering controls and respiratory protection.

Post-Bronchoscopy Sputum

A post-bronchoscopy sputum specimen may increase yield and should be obtained when feasible.

AFB Smear Interpretation

What a Positive Smear Means

A positive smear indicates acid-fast organisms but does not prove M. tuberculosis.

Differential of a Positive AFB Smear

  • M. tuberculosis complex
  • Nontuberculous mycobacteria
  • Occasional partially acid-fast organisms

What Smear Grade Reflects

  • Approximate organism burden
  • Relative infectiousness
  • Response over time

Limitations

  • Lower sensitivity than culture or NAAT
  • Frequently negative in extrapulmonary disease
  • Frequently negative in advanced HIV
  • May remain positive from nonviable organisms during treatment
A negative AFB smear does not exclude pulmonary tuberculosis.

Nucleic-Acid Amplification Testing

Advantages

  • Rapid identification of M. tuberculosis complex
  • Higher sensitivity than smear microscopy
  • Helps distinguish TB from nontuberculous mycobacteria
  • Some assays detect rifampin-resistance mutations

When to Use

Perform NAAT on at least one respiratory specimen from a patient being evaluated for pulmonary TB.

Positive NAAT

A positive respiratory NAAT in the appropriate syndrome strongly supports active pulmonary TB.

Negative NAAT

A negative NAAT does not completely exclude TB, especially in smear-negative, paucibacillary or extrapulmonary disease.

Rifampin Resistance Detected

Immediately:

  • Continue airborne isolation.
  • Contact the health department.
  • Consult a drug-resistant TB expert.
  • Send confirmatory molecular and phenotypic testing.
  • Avoid relying on a routine drug-susceptible regimen.

NAAT Does Not Replace:

  • AFB smear
  • Mycobacterial culture
  • Phenotypic susceptibility testing
  • Genotyping for public-health investigation

Culture and Drug-Susceptibility Testing

Culture

Mycobacterial Culture Remains the Microbiologic Gold Standard

Why Culture Matters

  • Confirms viable organisms
  • Allows species identification
  • Permits phenotypic susceptibility testing
  • Supports treatment-response monitoring
  • Provides isolates for public-health genotyping

Baseline Susceptibility Testing

Test the initial isolate for susceptibility to first-line drugs, including:

  • Isoniazid
  • Rifampin
  • Ethambutol
  • Pyrazinamide when appropriate

Rapid Molecular Resistance Testing

Prioritize when:

  • Rifampin resistance is detected
  • Previous TB treatment occurred
  • There was contact with drug-resistant TB
  • The patient comes from a setting with high resistance prevalence
  • Culture remains positive despite treatment
  • Clinical response is inadequate

Repeat Susceptibility Testing

Repeat susceptibility testing when cultures remain positive after several months or treatment failure is suspected.

Imaging

Chest Radiograph

Potential findings include:

  • Upper-lobe infiltrate
  • Cavitation
  • Hilar or mediastinal adenopathy
  • Pleural effusion
  • Miliary nodules
  • Fibrotic or calcified lesions

Chest CT

CT can better identify:

  • Small cavities
  • Tree-in-bud nodules
  • Endobronchial spread
  • Adenopathy
  • Miliary disease
  • Alternative diagnoses

Normal Chest Radiograph

A normal radiograph generally lowers the probability of pulmonary TB, but active disease can occur with normal imaging in:

  • Advanced HIV
  • Very early disease
  • Selected immunocompromised hosts

Imaging Cannot Establish Microbiology

Neither cavitation nor a tree-in-bud pattern is specific for tuberculosis. Obtain microbiologic specimens.

IGRA and Tuberculin Skin Testing

Interferon-Gamma Release Assay

IGRAs measure T-cell release of interferon gamma after stimulation with TB-specific antigens.

Advantages

  • Single patient visit
  • Not affected by prior BCG vaccination
  • More specific than TST in BCG-vaccinated patients

Limitations

  • Cannot distinguish latent from active disease
  • False-negative results with severe immune suppression
  • Indeterminate results may occur
  • Requires appropriate specimen processing

Tuberculin Skin Test

Read induration—not erythema—48 to 72 hours after placement.

Five-Millimeter Threshold

An induration of at least 5 mm is positive in:

  • People with HIV
  • Recent close contacts
  • People with fibrotic chest-radiograph changes consistent with old TB
  • Organ-transplant recipients
  • Other substantially immunosuppressed patients

Ten-Millimeter Threshold

An induration of at least 10 mm is positive in selected higher-risk populations, including:

  • People born in high-incidence countries
  • Residents or workers in high-risk congregate settings
  • Mycobacteriology laboratory personnel
  • People with medical conditions increasing progression risk
  • Young children and exposed adolescents

Fifteen-Millimeter Threshold

An induration of at least 15 mm is positive in a person with no known risk factors.

BCG Vaccination

Interpret a TST according to risk factors, not according to BCG history. IGRA is generally preferred in BCG-vaccinated people.

Negative Test Does Not Exclude Active TB

False-negative tests occur with:

  • Advanced HIV
  • Miliary TB
  • Severe acute illness
  • Recent infection before immune conversion
  • Very young age
  • Immunosuppressive therapy

Diagnosing Latent Tuberculosis Infection

Required Elements

  • Positive IGRA or TST, or significant recent exposure
  • No symptoms suggesting active disease
  • No radiographic evidence requiring active-disease evaluation
  • No microbiologic evidence of active TB
Exclude active tuberculosis before starting an LTBI regimen. Rifampin or isoniazid monotherapy given to unrecognized active disease can select resistance.

Evaluation After a Positive Test

  1. Review TB symptoms.
  2. Perform physical examination.
  3. Obtain chest radiography.
  4. Obtain sputum testing if symptoms or imaging are abnormal.
  5. Review prior treatment documentation.
  6. Assess source-case drug susceptibility when exposure is known.

Old Fibrotic Lesions

Fibrotic lesions compatible with previous untreated TB warrant careful exclusion of active disease and generally strengthen the indication for treatment.

Previously Treated Patients

Do not repeat IGRA or TST to document treatment response. These tests commonly remain positive.

Treatment of Drug-Susceptible Pulmonary TB

Current Major Options

  • Four-month rifapentine–moxifloxacin regimen for selected eligible pulmonary TB
  • Traditional six-month rifampin-based regimen
  • Longer individualized regimens when standard drugs cannot be used or relapse risk is increased

Initial Four-Drug Coverage

When drug susceptibility is not yet known, treatment generally begins with:

Isoniazid + Rifamycin + Pyrazinamide + Ethambutol

Why Ethambutol Is Included

Ethambutol protects the regimen against unrecognized isoniazid resistance while susceptibility results are pending.

When Ethambutol May Be Stopped

Ethambutol may generally be discontinued when:

  • The isolate is susceptible to isoniazid and rifampin
  • The regimen contains adequate active drugs
  • No other resistance concern remains

Use Public-Health TB Expertise

The local TB program assists with:

  • Regimen selection
  • Directly observed therapy
  • Medication provision
  • Contact investigation
  • Adherence support
  • Isolation and return-to-work decisions

Four-Month Rifapentine–Moxifloxacin Regimen

Eligible Population

The four-month regimen is an option for selected people aged 12 years or older with drug-susceptible pulmonary TB.

Regimen Structure

Intensive Phase: Eight Weeks

  • Rifapentine
  • Isoniazid
  • Pyrazinamide
  • Moxifloxacin

Continuation Phase: Nine Weeks

  • Rifapentine
  • Isoniazid
  • Moxifloxacin
8 Weeks RPT + INH + PZA + MOX → 9 Weeks RPT + INH + MOX

Common Adult Daily Doses

  • Rifapentine 1,200 mg daily
  • Isoniazid 300 mg daily
  • Moxifloxacin 400 mg daily
  • Pyrazinamide weight based
  • Pyridoxine when indicated

Important Eligibility Considerations

Do not use automatically when there is:

  • Known or suspected resistance to a regimen component
  • Most extrapulmonary TB
  • TB meningitis
  • Pregnancy or another population not supported by the regimen evidence
  • Major QT-prolongation risk
  • Important rifapentine or moxifloxacin interactions
  • Prior treatment or exposure raising resistance concern

HIV

The regimen can be considered in selected people with HIV when the ART regimen is compatible with rifapentine and other eligibility criteria are satisfied.

Advantages

  • Shorter total treatment duration
  • Potentially improved completion
  • Effective in appropriately selected drug-susceptible pulmonary disease

Limitations

  • Drug interactions
  • Fluoroquinolone adverse effects
  • Daily pill burden
  • Not appropriate for many extrapulmonary syndromes
  • Requires confident drug-susceptibility assessment

Standard Six-Month Regimen

Intensive Phase

Isoniazid + Rifampin + Pyrazinamide + Ethambutol for 8 Weeks

Continuation Phase

Isoniazid + Rifampin for 18 Weeks

Abbreviation

2HRZE / 4HR

Who Commonly Receives This Regimen?

  • Drug-susceptible pulmonary TB not using the four-month regimen
  • Many forms of extrapulmonary TB
  • Patients with contraindication to moxifloxacin or rifapentine
  • Patients with medication interactions that can be managed with rifampin

Daily Therapy

Daily dosing is generally preferred, especially during the intensive phase and in:

  • HIV
  • Cavitary disease
  • Advanced disease
  • Drug-resistance concern

Treatment Completion

Completion is based on the prescribed number of doses within an allowed time—not calendar duration alone.

A treatment interruption requires assessment of how many doses were taken, when interruption occurred and how long treatment was stopped. Some patients must restart part or all of the regimen.

Common Adult First-Line Drug Dosing

Drug Common Daily Dose Common Maximum
Isoniazid 5 mg/kg orally daily 300 mg daily
Rifampin 10 mg/kg orally daily 600 mg daily
Pyrazinamide Approximately 20–25 mg/kg daily, using program weight bands Usually 2,000 mg daily in many adult protocols
Ethambutol Approximately 15–20 mg/kg daily Weight based
Rifapentine in four-month regimen 1,200 mg orally daily 1,200 mg daily
Moxifloxacin 400 mg orally daily 400 mg daily
Use current weight, renal function, age, pregnancy status and local TB-program dosing tables. Do not rely on a remembered fixed dose for pyrazinamide or ethambutol.

Obesity

Dose selection in severe obesity should be coordinated with TB pharmacy or public-health expertise. Underdosing can promote failure and resistance, while excessive doses increase toxicity.

Administration With Food

Rifampin and isoniazid are often given on an empty stomach for optimal absorption. If GI intolerance threatens adherence, coordinate an individualized administration plan rather than allowing missed doses.

Pyridoxine With Isoniazid

Purpose

Pyridoxine reduces isoniazid-associated peripheral neuropathy.

Who Should Receive It?

  • Pregnant or breastfeeding patients
  • People with HIV
  • Diabetes
  • Chronic kidney disease
  • Alcohol-use disorder
  • Malnutrition
  • Preexisting neuropathy
  • Older adults
  • Seizure disorders

Common Preventive Dose

  • Pyridoxine 25–50 mg orally daily

Established Neuropathy

Higher doses may be used when neuropathy develops, commonly around 100 mg daily, with evaluation of whether isoniazid can continue.

Very high prolonged pyridoxine doses can themselves cause neuropathy.

Monitoring During Active TB Treatment

Baseline Assessment

  • CBC
  • AST, ALT, bilirubin and alkaline phosphatase
  • Creatinine
  • HIV test
  • Hepatitis B and C assessment when indicated
  • Pregnancy testing when relevant
  • Visual acuity and color discrimination for ethambutol
  • Medication-interaction review

Monthly Clinical Monitoring

  • Adherence
  • Symptoms
  • Weight
  • Medication adverse effects
  • Vision changes
  • Neuropathy
  • Drug interactions
  • New rash or systemic reaction

Laboratory Monitoring

Routine monthly liver tests are particularly appropriate in:

  • Baseline liver abnormalities
  • Chronic liver disease
  • Alcohol use
  • Pregnancy or early postpartum period
  • HIV
  • Concurrent hepatotoxic medication use
  • Symptoms of hepatitis

Weight Changes

Recalculate weight-based drug doses when weight changes meaningfully.

Directly Observed Therapy

Treatment support may include:

  • In-person DOT
  • Video DOT
  • Medication reminders
  • Transportation support
  • Food or housing support
  • Case management
DOT is not merely surveillance. Effective TB care identifies and removes practical barriers to completing treatment.

Sputum Culture Conversion

Definition

Culture conversion generally refers to the first of consecutive negative cultures that remain negative.

Monitoring

For culture-positive pulmonary TB, obtain sputum cultures at least monthly until conversion.

Two-Month Culture

Culture status near the end of the intensive phase helps assess:

  • Response
  • Relapse risk
  • Adherence
  • Drug resistance
  • Need for regimen extension in selected patients

Persistent Culture Positivity

Evaluate for:

  • Missed doses
  • Incorrect dosing
  • Drug interaction
  • Malabsorption
  • Resistance
  • Large cavitary burden
  • Incorrect diagnosis
Ongoing culture positivity after several months is not normal slow improvement. It requires urgent reassessment and repeat susceptibility testing.

First-Line Drug Adverse Effects

Drug Major Toxicities Important Counseling
Isoniazid Hepatitis, peripheral neuropathy, rarely seizures or lupus-like syndrome Report anorexia, nausea, abdominal pain, dark urine or numbness
Rifampin Hepatitis, drug interactions, thrombocytopenia, flu-like reaction Orange discoloration of urine, sweat and tears is expected
Pyrazinamide Hepatitis, hyperuricemia, arthralgia Asymptomatic uric-acid elevation alone does not require stopping
Ethambutol Optic neuropathy, red-green color impairment Report blurred vision or color change immediately
Rifapentine Hepatitis, hypersensitivity, interactions, orange secretions Interaction review is essential
Moxifloxacin QT prolongation, tendinopathy, neuropathy, CNS effects, dysglycemia Report palpitations, tendon pain, neuropathy or severe glucose disturbance

Orange Secretions

Rifampin and rifapentine may discolor:

  • Urine
  • Tears
  • Sweat
  • Saliva

Soft contact lenses may become permanently stained.

Rash

Mild rash without systemic findings may sometimes be managed symptomatically, but stop and urgently evaluate therapy when there is:

  • Mucosal involvement
  • Blistering
  • Facial edema
  • Hypotension
  • Eosinophilia
  • Organ injury
  • Concern for Stevens–Johnson syndrome or DRESS

TB Drug-Induced Liver Injury

Hepatotoxic First-Line Drugs

  • Isoniazid
  • Rifampin
  • Pyrazinamide

Symptoms

  • Anorexia
  • Nausea
  • Vomiting
  • Right-upper-quadrant pain
  • Dark urine
  • Jaundice
  • Severe fatigue

Common Threshold to Stop Hepatotoxic Drugs

ALT ≥3 Times ULN With Hepatitis Symptoms or ≥5 Times ULN Without Symptoms

Immediate Evaluation

  • Stop hepatotoxic TB drugs when criteria are met.
  • Check AST, ALT, bilirubin and INR.
  • Review alcohol, acetaminophen and other medications.
  • Evaluate viral hepatitis and other causes.
  • Assess severity and need for hospitalization.
  • Contact a TB expert.

Maintaining TB Treatment

If active TB is severe, a temporary nonhepatotoxic regimen may be required using agents such as:

  • Ethambutol
  • A fluoroquinolone
  • Selected injectable or other second-line drugs

This should be managed with TB expertise.

Reintroduction

After symptoms resolve and liver tests improve, drugs may be reintroduced sequentially according to the clinical situation.

Do not simply restart all hepatotoxic drugs simultaneously after severe liver injury without a structured plan.

Ethambutol and Vision Monitoring

Baseline Evaluation

  • Visual acuity
  • Red-green color discrimination
  • Review of preexisting eye disease

Higher-Risk Patients

  • Renal impairment
  • Higher ethambutol dose
  • Prolonged therapy
  • Older age
  • Preexisting optic disease
  • Inability to report visual symptoms reliably

Symptoms of Toxicity

  • Blurred vision
  • Central visual loss
  • Reduced color discrimination
  • Difficulty reading
  • Visual-field change
Stop ethambutol and arrange urgent ophthalmologic assessment when optic toxicity is suspected.

Renal Function

Ethambutol is renally cleared and requires dose or interval adjustment in significant renal impairment.

Rifamycin Drug Interactions

Mechanism

Rifampin is a powerful inducer of drug-metabolizing enzymes and transporters. Rifapentine and rifabutin also produce clinically important interactions.

Important Interacting Drug Groups

  • Antiretroviral therapy
  • Warfarin and direct oral anticoagulants
  • Hormonal contraception
  • Azole antifungals
  • Calcineurin and mTOR inhibitors
  • Antiseizure medications
  • Methadone
  • Corticosteroids
  • Many cardiovascular medications
  • Some chemotherapy agents

Hormonal Contraception

Rifamycins may reduce the effectiveness of estrogen- and progestin-containing contraceptives. Provide counseling regarding a reliable alternative or additional barrier method.

Anticoagulation

Rifampin can markedly reduce exposure to many oral anticoagulants. The anticoagulation plan may require:

  • Warfarin with close INR adjustment
  • Parenteral anticoagulation
  • An alternative rifamycin
  • Specialist and pharmacy input

Methadone

Rifampin may precipitate opioid withdrawal by reducing methadone concentrations.

A rifamycin should not be removed from an otherwise effective TB regimen merely because an interacting medication is inconvenient. First determine whether the interacting medication or its dose can be changed.

When Pulmonary Treatment May Need Extension

Classic Relapse-Risk Combination

Cavitation on Initial Imaging + Positive Culture After 2 Months

For the traditional six-month regimen, this combination may justify extending the continuation phase so that total treatment reaches approximately nine months.

Other Factors Supporting Individualization

  • Extensive cavitary disease
  • Slow culture conversion
  • Low body weight
  • Smoking
  • Diabetes
  • Immunosuppression
  • Missed doses
  • Drug malabsorption
  • Inability to use pyrazinamide

If Pyrazinamide Cannot Be Used

A regimen without pyrazinamide generally requires a longer total duration, often approximately nine months when isoniazid and rifampin remain active.

Do Not Extend Automatically

Treatment duration should account for:

  • Regimen used
  • Drug susceptibility
  • Culture conversion
  • Anatomic site
  • Doses completed
  • Clinical response

Treatment of Extrapulmonary TB

General Principle

Many extrapulmonary forms are treated with the same four-drug intensive phase and rifamycin–isoniazid continuation phase used for pulmonary TB.

Approximate Duration Framework

Site Typical Framework
Pleural TB Approximately 6 months
Lymph-node TB Approximately 6 months
Abdominal or genitourinary TB Often approximately 6 months
Bone or joint TB Often 6–9 months; many experts favor 9 months
TB meningitis Usually 9–12 months
Disseminated disease Often 6 months unless CNS, bone or another complicated site is involved

Source Control

Procedural or surgical management may be needed for:

  • Hydrocephalus
  • Spinal-cord compression
  • Large abscess
  • Pericardial tamponade
  • Obstructive uropathy
  • Diagnostic tissue acquisition

Tuberculous Meningitis

Clinical Presentation

  • Subacute headache
  • Fever
  • Vomiting
  • Confusion
  • Cranial neuropathy
  • Hydrocephalus
  • Stroke
  • Hyponatremia

CSF Pattern

  • Elevated opening pressure
  • Lymphocytic or mixed pleocytosis
  • High protein
  • Low glucose

Imaging Findings

  • Basilar meningeal enhancement
  • Hydrocephalus
  • Infarction
  • Tuberculomas
  • Cranial-nerve involvement

Diagnostic Testing

  • Large-volume CSF
  • AFB smear
  • Mycobacterial culture
  • TB NAAT
  • Sputum testing even without respiratory symptoms
  • Sampling of another accessible involved site

Treatment

Begin empiric multidrug treatment promptly when suspicion is high.

Isoniazid + Rifamycin + Pyrazinamide + Ethambutol Initially

Duration

  • Usually 9–12 months
  • Individualize by drug susceptibility, response and complications

Adjunctive Corticosteroids

Dexamethasone or prednisolone is recommended, followed by a gradual taper.

Complications

  • Hydrocephalus
  • Vasculitic infarction
  • Cranial neuropathy
  • Tuberculoma enlargement
  • Seizure
  • Spinal arachnoiditis
Negative initial CSF smear or NAAT does not reliably exclude TB meningitis. Culture sensitivity is imperfect, and treatment delay can be catastrophic.

Pericardial Tuberculosis

Clinical Syndromes

  • Pericardial effusion
  • Tamponade
  • Effusive-constrictive disease
  • Chronic constrictive pericarditis

Diagnosis

  • Echocardiography
  • Pericardial-fluid studies
  • AFB culture and NAAT
  • Pericardial biopsy when needed
  • Evaluation for TB elsewhere

Treatment

  • Standard multidrug TB therapy
  • Pericardiocentesis for tamponade or diagnostic necessity
  • Surgical management for selected constrictive disease

Corticosteroids

Routine corticosteroids are not recommended for every patient. They may be considered selectively when the inflammatory complication risk is high and contraindications are absent.

The decision is particularly nuanced in people with HIV because potential inflammatory benefit must be balanced against infection and malignancy risk.

Bone, Joint and Spinal TB

Spinal TB

Pott disease commonly affects vertebral bodies and may cause:

  • Back pain
  • Vertebral collapse
  • Kyphotic deformity
  • Paraspinal or psoas abscess
  • Spinal-cord compression

Diagnosis

  • MRI
  • Image-guided or operative biopsy
  • Histopathology
  • AFB culture
  • NAAT
  • Drug-susceptibility testing

Treatment

  • Multidrug TB therapy
  • Often approximately nine months
  • Longer treatment in selected extensive or poorly controlled disease

Surgical Indications

  • Neurologic deficit
  • Spinal instability
  • Severe deformity
  • Large compressive abscess
  • Failure of medical therapy
  • Need for diagnostic tissue

Peripheral Joint TB

Typically causes chronic monoarthritis with gradual pain, swelling and loss of motion.

Synovial-fluid AFB smear is insensitive. Synovial biopsy and culture may be required.

Tuberculous Lymphadenitis

Clinical Features

  • Painless cervical adenopathy
  • Matted nodes
  • Central necrosis
  • Draining sinus
  • Minimal systemic symptoms

Diagnosis

  • Fine-needle aspiration or core biopsy
  • AFB smear
  • Mycobacterial culture
  • NAAT
  • Histopathology

Treatment

  • Standard six-month regimen in most drug-susceptible disease

Paradoxical Enlargement

Nodes may enlarge or become fluctuant during otherwise effective therapy.

Paradoxical lymph-node enlargement does not automatically indicate treatment failure. Confirm adherence and susceptibility and exclude another diagnosis before changing therapy.

Procedural Management

  • Needle aspiration may relieve a large fluctuant node.
  • Routine incision and drainage may lead to chronic sinus formation.
  • Surgery is reserved for selected diagnostic or compressive indications.

Miliary and Disseminated TB

Clinical Features

  • Persistent fever
  • Weight loss
  • Night sweats
  • Hepatosplenomegaly
  • Lymphadenopathy
  • Cytopenias
  • Elevated alkaline phosphatase
  • Respiratory failure
  • Neurologic symptoms
  • Adrenal insufficiency

Imaging

Miliary disease may show innumerable small nodules distributed throughout both lungs. Early radiographs may be normal, and CT is more sensitive.

Diagnostic Samples

  • Sputum
  • Blood mycobacterial culture in advanced HIV
  • Bone marrow
  • Liver or lymph-node biopsy
  • Urine
  • CSF when neurologic involvement is possible

Search for CNS Disease

Disseminated TB increases the probability of occult CNS involvement. Headache, confusion, cranial neuropathy or meningeal symptoms require urgent evaluation.

Treatment

Many cases receive standard six-month therapy, but duration increases when meningitis, bone or another complicated site is involved.

Tuberculosis and HIV

Bidirectional Impact

  • HIV greatly increases progression from latent infection to active disease.
  • TB accelerates HIV-related inflammation and morbidity.
  • TB remains a major cause of death among people with HIV worldwide.

Clinical Differences in Advanced HIV

  • Less cavitation
  • More adenopathy
  • More extrapulmonary disease
  • More disseminated disease
  • More smear-negative disease
  • Possible normal chest radiograph
  • Negative IGRA or TST despite active disease

Diagnostic Evaluation

  • Obtain HIV testing in every patient with TB.
  • Obtain CD4 count and HIV RNA.
  • Collect sputum NAAT and culture even with atypical imaging.
  • Evaluate extrapulmonary sites aggressively.
  • Consider mycobacterial blood cultures when CD4 is very low.

TB Treatment

Drug-susceptible TB generally uses standard rifamycin-containing therapy, with close attention to ART interactions.

Daily vs Intermittent Therapy

Daily treatment is preferred in people with HIV. Highly intermittent regimens increase relapse and acquired rifamycin-resistance risk in advanced immune suppression.

Timing of ART During TB Treatment

Non-CNS TB With CD4 Below 50

Start ART Within Approximately 2 Weeks of TB Treatment

Non-CNS TB With CD4 50 or Higher

Start ART during TB treatment, generally within the first several weeks and no later than approximately eight weeks in most patients.

TB Meningitis

TB meningitis is an important exception to immediate ART. ART is delayed because early CNS immune reconstitution can worsen intracranial inflammation. Coordinate timing with HIV and TB experts; initiation is generally later in the first two months after meningitis treatment begins.

ART Selection

Rifampin-compatible approaches may include:

  • Dolutegravir with dose adjustment
  • Efavirenz-based ART in selected patients
  • Raltegravir with dose adjustment in selected situations

Rifabutin may sometimes replace rifampin when a protease inhibitor or another interacting ART regimen is necessary.

Bictegravir

Bictegravir should not be coadministered with rifampin.

Do Not Delay All ART Because of IRIS Fear

In non-CNS TB, the survival benefit of timely ART—especially at very low CD4 count—generally outweighs the increased IRIS risk.

TB-Associated Immune Reconstitution Inflammatory Syndrome

Paradoxical TB-IRIS

Known TB worsens after ART initiation despite effective treatment.

Unmasking TB-IRIS

Previously unrecognized TB becomes clinically apparent after ART-mediated immune recovery.

Clinical Manifestations

  • Fever
  • Enlarging lymph nodes
  • Worsening pulmonary infiltrates
  • New or enlarging effusions
  • Abscess formation
  • Worsening CNS inflammation

Diagnosis of Exclusion

Before diagnosing IRIS, evaluate:

  • Medication adherence
  • Drug resistance
  • Drug interaction causing low TB-drug exposure
  • Another opportunistic infection
  • Medication toxicity
  • Malignancy

Management

  • Continue effective TB therapy.
  • Continue ART in most cases.
  • Drain large abscesses or effusions when needed.
  • Use NSAIDs for mild inflammation.
  • Use corticosteroids for moderate or severe paradoxical TB-IRIS when appropriate.

Preventive Prednisone

In selected high-risk people with HIV beginning ART early during TB treatment, a short prednisone course may reduce paradoxical TB-IRIS risk. This requires specialist assessment and exclusion of contraindications.

Tuberculosis in Pregnancy

Active TB

Active tuberculosis during pregnancy should be treated promptly. Untreated maternal TB creates major maternal, fetal and neonatal risk.

Common First-Line Drugs

  • Isoniazid
  • Rifampin
  • Ethambutol
  • Pyrazinamide may be used after individualized expert discussion and is routinely used in many international settings

Pyridoxine

  • Give pyridoxine with isoniazid during pregnancy and breastfeeding.

Avoid or Use Only With Specialized Guidance

  • Streptomycin because of fetal ototoxicity
  • Many drug-resistant TB agents with limited pregnancy data
  • The four-month rifapentine–moxifloxacin regimen when pregnancy-specific evidence or guidance does not support its use

Latent TB During Pregnancy

For many pregnant patients without a major immediate progression risk, LTBI treatment may be deferred until approximately two to three months postpartum.

Do Not Delay LTBI Treatment When Risk Is High

  • Recent close contact
  • Recent test conversion
  • HIV or major immune suppression

3HP

The once-weekly 3HP regimen is not recommended during pregnancy because pregnancy safety has not been adequately established.

Renal and Hepatic Disease

Renal Impairment

Isoniazid and rifampin usually do not require major renal dose adjustment. Pyrazinamide and ethambutol require adjustment in significant renal dysfunction.

Hemodialysis

  • Administer renally cleared drugs after dialysis when appropriate.
  • Use extended intervals for pyrazinamide and ethambutol according to TB-program dosing.
  • Monitor vision especially closely with ethambutol.

Chronic Liver Disease

The regimen depends on:

  • Severity of liver dysfunction
  • Baseline transaminases
  • Evidence of synthetic failure
  • TB burden and site
  • Availability of less hepatotoxic drugs

Major Challenge

Isoniazid, rifampin and pyrazinamide are all potentially hepatotoxic, but rifampin and isoniazid are among the most important sterilizing drugs.

Complex hepatic or renal disease requires TB-expert and pharmacy involvement. Avoid improvising a weak regimen with too few active drugs.

Drug-Resistant Tuberculosis

Definitions

Term Definition
Isoniazid-resistant TB Resistance to isoniazid with rifampin susceptibility
Rifampin-resistant TB Resistance to rifampin, with or without resistance to other drugs
MDR-TB Resistance to at least isoniazid and rifampin
Pre-XDR-TB MDR/RR-TB plus resistance to a fluoroquinolone
XDR-TB MDR/RR-TB plus fluoroquinolone resistance and resistance to bedaquiline or linezolid

Risk Factors

  • Previous TB treatment
  • Contact with drug-resistant TB
  • Birth or residence in a region with high resistance
  • Treatment failure
  • Persistent culture positivity
  • Irregular therapy
  • Previous fluoroquinolone exposure

Immediate Management

  • Maintain airborne isolation.
  • Notify the health department.
  • Obtain rapid molecular resistance testing.
  • Obtain expanded phenotypic susceptibility testing.
  • Consult a drug-resistant TB center or expert.
  • Construct a regimen using drugs likely to be active.
Never add one drug to a failing TB regimen. This creates functional monotherapy and amplifies resistance.

Specimen Submission

Send isolates or specimens to specialized public-health or reference laboratories for:

  • Expanded molecular testing
  • Phenotypic susceptibility testing
  • Genotyping

BPaLM and BPaL Regimens

BPaLM

Bedaquiline + Pretomanid + Linezolid + Moxifloxacin

A six-month BPaLM regimen is an option for selected patients aged 14 years or older with rifampin-resistant pulmonary TB that remains fluoroquinolone susceptible.

BPaL

Bedaquiline + Pretomanid + Linezolid

BPaL may be used in selected rifampin-resistant pulmonary TB with fluoroquinolone resistance or intolerance.

Why These Regimens Matter

  • All-oral therapy
  • Approximately six-month duration
  • Shorter than many older MDR-TB regimens
  • High effectiveness in eligible patients

Major Toxicities

Bedaquiline

  • QT prolongation
  • Hepatotoxicity

Linezolid

  • Myelosuppression
  • Peripheral neuropathy
  • Optic neuropathy
  • Lactic acidosis

Moxifloxacin

  • QT prolongation
  • Tendinopathy
  • Neuropathy
  • Dysglycemia

Monitoring

  • ECG
  • Electrolytes
  • CBC
  • Liver tests
  • Visual and neurologic symptoms
  • Drug interactions
BPaLM and BPaL are specialized regimens. Eligibility, resistance testing, dosing and toxicity management require drug-resistant TB expertise.

Treatment of Latent TB Infection

Preferred Strategy

Prefer Short-Course Rifamycin-Based Regimens When Feasible

Preferred Regimens

  • Three months once-weekly isoniazid plus rifapentine: 3HP
  • Four months daily rifampin: 4R
  • Three months daily isoniazid plus rifampin: 3HR

Alternative Regimens

  • Six months daily isoniazid: 6H
  • Nine months daily isoniazid: 9H

Why Short Regimens Are Preferred

  • Higher treatment completion
  • Shorter exposure
  • Lower hepatotoxicity in many populations
  • Comparable preventive effectiveness

Before Treatment

  • Exclude active TB.
  • Review source-case susceptibility.
  • Review pregnancy status.
  • Review all medication interactions.
  • Assess baseline liver-disease risk.

Three Months Once-Weekly Isoniazid and Rifapentine: 3HP

Regimen

Isoniazid + Rifapentine Once Weekly for 12 Doses

Recommended For

  • Adults and children aged two years or older
  • Selected people with HIV taking compatible ART
  • People likely to benefit from a short regimen

Not Recommended For:

  • Pregnancy or anticipated pregnancy during treatment
  • Children younger than two years
  • Likely isoniazid- or rifampin-resistant infection
  • People taking medications with unacceptable rifapentine interactions

Adverse Effects

  • Flu-like systemic reaction
  • Hypotension or syncope in rare severe reactions
  • Hepatotoxicity
  • Rash
  • Orange body fluids

Administration

Treatment may be provided through directly observed, video-observed or selected self-administered strategies according to the TB program and patient circumstances.

Four Months Daily Rifampin: 4R

Regimen

Rifampin Daily for 4 Months

Advantages

  • Short duration
  • High completion rate
  • Generally lower hepatotoxicity than prolonged isoniazid monotherapy
  • Useful after exposure to isoniazid-resistant, rifampin-susceptible TB

Limitations

  • Extensive drug interactions
  • Not appropriate after exposure to rifampin-resistant disease
  • May be incompatible with selected ART, anticoagulant or transplant regimens

Important Interaction Review

  • Hormonal contraception
  • Antiretrovirals
  • Warfarin and DOACs
  • Methadone
  • Azoles
  • Immunosuppressants

Three Months Daily Isoniazid and Rifampin: 3HR

Regimen

Isoniazid + Rifampin Daily for 3 Months

Potential Uses

  • Adults and children with presumed drug-susceptible infection
  • Selected people with HIV taking compatible ART
  • Patients who prefer a short daily regimen

Considerations

  • Rifampin interactions
  • Isoniazid hepatotoxicity
  • Pyridoxine indication
  • Need to exclude active disease carefully

Six or Nine Months of Isoniazid

Regimens

  • 6H: daily isoniazid for six months
  • 9H: daily isoniazid for nine months

When Used

  • Rifamycin interactions prevent a short-course regimen
  • Other short-course drugs are contraindicated
  • Drug supply or program limitations

Disadvantages

  • Longer duration
  • Lower completion
  • Greater cumulative hepatotoxicity risk

Monitoring

  • Monthly clinical review
  • Hepatitis symptom counseling
  • Laboratory monitoring in higher-risk patients
  • Pyridoxine for neuropathy-risk groups

Close Contacts and Window Prophylaxis

Contact Investigation

The health department identifies and evaluates people with meaningful exposure to infectious TB.

Priority Contacts

  • Household members
  • People with prolonged indoor exposure
  • Young children
  • People with HIV
  • Immunocompromised contacts
  • Contacts exposed during aerosol-generating procedures

Window Period

IGRA and TST may remain negative for several weeks after infection. Repeat testing is commonly performed eight to ten weeks after the last exposure.

Window Prophylaxis

Young children and substantially immunocompromised contacts may receive treatment during the window period even when the initial test is negative, after active disease has been excluded.

After Repeat Testing

  • Continue and complete treatment if the repeat test becomes positive.
  • Selected contacts may stop window prophylaxis if repeat testing remains negative and exposure has ended.
  • Highly immunocompromised contacts require individualized expert management.

Source-Case Resistance

The preventive regimen should account for the drug susceptibility of the source case.

Do not provide standard isoniazid or rifampin preventive therapy after exposure to drug-resistant TB without public-health and TB-expert guidance.

Public-Health Management

Mandatory Reporting

Tuberculosis disease is nationally notifiable in the United States and must be reported promptly according to local and state requirements.

Health-Department Roles

  • Case investigation
  • Contact investigation
  • Medication provision
  • DOT or video DOT
  • Home-isolation planning
  • Work and school clearance
  • Drug-resistance consultation
  • Genotyping and outbreak investigation

Contact Investigation Is Not the Inpatient Team’s Independent Task

The clinical team should identify household and high-risk exposures, but contact notification and testing should be coordinated through public health to protect confidentiality and ensure complete follow-up.

Travel

Patients with infectious pulmonary TB should not travel on commercial transportation until cleared by public health.

Confidentiality

TB control requires balancing patient confidentiality with the legal responsibility to prevent transmission.

Hospital Discharge Planning

Discharge Does Not Require Completion of TB Therapy

A patient may continue treatment at home when a safe plan exists.

Before Discharge

  • Coordinate with the health department.
  • Confirm the complete medication regimen.
  • Arrange DOT or video DOT.
  • Assess whether the patient remains infectious.
  • Review the home setting.
  • Identify children or immunocompromised household contacts.
  • Provide respiratory precautions.
  • Arrange follow-up and laboratory monitoring.

Home Isolation Considerations

  • Ability to remain away from work, school and public settings
  • Ability to avoid visitors
  • Separate sleeping arrangement when possible
  • Adequate ventilation
  • No unavoidable exposure to vulnerable household members
  • Reliable adherence and public-health follow-up

Medication Supply

Confirm that the patient has the exact full regimen rather than relying on routine retail-pharmacy availability.

Written Counseling

  • Expected orange body-fluid discoloration
  • Hepatitis symptoms
  • Vision symptoms
  • Drug interactions
  • Importance of every dose
  • Contact information for the TB program

Treatment Failure, Relapse and Reinfection

Treatment Failure

Failure is suggested by persistent or recurrent positive cultures during treatment despite an adequate interval of therapy.

Potential Causes

  • Drug resistance
  • Missed doses
  • Incorrect dosing
  • Drug interaction
  • Malabsorption
  • Incorrect diagnosis
  • Laboratory contamination

Relapse

Relapse is recurrence after treatment completion, usually with the original strain.

Reinfection

Reinfection is a new infection with a different strain and is more common in high-transmission settings.

Evaluation of Recurrent Disease

  • Repeat respiratory NAAT, smear and culture.
  • Obtain expanded susceptibility testing.
  • Review the full treatment record.
  • Review DOT data and interruptions.
  • Review drug levels or malabsorption concerns.
  • Compare strain genotyping when available.
Do not restart the same empiric regimen without assessing resistance and prior treatment history.

Therapeutic Drug Monitoring

Drug concentrations may be useful with:

  • Slow response
  • Malabsorption
  • Severe gastrointestinal disease
  • Diabetes
  • HIV
  • Renal replacement therapy
  • Drug interactions
  • Unexpected toxicity

Daily Inpatient TB Checklist

  • Does the patient remain in appropriate airborne isolation?
  • Is pulmonary or laryngeal disease possible?
  • How many sputum specimens have been collected?
  • Was NAAT performed?
  • Are smear, culture and susceptibility results pending?
  • Was rifampin resistance assessed rapidly?
  • Has the health department been notified?
  • Is HIV testing complete?
  • Is extrapulmonary or disseminated disease present?
  • Is CNS involvement possible?
  • Is the treatment regimen fully active?
  • Are doses correct for current weight and renal function?
  • Is pyridoxine prescribed when indicated?
  • Are liver tests and vision being monitored?
  • Are rifamycin interactions addressed?
  • Is the patient clinically improving?
  • Are sputum cultures converting?
  • Does the patient need ART, and when should it start?
  • Is the discharge and DOT plan established?
  • Have vulnerable household contacts been identified for public-health follow-up?

How to Present TB on ID Rounds

  • Pulmonary vs extrapulmonary sites
  • Isolation status
  • Sputum smear, NAAT and culture results
  • Rapid and phenotypic susceptibility results
  • Current regimen and treatment day
  • Weight-based doses
  • HIV status and ART plan
  • Baseline and current liver and renal function
  • Drug interactions
  • Culture-conversion status
  • Health-department coordination
  • Discharge and adherence plan
Avoid saying only, “The patient is being ruled out for TB.” State the pretest probability, isolation status, number and timing of specimens, smear and NAAT results, imaging pattern and alternative diagnoses.

Common Clinical Scenarios

Case 1: Cough, Weight Loss and Upper-Lobe Cavity

A patient recently arrived from a high-incidence country and has three months of cough, weight loss and an upper-lobe cavity.

Place the patient in airborne isolation immediately. Obtain sputum NAAT, AFB smears, cultures and susceptibility testing.

Case 2: Smear Positive, NAAT Negative

A sputum AFB smear is strongly positive, but the initial TB NAAT is negative.

Consider nontuberculous mycobacteria, but do not dismiss TB automatically. Review specimen quality, repeat molecular testing when appropriate and await culture.

Case 3: Negative Smears but Positive NAAT

Three sputum smears are negative, but NAAT detects M. tuberculosis.

Treat as pulmonary TB in the appropriate syndrome and maintain isolation according to infection-control and public-health guidance.

Case 4: Positive IGRA and Cough

A patient with a positive IGRA reports weight loss and productive cough.

Do not prescribe latent-TB monotherapy. Evaluate for active pulmonary disease with imaging and respiratory microbiology.

Case 5: Positive IGRA After BCG

A BCG-vaccinated patient has a positive IGRA and normal chest radiograph without symptoms.

BCG does not cause a positive IGRA. After excluding active disease, offer an appropriate LTBI regimen.

Case 6: Cavitation and Positive Two-Month Culture

A patient on standard 2HRZE/4HR had an initial cavity and remains culture positive near the end of the intensive phase.

Confirm adherence and susceptibility and consider extending the continuation phase to produce approximately nine months of total therapy.

Case 7: Visual Blurring During RIPE Therapy

A patient taking ethambutol reports difficulty distinguishing red from green.

Stop ethambutol and obtain urgent ophthalmologic evaluation.

Case 8: Nausea and ALT Seven Times Normal

A patient taking isoniazid, rifampin and pyrazinamide develops nausea with ALT seven times the upper limit of normal.

Stop hepatotoxic TB drugs, assess severity and alternative causes, and construct a structured reintroduction or temporary nonhepatotoxic plan with TB experts.

Case 9: HIV With CD4 28 and Pulmonary TB

A patient with newly diagnosed HIV and CD4 28 begins pulmonary TB treatment without CNS involvement.

Select rifamycin-compatible ART and begin ART within approximately two weeks.

Case 10: HIV With TB Meningitis

A patient with advanced HIV has basilar meningitis, hydrocephalus and CSF findings compatible with TB.

Begin TB treatment and corticosteroids immediately. Delay ART because early CNS IRIS may be dangerous, and coordinate timing with TB and HIV experts.

Case 11: Rifampin Resistance Detected

A rapid sputum assay detects M. tuberculosis and a rifampin-resistance mutation.

Treat this as probable rifampin-resistant TB. Maintain isolation, notify public health, obtain expanded resistance testing and involve a drug-resistant TB expert immediately.

Case 12: Pregnant Close Contact With Negative IGRA

A pregnant patient recently had intense household exposure to smear-positive TB and has an initial negative IGRA.

Exclude active disease and involve the health department. High-risk recent contacts may need window-period preventive therapy despite an initially negative test.

Case 13: Lymph Node Enlarges During Treatment

A cervical lymph node becomes larger after six weeks of otherwise effective therapy, while symptoms and inflammatory markers improve.

Consider a paradoxical response. Confirm adherence, susceptibility and diagnosis before labeling the regimen ineffective.

Case 14: Positive Culture After Treatment Completion

A patient develops recurrent cough and a positive TB culture six months after completing therapy.

Obtain rapid and expanded susceptibility testing and review the complete prior treatment record before restarting treatment.

Common Pitfalls

  • Failing to place a patient with suspected pulmonary TB in airborne isolation
  • Using droplet precautions instead of airborne precautions
  • Sending only one sputum specimen
  • Failing to obtain a rapid NAAT
  • Assuming a negative AFB smear excludes TB
  • Assuming a positive AFB smear proves tuberculosis
  • Failing to send culture because NAAT is positive
  • Failing to obtain susceptibility testing
  • Using IGRA or TST to rule out active disease
  • Calling a positive IGRA active tuberculosis
  • Starting latent-TB monotherapy before excluding active disease
  • Stopping isolation solely because two weeks of therapy have elapsed
  • Ignoring atypical imaging in advanced HIV
  • Missing extrapulmonary disease because chest imaging is normal
  • Failing to test every patient with TB for HIV
  • Using intermittent therapy in advanced HIV without specialist guidance
  • Starting ART too late in non-CNS TB with CD4 below 50
  • Starting ART immediately in uncontrolled TB meningitis
  • Combining bictegravir with rifampin
  • Failing to review anticoagulant, contraceptive or methadone interactions
  • Failing to prescribe pyridoxine in a high-risk patient taking isoniazid
  • Ignoring new visual symptoms during ethambutol therapy
  • Continuing hepatotoxic drugs despite symptomatic severe transaminase elevation
  • Restarting all hepatotoxic drugs simultaneously after severe injury without a plan
  • Underdosing pyrazinamide or ethambutol by using outdated weight
  • Adding one drug to a failing regimen
  • Treating rifampin-resistant TB without expert support
  • Failing to notify public health
  • Discharging without a DOT and home-isolation plan
  • Repeating IGRA or TST as a test of cure

Board and ID Consult Pearls

  • TB is transmitted through airborne droplet nuclei.
  • Pulmonary and laryngeal TB are the most important transmissible forms.
  • Use an airborne infection-isolation room and N95 respirator.
  • Obtain three respiratory specimens eight to 24 hours apart, including an early-morning specimen.
  • Perform NAAT on at least one respiratory specimen.
  • Culture remains the microbiologic gold standard.
  • NAAT does not replace smear, culture or susceptibility testing.
  • A positive AFB smear does not distinguish TB from nontuberculous mycobacteria.
  • A negative smear does not exclude pulmonary TB.
  • A negative IGRA or TST does not exclude active TB.
  • IGRA is preferred after BCG vaccination.
  • IGRA and TST cannot distinguish latent infection from active disease.
  • Exclude active disease before treating latent infection.
  • Latent TB is not infectious and does not require respiratory isolation.
  • Preferred LTBI regimens are short rifamycin-based courses.
  • 3HP is 12 once-weekly doses of isoniazid and rifapentine.
  • 3HP is not recommended during pregnancy.
  • 4R is four months of daily rifampin.
  • Rifamycins have extensive drug interactions.
  • Standard active-TB treatment is 2HRZE followed by 4HR.
  • A four-month rifapentine–moxifloxacin regimen is available for selected drug-susceptible pulmonary TB.
  • Do not use the four-month pulmonary regimen automatically for extrapulmonary TB.
  • Ethambutol protects against unrecognized isoniazid resistance.
  • M. bovis is intrinsically resistant to pyrazinamide.
  • Give pyridoxine with isoniazid in neuropathy-risk groups.
  • Ethambutol can cause optic neuropathy and red-green color impairment.
  • Stop hepatotoxic drugs when ALT is at least three times normal with symptoms or five times normal without symptoms.
  • Cavitation plus positive culture at two months increases relapse risk.
  • TB meningitis requires prompt treatment, corticosteroids and a prolonged regimen.
  • Negative CSF testing does not reliably exclude TB meningitis.
  • Spinal TB can cause paraspinal abscess and cord compression.
  • Daily TB therapy is preferred in people with HIV.
  • Start ART within about two weeks for non-CNS TB when CD4 is below 50.
  • Delay ART in TB meningitis because CNS IRIS can be dangerous.
  • Bictegravir should not be given with rifampin.
  • Do not diagnose TB-IRIS before excluding resistance, poor adherence and another infection.
  • Rifampin-resistance detection requires immediate expert and public-health involvement.
  • BPaLM and BPaL are six-month all-oral options for selected rifampin-resistant pulmonary TB.
  • Never add a single drug to a failing TB regimen.
  • Treatment completion is based on doses taken within the prescribed time.
  • TB disease is reportable and requires health-department coordination.

Bottom Line

Tuberculosis should be considered in patients with persistent cough, weight loss, fever, night sweats, cavitary or miliary lung disease, lymphocytic effusions, chronic lymphadenopathy, subacute meningitis, destructive spinal disease, or unexplained systemic illness—particularly when epidemiologic or immune risk is present.

Suspected pulmonary or laryngeal TB requires immediate airborne isolation. Obtain multiple respiratory specimens for AFB smear and culture, perform a rapid NAAT, and obtain molecular and phenotypic susceptibility testing. A negative smear, IGRA, TST or chest radiograph does not reliably exclude disease in a high-risk or immunocompromised patient.

Selected drug-susceptible pulmonary TB can be treated with a four-month rifapentine–moxifloxacin regimen. The traditional regimen remains two months of isoniazid, rifampin, pyrazinamide and ethambutol followed by four months of isoniazid and rifampin. Treatment must account for weight, renal and hepatic function, culture conversion, cavitation, drug interactions and adherence.

Extrapulmonary disease frequently requires tissue diagnosis and may require longer therapy or procedural source control. TB meningitis requires immediate empiric multidrug treatment, adjunctive corticosteroids and usually 9–12 months of therapy. Rifampin resistance requires urgent expert management and should never be managed by adding one drug to an inadequate regimen.

Latent TB should be treated only after active disease is excluded. Short-course rifamycin-based regimens are preferred when compatible with pregnancy status, source-case susceptibility and concurrent medications. Every active TB case requires public-health notification, treatment support and contact investigation.

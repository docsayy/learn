DVT and Pulmonary Embolism: Practical Guide for Internal Medicine Residents

Added June 19, 2026 • Hematology • ~15 min read

A practical inpatient guide to deep venous thrombosis (DVT) and pulmonary embolism (PE), including risk factors, diagnosis, Wells score, PERC criteria, PE classification, anticoagulation strategies, thrombolysis, and treatment duration decisions.

Overview

Venous thromboembolism (VTE) encompasses both deep venous thrombosis (DVT) and pulmonary embolism (PE).

Most pulmonary emboli originate from thrombi that form within the deep venous system of the lower extremities.

When evaluating a patient with suspected VTE, the key questions are:
  • Does the patient have a DVT?
  • Does the patient have a PE?
  • How high is the risk?
  • Does the patient require anticoagulation alone or more aggressive intervention?

Virchow Triad

The classic risk factors for venous thrombosis are described by Virchow's Triad.

Component Description
Venous Stasis Immobility, hospitalization, paralysis
Endothelial Injury Trauma, surgery, vascular injury
Hypercoagulability Cancer, inherited thrombophilias, estrogen therapy
Virchow Triad = Venous Stasis + Endothelial Injury + Hypercoagulability

Deep Venous Thrombosis

Typical Symptoms

  • Unilateral leg swelling
  • Pain
  • Tenderness
  • Erythema
  • Warmth

Leg Circumference Difference

A difference greater than 3 cm between legs is considered significant unilateral swelling and contributes to DVT risk assessment tools.

Homan Sign

Homan sign refers to posterior calf pain during ankle dorsiflexion.

Homan sign has poor sensitivity and specificity and should never be used alone to diagnose or exclude DVT.

Proximal vs Distal DVT

Type Location Clinical Importance
Proximal DVT Above the knee Higher embolic risk
Distal DVT Below the knee Lower embolic risk

Treatment

Proximal DVTs should generally be anticoagulated.

Historically, some distal DVTs were managed with serial ultrasound surveillance. Current practice increasingly favors anticoagulation for many distal DVTs as well, depending on risk factors and bleeding risk.

Proximal DVTs carry the greatest risk for pulmonary embolism and should be treated aggressively.

Pulmonary Embolism Basics

Pulmonary embolism occurs when thrombotic material travels to the pulmonary arterial circulation.

Common Symptoms

  • Dyspnea
  • Pleuritic chest pain
  • Tachycardia
  • Hypoxia
  • Syncope
  • Hemodynamic instability in severe cases

ECG Findings in PE

Most Common ECG Finding

Sinus Tachycardia

Although many clinicians associate PE with S1Q3T3, sinus tachycardia is by far the most common ECG abnormality.

S1Q3T3 Pattern

Finding Description
S1 Prominent S wave in Lead I
Q3 Q wave in Lead III
T3 T-wave inversion in Lead III
S1Q3T3 suggests acute right heart strain but is neither sensitive nor specific for PE.

Diagnostic Approach

PERC Rule

The Pulmonary Embolism Rule-Out Criteria (PERC) helps identify extremely low-risk patients who may not require additional testing.

Wells Score

The Wells score is most useful when clinical suspicion for PE is low.

If clinical suspicion for PE is high, do not rely on Wells score. Proceed directly to CT pulmonary angiography.

Practical Approach

Situation Next Step
High clinical suspicion CT pulmonary angiography
Low suspicion + Wells < 4 D-dimer
Low suspicion + Wells ≥ 4 CT pulmonary angiography

D-Dimer Pitfalls

D-dimer is an acute phase reactant and may be elevated in:

  • Infection
  • Inflammation
  • Recent surgery
  • Hospitalization
  • Cancer
An elevated D-dimer does not diagnose PE. It only indicates the need for further evaluation.

PE Classification

Category Hemodynamics RV Dysfunction
Low Risk (Non-Massive) Stable No
Intermediate Risk (Submassive) Stable Yes
High Risk (Massive) Unstable Usually Present

Massive PE

Defined by hemodynamic instability.

  • Hypotension
  • Shock
  • Cardiac arrest

Submassive PE

Patients are hemodynamically stable but have evidence of right ventricular strain.

Markers of RV Dysfunction

  • Elevated troponin
  • Elevated BNP
  • RV dilation on echocardiography
  • McConnell sign
  • D-sign on echocardiography
Submassive PE is where much of the decision-making occurs regarding advanced therapies.

Treatment

Anticoagulation

All PE classifications generally require anticoagulation unless contraindicated.

PE Type Treatment
Low Risk Anticoagulation
Submassive Anticoagulation ± advanced therapy
Massive Anticoagulation + reperfusion therapy

Massive PE

Patients with massive PE often require systemic thrombolysis.

  • Systemic alteplase (tPA)
  • Catheter thrombectomy
  • Surgical embolectomy

Submassive PE

Intermediate-risk PE frequently warrants consultation with pulmonary, critical care, or PERT teams to determine whether advanced therapies are appropriate.

PESI Score

The Pulmonary Embolism Severity Index (PESI) helps estimate mortality risk and identify candidates for outpatient treatment.

Provoked vs Unprovoked VTE

Provoked VTE

A provoking factor is clearly present:

  • Major surgery
  • Trauma
  • Prolonged immobility
  • Hospitalization

Typical anticoagulation duration:

3 months (sometimes 6 months)

Unprovoked VTE

No clear provoking factor can be identified.

Unprovoked VTE carries a substantially higher recurrence risk and often leads to consideration of indefinite anticoagulation.

Cancer-Associated Thrombosis

Although technically provoked, active cancer remains an ongoing risk factor.

Anticoagulation is generally continued as long as the provoking factor remains present.

Extended-Dose Therapy

After the initial treatment phase, some patients may transition to lower-dose extended anticoagulation:

  • Apixaban 2.5 mg BID
  • Rivaroxaban 10 mg daily

This strategy may reduce bleeding risk while maintaining protection against recurrence.

IVC Filters

Inferior vena cava (IVC) filters have a limited role.

Potential Indications

  • Acute VTE with absolute contraindication to anticoagulation
  • Major bleeding preventing anticoagulant therapy
If a patient can safely receive anticoagulation, an IVC filter is usually not recommended.

High-Yield Pearls

  • Virchow triad = venous stasis, endothelial injury, hypercoagulability.
  • Greater than 3 cm unilateral leg swelling is clinically significant.
  • Most common ECG finding in PE is sinus tachycardia.
  • S1Q3T3 is classic but uncommon.
  • Only use Wells score when clinical suspicion is low.
  • High clinical suspicion should lead directly to CT pulmonary angiography.
  • Massive PE is defined by hemodynamic instability.
  • Submassive PE has RV dysfunction despite stable blood pressure.
  • All PE patients generally require anticoagulation.
  • Provoked VTE usually receives 3 months of therapy.
  • Unprovoked VTE often requires consideration of indefinite anticoagulation.
  • IVC filters should be reserved for rare situations when anticoagulation cannot be given.

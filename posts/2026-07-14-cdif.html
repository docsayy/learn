Clostridioides difficile Infection: The Complete Advanced Resident Guide

Added July 14, 2026 • Infectious Disease • ~43 min read

Clostridioides difficile infection ranges from mild antibiotic-associated diarrhea to fulminant colitis, ileus, toxic megacolon, perforation, shock, and recurrent disease driven by persistent microbiome disruption. This guide reviews appropriate testing, NAAT and toxin interpretation, colonization, disease severity, initial and recurrent treatment, fulminant disease, surgical rescue, bezlotoxumab, FDA-approved microbiota products, fecal microbiota transplantation, infection prevention, and high-yield inpatient and board-review principles.

Clinical Overview

Clostridioides difficile is an anaerobic, spore-forming, gram-positive bacillus that causes toxin-mediated colonic injury after disruption of the normal intestinal microbiota.

The clinical spectrum includes:

  • Asymptomatic colonization
  • Mild watery diarrhea
  • Antibiotic-associated colitis
  • Pseudomembranous colitis
  • Severe colitis
  • Fulminant colitis
  • Ileus
  • Toxic megacolon
  • Colonic perforation
  • Sepsis and multiorgan failure
  • Recurrent infection
Compatible Diarrheal Syndrome + Evidence of Toxigenic C. difficile = CDI

The Major Clinical Questions

  1. Does the patient have clinically significant unexplained diarrhea?
  2. Could the positive test represent colonization rather than infection?
  3. Is disease nonsevere, severe, or fulminant?
  4. Can the precipitating antibiotic be stopped or narrowed?
  5. Is this the first episode or a recurrence?
  6. Does the patient need recurrence-prevention therapy?
  7. Are ileus, megacolon, perforation, or shock developing?
  8. Should surgery be involved now rather than later?
A positive C. difficile test does not independently diagnose infection. Testing should be restricted to patients with an appropriate clinical syndrome.

Why CDI Is Frequently Mismanaged

  • Formed stool is tested.
  • Patients receiving laxatives are tested without reassessment.
  • NAAT positivity is interpreted as active toxin-mediated disease in every patient.
  • Repeat tests are ordered as a test of cure.
  • Fulminant disease is missed because diarrhea has stopped after ileus develops.
  • Fidaxomicin, bezlotoxumab, or microbiota-based recurrence prevention is considered too late.
  • Surgical consultation is delayed until irreversible shock occurs.

Microbiology

Organism Characteristics

  • Gram-positive bacillus
  • Obligate anaerobe
  • Forms environmentally persistent spores
  • Colonizes the intestinal tract
  • Produces toxins that injure colonic epithelial cells

Toxins

Toxin Function
Toxin A Enterotoxic and inflammatory effects
Toxin B Major cytotoxic toxin responsible for epithelial injury
Binary toxin Produced by selected strains; clinical significance varies

Spores

Spores resist:

  • Desiccation
  • Many routine disinfectants
  • Alcohol-based hand sanitizer
  • Prolonged environmental exposure

Spores may contaminate:

  • Toilets and commodes
  • Bed rails
  • Medical equipment
  • Floors
  • Healthcare personnel hands
  • Shared patient-care surfaces

Pathogenesis

Step 1: Microbiome Disruption

Antibiotics reduce normal colonization resistance and create an environment in which C. difficile can proliferate.

Step 2: Exposure or Expansion

The patient may acquire spores from the environment or experience expansion of an already colonizing strain.

Step 3: Toxin Production

Toxin production causes:

  • Epithelial-cell injury
  • Neutrophilic inflammation
  • Increased intestinal permeability
  • Fluid secretion
  • Colonic edema
  • Pseudomembrane formation

Step 4: Inadequate Microbiome Recovery

After treatment, persistent microbiome disruption may permit recurrence from residual spores or re-exposure.

Antibiotic Exposure → Loss of Colonization Resistance → Toxin Production → Colitis

Why Recurrence Occurs

  • C. difficile spores survive antibacterial therapy.
  • Normal gut microbiota recover slowly.
  • Additional systemic antibiotics perpetuate dysbiosis.
  • Some patients mount an inadequate antitoxin immune response.
  • Healthcare or environmental re-exposure may occur.

Definitions

Term Practical Definition
CDI Compatible clinical symptoms, usually diarrhea, plus evidence of toxigenic C. difficile or pseudomembranous colitis
Colonization Presence of toxigenic C. difficile without toxin-mediated clinical disease
Initial episode First recognized CDI episode
Recurrence Return of compatible symptoms after apparent resolution, typically within 2–8 weeks
Reinfection New infection with a different strain; clinically often indistinguishable from relapse
Nonsevere CDI CDI without laboratory features used to define severe disease
Severe CDI WBC greater than 15,000 cells/µL or serum creatinine at least 1.5 mg/dL under IDSA/SHEA criteria
Fulminant CDI Hypotension, shock, ileus, or toxic megacolon attributable to CDI

Community-Associated vs Healthcare-Associated

Epidemiologic classification describes where and when disease developed but does not determine the treatment regimen by itself.

Patients may develop CDI in the community without a recent hospitalization. Conversely, diarrhea beginning in the hospital may have a noninfectious cause.

Risk Factors

Antibiotic Exposure

Nearly every systemic antibacterial can increase CDI risk, but particularly important classes include:

  • Clindamycin
  • Fluoroquinolones
  • Third- and fourth-generation cephalosporins
  • Carbapenems
  • Broad-spectrum penicillins
  • Prolonged or multiple antibiotic courses

Patient Risk Factors

  • Age 65 years or older
  • Previous CDI
  • Recent hospitalization
  • Residence in a long-term care facility
  • Immunocompromise
  • Inflammatory bowel disease
  • Chronic kidney disease
  • Solid-organ or stem-cell transplantation
  • Gastrointestinal surgery
  • Tube feeding
  • Severe underlying illness

Medication-Related Factors

  • Recent antibiotics
  • Continued non-CDI antibiotics during treatment
  • Acid-suppressive therapy, especially when not clearly indicated
  • Chemotherapy
  • Immunosuppressive therapy

Risk Factors for Recurrence

  • Older age
  • Previous recurrence
  • Severe initial CDI
  • Immunocompromise
  • Continued systemic antibiotics
  • Chronic kidney disease
  • Low serum albumin
  • Frequent healthcare exposure
The most important modifiable risk factor is unnecessary antimicrobial exposure.

Clinical Presentation

Typical Symptoms

  • Three or more unformed stools within 24 hours
  • Watery diarrhea
  • Lower abdominal cramping
  • Fever
  • Nausea
  • Anorexia
  • Abdominal tenderness

Severe Disease Features

  • Marked leukocytosis
  • Acute kidney injury
  • Hypoalbuminemia
  • Severe abdominal pain
  • Abdominal distention
  • Systemic toxicity
  • CT evidence of extensive colitis

Fulminant Disease Features

  • Hypotension
  • Vasopressor requirement
  • Ileus
  • Toxic megacolon
  • Severe abdominal distention
  • Lactic acidosis
  • Peritonitis
  • Altered mental status
  • Multiorgan failure
The absence of diarrhea does not exclude fulminant CDI. Diarrhea may diminish or disappear after ileus develops.

Neutropenia

A neutropenic patient may have severe or fulminant CDI without leukocytosis. Severity must be judged clinically rather than by WBC count alone.

Differential Diagnosis of Diarrhea

Medication-Related

  • Laxatives
  • Magnesium
  • Metformin
  • Colchicine
  • Mycophenolate
  • Enteral feeding
  • Antibiotic-associated diarrhea without CDI

Infectious

  • Norovirus
  • Salmonella
  • Campylobacter
  • Shigella
  • Enteropathogenic or shiga-toxin-producing E. coli
  • Cryptosporidium
  • CMV colitis

Inflammatory or Ischemic

  • Ulcerative colitis
  • Crohn disease
  • Ischemic colitis
  • Microscopic colitis
  • Graft-versus-host disease
  • Immune-checkpoint-inhibitor colitis

Hospital-Associated Noninfectious Causes

  • Tube feeds
  • Overflow diarrhea from fecal impaction
  • Postoperative bowel dysfunction
  • Bile-acid diarrhea
  • Short-bowel syndrome
  • Pancreatic insufficiency
A positive NAAT in a patient with another convincing cause of diarrhea may indicate colonization rather than CDI.

When to Test

Appropriate Testing Population

New, Unexplained, Clinically Significant Unformed Stools

A commonly used threshold is:

  • At least three unformed stools in 24 hours
  • No clear alternative explanation
  • Stool conforms to the specimen container

Before Ordering the Test

  • Review the bowel-movement record.
  • Confirm stool consistency.
  • Review laxative administration.
  • Review tube feeds and medications.
  • Assess for ileus or fulminant colitis.
  • Determine whether a previous positive test already exists.

Laxative Exposure

When clinically safe, discontinue laxatives and reassess. CDC guidance advises waiting approximately 48 hours before testing if symptoms persist.

Patients With Ileus

A patient with suspected fulminant CDI and ileus may not produce stool. Do not delay empiric therapy while waiting for a specimen when the clinical syndrome is compelling.

When Not to Test

  • Formed stool
  • Asymptomatic patient
  • Routine screening for colonization
  • Test of cure
  • Repeat testing during the same diarrheal episode without a major clinical change
  • Recently positive patient who is clinically improving
  • Diarrhea clearly caused by laxatives without persistent symptoms

Test of Cure

Do not order a C. difficile test to document cure. NAAT and other tests may remain positive for weeks after symptoms resolve.

Repeated Testing

Repeat testing within approximately seven days is generally discouraged because the diagnostic yield is low and false-positive interpretation increases.

Asymptomatic Contacts

Do not test roommates, family members, or healthcare personnel solely because they were exposed.

Diagnostic Tests

Nucleic Acid Amplification Test

NAAT detects genes associated with toxin production.

Advantages

  • High sensitivity
  • Rapid result
  • Detects toxigenic strains

Limitation

NAAT detects the ability to produce toxin, not necessarily active toxin production. It may be positive in asymptomatic colonization.

Toxin Enzyme Immunoassay

Toxin EIA detects free toxin A, toxin B, or both.

Advantages

  • More specific for active toxin presence
  • Rapid
  • Helpful in multistep algorithms

Limitations

  • Lower sensitivity
  • Toxin degrades rapidly at room temperature
  • False-negative testing can occur if specimens are delayed

Glutamate Dehydrogenase

GDH is an antigen produced by toxigenic and nontoxigenic C. difficile.

  • Highly sensitive screening test
  • Not specific for toxigenic strains
  • Requires confirmation with toxin or NAAT testing

Toxigenic Culture

  • Very sensitive
  • Slow
  • May detect colonization
  • Primarily used by reference or public-health laboratories

Endoscopy

Colonoscopy or sigmoidoscopy may show pseudomembranes but is not routinely needed and carries risk in severe colitis.

Imaging

CT abdomen and pelvis may show:

  • Colonic wall thickening
  • Pericolonic inflammatory change
  • Ascites
  • Colonic dilation
  • Megacolon
  • Perforation

Imaging supports severity and complication assessment but does not replace stool testing in routine disease.

Diagnostic Testing Algorithms

When Strict Stool-Submission Criteria Are Enforced

NAAT alone may be used in institutions where only appropriately selected symptomatic patients are tested.

When Testing Appropriateness Is Variable

Use a multistep algorithm such as:

GDH + Toxin, With NAAT Arbitration

or:

NAAT Followed by Toxin Testing

Why Use a Multistep Algorithm?

  • Improves diagnostic specificity
  • Reduces treatment of colonization
  • Provides information about detectable free toxin
  • Encourages clinical interpretation rather than binary PCR treatment
The best test depends on the quality of pretest patient selection. Even an excellent assay performs poorly when ordered in patients without compatible disease.

Interpreting NAAT and Toxin Results

Result Possible Interpretation Clinical Approach
NAAT positive, toxin positive Active toxigenic infection is likely in a compatible syndrome Treat if symptoms are consistent
NAAT positive, toxin negative Colonization, low-level toxin production, early disease, or partially treated CDI Interpret symptoms, stool frequency, alternative causes, severity, and host risk
NAAT negative CDI is unlikely because NAAT is highly sensitive Search for another cause unless exceptional circumstances exist
GDH positive, toxin negative Organism present, toxin not detected Use NAAT or another arbitration test according to the laboratory algorithm

NAAT Positive and Toxin Negative

Factors supporting treatment include:

  • New significant unexplained diarrhea
  • No laxative exposure or stronger alternative cause
  • Recent high-risk antibiotic exposure
  • Compatible abdominal pain or fever
  • Leukocytosis or acute kidney injury
  • History of CDI
  • Clinical worsening consistent with colitis

Factors supporting colonization include:

  • Formed or minimally loose stool
  • Laxative-associated diarrhea
  • Tube-feed diarrhea
  • Longstanding chronic diarrhea without change
  • Clear alternative infectious or inflammatory diagnosis
  • No inflammatory or systemic features
Do not automatically treat every NAAT-positive, toxin-negative patient. The laboratory result must be interpreted within the clinical syndrome.

Severity Classification

Category IDSA/SHEA Criteria Typical Features
Nonsevere WBC ≤15,000 cells/µL and creatinine <1.5 mg/dL Diarrhea without severe systemic or organ dysfunction
Severe WBC >15,000 cells/µL or creatinine ≥1.5 mg/dL Greater inflammatory burden or renal dysfunction
Fulminant Hypotension, shock, ileus, or megacolon Critical illness requiring urgent escalation

Limitations of Severity Criteria

  • Absolute creatinine may misclassify patients with low baseline muscle mass.
  • Neutropenic patients cannot mount leukocytosis.
  • Older adults may deteriorate without extreme laboratory findings.
  • WBC and creatinine do not replace bedside assessment.

Additional Poor-Prognosis Features

  • Hypoalbuminemia
  • Elevated lactate
  • Rapidly rising WBC
  • Progressive acute kidney injury
  • Diffuse colitis on CT
  • Ascites
  • Abdominal compartment physiology
  • Vasopressor requirement
  • Altered mental status
Fulminant CDI is defined by physiology, not merely by the number of bowel movements.

Initial Management

Immediate Actions

  1. Initiate contact precautions.
  2. Assess hemodynamics and abdominal examination.
  3. Stop the inciting antibiotic when possible.
  4. Narrow or shorten essential non-CDI antibiotics.
  5. Start appropriate CDI treatment promptly.
  6. Review laxatives and unnecessary acid suppression.
  7. Monitor WBC, creatinine, electrolytes, albumin, and lactate when severe.
  8. Evaluate for ileus, megacolon, perforation, and shock.

Do Not Delay Treatment When:

  • Fulminant disease is suspected
  • Testing will be significantly delayed
  • Ileus prevents stool production
  • The clinical syndrome is rapidly progressive

Stop or Modify Other Antibiotics

If a concurrent infection still requires treatment:

  • Use the narrowest effective agent.
  • Choose the shortest effective duration.
  • Avoid duplicate coverage.
  • Consider agents with lower microbiome disruption when clinically appropriate.

Treatment of an Initial CDI Episode

Clinical Category Preferred Treatment Acceptable Alternative
Initial nonfulminant CDI Fidaxomicin 200 mg orally twice daily for 10 days Vancomycin 125 mg orally four times daily for 10 days
Fulminant CDI Vancomycin 500 mg orally or by NG tube four times daily plus IV metronidazole Add rectal vancomycin when ileus limits delivery

Preferred Does Not Mean Mandatory

Fidaxomicin is preferred for nonfulminant initial CDI because sustained response and recurrence outcomes are generally better. Oral vancomycin remains an acceptable and effective alternative when:

  • Fidaxomicin is unavailable
  • Cost or formulary access is prohibitive
  • The patient cannot obtain the medication after discharge
  • Other logistical barriers exist

Do Not Use IV Vancomycin

Intravenous vancomycin does not reach therapeutic concentrations within the colonic lumen and does not treat CDI.

Fidaxomicin

Standard Regimen

Fidaxomicin 200 mg Orally Twice Daily for 10 Days

Advantages

  • Narrow activity against C. difficile
  • Less disruption of normal intestinal microbiota
  • Lower recurrence risk than standard-course vancomycin in many patients
  • Minimal systemic absorption

Particularly Attractive For:

  • Older patients
  • Immunocompromised patients
  • Patients with previous CDI
  • Patients requiring ongoing systemic antibiotics
  • Patients at high risk of recurrence

Extended-Pulsed Regimen

One recurrence regimen used in clinical practice is:

  • 200 mg orally twice daily on days 1–5
  • Then 200 mg orally every other day on days 7–25

Institutional protocols and insurance coverage may vary.

Limitations

  • Cost
  • Formulary restrictions
  • Limited evidence in fulminant disease
Fidaxomicin is not the recommended primary regimen for fulminant CDI because evidence in shock, ileus, and megacolon is insufficient.

Oral Vancomycin

Standard Nonfulminant Regimen

Vancomycin 125 mg Orally Four Times Daily for 10 Days

Mechanism

Oral vancomycin is poorly absorbed and achieves high intraluminal colonic concentrations.

Advantages

  • Highly effective for active CDI
  • Extensive clinical experience
  • Available as capsule or compounded oral solution
  • Acceptable alternative to fidaxomicin

Limitations

  • Greater microbiome disruption than fidaxomicin
  • Higher recurrence risk than fidaxomicin in selected populations
  • Does not eradicate spores

Compounded Solution

IV vancomycin powder may be compounded for oral administration when capsules are unavailable or swallowing is difficult. This is still administered enterally, not intravenously.

Role of Metronidazole

Not Preferred for Routine Initial CDI

Oral metronidazole is no longer preferred for initial adult CDI because it is less effective than vancomycin or fidaxomicin, particularly in severe disease.

When It May Be Considered

  • Nonsevere CDI
  • Fidaxomicin and oral vancomycin are unavailable
  • Use is temporary while obtaining preferred therapy

Fulminant Disease

IV metronidazole remains important as an adjunct to high-dose enteral vancomycin, particularly when ileus may impair delivery of vancomycin through the colon.

Toxicity

  • Nausea
  • Metallic taste
  • Peripheral neuropathy with prolonged or repeated use
  • Drug interactions
Metronidazole monotherapy is not adequate treatment for severe or fulminant adult CDI.

Fulminant CDI

Definition

  • Hypotension
  • Shock
  • Ileus
  • Toxic megacolon

Immediate Treatment

Vancomycin 500 mg PO/NG Every 6 Hours + Metronidazole 500 mg IV Every 8 Hours

Add Rectal Vancomycin When:

  • Ileus is present
  • Oral or NG drug delivery may not reach the distal colon
  • Severe colonic dilation limits transit

Immediate Additional Actions

  • ICU-level monitoring
  • Fluid and vasopressor resuscitation
  • Serial abdominal examinations
  • Frequent lactate, WBC, creatinine, and electrolyte monitoring
  • Abdominal imaging
  • Early colorectal or acute-care surgical consultation
  • Infectious disease and gastroenterology involvement

Signs of Deterioration

  • Increasing vasopressor requirement
  • Rising lactate
  • Worsening abdominal distention
  • Peritoneal signs
  • Progressive organ dysfunction
  • Increasing colonic dilation
  • Loss of bowel sounds
  • New respiratory failure
Do not wait for perforation before consulting surgery. Operative mortality increases substantially after profound shock and multiorgan failure develop.

Ileus and Rectal Vancomycin

Why Ileus Is Dangerous

  • Diarrhea may stop, creating false reassurance.
  • Oral vancomycin may not reach the entire colon.
  • Colonic dilation and toxin exposure continue.
  • Megacolon and perforation risk increase.

Rectal Vancomycin

A commonly used adult regimen is:

Vancomycin 500 mg in Approximately 100 mL Normal Saline Per Rectum Every 6 Hours

Administer as a retention enema according to institutional protocol.

Important Limitations

  • Rectal administration may not reach the proximal colon.
  • It does not replace oral or NG vancomycin when enteral therapy is feasible.
  • It does not replace IV metronidazole.
  • It does not replace surgical evaluation.

Safety

Use caution with:

  • Severe colonic dilation
  • Perforation concern
  • Recent colorectal surgery
  • Rectal injury

Surgical Management

Consult Surgery Early With:

  • Fulminant CDI
  • Toxic megacolon
  • Peritonitis
  • Perforation
  • Progressive shock
  • Rising lactate
  • Worsening organ failure
  • Failure of maximal medical therapy

Subtotal Colectomy

Subtotal colectomy with preservation of the rectum is the traditional operation for fulminant CDI.

Advantages

  • Removes the majority of diseased colon
  • Provides definitive source control
  • Appropriate for perforation, necrosis, and uncontrolled megacolon

Diverting Loop Ileostomy With Colonic Lavage

A colon-preserving approach may be considered in selected patients and experienced centers.

The procedure may include:

  • Diverting loop ileostomy
  • Antegrade colonic lavage
  • Intracolonic vancomycin
  • Systemic treatment

Timing Matters

Surgical consultation should occur while the patient remains potentially operable—not after irreversible cardiovascular collapse develops.

Recurrent CDI

Clinical Definition

Recurrence is return of compatible diarrhea after initial clinical resolution, usually within approximately 2–8 weeks.

Before Diagnosing Recurrence

  • Confirm that diarrhea truly returned.
  • Review laxatives and medications.
  • Consider postinfectious irritable bowel symptoms.
  • Consider inflammatory bowel disease.
  • Consider another enteric infection.
  • Do not diagnose recurrence from persistent PCR positivity alone.

First Recurrence

Preferred

  • Standard fidaxomicin
  • Extended-pulsed fidaxomicin

Acceptable Alternatives

  • Vancomycin taper-and-pulse regimen
  • Standard oral vancomycin if metronidazole was used for the initial episode

Multiple Recurrences

Potential strategies include:

  • Fidaxomicin
  • Vancomycin taper and pulse
  • Vancomycin followed by rifaximin
  • FDA-approved microbiota-based therapy
  • Conventional FMT under appropriate regulatory and safety procedures
  • Bezlotoxumab during antibacterial treatment in selected high-risk patients
Persistent loose stool immediately after treatment is not automatically recurrence. Symptoms may reflect slow colonic recovery or postinfectious bowel dysfunction.

Vancomycin Taper-and-Pulse Regimens

Rationale

A taper-and-pulse regimen suppresses vegetative C. difficile while allowing spores to germinate between doses and the microbiota to recover.

Example Regimen

  • Vancomycin 125 mg orally four times daily for 10–14 days
  • Then 125 mg twice daily for 7 days
  • Then 125 mg once daily for 7 days
  • Then 125 mg every 2–3 days for 2–8 weeks
There is no single universally required taper schedule. Follow the local infectious disease or pharmacy protocol and document the complete schedule clearly.

Vancomycin Followed by Rifaximin

One multiple-recurrence strategy is:

  • Vancomycin 125 mg orally four times daily for 10 days
  • Then rifaximin 400 mg orally three times daily for 20 days

Rifaximin resistance may emerge, and this strategy should be individualized.

Bezlotoxumab

What It Is

Bezlotoxumab is a monoclonal antibody that binds and neutralizes C. difficile toxin B.

Bezlotoxumab Prevents Recurrence; It Does Not Treat Active CDI Alone

Dose

  • 10 mg/kg IV once
  • Administered during the antibacterial treatment course

Who May Benefit Most?

  • CDI recurrence within the previous 6 months
  • Age 65 years or older
  • Immunocompromised host
  • Severe CDI
  • Multiple recurrence risk factors
  • Need for ongoing systemic antibiotics

Important Limitations

  • It does not replace fidaxomicin or vancomycin.
  • It is not a treatment for fulminant shock.
  • Access and infusion logistics vary.
  • Data with concurrent fidaxomicin are more limited than with vancomycin.

Heart Failure Warning

In patients with congestive heart failure, reserve bezlotoxumab for situations in which the anticipated recurrence-prevention benefit outweighs the potential risk.

FDA-Approved Microbiota-Based Therapies

Microbiota restoration is intended to prevent another recurrence after antibacterial treatment has controlled recurrent CDI.

Microbiota products are not substitutes for antibacterial treatment of an active CDI episode.

REBYOTA

  • Fecal microbiota, live-jslm
  • Indicated for adults following antibacterial treatment for recurrent CDI
  • Administered rectally
  • Single 150-mL dose
  • Given 24–72 hours after the last CDI antibiotic dose

VOWST

  • Fecal microbiota spores, live-brpk
  • Indicated for adults following antibacterial treatment for recurrent CDI
  • Administered orally
  • Four capsules once daily for three consecutive days
  • Started 2–4 days after completing antibacterial treatment
  • Requires the label-directed bowel-clearing preparation before the first dose
  • Antibacterial drugs should not be administered concurrently

Patient Selection

Consider microbiota-based therapy after recurrent CDI, particularly when recurrence risk remains high despite an appropriate antibacterial course.

Safety Considerations

  • Potential transmission of infectious agents
  • Potential food allergens
  • Abdominal symptoms
  • Need to coordinate future antibiotics carefully
Confirm that active diarrhea has responded to CDI antibiotics before administering a microbiota-restoration product.

Conventional Fecal Microbiota Transplantation

Potential Indication

Conventional FMT may be considered for patients with multiple CDI recurrences who have failed appropriate antibiotic treatments.

Routes

  • Colonoscopy
  • Enema
  • Nasogastric or nasoenteric delivery
  • Capsules

FDA Regulatory Context

FMT remains regulated as an investigational biological product. FDA enforcement discretion applies only under defined circumstances for CDI not responding to standard therapies.

Safety Requirements

  • Informed consent
  • Qualified donor screening
  • Testing for multidrug-resistant organisms
  • Testing for enteric bacterial, viral, and parasitic pathogens
  • Appropriate SARS-CoV-2 safeguards
  • Traceability of donor material
  • Monitoring for transmitted infection

Potential Adverse Events

  • Transmission of infectious organisms
  • Aspiration with upper-GI delivery
  • Procedure-related perforation or bleeding
  • Abdominal discomfort
  • Uncertain long-term microbiome effects
Do not use unscreened donor stool or informal “home FMT.” Serious and fatal transmission of infectious organisms has occurred.

Suspected Treatment Failure

Expected Clinical Course

Diarrhea and systemic findings should begin improving over several days, but stool frequency may normalize gradually.

Reasons for Nonresponse

  • Incorrect diagnosis
  • Fulminant disease requiring higher-dose therapy
  • Ileus limiting drug delivery
  • Ongoing precipitating antibiotics
  • Inadequate adherence or administration
  • Inflammatory bowel disease flare
  • CMV colitis
  • Ischemic colitis
  • Other enteric infection
  • Toxic megacolon or perforation

Evaluation

  1. Repeat abdominal and hemodynamic assessment.
  2. Confirm medication route and dose.
  3. Assess for ileus.
  4. Review concurrent antibiotics and laxatives.
  5. Trend WBC, creatinine, lactate, and albumin.
  6. Obtain abdominal imaging when severe or worsening.
  7. Consult surgery early in fulminant disease.
  8. Reconsider the diagnosis.
Do not order repeated stool PCR to determine whether treatment is working.

Complications

Gastrointestinal

  • Severe colitis
  • Ileus
  • Toxic megacolon
  • Colonic perforation
  • Peritonitis
  • Protein-losing enteropathy
  • Postinfectious irritable bowel syndrome

Systemic

  • Hypovolemia
  • Electrolyte abnormalities
  • Acute kidney injury
  • Lactic acidosis
  • Septic shock
  • Respiratory failure
  • Multiorgan failure

Toxic Megacolon

Features may include:

  • Severe systemic toxicity
  • Marked abdominal distention
  • Colonic dilation
  • Ileus
  • Fever
  • Tachycardia
  • Hypotension
  • Altered mental status
Toxic megacolon requires immediate multidisciplinary management with surgery, intensive care, infectious disease, and gastroenterology.

Supportive Care

Fluid and Electrolytes

  • Replace volume losses.
  • Monitor sodium, potassium, magnesium, and bicarbonate.
  • Monitor renal function.
  • Use balanced resuscitation based on hemodynamics.

Nutrition

  • Continue nutrition when tolerated.
  • Avoid unnecessary prolonged bowel rest in nonfulminant disease.
  • Individualize feeding during ileus or surgical evaluation.

Venous Thromboembolism Prevention

Hospitalized patients should receive appropriate VTE prophylaxis unless contraindicated.

Medication Review

  • Stop unnecessary laxatives.
  • Stop unnecessary proton-pump inhibitors.
  • Avoid unnecessary antibiotics.
  • Review immunosuppression and chemotherapy timing.

Monitoring Severe Disease

  • Serial vital signs
  • Urine output
  • Abdominal examination
  • WBC and creatinine
  • Lactate
  • Colonic diameter when indicated

Antimotility Agents

General Principle

Do not use loperamide or similar agents as isolated treatment for suspected or untreated CDI.

Why There Is Concern

  • Reduced toxin clearance
  • Worsening ileus
  • Progression to megacolon
  • Masking of clinical deterioration

Possible Cautious Use

Selected patients with nonfulminant CDI who are already receiving effective therapy and have no ileus, megacolon, or significant distention may occasionally receive an antimotility agent under close supervision.

Avoid antimotility agents in severe abdominal distention, ileus, toxic megacolon, or fulminant disease.

Probiotics

Primary Prevention

Evidence is inconsistent regarding routine probiotic use to prevent CDI during antibiotic exposure.

Prevention of Recurrence

Probiotics are not a substitute for:

  • Fidaxomicin
  • Vancomycin taper
  • Bezlotoxumab
  • Microbiota-based therapy
  • FMT

Potential Risks

  • Lactobacillus bacteremia
  • Saccharomyces fungemia
  • Central-line contamination
  • Risk in severely immunocompromised or critically ill patients
Routine probiotic administration is not recommended as standard inpatient CDI treatment or recurrence prevention.

Special Populations

Older Adults

  • May present with delirium or weakness
  • Higher recurrence risk
  • Higher risk of dehydration and renal injury
  • May particularly benefit from recurrence-prevention strategies

Chronic Kidney Disease

  • Higher risk of severe disease and recurrence
  • Oral fidaxomicin and vancomycin generally require no renal dose adjustment because systemic absorption is minimal
  • IV metronidazole and systemic therapies require appropriate assessment

Post-Colectomy or Ostomy Patients

C. difficile may rarely cause:

  • Small-bowel enteritis
  • High-output ileostomy
  • Abdominal pain and systemic illness

Pediatric Patients

Infants frequently carry toxigenic C. difficile without disease. Pediatric testing and treatment require age-specific guidance and are outside the primary scope of this adult review.

CDI in Inflammatory Bowel Disease

Why Diagnosis Is Difficult

  • IBD flare and CDI may produce identical symptoms.
  • IBD patients may be colonized.
  • Blood in stool does not distinguish the diagnoses.
  • Endoscopic pseudomembranes may be absent.

Testing

Test for CDI during an acute IBD flare with increased diarrhea, even when antibiotic exposure is absent.

Treatment

  • Fidaxomicin or oral vancomycin
  • Avoid metronidazole as preferred therapy
  • Coordinate immunosuppression decisions with gastroenterology
  • Monitor closely for toxic megacolon

Immunosuppression

Do not automatically stop all IBD therapy. Decisions depend on:

  • CDI response
  • Severity of the IBD flare
  • Risk of uncontrolled inflammation
  • Current immunosuppressive regimen
Failure to improve after CDI treatment may reflect uncontrolled IBD, persistent CDI, CMV colitis, or a combination.

CDI in Immunocompromised Patients

Risk Groups

  • Solid-organ transplant
  • Stem-cell transplant
  • Hematologic malignancy
  • Neutropenia
  • Chronic corticosteroids
  • Biologic therapy
  • Advanced HIV

Important Nuances

  • WBC-based severity criteria may be unreliable.
  • Other infections may coexist.
  • CMV and graft-versus-host disease may mimic CDI.
  • Recurrence risk is high.
  • Probiotic complications are more concerning.

Recurrence Prevention

High-risk patients may merit early consideration of:

  • Fidaxomicin
  • Bezlotoxumab
  • Microbiota-based therapy after recurrent disease
  • A carefully planned strategy when additional antibiotics are unavoidable

CDI in Pregnancy

Clinical Concerns

  • Dehydration
  • Electrolyte disturbance
  • Sepsis
  • Fulminant colitis
  • Maternal and fetal compromise

Treatment

Oral vancomycin has minimal systemic absorption and extensive pregnancy experience. Fidaxomicin also has minimal systemic absorption, although pregnancy data are more limited.

Management Principles

  • Do not delay effective therapy.
  • Coordinate severe disease with maternal-fetal medicine.
  • Treat fulminant CDI using standard life-saving principles.
  • Use multidisciplinary assessment for bezlotoxumab or microbiota products because pregnancy data are limited.

Infection Prevention

Core Measures

  • Rapid identification of symptomatic patients
  • Contact precautions
  • Private room or cohorting when needed
  • Dedicated equipment
  • Appropriate hand hygiene
  • Sporicidal environmental cleaning
  • Antimicrobial stewardship
  • Diagnostic stewardship

Preemptive Isolation

Place a patient with clinically significant unexplained diarrhea on contact precautions while testing is pending when CDI is a meaningful possibility.

Duration of Precautions

Follow facility policy. Many institutions continue precautions for at least 48 hours after diarrhea resolves, while some continue for the duration of hospitalization because spore shedding may persist.

Do Not Isolate Based on PCR Alone Without Clinical Context

Infection-prevention policies may require isolation after a positive result, but clinical teams should still distinguish colonization from infection before prescribing treatment.

Hand Hygiene

Soap and Water

Soap-and-water handwashing physically removes C. difficile spores and is particularly important:

  • After caring for a patient with CDI
  • After glove removal
  • During outbreaks
  • When hands are visibly soiled

Alcohol-Based Hand Rub

Alcohol hand sanitizer does not reliably kill C. difficile spores.

Glove use remains essential. Soap-and-water handwashing complements rather than replaces appropriate glove use and standard hand-hygiene practice.

Patient and Family Education

  • Wash hands with soap and water after toileting.
  • Clean bathrooms carefully.
  • Avoid sharing contaminated personal items.
  • Complete prescribed treatment.
  • Seek care if significant diarrhea returns.

Environmental Cleaning

Sporicidal Agents

Use an EPA-approved C. difficile sporicidal disinfectant for:

  • Daily room cleaning
  • Terminal cleaning
  • High-touch surfaces
  • Shared equipment
  • Bathrooms and commodes

High-Touch Surfaces

  • Bed rails
  • Call buttons
  • Door handles
  • Toilet seats
  • Faucets
  • Bedside tables
  • Medical equipment

Shared Equipment

  • Thermometers
  • Blood-pressure cuffs
  • Wheelchairs
  • Commodes
  • Glucometers

Use dedicated equipment whenever feasible and disinfect shared equipment before use with another patient.

Antimicrobial and Diagnostic Stewardship

Antimicrobial Stewardship

  • Avoid antibiotics for viral infections.
  • Narrow broad empiric therapy promptly.
  • Use the shortest effective duration.
  • Review duplicate anaerobic coverage.
  • Limit high-risk antibiotics when alternatives are equally effective.
  • Reassess chronic antibiotic prophylaxis.

Diagnostic Stewardship

  • Test only unformed stool.
  • Use electronic restrictions after recent laxatives.
  • Block repeat testing within a defined period.
  • Provide toxin and NAAT interpretation in laboratory reports.
  • Avoid multiplex GI testing without an appropriate syndrome.

Proton-Pump Inhibitors

Discontinue acid suppression when there is no valid indication. Do not automatically stop a PPI that remains necessary for:

  • Active upper-GI bleeding
  • Recent high-risk ulcer
  • Specific Barrett or severe erosive disease indications
  • Other clearly documented indications

Discharge and Follow-Up

Discharge Readiness

  • Hemodynamically stable
  • Oral intake adequate
  • Stool frequency improving
  • Renal function and electrolytes stable
  • No ileus, megacolon, or surgical concern
  • Medication access confirmed
  • Complete taper or pulse schedule provided in writing

Medication Access

Before discharge:

  • Confirm fidaxomicin or vancomycin coverage.
  • Do not assume the outpatient pharmacy stocks compounded vancomycin.
  • Provide the exact number of capsules or tablets.
  • Clarify the complete taper schedule.
  • Arrange infusion if bezlotoxumab is planned.
  • Coordinate microbiota-product timing when planned.

Return Precautions

  • Increasing abdominal pain
  • Abdominal distention
  • Inability to pass stool or gas
  • Fever
  • Syncope
  • Reduced urine output
  • Bloody stool
  • Recurrent frequent watery diarrhea

Do Not Schedule a Test of Cure

Follow symptoms rather than laboratory positivity.

Daily Inpatient CDI Checklist

  • Does the patient have true clinically significant diarrhea?
  • Could the positive test represent colonization?
  • Is this an initial episode or recurrence?
  • Is disease nonsevere, severe, or fulminant?
  • Is ileus developing?
  • Is abdominal distention worsening?
  • Are WBC, creatinine, lactate, and albumin trending appropriately?
  • Can the inciting antibiotic be stopped?
  • Can essential antibiotics be narrowed or shortened?
  • Is the CDI drug, dose, route, and duration correct?
  • Does the patient need rectal vancomycin?
  • Has surgery been consulted early enough?
  • Is recurrence prevention indicated?
  • Would fidaxomicin, bezlotoxumab, REBYOTA, VOWST, or FMT be appropriate?
  • Are contact precautions active?
  • Is environmental cleaning using a sporicidal agent?
  • Is outpatient medication access confirmed?

How to Present CDI on ID Rounds

  • Stool frequency and consistency
  • Alternative diarrhea causes
  • Test method and toxin result
  • Initial episode vs recurrence number
  • Severity category
  • Abdominal examination
  • WBC, creatinine, lactate, and albumin
  • Imaging findings
  • Current CDI treatment and start date
  • Concurrent systemic antibiotics
  • Recurrence-prevention plan
  • Infection-control status
Avoid saying only, “The PCR is positive, so the patient has C. diff.” State the clinical syndrome, stool burden, toxin result, alternative causes, severity, and recurrence history.

Common Clinical Scenarios

Case 1: Positive PCR After Laxatives

A hospitalized patient receives polyethylene glycol and senna, develops two loose stools, and has a positive NAAT with negative toxin.

Colonization is possible. Stop laxatives, reassess the clinical syndrome, and do not automatically treat based on NAAT alone.

Case 2: Initial Nonsevere CDI

A patient has six watery stools daily, positive toxin, WBC 11,000 cells/µL, and creatinine 0.9 mg/dL.

Use fidaxomicin when feasible. Oral vancomycin 125 mg four times daily for 10 days is an acceptable alternative.

Case 3: Severe CDI

A patient has watery diarrhea, WBC 23,000 cells/µL, and creatinine 1.8 mg/dL but no hypotension or ileus.

This is severe but not fulminant CDI. Fidaxomicin or standard-dose oral vancomycin is appropriate.

Case 4: No Diarrhea but Worsening Distention

A patient with CDI develops abdominal distention, hypotension, and no bowel movements.

Suspect fulminant CDI with ileus. Use high-dose oral or NG vancomycin, IV metronidazole, rectal vancomycin, ICU support, imaging, and immediate surgical consultation.

Case 5: First Recurrence

Diarrhea and toxin positivity return three weeks after a standard oral-vancomycin course.

Use fidaxomicin, including an extended-pulsed option when appropriate, or a vancomycin taper-and-pulse regimen.

Case 6: Multiple Recurrences

A patient has a third CDI episode despite vancomycin and fidaxomicin.

Consider microbiota restoration with REBYOTA, VOWST, or appropriately screened FMT after antibacterial control of the active episode.

Case 7: High Recurrence Risk

A 76-year-old immunocompromised patient is being treated for recurrent severe CDI.

Consider bezlotoxumab during the antibacterial course if no prohibitive heart-failure risk or access barrier exists.

Case 8: Fulminant CDI and Rising Lactate

A patient receiving maximal medical therapy develops increasing vasopressor needs and rising lactate.

This is not a reason to wait another day. Escalate surgical decision-making immediately.

Case 9: Persistent Positive PCR After Recovery

A patient is asymptomatic after completing treatment but remains NAAT positive.

This is not treatment failure. Do not retreat and do not perform tests of cure.

Case 10: IBD Flare and Positive NAAT

A patient with ulcerative colitis has worsening bloody diarrhea and a positive NAAT but negative toxin.

CDI, colonization, and active IBD may coexist. Treat and escalate immunosuppression decisions using the complete clinical picture with gastroenterology input.

Case 11: Patient Needs Continued Antibiotics

A patient with CDI must complete treatment for MSSA bacteremia.

Do not stop essential bacteremia therapy. Use the narrowest effective antibiotic, avoid unnecessary additional agents, and consider recurrence-prevention strategies.

Case 12: Milky FMT Product From an Unverified Source

A patient asks whether a family member can provide stool for home treatment.

Do not use unscreened home FMT. Donor-derived pathogenic and multidrug-resistant infections have caused serious illness and death.

Common Pitfalls

  • Testing formed stool
  • Testing after laxatives without reassessment
  • Treating every NAAT-positive patient
  • Ignoring the toxin result and clinical syndrome
  • Ordering repeat PCR to assess treatment response
  • Ordering a test of cure
  • Diagnosing recurrence from persistent PCR positivity
  • Using IV vancomycin for CDI
  • Using metronidazole monotherapy for severe CDI
  • Using standard-dose vancomycin for fulminant CDI
  • Forgetting IV metronidazole in fulminant disease
  • Failing to add rectal vancomycin with ileus
  • Missing fulminant disease because diarrhea has stopped
  • Waiting for perforation before calling surgery
  • Failing to stop the inciting antibiotic
  • Stopping an essential antibiotic without treating the underlying infection appropriately
  • Continuing unnecessary proton-pump inhibitor therapy
  • Using antimotility agents in ileus or megacolon
  • Using probiotics in a severely immunocompromised patient without considering bloodstream-infection risk
  • Giving bezlotoxumab as a substitute for CDI antibiotics
  • Giving microbiota therapy before active CDI has been controlled
  • Using unscreened FMT material
  • Discharging without confirming medication access
  • Providing an unclear vancomycin taper prescription
  • Failing to use sporicidal environmental cleaning

Board and ID Consult Pearls

  • CDI requires symptoms plus evidence of toxigenic C. difficile.
  • Do not test formed stool.
  • Three or more new unformed stools in 24 hours is a commonly used testing threshold.
  • Review and discontinue laxatives before testing when clinically appropriate.
  • NAAT detects toxin genes and may be positive in colonized patients.
  • Toxin positivity increases confidence that active toxin-mediated disease is present.
  • NAAT-positive, toxin-negative results require clinical interpretation.
  • Do not order a test of cure.
  • Do not routinely repeat testing during the same diarrheal episode.
  • Fidaxomicin is preferred for an initial nonfulminant episode when resources permit.
  • Oral vancomycin remains an acceptable initial treatment.
  • IV vancomycin does not treat CDI.
  • Metronidazole is no longer preferred for routine initial adult CDI.
  • Severe CDI is defined by WBC greater than 15,000 or creatinine at least 1.5 mg/dL under IDSA/SHEA criteria.
  • Fulminant CDI is defined by hypotension, shock, ileus, or megacolon.
  • Fulminant treatment is high-dose oral or NG vancomycin plus IV metronidazole.
  • Add rectal vancomycin when ileus limits colonic drug delivery.
  • Diarrhea may disappear when fulminant ileus develops.
  • Surgical consultation should occur early in fulminant CDI.
  • Crystals, imaging, or pseudomembranes are not required to diagnose CDI.
  • First recurrence is preferably treated with fidaxomicin.
  • Vancomycin taper-and-pulse therapy is an acceptable recurrence strategy.
  • Bezlotoxumab is a one-time toxin-B monoclonal antibody used to reduce recurrence.
  • Bezlotoxumab does not treat active CDI by itself.
  • Use additional caution with bezlotoxumab in congestive heart failure.
  • REBYOTA and VOWST are FDA-approved to prevent recurrence after antibacterial treatment of recurrent CDI.
  • Microbiota products are not treatments for uncontrolled active CDI.
  • Conventional FMT requires screened donors and FDA safety safeguards.
  • Soap and water physically remove C. difficile spores.
  • Alcohol sanitizer does not reliably kill spores.
  • Use a sporicidal environmental disinfectant.
  • Antibiotic stewardship is essential for primary and secondary prevention.
  • In IBD, CDI and inflammatory disease activity may coexist.
  • Neutropenic patients may have severe CDI without leukocytosis.
  • Persistent loose stools after therapy do not automatically represent recurrence.

Bottom Line

Clostridioides difficile infection is a clinical diagnosis supported by appropriate laboratory testing. Test only patients with new, unexplained, clinically significant unformed stools. NAAT is highly sensitive but may detect colonization; toxin results, stool burden, alternative causes, host factors, and disease severity must be integrated before treatment.

Fidaxomicin is preferred for an initial nonfulminant episode when available, while oral vancomycin remains an effective alternative. Metronidazole is not preferred for routine adult CDI. Fulminant disease requires vancomycin 500 mg enterally every six hours plus IV metronidazole, with rectal vancomycin when ileus limits drug delivery.

Recurrence management should focus on sustained response and restoration of colonization resistance. Fidaxomicin, vancomycin taper-and-pulse therapy, bezlotoxumab, FDA-approved microbiota products, and carefully screened FMT each have roles depending on recurrence number, risk factors, access, and patient-specific safety considerations.

The most important preventive interventions are appropriate testing, prompt contact precautions, soap-and-water handwashing when caring for CDI patients, sporicidal environmental cleaning, and reduction of unnecessary antibiotic exposure. Fulminant disease requires early surgical involvement before irreversible shock develops.

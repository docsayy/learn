Post–Cardiac Arrest Care

Added July 15, 2026 • Pulmonary and Critical Care • ~30 min read

A practical guide to the first hours after return of spontaneous circulation, oxygen and ventilation targets, hemodynamics, coronary angiography, temperature control, seizure detection, cause evaluation, organ support, multimodal neurologic prognostication, and family communication.

Clinical Overview

Post–cardiac arrest syndrome includes brain injury, myocardial dysfunction, systemic ischemia-reperfusion injury, and the unresolved cause of arrest. Early care must prevent secondary injury while defining the arrest etiology.

Stabilize → Treat Cause → Prevent Secondary Brain Injury → Prognosticate Late

Immediate Stabilization

  • Confirm airway and waveform capnography.
  • Avoid hypoxemia and hyperoxemia.
  • Target normocapnia unless severe acidemia or another indication requires adjustment.
  • Treat hypotension and assess cardiac function.
  • Obtain ECG, glucose, electrolytes, lactate, and targeted imaging.
  • Search for reversible causes.

Oxygen and Ventilation

  • After reliable measurement, titrate oxygen rather than leaving FiO₂ at 1.0.
  • The 2025 AHA algorithm targets PaO₂ roughly 60–105 mm Hg.
  • Target PaCO₂ about 35–45 mm Hg in most patients, avoiding severe hypocapnia.
  • Use lung-protective ventilation and monitor for aspiration or ARDS.

Hemodynamics

  • Target MAP at least about 65 mm Hg and individualize to perfusion and chronic hypertension.
  • Use norepinephrine for vasodilatory hypotension.
  • Assess post-arrest myocardial dysfunction with echo.
  • Use cautious fluid and add inotrope for low output when needed.
  • Consider mechanical circulatory support in selected refractory shock.

Coronary and Cause Evaluation

  • Urgent angiography is indicated when STEMI or ongoing coronary occlusion is strongly suspected.
  • In patients without ST elevation, immediate routine angiography is not required for everyone; individualize for shock, electrical instability, ischemia, and likelihood of coronary cause.
  • Evaluate PE, hemorrhage, toxicologic, respiratory, metabolic, and neurologic causes.

Temperature Control

  • Actively prevent fever in patients who do not follow commands after ROSC.
  • Use a deliberate temperature-control strategy according to current protocol.
  • Control shivering with stepwise nonpharmacologic and pharmacologic measures.
  • Avoid rapid uncontrolled rewarming.

Seizures and EEG

  • Obtain EEG in patients not following commands, especially with myoclonus or abnormal movements.
  • Treat electrographic seizures and status epilepticus.
  • Do not assume early myoclonus alone proves hopeless prognosis.
  • Correct glucose, sodium, temperature, and sedative confounders.

Neurologic Prognostication

Use a multimodal approach after confounders are minimized and sufficient time has passed, commonly at least 72 hours after ROSC or normothermia. Integrate examination, EEG, imaging, biomarkers, and somatosensory evoked potentials where available.

No single early sign should determine withdrawal of life-sustaining treatment.

Communication and Organ Support

  • Explain uncertainty and the timeline for prognostication.
  • Use clear daily goals.
  • Treat infection, AKI, hepatic injury, and coagulopathy.
  • Provide DVT prophylaxis, nutrition, pressure-injury prevention, and rehabilitation planning.

Common Pitfalls

  • Hyperoxia after ROSC
  • Severe hypocapnia
  • Premature prognostication
  • Using early myoclonus alone
  • Ignoring the cause of arrest
  • No fever-prevention plan
  • Unstructured family communication

Bottom Line

After ROSC, normalize oxygen and ventilation, maintain perfusion, treat the arrest cause, prevent fever, detect seizures, and delay neurologic conclusions until a multimodal assessment is reliable.

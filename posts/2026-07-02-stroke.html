Stroke Localization, Imaging, TIA Mimics, and Acute Ischemic Stroke: Complete Resident Guide

Added July 2, 2026 • Neurology • ~45 min read

Stroke care is pattern recognition plus speed. This guide combines bedside neurologic exam, vascular territories, stroke syndromes, acute ischemic stroke pathophysiology, TIA versus mimics, brain imaging, thrombolysis, thrombectomy, complications, and secondary prevention into one practical resident-level framework.

Core Mental Model

The fastest stroke approach is: time course → focal deficit → vascular territory → imaging → reperfusion decision → secondary prevention.

  • Sudden focal deficit is vascular until proven otherwise.
  • Cortical signs localize to cortex: aphasia, neglect, gaze preference, visual field cut, apraxia.
  • Pure motor or pure sensory deficits without cortical signs suggest lacunar/subcortical stroke.
  • Crossed findings suggest brainstem: ipsilateral cranial nerve deficit plus contralateral body weakness or sensory loss.
  • Posterior circulation strokes often present with dizziness, diplopia, dysarthria, dysphagia, ataxia, or visual symptoms.

Memory Aid: “Cortex talks, capsule moves, brainstem crosses.”

  • Cortex talks: aphasia, neglect, visual field deficit.
  • Capsule moves: dense pure motor face/arm/leg weakness.
  • Brainstem crosses: cranial nerve sign on one side, body deficit on the other.

Stepwise Neurologic Exam

Step 0: Safety and One-Minute Triage

  • Check airway and respiratory effort, especially with bulbar symptoms, myasthenia, GBS, or depressed mental status.
  • Check point-of-care glucose immediately for any focal deficit or altered mental status.
  • Ask last known well and symptom time course.
  • Sudden onset suggests vascular; progressive onset suggests infectious, inflammatory, toxic-metabolic, or compressive etiologies.
If the story is sudden and focal, treat as stroke until proven otherwise.

Mental Status

  • Level of arousal: alert, drowsy, stuporous, comatose.
  • Attention: months backward or WORLD backward.
  • Orientation: person, place, time, situation.
  • Immediate and delayed recall.

Poor attention suggests delirium or diffuse encephalopathy. Profound arousal impairment suggests brainstem, thalamic, or severe systemic disease.

Speech and Language

  • Fluency: spontaneous speech.
  • Comprehension: follow a two-step command.
  • Repetition: “No ifs, ands, or buts.”
  • Naming: pen, watch, badge.
  • Articulation: dysarthria versus aphasia.
Aphasia localizes to dominant hemisphere cortex, usually MCA territory. Dysarthria can be motor pathway, brainstem, cerebellar, or bulbar.

Cranial Nerves

  • CN II: pupils, visual fields, fundus if possible.
  • CN III/IV/VI: extraocular movements, diplopia, ptosis, nystagmus.
  • CN V/VII: facial sensation and facial movement.
  • CN VIII: hearing and vestibular symptoms.
  • CN IX/X: palate elevation, voice quality, dysphagia, cough/gag context.
  • CN XI: shoulder shrug.
  • CN XII: tongue deviation and fasciculations.

Motor Exam

  • Observe bulk, atrophy, and fasciculations.
  • Check tone: spastic versus flaccid.
  • Check pronator drift.
  • Grade proximal and distal strength in all limbs.

Reflexes and Babinski

  • Hyperreflexia, spasticity, and Babinski suggest UMN lesion.
  • Atrophy, fasciculations, and hyporeflexia suggest LMN lesion.

Sensory Exam

  • Light touch and pinprick.
  • Vibration and proprioception at the toes.
  • Look for a sensory level if myelopathy is possible.

Coordination and Gait

  • Finger-nose-finger, heel-shin, rapid alternating movements.
  • Truncal ataxia and gait are critical in posterior circulation presentations.
  • Romberg positive suggests proprioceptive loss.
In a dizzy patient, inability to walk normally is a major central red flag.

HINTS Exam

HINTS is only for acute vestibular syndrome: continuous vertigo lasting hours to days, spontaneous nystagmus, nausea/vomiting, and gait instability. It is not for brief positional dizziness.

Component Peripheral Pattern Central/Stroke Pattern
Head impulse Abnormal corrective saccade Normal head impulse in continuous AVS
Nystagmus Unidirectional horizontal Direction-changing, vertical, or pure torsional
Test of skew No vertical skew Vertical corrective movement

Memory Aid: “INFARCT” for Central HINTS

  • Impulse normal
  • Nystagmus direction-changing
  • Failure of vertical alignment: skew
  • Ataxia severe
  • Risk factors present
  • Cranial nerve signs
  • Treat as posterior stroke

Arterial Supply to the Brain

The brain receives blood from two paired systems:

  • Internal carotid arteries: anterior circulation.
  • Vertebral arteries: posterior circulation.

The Circle of Willis provides potential collateral flow via anterior and posterior communicating arteries, but it is often anatomically incomplete.

Memory Aid: “Anterior = Arm/Speech; Posterior = Vision/Vertigo”

  • Anterior circulation: weakness, sensory loss, aphasia, neglect.
  • Posterior circulation: dizziness, diplopia, dysarthria, dysphagia, ataxia, vision loss.

Anterior Circulation

Internal Carotid Artery

  • Feeds ACA and MCA territories.
  • Can produce large hemispheric stroke patterns.
  • May cause severe contralateral weakness, aphasia if dominant hemisphere, neglect if nondominant hemisphere.
  • Can cause transient monocular vision loss through ophthalmic/retinal circulation: amaurosis fugax.
  • ICA terminus occlusion is a high-risk LVO and thrombectomy target.
If the exam looks like “half the brain is offline,” think ICA terminus or proximal MCA.

Anterior Cerebral Artery

  • Supplies medial frontal and parietal lobes.
  • Controls leg-predominant motor and sensory cortex.
  • Can affect paracentral lobule causing urinary, and sometimes fecal, incontinence.
  • Can affect prefrontal/anterior cingulate regions causing abulia or akinetic mutism.
  • Dominant ACA involvement can cause transcortical motor aphasia.
ACA Finding Localization
Contralateral leg > arm weakness Medial motor cortex
Contralateral leg sensory loss Medial sensory cortex
Urinary incontinence Paracentral lobule
Abulia Prefrontal/anterior cingulate
Akinetic mutism Bilateral ACA involvement
Nonfluent speech with preserved repetition Transcortical motor aphasia

Middle Cerebral Artery

  • Supplies lateral frontal, parietal, and temporal lobes.
  • Most common large-vessel stroke territory.
  • Face and arm deficits are greater than leg deficits.
  • Dominant MCA causes aphasia.
  • Nondominant MCA causes neglect, anosognosia, constructional apraxia.
  • Large MCA strokes often cause gaze deviation toward the lesion.
MCA Division High-Yield Deficits
Superior division Face/arm weakness and sensory loss, Broca aphasia, frontal eye field deficit
Inferior division Wernicke aphasia, contralateral homonymous hemianopia
Dominant hemisphere Aphasia
Nondominant hemisphere Neglect and apraxia

Memory Aid: “MCA = Mouth, hand, speech, eyes.”

  • Mouth/hand: face and arm weakness.
  • Speech: aphasia if dominant.
  • Eyes: gaze preference and visual field cut.

Watershed Zones

  • ACA-MCA watershed: proximal arm and leg weakness/sensory loss; “man-in-a-barrel” pattern.
  • MCA-PCA watershed: higher visual association deficits such as prosopagnosia or Balint syndrome.
  • Usually caused by global hypoperfusion, severe carotid disease plus hypotension, cardiac arrest, shock, or aggressive blood pressure lowering.

Posterior Circulation

Vertebral Arteries

  • Arise from subclavian arteries and form the basilar artery.
  • Supply medulla through vertebral/anterior spinal branches.
  • Give rise to PICA, which supplies lateral medulla and posterior inferior cerebellum.

Basilar Artery

  • Supplies pons and contributes to cerebellar blood supply.
  • Branches include paramedian pontine branches, AICA, SCA, and PCA.
  • Basilar occlusion can cause coma, quadriparesis, locked-in syndrome, and respiratory compromise.
Basilar artery occlusion is a neurologic catastrophe. If posterior circulation stroke is suspected, CTA head and neck is critical.

Posterior Cerebral Artery

  • Supplies occipital lobes, inferior/medial temporal lobes, thalamus, and parts of midbrain.
  • Classic finding: contralateral homonymous hemianopia.
  • Can cause memory impairment, confusion, thalamic pain syndrome, or midbrain syndromes.
  • Weakness may be minimal, so NIHSS can underestimate disability.

Lacunar Stroke Syndromes

Lacunar strokes are small subcortical infarcts usually due to small-vessel disease from hypertension and diabetes. They typically lack cortical signs.

Syndrome Findings Typical Location
Pure motor hemiparesis Face, arm, and leg weakness without cortical signs Posterior limb internal capsule, pons
Pure sensory stroke Isolated hemisensory loss Thalamus
Ataxic hemiparesis Weakness plus ataxia Internal capsule, pons
Dysarthria-clumsy hand Slurred speech plus hand clumsiness Pons or internal capsule

Memory Aid: “Lacunes are PURE.”

  • Pure motor
  • Unilateral sensory
  • Rough speech/clumsy hand
  • Equal face-arm-leg pattern without cortical signs

Brainstem Stroke Syndromes

General Rule

Crossed findings = brainstem until proven otherwise.

Brainstem strokes commonly produce ipsilateral cranial nerve findings and contralateral long-tract findings.

Midbrain: PCA Territory

Syndrome Structures Findings
Weber CN III + corticospinal tract Ipsilateral CN III palsy + contralateral hemiplegia
Claude CN III + red nucleus Ipsilateral CN III palsy + contralateral ataxia
Benedikt CN III + red nucleus + corticospinal tract Ipsilateral CN III palsy + contralateral ataxia + contralateral hemiplegia

Medial Pons: Paramedian Basilar Branches

  • Ipsilateral CN VI palsy.
  • Internuclear ophthalmoplegia if MLF involved.
  • Contralateral loss of vibration/proprioception if medial lemniscus involved.
  • Contralateral hemiplegia if corticospinal tract involved.

Lateral Pons: AICA

  • Ipsilateral facial weakness.
  • Ipsilateral facial sensory loss.
  • Contralateral body pain/temperature loss.
  • Vertigo, nausea/vomiting, nystagmus.
  • Hearing loss or tinnitus.
  • Ipsilateral ataxia.
  • Ipsilateral Horner syndrome.

Medial Medulla: Vertebral / Anterior Spinal Artery

  • Ipsilateral hypoglossal palsy: tongue deviates toward the lesion.
  • Contralateral hemiplegia.
  • Contralateral loss of vibration/proprioception.

Memory Aid: Medial Medulla = “Dejerine has 3 D’s”

  • Deviation of tongue toward lesion.
  • Down body weakness on opposite side.
  • Dorsal column loss on opposite side.

Lateral Medulla: PICA / Vertebral Artery

  • Dysphagia, dysphonia, hoarseness from nucleus ambiguus involvement.
  • Decreased gag/cough reflex.
  • Vertigo, nausea/vomiting, nystagmus.
  • Ipsilateral facial pain/temperature loss.
  • Contralateral body pain/temperature loss.
  • Ipsilateral Horner syndrome.
  • Ipsilateral ataxia.

Memory Aid: Wallenberg = “Don’t PICA horse that can’t swallow.”

  • PICA supplies lateral medulla.
  • Horse voice = hoarseness.
  • Can’t swallow = dysphagia from nucleus ambiguus.
  • Add vertigo, ataxia, crossed pain-temperature loss, and Horner syndrome.

Etiology and Pathophysiology of Acute Ischemic Stroke

Major Mechanisms

Mechanism Examples Pattern
Global hypoperfusion Cardiac arrest, severe shock, severe hypoxemia, critical carotid stenosis + hypotension Watershed infarcts
Large-vessel thrombosis ICA, proximal MCA, vertebral, basilar atherosclerosis Territorial stroke or LVO
Small-vessel thrombosis Lenticulostriate arteries, pontine perforators Lacunar stroke
Artery-to-artery embolus ICA plaque, aortic arch plaque Embolic territorial infarct
Cardioembolism AF, atrial flutter, LV thrombus, LV aneurysm, severe HFrEF, valve disease, endocarditis, mechanical valve Multiple territories or cortical infarcts
Paradoxical embolus DVT crossing PFO or atrial septal defect Cryptogenic stroke pattern, young patient, VTE history
Other Vasculitis, dissection, hypercoagulable disorders Depends on vessel involved

Thrombotic Risk Factors

  • Hypertension
  • Diabetes mellitus
  • Hyperlipidemia
  • Smoking
  • Obesity
  • Older age, male sex, family history, genetic dyslipidemia

Vasculitis Causes

  • Autoimmune: Takayasu arteritis, giant cell arteritis, primary CNS vasculitis.
  • Infectious: tuberculosis, varicella zoster virus, syphilis.

Dissection Causes

  • Trauma or neck manipulation.
  • Iatrogenic vascular injury.
  • Connective tissue disorders such as Marfan syndrome or Ehlers-Danlos syndrome.

Hypercoagulable Causes

  • Factor V Leiden
  • Protein C or S deficiency
  • Antithrombin III deficiency
  • Prothrombin gene mutation
  • Antiphospholipid syndrome
  • Polycythemia
  • Heparin-induced thrombocytopenia

TIA vs Mimics

A TIA is a transient focal neurologic deficit caused by ischemia without persistent infarction. Clinically, treat it as a stroke warning shot until proven otherwise.

Sudden negative symptoms are more TIA-like. Gradual spreading positive symptoms are more mimic-like.
Feature TIA More Likely Mimic More Likely
Onset Sudden, clear time Gradual build or march
Symptom type Negative: weakness, numbness, aphasia, vision loss Positive: tingling, flashing lights, jerking
Pattern All symptoms start together Symptoms spread across modalities
Common clues Vascular risk factors, AF, carotid disease Migraine history, seizure history, syncope prodrome

Common Mimics

Mimic Clues Why It Mimics Stroke
Migraine aura Visual scintillation, sensory march, gradual spread Positive symptoms can resemble focal deficits
Focal seizure Stereotyped episodes, automatisms, postictal confusion Todd paralysis may look like stroke
Syncope Lightheadedness, pallor, diaphoresis, loss of consciousness Global hypoperfusion can cause transient neurologic symptoms
Hypoglycemia Sweating, tremor, confusion, glucose abnormality Can produce focal deficits
Functional neurologic disorder Inconsistent or non-anatomic exam Diagnosis of exclusion in acute stroke setting

High-Risk TIA Features

  • Ongoing or recurrent symptoms: crescendo TIAs.
  • Weakness or aphasia.
  • Posterior circulation symptoms with inability to walk.
  • New AF or known cardioembolic source.
  • Carotid bruit or suspected high-grade stenosis.
  • Need for anticoagulation decisions.

Stroke Imaging

Non-Contrast CT Head

  • Answers: is there hemorrhage?
  • Answers: is there a large established infarct?
  • Does not rule out early ischemic stroke.
  • Poor sensitivity for posterior circulation stroke.
  • Determines safety of thrombolysis.
A normal CT head does not mean there is no stroke.

CTA Head and Neck

  • Answers: is there a large-vessel occlusion?
  • Identifies ICA, MCA M1/proximal M2, basilar, vertebral, and PCA occlusions.
  • Can identify arterial dissection or critical stenosis.
  • Determines thrombectomy eligibility and need for transfer.

CT Perfusion

  • Estimates infarct core and salvageable penumbra.
  • Low blood flow plus low blood volume suggests infarct core.
  • Delayed flow with preserved volume suggests penumbra.
  • Helps select patients for late-window thrombectomy.
Modern stroke care treats tissue, not just the clock.

MRI DWI and ADC

  • DWI detects acute infarction within minutes.
  • Acute infarct is bright on DWI and dark on ADC.
  • MRI is more sensitive than CT, especially for small or posterior circulation infarcts.

MRI FLAIR and DWI-FLAIR Mismatch

  • FLAIR helps estimate infarct age.
  • DWI positive with FLAIR negative suggests recent stroke.
  • DWI-FLAIR mismatch can support treatment decisions in wake-up or unknown-onset stroke.

Memory Aid: “CT = Blood, CTA = Block, CTP/MRI = Brain.”

  • CT: is there blood?
  • CTA: is there a blocked artery?
  • CTP/MRI: is tissue dead or salvageable?

Diagnostic Workup After Acute Stabilization

Immediate Tests

  • Point-of-care glucose.
  • Non-contrast CT head.
  • CTA head and neck if LVO is possible.
  • INR/PT/PTT if anticoagulation or thrombolysis decision is relevant.
  • CBC for anemia, thrombocytopenia, polycythemia.
  • BMP, especially renal function when contrast decisions are needed.

Etiology Workup

  • ECG and telemetry for AF/flutter.
  • TTE or TEE for cardiac source: atrial thrombus, LV thrombus, vegetation, low EF, wall motion abnormality, PFO.
  • Lipid panel and hemoglobin A1c.
  • Troponin if concern for myocardial ischemia or cardioembolic context.
  • TSH if concern for hyperthyroidism-triggered AF.
  • Hypercoagulable workup in young patients, recurrent thrombosis, unusual clotting history, or cryptogenic stroke context.
  • ESR/CRP, ANA/ANCA, infectious testing if vasculitis is suspected.

Acute Ischemic Stroke Treatment

Initial Priorities

  1. Airway, breathing, circulation.
  2. Last known well.
  3. Glucose correction if abnormal.
  4. Non-contrast CT to exclude hemorrhage.
  5. CTA to identify LVO.
  6. Determine thrombolysis and thrombectomy eligibility.

IV Thrombolysis

Alteplase/tPA activates plasminogen to plasmin, which breaks down fibrin in the clot.

  • Requires disabling neurologic deficit.
  • Requires no intracranial hemorrhage on CT.
  • Requires known treatment window or imaging-selected eligibility.
  • Classic alteplase dose: 0.9 mg/kg, 10% bolus, remainder over 60 minutes.
  • Blood pressure must usually be below 185/110 before treatment.

Mechanical Thrombectomy

  • For large-vessel occlusion such as ICA, MCA M1/proximal M2, basilar, and selected other proximal occlusions.
  • Can be performed after tPA if eligible.
  • Best as early as possible.
  • Late-window thrombectomy depends on imaging showing salvageable tissue.
  • Uses stent retriever and/or aspiration techniques.
Do not delay CTA in a patient who may have LVO. Thrombectomy eligibility depends on finding the blocked artery.

Blood Pressure in Acute Ischemic Stroke

Scenario Typical BP Goal
Before thrombolysis Less than 185/110
After thrombolysis, first 24 hours Less than 180/105 or institution-specific similar goal
No thrombolysis or thrombectomy Permissive hypertension, often treat only if above 220 systolic or 120 diastolic unless another emergency exists

Common IV Agents

  • Nicardipine infusion.
  • IV labetalol.
  • Hydralazine in selected settings, though effect can be variable.
  • Clevidipine or enalaprilat depending on institutional practice.

Secondary Prevention

Antiplatelet Therapy

  • Aspirin is commonly used after ischemic stroke when thrombolysis-related timing allows.
  • Clopidogrel may be used depending on syndrome, recurrence, or stent context.
  • Dual antiplatelet therapy is used in selected minor stroke/TIA or stent scenarios, depending on protocol.

Anticoagulation

  • Used for cardioembolic sources such as AF/flutter, selected LV thrombus, mechanical valves, or other high-risk cardioembolic causes.
  • Warfarin is preferred for mechanical valves and some valvular contexts.
  • DOACs such as apixaban or rivaroxaban are commonly used for nonvalvular AF when appropriate.
  • Avoid immediate anticoagulation after large infarcts because of hemorrhagic conversion risk.

PFO / Paradoxical Embolus

  • Consider in cryptogenic stroke, especially young patient with DVT/VTE history.
  • Management may include antiplatelet therapy, anticoagulation in selected cases, or percutaneous closure depending on patient selection.

Carotid Revascularization

  • Symptomatic high-grade carotid stenosis, especially greater than 70%, often needs vascular intervention.
  • Options include carotid endarterectomy or carotid artery stenting.
  • Choice depends on anatomy, surgical risk, age, timing, and institutional expertise.

Risk Factor Modification

  • High-intensity statin.
  • Blood pressure control after acute phase.
  • Diabetes optimization.
  • Smoking cessation.
  • Weight management and exercise when safe.
  • Sleep apnea evaluation when appropriate.

Complications After Stroke

Hemorrhagic Conversion After Thrombolysis

  • New neurologic decline after tPA requires stat CT head.
  • Stop tPA if still infusing.
  • Check fibrinogen.
  • Consider cryoprecipitate, tranexamic acid, and platelet transfusion per local protocol.

tPA-Associated Angioedema

  • Facial, tongue, or airway swelling can occur.
  • Treat with antihistamines, H2 blocker, steroids, and airway planning.
  • Low threshold for intubation if airway edema is progressing.

Cerebral Edema and Malignant MCA Infarct

  • Large hemispheric infarcts can develop cytotoxic edema and midline shift.
  • Consider neurosurgery for decompressive hemicraniectomy.
  • Temporizing medical therapy includes hypertonic saline or mannitol.
  • Hypertonic saline strategies often target serum sodium goals such as 150–155 depending on ICU protocol.

Seizures

  • More common with cortical infarcts.
  • Can be focal, focal-to-bilateral tonic-clonic, or nonconvulsive status epilepticus.
  • Treat with antiseizure medications when seizures occur; consider EEG if unexplained altered mental status.

Aspiration and Dysphagia

  • Stroke can impair swallowing and cough protection.
  • Keep NPO until swallow screen is passed.
  • Speech-language pathology evaluation is essential.
  • Use enteral feeding access if unsafe swallow persists.
  • Treat aspiration pneumonia with antibiotics when infection develops, then narrow based on cultures and course.

Bottom Line

Stroke care starts with recognizing a sudden focal deficit and rapidly localizing it. Cortical signs point to MCA/ACA/PCA cortex, pure motor or sensory deficits suggest lacunar disease, and crossed cranial nerve plus body findings suggest brainstem stroke. Non-contrast CT answers “is there blood?”, CTA answers “is there a blocked artery?”, and perfusion/MRI answers “is there salvageable brain?” Acute therapy depends on time, tissue, deficit severity, bleeding risk, and LVO status. After the acute decision, the work becomes finding the mechanism: large-vessel disease, small-vessel disease, cardioembolism, paradoxical embolus, dissection, vasculitis, or hypercoagulability — then preventing the next stroke.

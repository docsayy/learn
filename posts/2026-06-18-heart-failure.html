Heart Failure: A Practical Guide for Internal Medicine Residents

Added June 18, 2026 • Cardiology • ~15 min read

A practical resident-level review of heart failure, including symptoms, HFrEF vs HFpEF, ejection fraction cutoffs, systolic and diastolic physiology, GDMT, diuresis, device therapy, iron replacement, and documentation pearls.

Overview

Heart failure occurs when the heart cannot generate enough effective forward flow to meet the body’s needs, or can only do so at the cost of elevated filling pressures.

The key concept is that poor forward flow leads to blood backing up into the lungs, venous system, liver, and peripheral tissues.

Heart failure is not just “too much fluid.” It is a syndrome of impaired forward flow and elevated filling pressures.

Signs and Symptoms of Heart Failure

Pulmonary Congestion

When blood backs up into the lungs, patients develop pulmonary edema and respiratory symptoms.

  • Shortness of breath
  • Dyspnea on exertion
  • Orthopnea
  • Paroxysmal nocturnal dyspnea
  • Pleural effusions
  • Pulmonary edema

Chest X-Ray Findings

  • Bilateral hazy opacities
  • Bat-wing pulmonary edema pattern
  • Pleural effusions
  • Vascular congestion

Systemic Venous Congestion

Fluid can also back up into the venous system.

  • Jugular venous distension
  • Peripheral edema
  • Congestive hepatopathy
  • Abnormal liver tests
  • Elevated BNP from chamber stretch
Congestive hepatopathy may cause bilirubin and liver test abnormalities due to hepatic venous congestion.

HFrEF, HFmrEF, and HFpEF

The first major branch point in heart failure management is the ejection fraction.

Type Older Term Ejection Fraction
HFrEF Systolic heart failure EF ≤ 40%
HFmrEF Mildly reduced / mid-range EF EF 41–49%
HFpEF Diastolic heart failure EF ≥ 50%
It is important to distinguish HFrEF from HFpEF because the evidence-based treatments differ substantially.

Systolic vs Diastolic Physiology

HFrEF: Systolic Dysfunction

HFrEF is primarily a problem of impaired contraction. The ventricle often becomes dilated, thin-walled, and unable to pump blood forward effectively.

  • Reduced contractility
  • Reduced ejection fraction
  • Often associated with eccentric remodeling
  • May occur after ischemic injury, myocarditis, toxins, or other causes

HFpEF: Diastolic Dysfunction

HFpEF is primarily a problem of impaired filling. The ventricle can contract, but it is stiff and cannot relax or fill normally.

  • Preserved ejection fraction
  • Impaired ventricular relaxation
  • High filling pressures
  • Often associated with concentric hypertrophy
  • Commonly related to long-standing hypertension and chronic metabolic disease
HFrEF is a squeeze problem. HFpEF is usually a filling problem.

Etiology of Heart Failure

HFrEF: Ischemic vs Non-Ischemic

For HFrEF, determine whether the cause is ischemic or non-ischemic.

Category Common Causes
Ischemic Coronary artery disease, myocardial infarction
Non-ischemic Viral myocarditis, alcohol, drugs, chemotherapy, genetic disorders, pregnancy, left-to-right shunts, Takotsubo cardiomyopathy
Ischemic cardiomyopathy carries a higher risk of malignant arrhythmias and may affect ICD decision-making.

HFpEF Etiologies

  • Long-standing hypertension
  • Obesity
  • Type 2 diabetes
  • Chronic kidney disease
  • COPD
  • Obstructive sleep apnea
  • Amyloidosis
  • Sarcoidosis
  • Scleroderma
  • Constrictive pericarditis
  • Radiation fibrosis or pericardial calcification

Guideline-Directed Medical Therapy for HFrEF

The core of HFrEF management is guideline-directed medical therapy, or GDMT.

GDMT means guideline-directed medical therapy, not goal-directed medical therapy.

The Four Pillars of HFrEF Therapy

Pillar Examples Benefit
Beta blocker Bisoprolol, metoprolol succinate, carvedilol Mortality benefit
ACE / ARB / ARNI Lisinopril, losartan, sacubitril-valsartan Mortality benefit
SGLT2 inhibitor Empagliflozin, dapagliflozin Mortality and hospitalization benefit
Mineralocorticoid receptor antagonist Spironolactone, eplerenone Mortality benefit

Beta Blockers in HFrEF

Only certain beta blockers have proven mortality benefit in HFrEF.

Beta Blocker Key Point
Bisoprolol More beta-1 selective; useful when reactive airway disease is a concern
Metoprolol succinate Once-daily extended-release formulation with mortality benefit
Carvedilol Beta and alpha blockade; useful for blood pressure control

Metoprolol Tartrate vs Succinate

Metoprolol tartrate is often used inpatient because it is easier to titrate. Metoprolol succinate is the once-daily formulation with mortality benefit in HFrEF.

Metoprolol tartrate 25 mg twice daily is approximately equivalent to metoprolol succinate 50 mg daily.

Choosing a Beta Blocker

  • Reactive airway disease: consider bisoprolol or a more beta-1 selective agent.
  • Adherence concerns: once-daily metoprolol succinate may be easier than carvedilol.
  • Need more blood pressure control: carvedilol may be helpful because of alpha blockade.

ACE Inhibitors, ARBs, and ARNIs

Neurohormonal blockade is central in HFrEF.

Class Example Notes
ACE inhibitor Lisinopril Cost-effective; can cause dry cough or angioedema
ARB Losartan, valsartan Alternative if ACE inhibitor cough occurs
ARNI Sacubitril-valsartan Strong evidence for improved outcomes but may cause hypotension and cost issues

ACE to ARNI Washout

When switching from an ACE inhibitor to an ARNI, allow a 36-hour washout period to reduce risk of angioedema.

If switching from an ARB to an ARNI, a washout period is generally not required.

SGLT2 Inhibitors and Mineralocorticoid Receptor Antagonists

SGLT2 Inhibitors

SGLT2 inhibitors improve outcomes in HFrEF regardless of whether the patient has diabetes.

  • Empagliflozin
  • Dapagliflozin
  • Often used at 10 mg daily for heart failure
  • Generally require adequate kidney function, commonly eGFR above approximately 20 depending on agent and indication

Mineralocorticoid Receptor Antagonists

  • Spironolactone is commonly used first.
  • Eplerenone can be used if gynecomastia or endocrine side effects occur.
  • Monitor potassium and kidney function.

Iron Replacement in HFrEF

Iron deficiency is common in HFrEF and may worsen exercise tolerance and quality of life.

Iron Deficiency Threshold in HFrEF Interpretation
Ferritin < 100 Iron deficiency
Ferritin < 300 with transferrin saturation < 20% Iron deficiency in HFrEF trial definitions
IV iron replacement in HFrEF may improve symptoms, six-minute walk distance, and quality of life.

One commonly used inpatient regimen is iron sucrose 200 mg IV daily for five days, depending on local protocols and patient-specific factors.

ICD and CRT

ICD for Primary Prevention

An implantable cardioverter-defibrillator, or ICD, can reduce risk of sudden cardiac death in selected patients with persistently reduced EF.

Scenario Typical Consideration
Ischemic cardiomyopathy EF < 35% after at least 3 months of optimized GDMT
Non-ischemic cardiomyopathy Consider after a longer period of optimized therapy, often around 6 months depending on context
Prior sudden cardiac death or malignant ventricular arrhythmia Secondary prevention ICD consideration

Cardiac Resynchronization Therapy

Cardiac resynchronization therapy, or CRT, can improve outcomes in selected patients with reduced EF and electrical dyssynchrony.

Classic CRT candidate: EF < 35%, left bundle branch block, and QRS duration > 150 ms.

In left bundle branch block, the ventricles contract out of sync. CRT helps coordinate ventricular contraction and can improve systolic function.

Diuresis and Preload Management

Diuretics improve congestion and symptoms by reducing preload. They do not provide the same mortality benefit as the four pillars of GDMT, but they are essential for decongestion.

Loop Diuretics

  • Furosemide
  • Bumetanide
  • Torsemide

Initial Inpatient Diuretic Dose

For patients admitted with heart failure exacerbation, a common approach is to convert the home loop diuretic dose to IV dosing and give at least the equivalent or higher IV dose.

The DOSE trial supports using higher-dose diuresis strategies in hospitalized heart failure. A common practical approach is about 2–2.5 times the home dose depending on severity and clinical context.

Choosing a Loop Diuretic

Medication Clinical Notes
Furosemide Common first-line loop diuretic
Bumetanide May be considered if poor gut absorption or severe congestion; can cause myalgias
Torsemide Longer duration of action; sometimes used when furosemide effect seems short-lived
Severe heart failure, cardiogenic shock, or poor diuretic response requires close monitoring and may need more aggressive diuretic strategy or ICU-level management.

HFpEF Management

HFpEF has fewer therapies with clear mortality benefit compared with HFrEF. The mainstay remains treating underlying contributors and managing congestion.

Core Approach

  • Treat hypertension
  • Manage volume overload
  • Treat obesity, diabetes, CKD, COPD, and sleep apnea
  • Evaluate for infiltrative or restrictive disease when suspected
  • Address atrial fibrillation and other rhythm issues

Medications With Evidence in HFpEF

Medication Class Potential Benefit
SGLT2 inhibitors Reduced cardiovascular death and/or heart failure hospitalization in HFpEF trials
Mineralocorticoid receptor antagonists May reduce recurrent heart failure hospitalizations in selected patients
HFpEF treatment is still mostly about treating the underlying disease and controlling congestion, but SGLT2 inhibitors now have important supporting evidence.

Mortality in Heart Failure

Heart failure is a high-mortality condition, even when it appears chronic or stable.

Five-year mortality for both systolic and diastolic heart failure is often cited around 50%.

This is why heart failure should be treated seriously and optimized aggressively when appropriate.

Documentation Pearls

Heart failure documentation matters for clinical clarity and coding.

Include Acuity

  • Acute
  • Chronic
  • Acute on chronic

Include Type

  • Systolic heart failure
  • Diastolic heart failure
  • Combined systolic and diastolic heart failure

Examples

Better Documentation Less Helpful Documentation
Acute on chronic systolic heart failure exacerbation HFrEF
Chronic diastolic heart failure HFpEF
Acute on chronic combined systolic and diastolic heart failure CHF

Additional Useful Details

  • Ischemic vs non-ischemic etiology
  • NYHA class
  • ACC/AHA stage
  • Most recent EF
  • Dry weight
  • Admission weight and current weight
  • Home diuretic regimen
  • Current GDMT and barriers to optimization
Dry weight helps the team understand how much additional fluid likely needs to be removed before discharge.

Take-Home Pearls

  • Heart failure causes poor forward flow and fluid backup into the lungs, venous system, liver, and legs.
  • HFrEF is EF ≤ 40%, HFmrEF is EF 41–49%, and HFpEF is EF ≥ 50%.
  • HFrEF is mainly a systolic squeeze problem; HFpEF is mainly a diastolic filling problem.
  • The four pillars of HFrEF GDMT are beta blocker, ACE/ARB/ARNI, SGLT2 inhibitor, and MRA.
  • Only bisoprolol, metoprolol succinate, and carvedilol have proven mortality benefit among beta blockers for HFrEF.
  • Switching from ACE inhibitor to ARNI requires a 36-hour washout.
  • SGLT2 inhibitors benefit HFrEF even without diabetes.
  • Diuretics improve symptoms and congestion but are not the core mortality-reducing therapy.
  • ICD and CRT should be considered in appropriate patients with persistently reduced EF.
  • HFpEF therapy focuses on treating underlying disease, managing congestion, and considering SGLT2 inhibitors.
  • Heart failure has high five-year mortality and should not be treated casually.
  • Document acuity, type, EF, ischemic status, NYHA class, ACC/AHA stage, and dry weight when possible.

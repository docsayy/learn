Hyponatremia: The Complete Resident Guide

Added June 17, 2026 • Nephrology • ~10 min read

A practical resident-focused guide to evaluating and treating hyponatremia using serum osmolality, volume status, urine osmolality, urine sodium, and safe correction principles.

Overview

Hyponatremia is one of the most common electrolyte problems in hospital medicine. It is also one of the most commonly misunderstood because the correct treatment depends heavily on the underlying cause.

The key is not to memorize random causes. The key is to approach every patient with the same simple framework: first determine the serum osmolality, then determine the patient’s volume status.

Clinical Pearl: Hyponatremia treatment depends on etiology. Normal saline, fluid restriction, and diuresis can each be correct in different patients — and harmful in the wrong patient.

Definition of Hyponatremia

Hyponatremia is defined as a serum sodium concentration below 135 mEq/L.

Serum Sodium Interpretation
<135 mEq/L Hyponatremia
135-145 mEq/L Normal range
>145 mEq/L Hypernatremia

Once hyponatremia is identified, the next step is not immediately treatment. The next step is classification.

The First Two Questions

For every patient with hyponatremia, ask two questions:

  1. What is the serum osmolality?
  2. What is the patient’s volume status?

These two questions determine the rest of the workup and usually point toward the correct treatment.

Simple Framework: Serum osmolality tells you what type of hyponatremia the patient has. Volume status tells you the likely cause and treatment.

Step 1: Check Serum Osmolality

Serum osmolality divides hyponatremia into three major categories: hypotonic, isotonic, and hypertonic.

Serum Osmolality Category Common Meaning
<280 mOsm/kg Hypotonic hyponatremia Most true hyponatremia
280-290 mOsm/kg Isotonic hyponatremia Pseudohyponatremia
>290 mOsm/kg Hypertonic hyponatremia Usually hyperglycemia

Most patients with hyponatremia have hypotonic hyponatremia, but checking osmolality prevents you from missing pseudohyponatremia or hyperglycemia-related hyponatremia.

Pseudohyponatremia and Hypertonic Hyponatremia

Pseudohyponatremia

Pseudohyponatremia is an apparent low sodium caused by laboratory interference rather than true hypotonic hyponatremia.

Common causes include markedly elevated:

  • Triglycerides
  • Lipids
  • Proteins

In this situation, the patient does not truly have low effective serum tonicity. Whole blood electrolytes can help confirm the true sodium.

Hypertonic Hyponatremia

Hypertonic hyponatremia is most commonly caused by severe hyperglycemia. Glucose raises serum osmolality and pulls water out of cells into the extracellular space, diluting the measured sodium.

Important: Hyperglycemia-associated hyponatremia is not pseudohyponatremia. It is a real dilutional sodium change caused by osmotic water shifts.

Corrected Sodium for Hyperglycemia

When glucose is elevated, sodium should be corrected to estimate the true sodium level.

Corrected Na = Measured Na + 2 × ((Glucose - 100) / 100)

A common practical rule is to add about 2 mEq/L to the sodium for every 100 mg/dL increase in glucose above 100 mg/dL.

Example 1

A patient has glucose 400 mg/dL and sodium 129 mEq/L.

Glucose above 100 = 300
300 / 100 = 3
3 × 2 = 6
Corrected Na = 129 + 6 = 135 mEq/L

Example 2

A patient has glucose 800 mg/dL and sodium 120 mEq/L.

Glucose above 100 = 700
700 / 100 = 7
7 × 2 = 14
Corrected Na = 120 + 14 = 134 mEq/L

Different references may use correction factors such as 1.6 or 2.4. Using 2 is a simple bedside approximation.

Step 2: Hypotonic Hyponatremia

After confirming hypotonic hyponatremia, the next step is determining volume status.

Volume Status Typical Problem Typical Treatment
Hypovolemic Total body sodium and water loss, sodium loss greater than water loss Normal saline
Euvolemic Excess free water without obvious edema or dehydration Fluid restriction
Hypervolemic Total body water excess with edema states Diuresis and fluid restriction
Common Mistake: Giving normal saline to every patient with hyponatremia can worsen sodium in SIADH or hypervolemic states.

Urine Studies: Urine Sodium and Urine Osmolality

Two urine studies are especially useful before major fluid resuscitation is given:

  • Urine sodium
  • Urine osmolality

Urine Sodium

Urine Sodium Interpretation
<20 mEq/L Kidneys are holding onto sodium appropriately
>20 mEq/L Kidneys are losing sodium or unable to retain it appropriately

Urine Osmolality

Urine Osmolality Interpretation
<100 mOsm/kg ADH is suppressed or not active
>100 mOsm/kg ADH is active
Bedside Translation: Urine sodium tells you whether the kidney is holding sodium. Urine osmolality tells you whether ADH is acting.

Hypovolemic Hyponatremia

Hypovolemic hyponatremia occurs when the patient has low effective circulating volume. The body releases ADH to preserve volume, which causes water retention and worsens hyponatremia.

Urine Sodium <20 mEq/L

This means the kidneys are appropriately trying to retain sodium because volume is being lost somewhere outside the kidney.

  • Vomiting
  • Diarrhea
  • GI losses
  • Third spacing

Urine Sodium >20 mEq/L

This suggests renal sodium loss despite hypovolemia.

  • Diuretic use
  • Renal salt wasting
  • Mineralocorticoid deficiency

Treatment

  • Give isotonic fluids, usually normal saline
  • Hold offending diuretics when appropriate
  • Treat the cause of volume loss

Euvolemic Hyponatremia

Euvolemic hyponatremia means the patient does not appear clearly volume depleted or overloaded. The most common cause is SIADH.

SIADH

SIADH occurs when ADH remains active despite low serum osmolality, causing free water retention.

Common triggers include:

  • Pain
  • Nausea
  • Vomiting
  • Pulmonary disease
  • CNS disease
  • Malignancy, especially lung cancer
  • Medications

In SIADH, urine osmolality is usually greater than 100 mOsm/kg because ADH is active.

Clinical Clue: If normal saline makes the sodium worse, SIADH becomes more likely.

Other Causes to Rule Out

  • Hypothyroidism
  • Adrenal insufficiency

Low Solute or Excess Water Intake

If urine osmolality is less than 100 mOsm/kg, ADH is not the main driver. Consider conditions where the patient has too much free water or too little solute.

  • Tea and toast diet
  • Beer potomania
  • Primary polydipsia

Treatment

  • Fluid restriction
  • Treat the underlying trigger
  • Increase solute intake when low-solute intake is the cause

Hypervolemic Hyponatremia

Hypervolemic hyponatremia occurs when total body water and sodium are both increased, but water is increased more than sodium. These patients often have edema or ascites.

Urine Sodium <20 mEq/L

This suggests the kidneys are sensing low effective arterial volume and are retaining sodium.

  • Heart failure
  • Cirrhosis
  • Nephrotic syndrome

Urine Sodium >20 mEq/L

This suggests impaired renal sodium handling.

  • Renal failure

Treatment

  • Fluid restriction
  • Loop diuretics when appropriate
  • Treat the underlying heart, liver, or kidney disease

Practical Bedside Algorithm

  1. Confirm sodium is below 135 mEq/L.
  2. Check serum osmolality.
  3. If isotonic, consider pseudohyponatremia.
  4. If hypertonic, check glucose and correct sodium.
  5. If hypotonic, assess volume status.
  6. Check urine osmolality and urine sodium before giving fluids when possible.
  7. Treat based on category: fluids for hypovolemic, fluid restriction for euvolemic, diuresis/fluid restriction for hypervolemic.
One-Line Algorithm: Osmolality first, volume status second, urine studies third, treatment fourth.

Symptomatic Hyponatremia

The above approach applies mainly to stable or asymptomatic patients. Symptomatic hyponatremia is treated differently and more urgently.

Concerning symptoms include:

  • Seizures
  • Severe confusion
  • Coma
  • Severe neurologic symptoms

Symptomatic severe hyponatremia generally requires 3% hypertonic saline.

3% hypertonic saline: 100 mL bolus
May repeat up to two additional 100 mL boluses if severe symptoms persist

The initial goal is usually to raise serum sodium by about 4-6 mEq/L over the first few hours to stabilize neurologic symptoms.

Safety Point: Severe symptomatic hyponatremia is a medical emergency. Hypertonic saline should be given with close sodium monitoring.

Safe Correction and Osmotic Demyelination

The speed of correction depends on whether hyponatremia is acute or chronic.

Duration Definition Correction Approach
Acute <48 hours Can generally correct more rapidly if needed
Chronic >48 hours or unknown duration Correct slowly to avoid osmotic demyelination

For chronic hyponatremia, a common target is correction by about 6-8 mEq/L over 24 hours.

Overcorrection Risk: Correcting chronic hyponatremia too quickly can cause osmotic demyelination syndrome, historically called central pontine myelinolysis.
Memory Aid: “Low to high, pons will die. High to low, brains will blow.” Rapid correction of hyponatremia risks osmotic demyelination; rapid correction of hypernatremia risks cerebral edema.

Key Takeaways

  • Hyponatremia is sodium below 135 mEq/L.
  • The first step is serum osmolality.
  • Most true hyponatremia is hypotonic.
  • Hyperglycemia causes hypertonic hyponatremia and requires sodium correction.
  • After confirming hypotonic hyponatremia, assess volume status.
  • Urine sodium helps determine whether the kidney is holding or losing sodium.
  • Urine osmolality helps determine whether ADH is active.
  • Hypovolemic hyponatremia is usually treated with normal saline.
  • Euvolemic hyponatremia, especially SIADH, is usually treated with fluid restriction.
  • Hypervolemic hyponatremia is usually treated with fluid restriction and diuresis.
  • Symptomatic severe hyponatremia requires hypertonic saline.
  • Chronic hyponatremia must be corrected slowly to avoid osmotic demyelination.

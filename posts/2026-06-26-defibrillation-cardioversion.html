Defibrillation and Cardioversion: The Complete Resident Guide

Added June 25, 2026 • Cardiology • ~16 min read

Defibrillation and cardioversion are life-saving electrical therapies used to terminate dangerous tachyarrhythmias. This guide reviews the difference between unsynchronized defibrillation and synchronized cardioversion, shock indications, energy selection, pad placement, sedation, electrical storm, special populations, complications, and a practical bedside checklist for residents.

Fundamental Concepts

Defibrillation and cardioversion both deliver electrical current across the myocardium to depolarize a critical mass of cardiac tissue and interrupt dangerous arrhythmia circuits.

Therapy Synchronization Used For
Defibrillation Unsynchronized Pulseless rhythms such as VF and pulseless VT
Cardioversion Synchronized to the R-wave Unstable or symptomatic tachyarrhythmias with a pulse
Defibrillation is the reset button for pulseless arrest rhythms. Cardioversion is the synchronized reboot for unstable tachyarrhythmias with a pulse.

Defibrillation: The Pulseless Algorithm

Defibrillation is used for shockable cardiac arrest rhythms.

Indications

  • Ventricular fibrillation
  • Pulseless ventricular tachycardia
  • Ventricular flutter

Shockable Rhythm Features

Rhythm ECG Appearance Clinical State
Ventricular fibrillation Chaotic irregular waveform without organized QRS complexes No pulse
Pulseless ventricular tachycardia Wide-complex tachycardia, often regular or semi-regular No pulse
Ventricular flutter Sine-wave pattern, usually very rapid Often pulseless or unstable

Defibrillation Principles

  • Use biphasic defibrillation when available.
  • Start at 200 J or manufacturer-recommended dose for biphasic devices.
  • Subsequent shocks should be the same or higher energy.
  • Do not decrease energy after failed shocks.
  • Minimize interruptions in CPR.
  • Resume CPR immediately after shock delivery.
Time to shock is critical. Every minute of delayed defibrillation reduces survival substantially.

Cardioversion: The Synchronized Shock

Cardioversion is synchronized to the R-wave so that the shock is not delivered during the vulnerable repolarization period.

Indications

Rhythm When to Cardiovert
Atrial fibrillation Hemodynamic instability or selected symptomatic patients
Atrial flutter Unstable or symptomatic; often converts at lower energy
SVT Unstable or refractory to vagal maneuvers/adenosine
Monomorphic VT with pulse Synchronized cardioversion if unstable but pulse present

Synchronization Explained

The defibrillator detects the R-wave and times shock delivery to avoid the T-wave. Shocking during the T-wave can trigger the R-on-T phenomenon and precipitate ventricular fibrillation.

For tachyarrhythmias with a pulse, always confirm the machine is in SYNC mode before delivering cardioversion.

Monophasic vs Biphasic Waveforms

Feature Monophasic Biphasic
Current Direction Single direction Reverses direction mid-shock
Typical Energy 360 J 120–200 J initially, depending on device and rhythm
Myocardial Injury Greater Less
First-Shock Success Lower Higher
Current Role Largely obsolete Standard of care
Biphasic waveforms achieve effective defibrillation at lower energy with less post-shock myocardial dysfunction.

Pad Placement

Standard Positions

Position Placement Common Use
Anterior-Lateral Right sternal border below clavicle + left mid-axillary line General resuscitation and rapid placement
Anterior-Posterior Anterior pad near left sternal border + posterior pad near left scapula Cardioversion, atrial arrhythmias, obesity, pregnancy, small children

Practical Pad Pearls

  • Anterior-lateral is fast and appropriate in most arrests.
  • Anterior-posterior may improve current vector for atrial arrhythmias.
  • Do not place pads over implanted pacemakers or ICD generators.
  • Ensure pads are firmly adhered with good skin contact.
  • Remove medication patches from the shock pathway.
In an emergency, the fastest effective pad placement is usually the best pad placement.

Energy Selection by Rhythm

Rhythm Initial Energy Shock Type
VF / Pulseless VT 200 J biphasic or device recommendation Unsynchronized defibrillation
Atrial fibrillation 200 J biphasic Synchronized cardioversion
Atrial flutter 50–100 J Synchronized cardioversion
SVT 50–100 J Synchronized cardioversion
Monomorphic VT with pulse 100 J Synchronized cardioversion
Polymorphic VT with instability Defibrillation energy Usually unsynchronized if sync unreliable
If the rhythm is pulseless, do not waste time synchronizing. Defibrillate.

Sedation for Elective Cardioversion

Synchronized cardioversion is painful. Stable patients should receive procedural sedation when time allows.

Pre-Sedation Requirements

  • Assess airway risk.
  • Confirm monitoring: ECG, blood pressure, pulse oximetry.
  • Have airway equipment available.
  • Have suction available.
  • Confirm IV access.
  • Verify anticoagulation strategy when AF duration is greater than 48 hours or unknown.

Common Sedation Agents

Agent Typical Dose Advantages Disadvantages
Propofol 0.5–1 mg/kg IV Rapid onset and recovery Hypotension, respiratory depression
Etomidate 0.15–0.3 mg/kg IV Hemodynamically stable Myoclonus, adrenal suppression concern
Methohexital 0.5–1.5 mg/kg IV Rapid onset and recovery Respiratory depression, hypotension
Midazolam 0.05–0.1 mg/kg IV Anxiolysis and amnesia Slower recovery, respiratory depression
Choose sedation based on hemodynamics, airway risk, comorbidities, and local practice.

Electrical Storm: When Shocks Keep Happening

Electrical storm is recurrent ventricular arrhythmia requiring repeated therapy.

Electrical storm = ≥3 episodes of sustained VT/VF or ICD shocks within 24 hours

Immediate Management

  1. Correct reversible causes: ischemia, hypokalemia, hypomagnesemia, hypoxia, acidosis, drug toxicity.
  2. Provide sedation to reduce catecholamine surge.
  3. Use antiarrhythmics such as amiodarone or lidocaine when appropriate.
  4. Use beta-blockade to reduce sympathetic drive if tolerated.
  5. Consult electrophysiology early.
  6. Consider catheter ablation for refractory VT.
  7. Consider mechanical circulatory support if shock develops.

Medication Options

Medication Role
Amiodarone First-line antiarrhythmic in many VT/VF storm cases
Lidocaine Useful especially when ischemia-related VT is suspected
Beta blockers Reduce sympathetic tone; esmolol useful in ICU due to short half-life
Magnesium Essential for torsades; useful adjunct in ventricular arrhythmias

Double-Sequential Defibrillation

In refractory VF, double-sequential defibrillation may be considered in some systems. This uses two defibrillators with two pad vectors, commonly anterior-lateral and anterior-posterior.

Electrical storm is not just an arrhythmia problem. Treat ischemia, electrolytes, catecholamine surge, hypoxia, acidosis, and shock physiology.

Special Populations

Pregnancy

  • Use standard adult energy doses.
  • Place patient in left lateral tilt when possible.
  • Use anterior-posterior pads when practical.
  • Do not delay life-saving shock therapy.
  • Use fetal monitoring when viable gestation and feasible after maternal stabilization.

Pacemakers and ICDs

  • Avoid placing pads directly over the device generator.
  • Place pads at least several centimeters away from the device.
  • Use a current vector that avoids passing directly through the generator when possible.
  • Interrogate the device after shock when clinically appropriate.
  • Magnet application may be needed in selected situations to prevent ICD therapies.

Obesity

  • Higher energy may be required due to increased transthoracic impedance.
  • Anterior-posterior pad placement may improve current vector.
  • Ensure firm pad contact.
  • Escalate energy if initial shocks fail.

Pacemaker-Dependent Patients

  • Transient capture failure can occur after shocks.
  • Have transcutaneous pacing available.
  • Temporary pacing may be needed if capture is lost.

Complications and Pitfalls

Complication Mechanism Prevention / Management
Skin burns High energy, poor pad contact Good pad adhesion, avoid reused/damaged pads
Myocardial injury High cumulative energy Use biphasic waveform and appropriate energy
Post-shock PVCs or NSVT Myocardial irritation Usually monitor if self-limited
VF induction Unsynchronized shock during vulnerable period Use SYNC for tachyarrhythmias with pulse
Bradycardia or asystole Sinus node suppression or conduction disease Atropine, pacing if unstable
Embolic stroke LAA thrombus dislodgment after AF cardioversion Anticoagulation or TEE strategy when indicated
Respiratory depression Sedation medication Airway readiness and monitoring
The most dangerous procedural mistake is delivering an unsynchronized shock to a patient with a pulse when synchronized cardioversion is required.

TEE-Guided Cardioversion Pathway

For atrial fibrillation lasting more than 48 hours or of unknown duration, cardioversion requires a strategy to reduce embolic stroke risk.

Option 1: Conventional Anticoagulation Strategy

  1. Therapeutic anticoagulation for at least 3 weeks.
  2. Cardioversion.
  3. Continue anticoagulation for at least 4 weeks afterward.
  4. Continue long-term anticoagulation if CHA₂DS₂-VASc indicates.

Option 2: TEE-Guided Strategy

  1. Perform TEE to exclude left atrial appendage thrombus.
  2. If no thrombus, cardiovert.
  3. Continue anticoagulation for at least 4 weeks afterward.
  4. Continue long-term anticoagulation based on stroke risk.
If TEE shows left atrial appendage thrombus, cancel cardioversion and anticoagulate before reassessment.

Practical Checklist for Residents

Before Cardioversion

  • Confirm the rhythm.
  • Confirm the patient has a pulse.
  • Determine stability.
  • Obtain consent if non-emergent.
  • Review anticoagulation status if AF duration is greater than 48 hours or unknown.
  • Attach pads in appropriate position.
  • Turn on SYNC mode.
  • Confirm sync markers are on R-waves, not T-waves.
  • Select energy.
  • Prepare sedation and airway equipment.
  • Announce “all clear.”
  • Deliver shock.

After the Shock

  • Check rhythm.
  • Check pulse and blood pressure.
  • Assess for bradycardia or asystole.
  • Repeat shock at same or higher energy if needed.
  • Monitor for neurologic symptoms after AF cardioversion.
  • Continue anticoagulation when indicated.
Always verify SYNC immediately before cardioversion. Some defibrillators reset sync mode after a shock.

Common Clinical Scenarios

Scenario 1: VF After Initial 200 J Failure

Continue CPR, give ACLS medications as indicated, correct reversible causes, and deliver the next shock at the same or higher energy.

Scenario 2: AF with RVR and Hemodynamic Instability

Perform synchronized cardioversion at 200 J. Verify markers are on the R-waves before shocking. Anticoagulate after stabilization when appropriate.

Scenario 3: Torsades de Pointes

  • Defibrillate if pulseless.
  • Give magnesium 2 g IV.
  • Correct potassium and magnesium.
  • Stop QT-prolonging medications.
  • Consider overdrive pacing if recurrent.

Scenario 4: Atrial Flutter with 2:1 Block

Atrial flutter with 2:1 conduction often presents with a ventricular rate around 150 and can be mistaken for sinus tachycardia.

Atrial flutter often converts with lower energy than atrial fibrillation, commonly 50–100 J synchronized.

Scenario 5: Monomorphic VT with a Pulse

If unstable but pulse is present, perform synchronized cardioversion. If pulseless, defibrillate.

Clinical Pearls

  • Defibrillation is unsynchronized and used for VF or pulseless VT.
  • Cardioversion is synchronized and used for unstable tachyarrhythmias with a pulse.
  • For cardioversion, verify sync markers are on R-waves.
  • Unsynchronized shocks can trigger R-on-T and VF.
  • Biphasic defibrillation is standard of care.
  • Do not decrease energy after failed shocks.
  • Resume CPR immediately after defibrillation in cardiac arrest.
  • AF cardioversion usually starts at 200 J biphasic.
  • Atrial flutter often converts with 50–100 J.
  • Use sedation for elective cardioversion when time allows.
  • Etomidate is useful when hypotension is a concern.
  • Electrical storm requires sedation, antiarrhythmics, beta blockade, and correction of triggers.
  • Pregnancy uses standard adult energy doses.
  • Avoid placing pads directly over pacemaker or ICD generators.
  • Cardioversion for AF longer than 48 hours requires anticoagulation or TEE strategy.

Bottom Line

Defibrillation and cardioversion are related but distinct electrical therapies. Defibrillation is unsynchronized and used for pulseless shockable rhythms such as VF and pulseless VT. Cardioversion is synchronized to the R-wave and used for unstable tachyarrhythmias with a pulse. Always confirm whether the patient has a pulse, choose the correct shock mode, select appropriate energy, use sedation when feasible, and verify synchronization before cardioversion. In AF longer than 48 hours or of unknown duration, use anticoagulation or a TEE-guided pathway to reduce embolic risk.

Central Nervous System Infections: The Complete Advanced Resident Guide

Added July 14, 2026 • Infectious Disease • ~48 min read

Central nervous system infections are neurologic and infectious disease emergencies in which delays in antimicrobial therapy, neuroimaging, lumbar puncture, neurosurgical source control, or management of intracranial pressure can produce irreversible disability or death. This guide reviews acute bacterial meningitis, viral and autoimmune encephalitis, brain abscess, subdural empyema, healthcare-associated ventriculitis, chronic meningitis, opportunistic CNS infection, CSF interpretation, empiric treatment, neurologic complications, public-health management, and high-yield board and bedside pearls.

Clinical Overview

Central nervous system infection may involve the meninges, brain parenchyma, ventricular system, epidural or subdural spaces, spinal cord, nerve roots, or a localized intracranial collection.

Major Categories

  • Meningitis
  • Encephalitis
  • Meningoencephalitis
  • Brain abscess
  • Subdural or epidural empyema
  • Healthcare-associated ventriculitis
  • Chronic meningitis
  • Myelitis, radiculitis, or neuritis
Suspected Bacterial Meningitis or HSV Encephalitis = Treat Before Diagnostic Certainty

The Major Clinical Questions

  1. Is the syndrome meningitis, encephalitis, a focal mass lesion, or a combination?
  2. Does the patient need antimicrobials before imaging or lumbar puncture?
  3. Is lumbar puncture safe now?
  4. Which host factors and exposures change the differential?
  5. Does the patient require neurosurgical drainage or device removal?
  6. Is intracranial pressure, seizure activity, hydrocephalus, or vascular injury present?
  7. What public-health precautions or prophylaxis are required?
The first priority is not identifying the exact organism. The first priority is preventing treatment delay in a time-sensitive syndrome while preserving diagnostic yield whenever safely possible.

Time-Critical CNS Infections

  • Acute bacterial meningitis
  • HSV encephalitis
  • Brain abscess with mass effect
  • Subdural empyema
  • Ventriculitis with infected hardware
  • Cryptococcal meningitis with severe intracranial hypertension
  • Tuberculous meningitis
  • Any CNS infection with herniation, status epilepticus, shock, or declining consciousness

Major CNS Infection Syndromes

Syndrome Core Features Common Diagnostic Priority
Meningitis Headache, fever, neck stiffness, photophobia, altered mental status Blood cultures, lumbar puncture, immediate antibiotics when bacterial disease is possible
Encephalitis Altered cognition or behavior, seizures, focal deficits, movement disorder MRI, CSF PCR, EEG, empiric acyclovir
Brain abscess Headache, focal deficit, seizure, mass effect; fever may be absent Contrast MRI, neurosurgical aspiration or excision
Subdural empyema Fever, severe headache, rapidly progressive focal deficits or seizures Emergency MRI and neurosurgical drainage

Meningitis vs Encephalitis

Meningitis primarily produces meningeal symptoms:

  • Headache
  • Neck stiffness
  • Photophobia
  • Pain with movement

Encephalitis produces evidence of brain dysfunction:

  • Confusion
  • Personality or behavioral change
  • Memory impairment
  • Seizures
  • Focal neurologic deficits
  • Movement disorder
Many patients have meningoencephalitis and do not fit neatly into one category.

Host Factors That Change the Differential

  • Age
  • Pregnancy
  • Alcohol use disorder
  • Cellular immune deficiency
  • Neutropenia
  • Solid-organ or stem-cell transplantation
  • Recent neurosurgery
  • CSF shunt or drain
  • Head trauma or CSF leak
  • Sinus, dental, or otologic infection
  • Travel and geographic exposure
  • Mosquito or tick exposure
  • Animal exposure
  • Injection drug use

Initial Emergency Approach

Step 1: Recognize the Syndrome

Suspect bacterial meningitis with:

  • Fever
  • Severe headache
  • Neck stiffness
  • Altered mental status
  • Seizure
  • Petechial or purpuric rash

Suspect encephalitis with altered mental status lasting more than a transient period plus one or more of:

  • Fever
  • Seizure
  • Focal deficit
  • CSF inflammation
  • Compatible MRI or EEG findings

Step 2: Obtain Blood Cultures

  • Obtain two blood-culture sets before antibiotics when this does not create delay.
  • Blood cultures may establish the organism even when CSF culture becomes negative after antibiotics.

Step 3: Decide Whether LP Can Occur Immediately

  • If no clinical reason for neuroimaging first exists, perform prompt lumbar puncture.
  • If CT or MRI is needed before LP, obtain cultures and begin treatment first.

Step 4: Begin Empiric Treatment

Blood Cultures → Dexamethasone When Indicated → Antibiotics ± Acyclovir

Do not wait for:

  • CT completion
  • Radiology interpretation
  • Lumbar puncture
  • CSF Gram stain
  • PCR results
  • Infectious disease consultation

Step 5: Use Appropriate Isolation

Use droplet precautions when meningococcal disease is possible until at least 24 hours of effective therapy have been completed.

A patient sent for CT before LP should not remain untreated while waiting in radiology.

Stabilization and ICU Care

Airway

Consider intubation for:

  • Loss of airway protection
  • Refractory seizure
  • Impending herniation
  • Severe agitation preventing safe care
  • Respiratory failure

Circulation

  • Treat septic shock promptly.
  • Avoid hypotension, particularly when cerebral perfusion may be compromised.
  • Use isotonic crystalloid and vasopressors according to the hemodynamic state.

Neurologic Monitoring

  • Serial Glasgow Coma Scale
  • Pupillary examination
  • Motor examination
  • Seizure monitoring
  • Signs of hydrocephalus or herniation

ICU Indications

  • Shock
  • Coma or rapidly declining mental status
  • Status epilepticus
  • Mechanical ventilation
  • Raised intracranial pressure
  • Hydrocephalus
  • Extensive brain abscess or empyema
  • Severe electrolyte disturbance
  • Meningococcemia with DIC

Fluids

Do not routinely fluid-restrict every patient with meningitis. Treat shock and dehydration while monitoring sodium, urine output, cerebral edema, and the possibility of SIADH or cerebral salt wasting.

When to Obtain Neuroimaging Before Lumbar Puncture

Neuroimaging before LP is intended to identify patients with substantial mass effect, obstructive hydrocephalus, or another condition that may increase procedural risk. It should not become an automatic step for every patient.

Consider CT or MRI Before LP With:

  • Focal neurologic deficit
  • Papilledema
  • New-onset seizure
  • Markedly impaired consciousness
  • Known intracranial mass lesion
  • History of major CNS disease
  • Significant immunocompromise
  • Concern for brain abscess or subdural empyema
  • Concern for obstructive hydrocephalus

Imaging Does Not Make LP Universally Safe

A normal CT does not eliminate all risk. Clinical examination, coagulation status, platelet count, and the suspected diagnosis remain important.

When LP Should Be Deferred

  • Evidence of impending herniation
  • Large mass lesion with significant mass effect
  • Obstructive hydrocephalus
  • Severe cardiorespiratory instability
  • Uncorrected major coagulopathy
  • Severe thrombocytopenia
  • Infection over the puncture site
If imaging is required before LP, administer empiric therapy immediately after blood cultures rather than waiting for imaging.

Lumbar Puncture

Essential Components

  1. Measure opening pressure with the patient appropriately positioned.
  2. Record CSF appearance.
  3. Collect sufficient CSF in sequential sterile tubes.
  4. Send cell count and differential.
  5. Send protein and glucose with paired serum glucose.
  6. Send Gram stain and bacterial culture.
  7. Add syndrome-specific molecular, fungal, mycobacterial, or cytologic testing.

Opening Pressure

Opening pressure is especially important in suspected:

  • Cryptococcal meningitis
  • Tuberculous meningitis
  • Idiopathic intracranial hypertension mimic
  • Cerebral venous sinus thrombosis
  • Chronic meningitis

CSF Volume

Larger-volume CSF collection improves diagnostic yield in chronic meningitis, particularly for:

  • Mycobacterial culture
  • Fungal culture
  • Cytology
  • Flow cytometry

Traumatic Tap

A falling RBC count between tubes supports but does not prove a traumatic tap. RBC correction formulas are imprecise and should not overrule the clinical syndrome.

Xanthochromia

Xanthochromia may occur with:

  • Subarachnoid hemorrhage
  • Markedly elevated CSF protein
  • Hyperbilirubinemia

Recommended CSF Studies

Core Tests

  • Opening pressure
  • WBC count and differential
  • RBC count
  • Protein
  • Glucose with paired serum glucose
  • Gram stain
  • Bacterial culture

Acute Encephalitis Tests

  • HSV-1 and HSV-2 PCR
  • VZV PCR
  • Enterovirus PCR
  • Arboviral serology according to season and exposure
  • Additional pathogen testing based on host factors

Chronic Meningitis Tests

  • Cryptococcal antigen
  • Fungal culture
  • AFB smear and mycobacterial culture
  • TB molecular testing
  • Syphilis testing
  • Lyme antibody index when appropriate
  • Cytology
  • Flow cytometry

Immunocompromised Host Tests

  • CMV PCR
  • EBV PCR in selected CNS-lymphoma evaluations
  • JC-virus PCR
  • Toxoplasma PCR
  • Fungal markers or sequencing
  • Broad-range bacterial or fungal PCR
  • Metagenomic next-generation sequencing in selected unresolved cases
Do not send every available CSF test indiscriminately. Low-pretest-probability testing increases false-positive and clinically misleading results.

CSF Pattern Interpretation

Condition Opening Pressure Cells Glucose Protein
Typical bacterial meningitis Often elevated Neutrophilic pleocytosis Low; CSF/serum ratio often reduced Elevated
Viral meningitis Normal or mildly elevated Lymphocytic, though neutrophils may predominate early Usually normal Mildly elevated
Tuberculous meningitis Often elevated Lymphocytic or mixed Low Elevated
Cryptococcal meningitis Frequently elevated Lymphocytic; may be minimal in severe immunosuppression Low or normal Elevated
Fungal meningitis Often elevated Lymphocytic or mixed Often low Elevated
Healthcare-associated ventriculitis Variable May be neutrophilic or minimally abnormal May be low May be elevated

Important Exceptions

  • Early viral meningitis may be neutrophil predominant.
  • Partially treated bacterial meningitis may become lymphocyte predominant.
  • Listeria may produce a mixed or lymphocytic pattern.
  • Severe immunosuppression may produce little pleocytosis.
  • Normal CSF chemistry does not reliably exclude device-associated ventriculitis.
  • A negative Gram stain does not exclude bacterial meningitis.

CSF Glucose

Interpret CSF glucose relative to the serum glucose obtained near the time of LP.

Low CSF Glucose → Bacterial, TB, Fungal, Malignant, or Inflammatory Meningitis

Extremely High CSF Protein

Very high protein may occur with:

  • Spinal block
  • TB or fungal meningitis
  • Severe bacterial meningitis
  • Neoplastic meningitis
  • Guillain–Barré syndrome

PCR and Molecular Testing

Multiplex Meningitis-Encephalitis Panels

Multiplex panels may rapidly identify selected bacterial, viral, and fungal pathogens, but results require clinical interpretation.

Limitations

  • Not all pathogens are included.
  • False-positive results occur.
  • Latent viral DNA may occasionally be detected.
  • A negative panel does not exclude meningitis or encephalitis.
  • Culture remains necessary for susceptibility testing.
  • Prior probability and CSF pattern matter.

HSV PCR

  • Highly useful but may be negative very early.
  • If suspicion remains high, continue acyclovir and repeat CSF PCR after several days.
  • Blood contamination and late testing can affect interpretation.

VZV Testing

VZV PCR may be useful early. Intrathecal VZV antibody can improve sensitivity in delayed vasculopathy presentations.

Metagenomic Sequencing

Metagenomic next-generation sequencing may identify unexpected pathogens in selected unresolved cases, but:

  • Turnaround may be prolonged.
  • Contamination and incidental detection occur.
  • Negative testing does not eliminate infection.
  • Results should be interpreted with microbiology and infectious disease expertise.

Acute Bacterial Meningitis

Clinical Presentation

  • Fever
  • Headache
  • Neck stiffness
  • Altered mental status
  • Nausea or vomiting
  • Photophobia
  • Seizure
  • Focal neurologic deficit
  • Petechial or purpuric rash
The classic triad of fever, neck stiffness, and altered mental status is not present in every patient. Its absence does not exclude bacterial meningitis.

Older or Immunocompromised Adults

Presentations may be subtle and include:

  • Delirium
  • Lethargy
  • Hypothermia
  • Minimal neck stiffness
  • Falls
  • Seizure

Initial Management Bundle

  1. Droplet precautions when meningococcus is possible.
  2. Two blood-culture sets.
  3. Dexamethasone immediately before or with the first antibiotic dose when indicated.
  4. Empiric bactericidal meningitis-dose antibiotics.
  5. Acyclovir when encephalitis remains possible.
  6. LP without delay when safe.
  7. Neuroimaging first only when clinically indicated.
  8. ICU and neurologic monitoring when severe.

Empiric Therapy for Community-Acquired Adult Meningitis

Adults Approximately 18–50 Years

Vancomycin + Ceftriaxone

Typical Doses

  • Vancomycin using serious-CNS-infection dosing and therapeutic monitoring
  • Ceftriaxone 2 g IV every 12 hours

Adults Older Than Approximately 50 Years or With Listeria Risk

Vancomycin + Ceftriaxone + Ampicillin

Listeria Risk Factors

  • Older age
  • Pregnancy
  • Alcohol use disorder
  • Cellular immunosuppression
  • Malignancy
  • Transplantation
  • Chronic corticosteroid use

Typical Ampicillin Dose

  • Ampicillin 2 g IV every 4 hours with renal adjustment

Severe Beta-Lactam Allergy

Management depends on the nature of the allergy, local resistance, organism risk, and availability of alternatives. Potential components may include:

  • Vancomycin
  • Aztreonam for selected gram-negative coverage
  • Moxifloxacin in selected circumstances
  • TMP-SMX for Listeria coverage
  • Rapid allergy assessment or desensitization when the preferred beta-lactam is essential
Do not substitute a lower-dose nonmeningitis regimen. CNS infection requires drug selection and dosing that reliably achieve therapeutic CSF exposure.

Recent Neurosurgery, Penetrating Trauma, or CSF Device

Vancomycin + Cefepime, Ceftazidime, or Meropenem

Choose the antipseudomonal beta-lactam according to:

  • Local susceptibility
  • Previous patient cultures
  • Recent antimicrobial exposure
  • Known colonization with resistant organisms

Adjunctive Dexamethasone

Purpose

Dexamethasone reduces the inflammatory injury caused by rapid bacterial killing, particularly in pneumococcal meningitis.

Timing

Give Before or With the First Antibiotic Dose

Benefit is less certain when dexamethasone is started well after antibiotics.

Typical Adult Regimen

  • Dexamethasone 10 mg IV every 6 hours for 4 days

When to Continue

Continuation is most strongly supported when pneumococcal meningitis is confirmed or remains highly likely.

When to Reconsider

  • Alternative nonbacterial diagnosis becomes clear
  • CSF findings are inconsistent with bacterial meningitis
  • Another steroid contraindication becomes dominant

Important Nuances

  • Do not delay antibiotics while obtaining dexamethasone.
  • Monitor glucose.
  • GI protection should be individualized.
  • Steroids may reduce CSF penetration of vancomycin, reinforcing the importance of appropriate dosing and monitoring.

Organism Typical Preferred Treatment Usual Duration Framework

S. pneumoniae Vancomycin plus ceftriaxone initially; narrow based on susceptibility Approximately 10–14 days
H. influenzae Ceftriaxone or cefotaxime Approximately 7 days
L. monocytogenes Ampicillin; TMP-SMX is an alternative At least approximately 21 days
Group B Streptococcus Penicillin G, ampicillin, or ceftriaxone Approximately 14–21 days

Duration Is Not Determined by Organism Alone

Extend or individualize treatment for:

  • Brain abscess
  • Ventriculitis
  • Endocarditis
  • Persistent bacteremia
  • Immunocompromise
  • Slow clinical response
  • Resistant organism
  • Uncontrolled parameningeal source

Meningococcal Meningitis and Meningococcemia

Clinical Features

  • Acute meningitis
  • Petechial or purpuric rash
  • Septic shock
  • DIC
  • Adrenal hemorrhage
  • Rapid progression

Isolation

Droplet Precautions Until 24 Hours of Effective Therapy

Public-Health Notification

Immediately notify the hospital infection-prevention team and appropriate public-health authority.

Who Is a Close Contact?

  • Household contacts
  • Childcare contacts
  • Anyone with direct exposure to oral secretions
  • Healthcare personnel with unprotected airway or secretion exposure

Who Usually Does Not Need Prophylaxis?

  • Casual workplace contacts
  • Healthcare personnel who provided routine care without secretion exposure
  • People merely present in the same building

Chemoprophylaxis Options

  • Rifampin
  • Ceftriaxone
  • Ciprofloxacin when local resistance permits
  • Azithromycin in selected ciprofloxacin-resistance circumstances
Ceftriaxone is generally preferred during pregnancy. Public-health guidance should determine the final prophylaxis choice because ciprofloxacin resistance varies geographically.

Carriage Eradication in the Patient

Ceftriaxone or cefotaxime generally eradicates nasopharyngeal carriage. If treatment used another agent that does not reliably eradicate carriage, additional eradication therapy may be required before discharge.

Pneumococcal Meningitis

Why It Is High Risk

  • High mortality
  • Hearing loss
  • Vascular complications
  • Seizures
  • Hydrocephalus
  • Resistant isolates

Empiric Therapy

  • Vancomycin plus ceftriaxone or cefotaxime
  • Dexamethasone before or with the first antibiotic dose

Definitive Therapy

Narrow according to penicillin and cephalosporin susceptibility.

Search for a Predisposing Condition

  • Otitis or mastoiditis
  • Sinusitis
  • Pneumonia
  • CSF leak
  • Basilar skull fracture
  • Cochlear implant
  • Asplenia
  • Humoral immune deficiency

Recurrent Pneumococcal Meningitis

Evaluate for:

  • Anatomic CSF leak
  • Skull-base defect
  • Cochlear-implant complication
  • Complement or antibody deficiency
  • Functional or anatomic asplenia

Listeria Meningitis and Rhombencephalitis

Risk Groups

  • Older adults
  • Pregnant patients
  • Transplant recipients
  • Malignancy
  • Chronic corticosteroid use
  • Cell-mediated immune deficiency
  • Alcohol use disorder

Clinical Patterns

  • Meningitis
  • Meningoencephalitis
  • Brainstem encephalitis
  • Multiple cranial neuropathies
  • Ataxia
  • Bacteremia

Treatment

Ampicillin at Meningitis Dose

TMP-SMX is an important alternative for severe beta-lactam allergy.

Gentamicin

Some clinicians add gentamicin in severe disease, but clinical benefit is uncertain and nephrotoxicity is important. Use should be individualized with infectious disease and pharmacy input.

Cephalosporins do not provide reliable Listeria coverage.

Repeat Lumbar Puncture

Not Routinely Required

A clinically improving patient with susceptible community-acquired bacterial meningitis generally does not require routine repeat LP.

Consider Repeat LP With:

  • No clinical improvement after appropriate therapy
  • Clinical deterioration
  • Resistant pneumococcus
  • Gram-negative bacillary meningitis
  • Healthcare-associated ventriculitis
  • Persistent positive cultures
  • Uncertain diagnosis
  • Immunocompromised host
  • Need for therapeutic pressure reduction in cryptococcosis

Do Not Use CSF Normalization Alone to Determine Cure

Cell count and protein may remain abnormal after effective treatment.

Encephalitis

Clinical Features

  • Altered consciousness
  • Confusion
  • Behavioral change
  • Memory loss
  • Seizure
  • Focal neurologic deficit
  • Movement disorder
  • Language dysfunction

Initial Evaluation

  1. Stabilize airway and circulation.
  2. Check glucose and major metabolic abnormalities.
  3. Obtain MRI brain with contrast when feasible.
  4. Perform LP when safe.
  5. Send HSV and VZV PCR.
  6. Obtain EEG.
  7. Begin IV acyclovir immediately.
  8. Add bacterial-meningitis therapy if meningitis cannot be excluded.

MRI

MRI is more sensitive than CT for:

  • Temporal-lobe HSV disease
  • Brainstem encephalitis
  • Small abscesses
  • Autoimmune encephalitis patterns
  • Vasculitic infarcts

EEG

EEG helps detect:

  • Nonconvulsive status epilepticus
  • Temporal periodic discharges
  • Diffuse encephalopathy
  • Ongoing seizure burden

Broad Differential

  • Infectious encephalitis
  • Autoimmune encephalitis
  • Toxic-metabolic encephalopathy
  • Nonconvulsive seizures
  • Stroke
  • Cerebral venous thrombosis
  • Malignancy
  • Medication toxicity

HSV Encephalitis

Classic Features

  • Fever
  • Altered behavior
  • Memory impairment
  • Aphasia
  • Focal seizure
  • Temporal-lobe MRI abnormalities

CSF Findings

  • Lymphocytic pleocytosis
  • Elevated protein
  • Normal glucose
  • RBCs may be present but are neither required nor specific

Empiric Treatment

Acyclovir 10 mg/kg IV Every 8 Hours

Adjust for renal function and use the locally recommended dosing weight in obesity.

Renal Protection

  • Ensure appropriate hydration.
  • Adjust for kidney function.
  • Infuse over an appropriate duration.
  • Monitor creatinine and urine output.
  • Avoid concurrent nephrotoxins when possible.

Initial Negative HSV PCR

If clinical suspicion remains high:

  • Continue acyclovir.
  • Repeat CSF HSV PCR after approximately 3–7 days.
  • Reassess MRI and EEG.

Duration

  • Usually 14–21 days
  • Longer courses may be used in immunocompromised patients
Do not stop acyclovir solely because an early HSV PCR is negative when the syndrome and MRI remain strongly suggestive.

Varicella-Zoster Virus CNS Infection

Clinical Syndromes

  • Meningitis
  • Encephalitis
  • Vasculopathy and stroke
  • Myelitis
  • Radiculitis
  • Cranial neuropathy
  • Cerebellitis

Rash May Be Absent

VZV vasculopathy and encephalitis can occur without a typical zoster rash.

Diagnosis

  • CSF VZV PCR
  • Intrathecal VZV antibody in selected delayed presentations
  • MRI brain or spine
  • Vascular imaging when vasculopathy is suspected

Treatment

  • IV acyclovir
  • Renal adjustment and hydration
  • Duration individualized by syndrome and immune status
  • Selected vasculopathy cases may receive adjunctive corticosteroids with specialist guidance

Arboviral Encephalitis

Important North American Viruses

  • West Nile virus
  • Eastern equine encephalitis virus
  • La Crosse virus
  • St. Louis encephalitis virus
  • Powassan virus

Diagnostic Approach

  • Season and geography
  • Mosquito or tick exposure
  • CSF and serum IgM testing
  • Public-health laboratory coordination

West Nile Clues

  • Older age or immune compromise
  • Tremor
  • Parkinsonian features
  • Myoclonus
  • Acute flaccid paralysis
  • Preserved sensation with motor weakness

Eastern Equine Encephalitis

May cause rapidly progressive severe encephalitis with cerebral edema, seizures, and high mortality.

Treatment

Treatment is primarily supportive:

  • Airway and ventilatory support
  • Seizure management
  • Intracranial-pressure management
  • Prevention of secondary complications
Arboviral PCR may be insensitive after the earliest phase. CSF IgM is often more useful.

Autoimmune Encephalitis

Autoimmune encephalitis may closely mimic infectious encephalitis.

Clues

  • Prominent psychiatric symptoms
  • New movement disorder
  • Autonomic instability
  • Refractory seizures
  • Orofacial dyskinesia
  • Rapid cognitive decline
  • Normal or mildly inflammatory CSF

Common Types

  • Anti-NMDA-receptor encephalitis
  • LGI1 encephalitis
  • CASPR2 encephalitis
  • GABA receptor encephalitis
  • Paraneoplastic encephalitis

Diagnostic Principles

  • Exclude or reasonably control infection before immunosuppression.
  • Send paired serum and CSF antibody testing.
  • Obtain EEG and MRI.
  • Search for associated malignancy.
  • Consult neurology early.
A positive serum antibody alone may be nonspecific. CSF findings and the clinical phenotype are essential.

Brain Abscess

A brain abscess is a localized collection of infection within brain parenchyma.

Routes of Infection

  • Contiguous spread from sinusitis, otitis, mastoiditis, or dental infection
  • Hematogenous spread from endocarditis, pulmonary infection, or bacteremia
  • Neurosurgery or penetrating trauma
  • Immunocompromised-host opportunistic infection

Clinical Presentation

  • Headache
  • Focal neurologic deficit
  • Seizure
  • Altered mental status
  • Fever may be absent
  • Nausea or vomiting
  • Papilledema
The classic triad of headache, fever, and focal deficit occurs in only a minority of patients.

Imaging

Contrast-Enhanced MRI With Diffusion-Weighted Imaging

Restricted diffusion in the abscess cavity supports pyogenic abscess but is not completely specific.

Lumbar Puncture

Lumbar puncture is usually low yield and may be dangerous when a brain abscess produces mass effect. Diagnosis generally relies on imaging, blood cultures, and neurosurgical sampling.

Neurosurgical Sampling

Aspiration or excision is generally recommended when feasible because it:

  • Establishes microbiology
  • Provides source control
  • Reduces mass effect
  • Distinguishes abscess from malignancy

When Medical Treatment Alone May Be Considered

  • Small lesion
  • Early cerebritis
  • Known organism from a reliable extracranial source
  • No significant mass effect
  • No impending rupture into the ventricle
  • Close MRI monitoring is feasible

Empiric Community-Acquired Regimen

Ceftriaxone or Cefotaxime + Metronidazole

Add Vancomycin With:

  • Recent neurosurgery
  • Penetrating trauma
  • Endocarditis or bacteremia with S. aureus
  • Known MRSA risk
  • Postoperative infection

Post-Neurosurgical Abscess

  • Vancomycin
  • Plus cefepime, ceftazidime, or meropenem
  • Add anaerobic coverage when the source requires it

Duration

  • Commonly 6–8 weeks
  • Individualize by surgery, organism, lesion number, immune status, and radiographic response

Corticosteroids

Avoid routine corticosteroids because they may reduce capsule formation and antimicrobial penetration.

Use when necessary for:

  • Life-threatening cerebral edema
  • Impending herniation
  • Severe mass effect

Seizure Prophylaxis

Treat documented seizures. Primary prophylaxis should be individualized with neurology or neurosurgery according to lesion location, cortical involvement, and operative factors.

Subdural Empyema

Subdural empyema is a purulent collection between the dura and arachnoid and can progress rapidly.

Risk Factors

  • Sinusitis
  • Otitis or mastoiditis
  • Neurosurgery
  • Head trauma
  • Dental infection

Clinical Features

  • Severe headache
  • Fever
  • Altered mental status
  • Focal deficit
  • Seizure
  • Rapid neurologic deterioration

Diagnosis

Emergency Contrast MRI

Treatment

  • Immediate neurosurgical drainage
  • Broad IV antibiotics
  • Treatment of the sinus, ear, or dental source
  • Seizure treatment and monitoring
Subdural empyema is a surgical emergency. Antibiotics alone are generally inadequate.

Healthcare-Associated Ventriculitis and Meningitis

Settings

  • External ventricular drain
  • Ventriculoperitoneal shunt
  • Ventriculoatrial shunt
  • Recent neurosurgery
  • Penetrating head trauma
  • Intrathecal infusion pump
  • Deep-brain stimulation hardware

Clinical Features

  • New headache
  • Nausea or vomiting
  • Lethargy
  • Altered mental status
  • Fever without another source
  • Shunt malfunction
  • Erythema or tenderness over shunt tubing
  • Abdominal symptoms with a peritoneal shunt

CSF Interpretation Is Difficult

  • Postoperative blood can elevate WBC and protein.
  • Neurosurgery can lower CSF glucose.
  • Infection may occur with nearly normal CSF parameters.
  • Ventricular CSF cell counts may be lower than lumbar CSF counts.
CSF culture is the most important diagnostic test in healthcare-associated ventriculitis. Normal cell count, glucose, and protein do not reliably exclude infection.

Culture Technique

  • Obtain CSF from the sampling port, not the drainage bag.
  • Collect cultures before antibiotics when possible.
  • Hold cultures long enough to detect slow-growing Cutibacterium.
  • Culture removed shunt or drain components when infection is suspected.

Empiric Treatment

Vancomycin + Antipseudomonal Beta-Lactam
  • Cefepime
  • Ceftazidime
  • Meropenem

Definitive Treatment

Organism Potential Preferred Agent
MSSA Nafcillin or oxacillin
MRSA Vancomycin
Coagulase-negative staphylococcus Susceptibility-directed therapy
Cutibacterium Penicillin G or another susceptible agent
Gram-negative bacillus Agent selected by susceptibility with reliable CNS penetration
Pseudomonas Cefepime, ceftazidime, or meropenem when susceptible

Intraventricular Antibiotics

Consider intraventricular therapy when:

  • Infection responds poorly to systemic therapy
  • The pathogen is highly resistant
  • Adequate CSF concentrations cannot otherwise be achieved

Use only with neurosurgery, infectious disease, and pharmacy guidance.

CSF Shunt and Device Infection

Biofilm Problem

Shunt organisms adhere to hardware and form biofilm, making cure with antibiotics alone unlikely.

Preferred Source-Control Strategy

Remove Infected Shunt + External Ventricular Drain + IV Antibiotics

Other Hardware

  • Remove an infected external CSF drain.
  • Remove an infected intrathecal infusion pump.
  • Remove infected deep-brain stimulation components.

Monitoring

  • Follow clinical improvement.
  • Repeat CSF cultures until sterile when treating shunt infection.
  • Do not obtain daily CSF samples from an uncomplicated external drain without a clinical indication.

Shunt Reimplantation

Timing depends on:

  • Organism
  • CSF culture clearance
  • CSF inflammation
  • Clinical response
  • Need for ongoing CSF diversion
Leaving infected hardware in place markedly increases relapse risk.

Chronic Meningitis

Chronic meningitis generally evolves over at least several weeks and may produce:

  • Persistent headache
  • Cranial neuropathies
  • Hydrocephalus
  • Gait dysfunction
  • Confusion
  • Constitutional symptoms
  • Radiculopathy

Infectious Causes

  • Tuberculosis
  • Cryptococcus
  • Histoplasma
  • Coccidioides
  • Blastomyces
  • Syphilis
  • Lyme disease
  • Brucella
  • Nontuberculous mycobacteria
  • Parasites

Noninfectious Causes

  • Leptomeningeal carcinomatosis
  • Lymphoma
  • Neurosarcoidosis
  • Vasculitis
  • IgG4-related disease
  • Drug-induced meningitis

Evaluation Strategy

  1. Obtain contrast MRI of the brain and relevant spine.
  2. Measure opening pressure.
  3. Collect adequate CSF volume.
  4. Send fungal and mycobacterial cultures.
  5. Send cytology and flow cytometry.
  6. Review geographic, travel, animal, and immune-status history.
  7. Consider meningeal or other tissue biopsy when CSF remains nondiagnostic.
Repeated low-volume lumbar punctures with only routine bacterial studies are inadequate for chronic meningitis.

Tuberculous Meningitis

Clinical Features

  • Subacute headache
  • Fever
  • Weight loss
  • Night sweats
  • Cranial neuropathy
  • Confusion
  • Hydrocephalus
  • Stroke
  • Hyponatremia

CSF Pattern

  • Elevated opening pressure
  • Lymphocytic or mixed pleocytosis
  • High protein
  • Low glucose

Imaging

  • Basilar meningeal enhancement
  • Hydrocephalus
  • Infarcts
  • Tuberculomas

Diagnostic Testing

  • Large-volume CSF
  • AFB smear
  • Mycobacterial culture
  • Molecular testing
  • Sputum testing even without pulmonary symptoms
  • Search for extracranial tissue that may be safer to biopsy

Treatment

Initial multidrug therapy generally includes:

  • Isoniazid
  • Rifampin
  • Pyrazinamide
  • Ethambutol

Treatment is prolonged, commonly 9–12 months, and should be coordinated with tuberculosis experts and public health.

Corticosteroids

Adjunctive dexamethasone or prednisolone is recommended for tuberculous meningitis.

Complications

  • Hydrocephalus
  • Vasculitic stroke
  • Cranial-nerve injury
  • Optic neuropathy
  • Tuberculoma enlargement during treatment
  • Immune-reconstitution inflammatory syndrome
Do not delay empiric TB treatment when clinical suspicion is high merely because the initial CSF smear or molecular test is negative.

Cryptococcal Meningitis

Risk Factors

  • Advanced HIV
  • Solid-organ transplantation
  • Prolonged corticosteroids
  • Hematologic malignancy
  • Other T-cell immune defects

Clinical Features

  • Subacute headache
  • Fever
  • Confusion
  • Visual symptoms
  • Cranial neuropathy
  • Nausea and vomiting
  • Minimal meningeal signs may occur

Diagnosis

  • Serum cryptococcal antigen
  • CSF cryptococcal antigen
  • Fungal culture
  • Opening pressure

Induction Treatment in Many High-Resource Settings

Liposomal Amphotericin B + Flucytosine

Common HIV-Associated Regimen

  • Liposomal amphotericin B 3–4 mg/kg IV daily
  • Flucytosine 25 mg/kg orally four times daily
  • Usually for at least 2 weeks before consolidation

Toxicity Monitoring

  • Creatinine
  • Potassium
  • Magnesium
  • CBC
  • Liver tests
  • Flucytosine dose adjustment for renal function

Intracranial Pressure

Opening Pressure ≥25 cm CSF or Pressure Symptoms → Therapeutic Lumbar Puncture

Remove enough CSF to reduce pressure toward normal or by approximately 50%, guided by symptoms and measured pressure.

What Does Not Adequately Treat Pressure?

  • Acetazolamide
  • Routine corticosteroids
  • Mannitol as chronic management

ART Timing in HIV

ART is generally delayed for several weeks after antifungal therapy begins to reduce the risk of fatal CNS immune-reconstitution inflammatory syndrome.

Failure to measure and actively manage opening pressure is a major cause of preventable morbidity and mortality in cryptococcal meningitis.

Toxoplasma Encephalitis

Clues

  • CD4 count usually below 100 cells/µL
  • Positive Toxoplasma IgG
  • Multiple ring-enhancing lesions
  • Basal ganglia involvement
  • Headache, seizure, or focal deficit

Treatment

Potential regimens include:

  • Pyrimethamine plus sulfadiazine plus leucovorin
  • TMP-SMX
  • Pyrimethamine plus clindamycin plus leucovorin

Response

Clinical improvement is generally expected within approximately 10–14 days. Lack of improvement should prompt reconsideration and possible biopsy.

Primary CNS Lymphoma

Clues

  • Often solitary or fewer lesions
  • Periventricular location
  • EBV DNA in CSF may support the diagnosis

PML

  • Progressive focal deficits
  • White-matter lesions without marked mass effect

Common Clinical Scenarios

Case 1: Suspected Meningitis Requiring CT Before LP

A patient has fever, headache, confusion, and a new focal arm deficit.

Obtain blood cultures, administer dexamethasone and empiric antibiotics immediately, then obtain urgent neuroimaging before LP.

Case 2: Older Adult With Subtle Presentation

A 74-year-old presents with delirium, fever, and no obvious neck stiffness.

Bacterial meningitis remains possible. Include ampicillin for Listeria coverage in the empiric regimen.

Case 3: Temporal-Lobe Encephalitis

A patient has fever, aphasia, focal seizures, and temporal-lobe MRI abnormalities.

Start IV acyclovir immediately. Do not wait for the HSV PCR result.

Case 4: Early Negative HSV PCR

HSV PCR is negative on the first day, but MRI and EEG remain strongly suggestive.

Continue acyclovir and repeat CSF HSV PCR after several days.

Case 5: Purpuric Rash and Shock

A young adult presents with fever, hypotension, meningismus, and rapidly spreading purpura.

Treat meningococcemia immediately, use droplet precautions, notify public health, and identify close contacts for prophylaxis.

Case 6: Brain Abscess After Dental Infection

A patient has headache, focal weakness, and a ring-enhancing lesion with restricted diffusion after a dental infection.

Begin ceftriaxone plus metronidazole and arrange neurosurgical aspiration. Avoid lumbar puncture.

Case 7: Fever With External Ventricular Drain

A postoperative patient with an EVD develops fever and confusion, but CSF WBC is only mildly elevated.

Normal or mildly abnormal CSF does not exclude ventriculitis. Obtain device-port CSF cultures and begin vancomycin plus an antipseudomonal beta-lactam when suspicion is meaningful.

Case 8: Positive Shunt Culture

A ventriculoperitoneal shunt culture grows S. aureus.

Treat as true infection and arrange complete shunt removal with temporary external drainage and IV antibiotics.

Case 9: Advanced HIV With Headache

A patient with CD4 count 32 has two weeks of headache, blurred vision, and a positive serum cryptococcal antigen.

Perform LP with opening pressure, CSF antigen, and culture. Elevated pressure requires serial therapeutic LPs in addition to antifungal therapy.

Case 10: Multiple Ring-Enhancing Lesions in HIV

A patient with advanced HIV and positive Toxoplasma IgG has multiple basal-ganglia ring-enhancing lesions.

Begin empiric toxoplasmosis therapy when the presentation is typical and reassess clinically and radiographically. Lack of response should prompt biopsy consideration.

Case 11: Chronic Meningitis With Cranial Neuropathies

A patient has weeks of headache, weight loss, sixth-nerve palsy, low CSF glucose, and basilar enhancement.

Prioritize tuberculosis, cryptococcosis, other fungi, and neoplastic meningitis. Obtain large-volume CSF and consider tissue diagnosis.

Case 12: Flaccid Paralysis During Mosquito Season

A patient develops fever, tremor, encephalopathy, and acute asymmetric weakness with preserved sensation.

Consider West Nile neuroinvasive disease with anterior-horn-cell involvement. Send serum and CSF IgM testing.

Common Pitfalls

  • Waiting for the complete meningitis triad
  • Sending every patient for CT before lumbar puncture
  • Delaying antibiotics while waiting for CT
  • Delaying acyclovir while waiting for MRI or HSV PCR
  • Forgetting ampicillin in a patient with Listeria risk
  • Using nonmeningitis antibiotic dosing
  • Giving dexamethasone long after antibiotics and expecting the same benefit
  • Stopping acyclovir after one early negative HSV PCR despite a classic syndrome
  • Assuming absence of CSF RBCs excludes HSV encephalitis
  • Assuming lymphocytic CSF proves viral infection
  • Assuming neutrophilic CSF proves bacterial infection
  • Ignoring paired serum glucose when interpreting CSF glucose
  • Failing to measure opening pressure in suspected cryptococcosis
  • Treating cryptococcal intracranial hypertension with acetazolamide instead of therapeutic LP
  • Performing LP in a patient with a brain abscess and mass effect
  • Treating subdural empyema without urgent surgery
  • Relying on normal CSF chemistry to exclude ventriculitis
  • Collecting EVD cultures from the drainage bag
  • Leaving an infected CSF shunt in place
  • Ignoring complement deficiency after recurrent meningococcal disease
  • Failing to provide meningococcal close-contact prophylaxis
  • Giving prophylaxis to casual contacts who do not meet exposure criteria
  • Using corticosteroids before biopsy in a possible CNS lymphoma without a compelling emergency indication
  • Failing to investigate nonconvulsive status epilepticus
  • Routinely fluid restricting all meningitis patients
  • Failing to arrange hearing and cognitive follow-up

Board and ID Consult Pearls

  • Bacterial meningitis is a medical emergency; antibiotics should not be delayed for CT or LP.
  • Obtain blood cultures before antibiotics when doing so does not delay therapy.
  • Not every patient needs CT before LP.
  • Focal deficit, papilledema, new seizure, severe altered consciousness, mass-lesion concern, or important immunocompromise support imaging first.
  • If CT is required first, treat before CT.
  • Adults with community-acquired meningitis generally receive vancomycin plus ceftriaxone.
  • Add ampicillin when Listeria risk is present.
  • Cephalosporins do not reliably treat Listeria.
  • Dexamethasone is most effective when given before or with the first antibiotic dose.
  • Droplet precautions for meningococcal disease continue through 24 hours of effective therapy.
  • Close contacts of meningococcal disease require prompt chemoprophylaxis.
  • Ceftriaxone, rifampin, and ciprofloxacin are major prophylaxis options; local resistance affects ciprofloxacin use.
  • HSV encephalitis commonly affects the temporal lobes.
  • Start acyclovir before HSV PCR results return.
  • An early negative HSV PCR does not always exclude HSV encephalitis.
  • VZV vasculopathy may occur without rash.
  • CSF neutrophils can occur early in viral meningitis.
  • Partially treated bacterial meningitis may become lymphocyte predominant.
  • Crucial CSF tests include opening pressure, cells, protein, glucose, Gram stain, and culture.
  • A negative Gram stain does not exclude bacterial meningitis.
  • MRI is preferred for encephalitis and brain abscess.
  • Lumbar puncture is generally avoided in brain abscess with mass effect.
  • Brain abscess treatment usually requires neurosurgical sampling plus prolonged antibiotics.
  • Community-acquired brain abscess is commonly treated empirically with ceftriaxone plus metronidazole.
  • Add vancomycin after neurosurgery, penetrating trauma, or significant staphylococcal risk.
  • Subdural empyema is a neurosurgical emergency.
  • Healthcare-associated ventriculitis requires vancomycin plus an antipseudomonal beta-lactam empirically.
  • CSF culture is the most important test for device-associated ventriculitis.
  • Normal CSF cells, glucose, and protein do not exclude device infection.
  • Infected CSF shunts generally require removal.
  • Cryptococcal meningitis requires opening-pressure measurement and aggressive pressure management.
  • Serial therapeutic LPs save lives in cryptococcal intracranial hypertension.
  • TB meningitis commonly causes basilar enhancement, hydrocephalus, cranial neuropathies, and infarction.
  • Negative initial TB CSF testing does not reliably exclude tuberculous meningitis.
  • West Nile virus may cause acute flaccid paralysis with preserved sensation.
  • Crystals, toxins, autoimmune disease, stroke, and nonconvulsive seizures can mimic CNS infection.
  • Arrange hearing, cognitive, and neurologic follow-up after bacterial meningitis.

Bottom Line

Central nervous system infections require simultaneous stabilization, empiric treatment, diagnostic sampling, and evaluation for neurologic complications. Acute bacterial meningitis should be treated immediately after blood cultures; antibiotics must not be delayed for CT or lumbar puncture. Add ampicillin when Listeria risk is present, and give dexamethasone before or with the first antibiotic dose when bacterial meningitis is suspected.

Encephalitis requires prompt acyclovir, MRI, CSF testing, and EEG. An early negative HSV PCR does not exclude HSV when the syndrome remains highly compatible. Brain abscess and subdural empyema require urgent imaging and early neurosurgical involvement; lumbar puncture is generally avoided when a focal mass lesion creates herniation risk.

Healthcare-associated ventriculitis is microbiologically and anatomically different from community meningitis. CSF cultures are central, routine CSF parameters may be misleading, empiric therapy requires vancomycin plus antipseudomonal coverage, and infected shunts or drains usually require removal.

Chronic meningitis demands a broad infectious, malignant, and inflammatory differential. Opening pressure, adequate CSF volume, fungal and mycobacterial cultures, cytology, and tissue diagnosis are often essential. Cryptococcal intracranial hypertension requires serial therapeutic lumbar punctures, while tuberculous meningitis requires early prolonged multidrug treatment and adjunctive corticosteroids.

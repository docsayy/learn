Low Back Pain: Evaluation, Red Flags, Imaging, and Management

June 20, 2026 Hospital Medicine

Low back pain is one of the most common reasons for outpatient visits and hospital encounters. The key challenge is identifying the small number of patients with dangerous pathology while avoiding unnecessary imaging in the vast majority who have benign, self-limited disease. This guide reviews an evidence-based approach to evaluation, red flags, imaging decisions, and treatment strategies. :contentReference[oaicite:0]{index=0}

Classifying Back Pain

The first step is defining symptom duration:

Category Duration
Acute < 4 weeks
Subacute 4–12 weeks
Chronic > 12 weeks

Although duration influences treatment decisions, every patient requires the same initial risk assessment: determine whether red flags are present. Patients with red flags require immediate evaluation, while those without red flags generally begin conservative therapy. :contentReference[oaicite:1]{index=1}

Red Flags

1. Cauda Equina Syndrome

The most important findings include:

  • Urinary retention
  • Bowel dysfunction
  • Saddle anesthesia
  • Progressive neurologic deficits
Urinary retention is often the earliest finding. Overflow incontinence may develop later and should not be confused with the initial presentation. :contentReference[oaicite:2]{index=2}

2. Spinal Infection

Risk factors include:

  • Fever
  • Immunosuppression
  • Current or recent bacteremia
  • IV drug use
  • Endocarditis
  • Indwelling vascular catheter
  • Recent spinal procedure

3. Malignancy

Important risk factors include:

  • Current cancer
  • Recent cancer history
  • Age >50 with unexplained weight loss
  • Failure of conservative therapy
Patients with suspected cauda equina syndrome, spinal infection, or severe neurologic deficits require urgent MRI and specialist evaluation. :contentReference[oaicite:3]{index=3}

Additional "Orange Flags"

  • Midline vertebral tenderness
  • Night pain
  • Pain that awakens from sleep
  • History of trauma
  • Osteoporosis
  • Chronic glucocorticoid use
  • Age >65 years

These findings do not automatically mandate emergent imaging but should lower the threshold for further evaluation. :contentReference[oaicite:4]{index=4}

When Imaging Is Needed

Clinical Scenario Preferred Imaging
Cauda equina syndrome Urgent MRI
Spinal infection Urgent MRI
Severe neurologic deficit Urgent MRI
Major cancer risk factors X-ray + ESR/CRP ± MRI
Compression fracture concern Plain radiographs
Radiculopathy or spinal stenosis after failed conservative therapy MRI

Most patients do not require immediate imaging. Imaging is generally reserved for patients with red flags or those who fail conservative management. :contentReference[oaicite:5]{index=5}

Why Not Image Everyone?

Routine imaging is discouraged for two major reasons:

Most Back Pain Improves Spontaneously

The majority of patients improve with time and conservative treatment alone.

High False Positive Rates

MRI findings such as:

  • Disc degeneration
  • Foraminal narrowing
  • Osteophytes
  • Mild stenosis

are common even in asymptomatic individuals and often do not explain the patient's pain. :contentReference[oaicite:6]{index=6}

Approximately 90% of patients fall into the non-red-flag category and can begin conservative therapy without imaging. :contentReference[oaicite:7]{index=7}

Acute and Subacute Management

Stay Active

Bed rest is not recommended. Patients should remain as active as tolerated and gradually increase activity. :contentReference[oaicite:8]{index=8}

Recommended Non-Pharmacologic Therapies

  • Superficial heat
  • Massage therapy
  • Acupuncture
  • Spinal manipulation

Physical Therapy?

Routine PT is generally not recommended during the acute phase unless:

  • Functional limitations are severe
  • Risk factors for chronic pain are present

Chronic Back Pain

Management becomes more multidisciplinary as symptoms persist beyond 12 weeks.

Exercise Therapy

  • Structured exercise programs
  • Physical therapy
  • Progressive strengthening

Mind-Body Therapies

  • Cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT)
  • Mindfulness-based stress reduction
  • Biofeedback

These therapies address central sensitization and the psychological components of chronic pain. :contentReference[oaicite:9]{index=9}

Interventional Options

  • Epidural steroid injections
  • Pain management referral
  • Selected surgical intervention

Surgery is generally reserved for persistent symptoms with substantial functional impairment after prolonged conservative management. :contentReference[oaicite:10]{index=10}

Medication Approach

Medication Recommendation
NSAIDs First-line medication
Muscle Relaxants Short-term option
Acetaminophen Not routinely recommended
Long-term Opioids Avoid
Duloxetine Consider for chronic pain
TCAs Consider for chronic pain

NSAIDs

NSAIDs remain the preferred medication for most patients and are generally trialed for 2–4 weeks. :contentReference[oaicite:11]{index=11}

Acetaminophen

High-quality studies have shown acetaminophen performs no better than placebo for nonspecific low back pain. :contentReference[oaicite:12]{index=12}

Muscle Relaxants

These may provide short-term benefit but often cause sedation and should be used cautiously. :contentReference[oaicite:13]{index=13}

Opioids

Long-term opioid therapy is not recommended because evidence for improved function and quality of life is poor. Short courses may occasionally be used for severe refractory pain. :contentReference[oaicite:14]{index=14} :contentReference[oaicite:15]{index=15}

Follow-Up and Reassessment

At 4 Weeks

If symptoms persist:

  • Consider MRI for radiculopathy
  • Consider imaging for low-risk cancer concerns
  • Evaluate for inflammatory spondyloarthropathy

At 12 Weeks

Patients without improvement may undergo:

  • Plain radiographs
  • Further specialist referral
  • Additional diagnostic evaluation

Imaging should only be pursued when results are likely to change management. :contentReference[oaicite:16]{index=16}

Waddell Signs

Waddell signs may suggest non-organic contributors to pain:

  • Superficial tenderness
  • Non-anatomic tenderness
  • Pain with axial loading
  • Pain with simulated rotation
  • Inconsistent straight-leg testing
  • Regional weakness not fitting anatomy
  • Disproportionate pain behaviors
Waddell signs do not mean pain is fake. They simply suggest psychosocial or non-organic contributors may be present. :contentReference[oaicite:17]{index=17}

Clinical Pearls

  • Always screen for red flags before treating low back pain.
  • Urinary retention is more concerning for cauda equina syndrome than urinary incontinence.
  • Most patients do not require imaging initially.
  • MRI is the preferred study for infection, cauda equina syndrome, and severe neurologic deficits.
  • Stay active—bed rest worsens outcomes.
  • Heat is generally preferred over ice.
  • NSAIDs are first-line pharmacologic therapy.
  • Acetaminophen is not routinely recommended for nonspecific low back pain.
  • Long-term opioids should generally be avoided.
  • Chronic pain management requires exercise, rehabilitation, and psychological interventions.
  • Epidural steroid injections may provide temporary relief but are not a definitive solution.
  • Imaging should only be obtained when results will change management.

Bottom Line

The most important step in evaluating low back pain is identifying red flags that suggest cauda equina syndrome, spinal infection, fracture, or malignancy. Most patients do not require immediate imaging and should begin conservative management with activity, heat, NSAIDs, and reassurance. Chronic pain requires a multidisciplinary approach emphasizing exercise, rehabilitation, and avoidance of long-term opioid therapy. :contentReference[oaicite:18]{index=18}

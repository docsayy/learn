Ventilator Liberation and Extubation

Added July 15, 2026 • Pulmonary and Critical Care • ~30 min read

A bedside guide to readiness screening, spontaneous-breathing trials, respiratory-muscle assessment, airway protection, cuff-leak testing, extubation to HFNC or NIV, secretion management, post-extubation stridor, and distinguishing weaning failure from extubation failure.

Clinical Overview

Liberation should begin as soon as the reason for ventilation is improving. The patient must pass two different questions: can the patient breathe without the ventilator, and can the patient maintain a safe natural airway after the tube is removed?

SBT Success ≠ Extubation Readiness

Daily Readiness Screen

  • Cause of respiratory failure is improving.
  • Acceptable oxygenation on modest FiO₂ and PEEP.
  • Hemodynamic stability with no or low stable vasopressor requirement.
  • Ability to initiate breaths.
  • Manageable acid-base status and respiratory drive.
  • Sedation light enough for assessment.

Spontaneous-Breathing Trial

  • Common methods: T-piece, low pressure support, or CPAP.
  • Typical duration: 30–120 minutes depending on protocol and patient.
  • Monitor respiratory rate, tidal volume, gas exchange, heart rate, blood pressure, distress, and diaphoresis.
  • Terminate for severe distress, hypoxemia, arrhythmia, hemodynamic instability, or altered mentation.

Why SBTs Fail

  • Unresolved pulmonary edema
  • Excessive fluid balance
  • Respiratory-muscle weakness
  • Cardiac ischemia or LV dysfunction
  • Auto-PEEP
  • Fever or metabolic demand
  • Anemia in selected patients
  • Anxiety, pain, or delirium
  • Electrolyte abnormalities

A failed SBT is diagnostic information. Identify the physiology rather than simply returning to the same settings for another day.

Airway Protection and Secretions

  • Adequate cough strength
  • Manageable secretion frequency
  • Ability to follow commands is helpful but not universally required
  • No progressive bulbar dysfunction
  • No major upper-airway obstruction
  • Reliable plan for suction and post-extubation support

Cuff-Leak Testing

Use cuff-leak testing in patients at increased risk for post-extubation laryngeal edema rather than routinely in everyone. An absent or low leak suggests increased risk but does not absolutely prohibit extubation.

  • Consider systemic corticosteroids before extubation in high-risk patients with failed leak testing.
  • Prepare a reintubation plan and airway equipment.

Planned Post-Extubation Support

  • HFNC can reduce respiratory effort and post-extubation failure in selected patients.
  • Prophylactic NIV benefits high-risk patients, especially chronic hypercapnia or COPD.
  • Do not use rescue NIV to delay reintubation in established post-extubation respiratory failure unless a specific reversible indication exists.

Post-Extubation Stridor

  • Give supplemental oxygen and nebulized epinephrine according to local practice.
  • Administer corticosteroids when laryngeal edema is suspected.
  • Use heliox as a temporary bridge in selected settings.
  • Reintubate promptly for progressive distress, fatigue, or gas-exchange failure.

Tracheostomy Considerations

Tracheostomy may improve comfort, secretion care, and rehabilitation in prolonged ventilation, but timing should be individualized. Early tracheostomy has not consistently improved mortality. Consider prognosis, neurologic recovery, airway anatomy, expected duration, and patient goals.

Daily Liberation Bundle

  • Treat pain first.
  • Use light sedation and daily awakening when appropriate.
  • Perform readiness screen and SBT.
  • Mobilize early.
  • Optimize sleep and delirium prevention.
  • Remove unnecessary fluid and devices.
  • Discuss goals and anticipated trajectory.

Common Pitfalls

  • Waiting for perfect chest imaging
  • Extubating after SBT without assessing cough and secretions
  • Using rapid shallow breathing index as the sole decision
  • Repeatedly failing SBT without cardiac or fluid assessment
  • Rescue NIV that delays needed reintubation
  • Deep sedation that prevents daily assessment

Bottom Line

Screen daily, conduct a structured SBT, diagnose reversible reasons for failure, and separately assess airway protection. Use planned HFNC or NIV in appropriate high-risk patients, but reintubate promptly when post-extubation failure is established.

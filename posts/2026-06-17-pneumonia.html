Pneumonia: A Practical Guide for Internal Medicine Residents

Added June 17, 2026 • Pulmonary / Infectious Disease • ~16 min read

A practical resident-focused guide to pneumonia covering community-acquired pneumonia, hospital-acquired pneumonia, ventilator-associated pneumonia, antibiotic selection, MRSA and Pseudomonas risk factors, aspiration pneumonia, diagnostic testing, treatment duration, vaccination, and high-yield clinical pitfalls.

Overview

Pneumonia is one of the most common infections treated in internal medicine. The first and most important step is deciding what type of pneumonia the patient has, because classification determines the likely organisms, antibiotic selection, diagnostic testing, and treatment duration.

The major practical categories are community-acquired pneumonia, hospital-acquired pneumonia, and ventilator-associated pneumonia. The older category of healthcare-associated pneumonia is no longer favored because it led to broad antibiotic use in many patients who did not truly need resistant-organism coverage.

Core approach: First decide whether the patient has CAP, HAP, or VAP. Then decide whether they have risk factors for MRSA or Pseudomonas.

CAP vs HAP vs VAP

Pneumonia classification is mostly based on where and when the infection developed.

Type Definition Usual Coverage
Community-Acquired Pneumonia Develops outside the hospital or is present on admission Typical and atypical respiratory pathogens
Hospital-Acquired Pneumonia Develops after at least 48 hours in the hospital Broader gram-negative coverage, including Pseudomonas when appropriate
Ventilator-Associated Pneumonia Develops in a mechanically ventilated patient Broad coverage, often including antipseudomonal therapy

Why HCAP Is Outdated

Healthcare-associated pneumonia, or HCAP, was previously used for patients with healthcare exposure such as nursing home residence or recent clinic visits. This term is no longer recommended as a routine treatment category because it often led to unnecessary broad-spectrum antibiotics.

Important: Do not automatically broaden antibiotics just because a patient lives in a nursing home or recently interacted with healthcare. Instead, look for specific MRSA or Pseudomonas risk factors.

Outpatient Community-Acquired Pneumonia

Outpatient CAP treatment depends on whether the patient is otherwise healthy or has important medical comorbidities.

Healthy Outpatient Without Major Comorbidities

Patients without major comorbid disease can often be treated with monotherapy.

Option Notes
Doxycycline Often favored because of increasing macrolide resistance
Amoxicillin or another beta-lactam Good typical bacterial coverage
Azithromycin Use depends on local macrolide resistance and QT risk

Outpatient With Medical Comorbidities

Patients with comorbidities such as diabetes, chronic heart disease, lung disease, liver disease, kidney disease, alcoholism, malignancy, or asplenia usually need combination therapy.

Regimen Example
Beta-lactam + doxycycline Amoxicillin-clavulanate + doxycycline
Beta-lactam + macrolide Amoxicillin-clavulanate + azithromycin
Respiratory fluoroquinolone Levofloxacin or moxifloxacin when combination therapy is not appropriate
Practical point: Fluoroquinolones are effective but are often reserved because of adverse effects and resistance concerns.

Inpatient Community-Acquired Pneumonia

For admitted patients with standard CAP and no MRSA or Pseudomonas risk factors, the common inpatient regimen is ceftriaxone plus azithromycin or doxycycline.

Scenario Common Treatment
Typical inpatient CAP Ceftriaxone + azithromycin
QT prolongation or macrolide concern Ceftriaxone + doxycycline
Severe beta-lactam allergy Respiratory fluoroquinolone
MRSA risk Add vancomycin or linezolid depending on local practice
Pseudomonas risk Use cefepime or piperacillin-tazobactam

Azithromycin is commonly used for atypical coverage, but doxycycline is a good alternative when QT prolongation is a concern.

MRSA and Pseudomonas Risk Factors

MRSA and Pseudomonas coverage should not be automatic for all pneumonia patients. It should be based on risk factors and illness severity.

Common Reasons to Broaden Coverage

  • Prior respiratory isolation of MRSA or Pseudomonas
  • Recent hospitalization with IV antibiotics
  • Severe pneumonia requiring ICU-level care
  • Structural lung disease such as bronchiectasis
  • Repeated healthcare exposure with known resistant organisms
  • Clinical instability or high mortality risk
Resident pearl: If resistant-organism risk is unclear but the patient is very sick, it is reasonable to start broad and de-escalate once cultures, MRSA nares, and clinical trajectory are available.

Hospital-Acquired and Ventilator-Associated Pneumonia

HAP and VAP generally require broader gram-negative coverage because the patient has been exposed to hospital flora and resistant organisms are more likely.

Clinical Situation Common Regimen
Standard HAP/VAP Cefepime or piperacillin-tazobactam
High mortality risk Add a second antipseudomonal from another class
MRSA risk Add vancomycin or linezolid

Second Antipseudomonal Options

  • Aminoglycoside such as gentamicin or amikacin
  • Fluoroquinolone with antipseudomonal activity
MRSA nares: A negative MRSA nares screen can help stop vancomycin early when MRSA pneumonia becomes unlikely.

Duration of Therapy

Duration is a common place where antibiotics are overused. Many patients do not need 10 to 14 days of therapy.

Pneumonia Type Typical Duration
Community-acquired pneumonia Usually 5 to 7 days if clinically improving
Hospital-acquired pneumonia 7 days
Ventilator-associated pneumonia 7 days
Common mistake: Extending HAP/VAP therapy to 10–14 days just because the patient was very sick is often unnecessary if the patient is clinically improving and there is no complication requiring longer treatment.

Diagnostic Testing

Not every patient with pneumonia needs blood cultures, sputum cultures, Legionella urine antigen, or Streptococcus pneumoniae urine antigen. These tests are most useful in severe CAP or when resistant organisms are suspected.

Test When Most Useful
Blood cultures Severe CAP, ICU admission, MRSA/Pseudomonas concern
Sputum culture Severe CAP, HAP/VAP, resistant organism concern
MRSA nares When considering stopping vancomycin
Legionella urine antigen Severe CAP or epidemiologic concern
Strep pneumoniae urine antigen Severe CAP in selected cases

Procalcitonin

Procalcitonin is a biomarker associated with bacterial inflammation, but it should not be used to decide whether to start antibiotics when clinical suspicion for pneumonia is high.

Key point: Do not withhold initial antibiotics for suspected pneumonia based only on a low procalcitonin.

It is sometimes followed during treatment, but in many cases it does not change management because pneumonia treatment durations are already defined by syndrome and clinical response.

Aspiration Pneumonia

Aspiration is common, but routine anaerobic coverage is no longer recommended for every aspiration pneumonia. The lungs are generally an aerobic environment, and routine anaerobic therapy has not clearly improved outcomes in uncomplicated aspiration pneumonia.

Add anaerobic coverage only when:
  • Lung abscess is present
  • Empyema is present
  • Post-obstructive pneumonia is suspected

Influenza and Viral Pneumonia

For influenza, a common mistake is withholding oseltamivir solely because symptoms started more than 48 hours ago.

Patient Group Oseltamivir Use
Healthy outpatient Most useful if symptoms started within 48 hours
Hospitalized patient Treat regardless of symptom duration
Age >65 or high-risk comorbidities Treat regardless of exact symptom duration if clinically appropriate

Follow-Up Chest X-Ray

Routine follow-up chest x-ray is usually not needed when the patient is clinically improving. Radiographic findings often lag behind clinical recovery, so a patient may feel well while imaging still shows residual infiltrate.

Practical point: Treat the patient, not the x-ray. Repeat imaging is usually reserved for persistent symptoms, worsening course, concern for malignancy, or complications.

Steroids in Severe Community-Acquired Pneumonia

Steroids are not routine for all pneumonia patients. However, newer evidence has increased interest in corticosteroids for severe community-acquired pneumonia, especially patients approaching ICU-level severity.

Use selectively: Consider steroids only in severe CAP or other clear indications such as COPD exacerbation, refractory septic shock, or asthma exacerbation. They are not standard for uncomplicated pneumonia.

Pneumococcal Vaccination

Vaccination is an important prevention strategy, especially for older adults and patients with comorbid disease.

Group Vaccine Consideration
Adults ≥65 years Pneumococcal vaccination, commonly PCV20 depending on prior vaccine history
Adults 19–64 with qualifying comorbidities Pneumococcal vaccination depending on risk factors and prior vaccination

The newer PCV20 option simplified what used to be a more complicated PCV13 and PPSV23 sequence for many patients.

CURB-65 and Pneumonia Severity Index

Risk scores help determine whether a patient can be treated outpatient, admitted to the floor, or considered for ICU-level care.

Score Common Use
CURB-65 Quick bedside estimate of need for admission
Pneumonia Severity Index More detailed risk stratification; often used by internists

CURB-65 Components

  • Confusion
  • Urea elevation
  • Respiratory rate elevation
  • Blood pressure low
  • Age ≥65

Daptomycin and Pneumonia

Daptomycin is sometimes considered when MRSA is present elsewhere, but it should not be used for pneumonia.

Board pearl: Daptomycin is inactivated by pulmonary surfactant, so it does not treat pneumonia.

Parapneumonic Effusions and Empyema

Pneumonia can be complicated by pleural fluid collections. The key distinction is whether the effusion is simple or complicated.

Condition Typical Management
Simple parapneumonic effusion Antibiotics alone
Complicated parapneumonic effusion Antibiotics + drainage
Empyema Antibiotics + drainage

Features Suggesting Complicated Effusion

  • Pleural fluid pH < 7.2
  • Pleural fluid glucose < 60
  • High pleural fluid white blood cell count
  • Positive Gram stain or culture
  • Loculations or frank pus

High-Yield Board Pearls

  • HCAP is outdated and should not be used as a routine category.
  • HAP develops after at least 48 hours in the hospital.
  • Outpatient CAP without comorbidities can often be treated with monotherapy.
  • Outpatient CAP with comorbidities usually needs beta-lactam plus doxycycline or azithromycin.
  • Inpatient CAP is commonly treated with ceftriaxone plus azithromycin or doxycycline.
  • Use doxycycline instead of azithromycin when QT prolongation is a concern.
  • MRSA nares testing helps de-escalate vancomycin.
  • HAP/VAP treatment duration is usually 7 days.
  • Do not routinely add anaerobic coverage for aspiration pneumonia unless abscess, empyema, or post-obstructive pneumonia is present.
  • Do not use procalcitonin to decide whether to start antibiotics in suspected pneumonia.
  • Hospitalized influenza patients should receive oseltamivir regardless of symptom duration.
  • Daptomycin does not treat pneumonia because pulmonary surfactant inactivates it.
  • Complicated parapneumonic effusion and empyema require drainage plus antibiotics.
Final takeaway: Pneumonia management becomes much easier when you first classify the pneumonia, then decide whether resistant-organism coverage is needed, then choose the shortest effective treatment duration.

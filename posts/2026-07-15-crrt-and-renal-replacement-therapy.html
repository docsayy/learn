CRRT and Renal Replacement Therapy in Critical Illness

Added July 15, 2026 • Pulmonary and Critical Care • ~30 min read

A practical guide to indications for kidney replacement therapy, modality selection, CRRT prescription, dose, filtration fraction, anticoagulation, electrolyte and phosphate management, drug dosing, troubleshooting alarms, and stopping therapy.

Clinical Overview

Kidney replacement therapy supports solute, acid-base, volume, and temperature control; it does not treat the cause of AKI. Start for clinical indications and trajectory rather than creatinine alone.

Indication + Urgency + Hemodynamics + Fluid Goal + Solute Goal

Indications

  • Refractory hyperkalemia
  • Severe metabolic acidosis unresponsive to appropriate therapy
  • Pulmonary edema or fluid overload causing organ dysfunction
  • Uremic complications such as pericarditis or encephalopathy
  • Selected toxin removal
  • Progressive azotemia with clinical complications

Timing

In the absence of an emergency indication, routine very-early KRT has not consistently improved outcomes. Monitor trajectory, cumulative fluid balance, catabolic state, organ dysfunction, and ability to manage medically.

CRRT Modes

  • CVVH: convection
  • CVVHD: diffusion
  • CVVHDF: combined diffusion and convection
  • Net ultrafiltration: patient fluid removal after accounting for all inputs and replacement/dialysate flows

Dose and Prescription

  • Target delivered effluent dose commonly about 20–25 mL/kg/hour.
  • Prescribe higher to account for downtime.
  • Use an appropriate dosing weight according to local protocol.
  • Set net fluid removal separately and titrate to perfusion and congestion.

Filtration Fraction

High filtration fraction promotes hemoconcentration and filter clotting in convective therapy. Increase prefilter replacement, reduce net filtration through the filter, optimize access, or use citrate anticoagulation when appropriate.

Anticoagulation

  • Regional citrate often prolongs filter life and reduces systemic bleeding.
  • Monitor systemic and post-filter ionized calcium.
  • Watch for citrate accumulation in severe shock or impaired metabolism.
  • Systemic heparin may be used when citrate is unsuitable.
  • Run without anticoagulation when bleeding risk is extreme, accepting shorter filter life.

Electrolytes and Nutrition

  • CRRT commonly causes phosphate, potassium, magnesium, amino-acid, vitamin, and heat losses.
  • Use scheduled monitoring and replacement.
  • Avoid rapid sodium correction by customizing fluids when severe dysnatremia exists.
  • Provide adequate protein for catabolic critical illness.

Drug Dosing

Antibiotic clearance varies with effluent dose, membrane, protein binding, residual renal function, and downtime. Give adequate loading doses, then use pharmacy support and therapeutic drug monitoring.

Troubleshooting

  • Access pressure alarms: check catheter position, kinking, hypovolemia, and thrombosis.
  • Rising transmembrane pressure: filter clotting or hemoconcentration.
  • Frequent clotting: assess access, filtration fraction, and anticoagulation.
  • Unexpected acidosis or hypercalcemia pattern: evaluate citrate delivery and accumulation.
  • Delivered dose falls when downtime is ignored.

Stopping CRRT

  • Improving urine output and solute control
  • Stable potassium and acid-base status
  • Ability to maintain volume without excessive ultrafiltration
  • Hemodynamic suitability for transition to intermittent therapy
  • Daily reassessment of ongoing indication

Common Pitfalls

  • Starting for creatinine alone
  • No explicit net-fluid goal
  • Ignoring downtime
  • Underloading antibiotics
  • Missing phosphate depletion
  • Continuing CRRT without daily need assessment

Bottom Line

Choose KRT for a clinical problem, prescribe an adequate delivered dose, separate solute clearance from net fluid removal, prevent filter clotting and electrolyte loss, and reassess every day whether CRRT remains necessary.

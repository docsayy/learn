COPD and Asthma: Acute Exacerbations, Maintenance Therapy, GOLD 2023 Updates, and Board Review

Overview

COPD and asthma are both obstructive lung diseases, but they differ in pathophysiology, diagnosis, maintenance therapy, and long-term management. In the hospital, clinicians most often encounter these diseases during acute exacerbations.

The bedside approach is similar at first: assess severity, look for triggers, give bronchodilators, treat airway inflammation, support oxygenation, and determine whether antibiotics or escalation of respiratory support is needed.

Core distinction: Asthma is usually characterized by reversible airway obstruction and bronchodilator response, whereas COPD is characterized by persistent airflow limitation that is not fully reversible.

Common Triggers

The first step in any asthma or COPD exacerbation is to look for a trigger.

Disease Common Triggers
Asthma Cold exposure, exercise, allergens, viral URI, irritants, medication triggers
COPD Viral URI, bacterial infection, air pollution, medication nonadherence, pulmonary embolism
Do not miss PE: COPD exacerbations can be triggered by pulmonary embolism. If no clear trigger is found, or the patient has disproportionate hypoxemia, pleuritic chest pain, tachycardia, or risk factors, consider PE evaluation.

Acute COPD Exacerbation

Treatment of an acute COPD exacerbation is usually built around bronchodilators, systemic steroids, oxygen when needed, and antibiotics when indicated.

Bronchodilators

Short-acting bronchodilators are first-line therapy.

Therapy Typical Use
DuoNebs Albuterol + ipratropium nebulizers
Initial frequency Every 1–2 hours for several doses if severe
After improvement Space to every 4 hours scheduled, then PRN

Steroids

Medication Dose Duration
Prednisone 40 mg PO daily 5 days
REDUCE Trial pearl: Five days of prednisone is generally sufficient for COPD exacerbation and is non-inferior to longer courses.

IV vs Oral Steroids

Oral prednisone is appropriate for most patients who can tolerate oral intake. IV methylprednisolone may be considered if the patient cannot take oral medications or has a very severe exacerbation.

Common misconception: IV steroids are not routinely superior to oral steroids for most COPD exacerbations.

Oxygen Targets

For COPD exacerbations, oxygen should be titrated carefully.

Goal SpO₂ in COPD Exacerbation = 88–92%

A common teaching is that excess oxygen suppresses respiratory drive in COPD. This can contribute, but the more important mechanism is worsening ventilation-perfusion mismatch through reversal of hypoxic pulmonary vasoconstriction.

Hypoxic Pulmonary Vasoconstriction

In poorly ventilated lung regions, pulmonary vessels constrict so blood is redirected toward better ventilated areas. When excessive oxygen is given, those poorly ventilated vessels may dilate despite ongoing poor ventilation. This sends more blood to areas with poor gas exchange, worsening V/Q mismatch and increasing CO₂ retention.

Key concept: Avoid over-oxygenation in COPD because excessive oxygen can worsen V/Q mismatch and hypercapnia.

Antibiotics in COPD Exacerbation

Antibiotics are not required for every COPD exacerbation. They are most useful when bacterial infection is likely.

When to Give Antibiotics

Consider antibiotics when the patient has at least two of the following:

  • Increased sputum production
  • Increased sputum purulence
  • Increased dyspnea

Azithromycin Regimens

Regimen Dose
Short course Azithromycin 500 mg daily for 3 days
Alternative course Azithromycin 500 mg once, then 250 mg daily for 4 additional days
Azithromycin may help through both antimicrobial activity and anti-inflammatory effects.

Acute Asthma Exacerbation

Acute asthma exacerbations are treated with rapid bronchodilation, systemic steroids for moderate to severe exacerbations, oxygen as needed, and escalation of respiratory support when signs of fatigue appear.

Initial Treatment

  • Short-acting bronchodilators such as albuterol
  • Ipratropium in moderate to severe exacerbations
  • Systemic corticosteroids
  • Oxygen if hypoxemic
  • IV magnesium for severe exacerbations or poor response

IV Magnesium

IV magnesium can be considered in severe asthma exacerbations and may also be used as an adjunct in COPD exacerbations.

Medication Typical Dose
Magnesium sulfate 2 g IV once
Watch for fatigue: A patient with severe asthma who stops wheezing but looks worse may have dangerously poor air movement.

Maintenance Therapy Overview

Asthma and COPD maintenance therapies use similar inhaler classes but in different sequences.

Disease Typical Escalation Direction
Asthma ICS → LABA → LAMA
COPD LAMA → LABA → ICS
Easy memory: Asthma starts with inflammation control using inhaled corticosteroids. COPD starts with bronchodilation using long-acting muscarinic antagonists.

Asthma Maintenance Therapy

Asthma management is centered around controlling airway inflammation and preventing exacerbations.

Rule of Twos

The rule of twos helps determine whether asthma control is inadequate.

Finding Meaning
Rescue inhaler use >2 times per week Consider stepping up therapy
Nocturnal symptoms >2 times per month Consider stepping up therapy

Adjunctive Therapies

  • Montelukast for allergic or exercise-associated symptoms
  • Omalizumab in selected patients with elevated IgE and severe allergic asthma
  • Specialty pulmonary or allergy referral for severe or difficult-to-control asthma

Asthma Rescue Inhaler

Historically, albuterol was the default rescue inhaler for asthma. Modern asthma guidance increasingly favors as-needed low-dose ICS-formoterol as the preferred reliever strategy for many patients.

Reliever Option Role
Low-dose ICS-formoterol Preferred reliever in many asthma guidelines
Albuterol Alternative reliever option
For asthma, shift the mental model from “albuterol is always the rescue inhaler” toward “ICS-formoterol is often the preferred reliever.”

For COPD, rescue therapy is still usually a short-acting bronchodilator such as albuterol.

COPD Maintenance Therapy

COPD maintenance therapy is primarily aimed at symptom reduction, exacerbation prevention, and quality-of-life improvement.

Medication Classes

Class Examples Role
LAMA Tiotropium, umeclidinium First-line long-acting bronchodilator in many patients
LABA Formoterol, salmeterol, vilanterol Added for persistent symptoms
ICS Fluticasone, budesonide Selected patients with exacerbations and eosinophilic phenotype

GOLD 2023 ABE Classification

The GOLD 2023 framework uses symptom burden and exacerbation history to classify patients into groups A, B, and E.

Group Symptoms Exacerbation History Initial Therapy
Group A Low symptom burden Low exacerbation risk Bronchodilator, often LAMA
Group B High symptom burden Low exacerbation risk LAMA + LABA
Group E Any symptom burden Frequent exacerbations or hospitalization LAMA + LABA; consider ICS if eosinophils are high

How Group E Is Defined

  • Two or more moderate COPD exacerbations
  • Or one or more COPD hospitalizations
The older ABCD framework has shifted toward ABE classification in GOLD 2023.

Inhaled Corticosteroids in COPD

Inhaled corticosteroids can reduce exacerbations in selected COPD patients, especially those with eosinophilic inflammation. However, they increase pneumonia risk and should not be used reflexively in every patient.

Blood Eosinophils ICS Role
>300 cells/µL Most likely to benefit
100–300 cells/µL Consider if frequent exacerbations or prior hospitalization
<100 cells/µL Benefit is less likely

Reasons to Avoid or Use Caution With ICS

  • Repeated pneumonia
  • Blood eosinophils less than 100 cells/µL
  • History of mycobacterial infection
ICS risk: Inhaled corticosteroids are associated with increased pneumonia events in COPD.

Roflumilast and Chronic Azithromycin

Hospitalists should consider whether patients admitted with frequent COPD exacerbations qualify for additional exacerbation-prevention therapy.

Roflumilast

Roflumilast is a phosphodiesterase-4 inhibitor used in selected COPD patients.

Consider Roflumilast When Details
FEV1 <50% More severe airflow limitation
Chronic bronchitis phenotype Chronic productive cough and mucus symptoms
Frequent exacerbations Especially despite inhaler optimization

Chronic Azithromycin

Chronic azithromycin can reduce COPD exacerbation frequency in selected patients.

Regimen Typical Use
Azithromycin 250 mg Monday/Wednesday/Friday Common chronic prophylaxis schedule

Potential Risks

  • QT prolongation
  • Nausea
  • Hearing changes
  • Antibiotic resistance concerns
Chronic azithromycin is often considered more strongly in former smokers with frequent exacerbations.

COPD Therapies With Mortality Benefit

Many COPD therapies improve symptoms and quality of life, but only a few interventions clearly improve mortality.

Therapy Mortality Benefit
Smoking cessation Yes
Long-term oxygen therapy for persistent hypoxemia Yes
Lung volume reduction surgery in selected patients Yes
Maintenance inhalers Improve symptoms and exacerbations, but not classic mortality therapy

Oxygen Mortality Benefit

Long-term oxygen therapy is considered when patients have persistent severe hypoxemia, classically oxygen saturation below 88%.

Lung Volume Reduction

Lung volume reduction can be surgical or bronchoscopic, including endobronchial valve placement in selected patients. The goal is to reduce hyperinflation from severely diseased lung regions and allow healthier lung to function better.

Pulmonary Rehabilitation

Pulmonary rehabilitation improves six-minute walk distance, symptoms, exercise tolerance, and quality of life.

Every patient with significant COPD should be considered for pulmonary rehabilitation.

Pathophysiology

Asthma

Asthma is characterized by airway inflammation, airway smooth muscle hyperreactivity, and reversible obstruction.

  • Airway smooth muscle inflammation
  • Bronchoconstriction
  • Mucus production
  • Reversible obstruction
  • Positive bronchodilator response

COPD

COPD is usually caused by long-term exposure to inhaled toxins, especially cigarette smoke. It causes airway and alveolar damage, loss of elastic recoil, air trapping, and persistent airflow limitation.

  • Elastin breakdown
  • Loss of alveolar recoil
  • Air trapping
  • Airway collapse
  • Persistent obstruction
  • No complete bronchodilator reversibility
Asthma = reversible airway obstruction
COPD = persistent airflow limitation with air trapping

Diagnosis and Pulmonary Function Testing

Asthma Diagnosis

Asthma diagnosis is supported by obstruction with bronchodilator response.

Bronchodilator Response = FEV1 increase >12% and >200 mL

If symptoms are intermittent and baseline testing is normal, a methacholine challenge can be used to provoke airway hyperresponsiveness.

COPD Diagnosis

COPD requires spirometric confirmation.

COPD = Post-bronchodilator FEV1/FVC <0.70
Do not accept the label blindly: Many patients carry a COPD diagnosis without documented pulmonary function testing.

GOLD Spirometric Staging

GOLD spirometric grades are based on FEV1 percent predicted.

GOLD Grade FEV1 Percent Predicted
GOLD 1 ≥80%
GOLD 2 50–79%
GOLD 3 30–49%
GOLD 4 <30%

Spirometric severity should be combined with symptom burden and exacerbation history to guide therapy.

Alpha-1 Antitrypsin Deficiency Screening

Patients with COPD should be tested for alpha-1 antitrypsin deficiency if they have not been tested previously.

GOLD pearl: Alpha-1 antitrypsin deficiency screening is recommended for patients with COPD, not only young patients or never-smokers.

High-Yield Board Pearls

Silent Chest in Asthma

A severe asthma patient may initially have respiratory alkalosis from tachypnea. If the patient becomes fatigued, the pCO₂ may begin to normalize or rise. This is a dangerous sign, not reassurance.

Board classic: Severe asthma + worsening mental status + normalizing pCO₂ + decreased wheezing = impending respiratory failure. Prepare to intubate.

Why Wheezing Can Disappear

Wheezing can disappear because the patient has improved, but it can also disappear because air movement has become so poor that wheezing can no longer be generated. The clinical appearance determines which interpretation is correct.

Oxygen in COPD

The COPD oxygen target of 88–92% is mainly to avoid worsening V/Q mismatch and hypercapnia, not simply because of loss of respiratory drive.

Clinical Pearls

  1. Always look for a trigger in asthma and COPD exacerbations.
  2. Consider PE when COPD exacerbation has no clear trigger.
  3. DuoNebs are first-line for acute COPD exacerbation.
  4. Prednisone 40 mg daily for 5 days is standard for many COPD exacerbations.
  5. Oral steroids are usually adequate if the patient can tolerate PO intake.
  6. Target SpO₂ 88–92% in COPD exacerbation.
  7. Antibiotics are reasonable when at least two cardinal symptoms are present.
  8. Asthma maintenance usually escalates ICS → LABA → LAMA.
  9. COPD maintenance usually escalates LAMA → LABA → ICS.
  10. ICS-formoterol is now preferred as reliever therapy in many asthma guidelines.
  11. COPD rescue therapy is still usually a SABA such as albuterol.
  12. Inhaled corticosteroids in COPD are most useful when eosinophils are elevated.
  13. Avoid ICS in COPD patients with recurrent pneumonia or very low eosinophils.
  14. Roflumilast may help COPD patients with FEV1 <50% and chronic bronchitis phenotype.
  15. Chronic azithromycin can reduce COPD exacerbations in selected patients.
  16. Smoking cessation, oxygen for persistent hypoxemia, and lung volume reduction are mortality-benefit therapies in COPD.
  17. Pulmonary rehab improves functional status and quality of life.
  18. COPD diagnosis should be confirmed with post-bronchodilator FEV1/FVC <0.70.
  19. Asthma diagnosis is supported by obstruction with bronchodilator response.
  20. Normalizing pCO₂ in a tiring severe asthma patient is a sign of impending respiratory failure.

Bottom Line

Asthma and COPD are both obstructive lung diseases, but their management differs. Acute exacerbations require bronchodilators, systemic steroids when appropriate, oxygen support, and treatment of triggers. COPD oxygen should target 88–92%, antibiotics should be used selectively, and maintenance therapy generally begins with LAMA-based bronchodilation. Asthma therapy centers on inhaled corticosteroids, and ICS-formoterol is now preferred as a reliever in many patients. Always confirm COPD with PFTs, screen for alpha-1 antitrypsin deficiency, consider PE when COPD has no clear trigger, and recognize that a silent chest with normalizing pCO₂ in severe asthma is a warning sign for impending respiratory failure.

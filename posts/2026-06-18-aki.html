Acute Kidney Injury (AKI): A Practical Guide for Internal Medicine Residents

Added June 18, 2026 • Nephrology • ~15 min read

A practical framework for evaluating acute kidney injury in hospitalized patients, including prerenal, intrinsic, and postrenal causes, urine microscopy, FENa interpretation, ATN, AIN, glomerular disease, and high-yield ward pearls.

Definition of Acute Kidney Injury

Acute kidney injury (AKI) is one of the most common diagnoses encountered in hospitalized patients and should always trigger a systematic evaluation.

AKI Criteria

Criterion Definition
Creatinine rise Increase ≥ 0.3 mg/dL from baseline
Relative increase Increase ≥ 50% from baseline creatinine
Urine output < 0.5 mL/kg/hr for 6 hours
The most commonly used definition in clinical practice is a creatinine rise of at least 0.3 mg/dL above baseline.

The AKI Approach

The simplest and most useful framework is:

AKI = Prerenal + Intrinsic Renal + Postrenal

Most evaluations should begin by ruling out prerenal and postrenal causes before pursuing more complicated intrinsic renal diagnoses.

Prerenal AKI

Prerenal AKI results from inadequate renal perfusion without structural kidney damage.

True Hypovolemia

  • Vomiting
  • Diarrhea
  • Poor oral intake
  • Hemorrhage
  • Overdiuresis

Decreased Effective Circulating Volume

In these conditions total body volume may be normal or increased, but the kidneys perceive inadequate perfusion.

Condition Syndrome
Heart failure Cardiorenal syndrome
Cirrhosis Hepatorenal syndrome
Sepsis Third-spacing and vasodilation

Medication-Induced Prerenal AKI

  • NSAIDs
  • IV contrast
  • Tacrolimus
  • Cyclosporine
  • ACE inhibitors
  • ARBs
  • Diuretics
  • Hypercalcemia

Mechanisms

Agent Mechanism
NSAIDs Afferent arteriole constriction
IV contrast Renal vasoconstriction
Calcineurin inhibitors Renal vasoconstriction
ACEi / ARB Efferent arteriole dilation
Diuretics Volume depletion

Treatment

Most suspected prerenal AKI should receive a fluid challenge unless cardiorenal syndrome or hepatorenal syndrome is suspected.

Prerenal Laboratory Findings

Finding Typical Result
BUN/Cr ratio > 20
FENa < 1%
FEUrea < 35%
Urine sodium < 20 mEq/L
The kidneys are functioning normally and avidly reabsorbing sodium and water to preserve volume.

Postrenal AKI

Postrenal AKI occurs when urine flow is obstructed anywhere along the urinary tract.

Common Causes

  • Benign prostatic hyperplasia (BPH)
  • Kidney stones
  • Pelvic malignancy
  • Retroperitoneal masses
  • Neurogenic bladder

Key Clinical Pearl

Patients can still be urinating and have a postrenal AKI. Unilateral obstruction can still cause kidney injury.

Evaluation

  • Renal ultrasound
  • Bladder scan
  • Post-void residual
  • Point-of-care ultrasound

Ultrasound Findings

  • Hydronephrosis
  • Hydroureter
  • Bladder distension
If you can clearly see a ureter on imaging, think hydroureter and obstruction until proven otherwise.

Treatment

  • Foley catheter
  • Relief of obstruction
  • Urology consultation when appropriate

Intrinsic Renal AKI

Intrinsic AKI refers to direct injury of renal structures.

The easiest way to organize intrinsic renal disease is by nephron compartment:

Compartment Examples
Vascular Vasculitis, infarction, APS, dissection
Interstitial AIN
Glomerular Nephritic and nephrotic syndromes
Tubular ATN

Urine Microscopy: The Liquid Biopsy

Urine sediment examination is one of the highest-yield tests in nephrology.

Many nephrologists refer to urine microscopy as the "liquid biopsy."

Important Findings

Finding Suggests
Hyaline casts Prerenal state
Muddy brown casts ATN
Granular casts ATN
Renal tubular epithelial cells ATN
WBC casts AIN
Eosinophiluria AIN
Dysmorphic RBCs Glomerulonephritis
RBC casts Glomerulonephritis

Acute Tubular Necrosis (ATN)

ATN is the most common intrinsic cause of AKI.

Causes

  • Sepsis
  • Hypotension
  • Shock
  • Aminoglycosides
  • Vancomycin + Piperacillin-Tazobactam
  • Contrast exposure
  • Rhabdomyolysis
  • Tumor lysis syndrome
  • Multiple myeloma

Classic Urine Findings

  • Muddy brown casts
  • Granular casts
  • Renal tubular epithelial cells
The classic board answer for ATN is muddy brown granular casts.

Acute Interstitial Nephritis (AIN)

AIN is an inflammatory process involving the renal interstitium.

Common Causes

  • NSAIDs
  • PPIs
  • Sulfa drugs
  • Antibiotics
  • Checkpoint inhibitors
  • Pyelonephritis

Clues

  • WBC casts
  • Eosinophiluria
  • Sterile pyuria

Glomerular Disease

Glomerular pathology is usually divided into nephritic and nephrotic syndromes.

Suggestive Findings

  • Proteinuria > 2 g/day
  • Dysmorphic RBCs
  • Hematuria
  • RBC casts
  • Elevated urine albumin-to-protein ratio
RBC casts should immediately raise concern for glomerulonephritis.

FENa and FEUrea

Traditional Interpretation

Test Prerenal Intrinsic
FENa < 1% > 2%
FEUrea < 35% > 50%
Urine Sodium < 20 > 40

Limitations

Modern nephrologists often place limited emphasis on FENa because performance is inconsistent outside carefully selected oliguric patients.

Clinical history, volume assessment, urine microscopy, and response to treatment are usually more informative.

Common Pitfalls

eGFR Is Inaccurate During AKI

eGFR formulas assume stable kidney function and become unreliable when creatinine is rapidly changing.

A patient whose creatinine rises from 1.0 to 2.0 mg/dL in 24 hours may have dramatically worse kidney function than the reported eGFR suggests.

Protein/Creatinine Ratios Can Mislead

Urine protein-to-creatinine ratios may be falsely elevated during AKI and should be interpreted cautiously.

Always correlate proteinuria with the clinical scenario and urine sediment findings.

High-Yield Ward Pearls

  • Always think Prerenal → Intrinsic → Postrenal.
  • Most AKI evaluations start with volume assessment and renal ultrasound.
  • Urine microscopy is often more useful than FENa.
  • Muddy brown casts = ATN until proven otherwise.
  • WBC casts suggest AIN.
  • RBC casts suggest glomerulonephritis.
  • Patients can still urinate with postrenal obstruction.
  • Renal ultrasound should be obtained whenever obstruction is a possibility.
  • Do not rely on eGFR during acute kidney injury.
  • Protein/creatinine ratios may be misleading during AKI.
  • ATN is the most common intrinsic cause of AKI.
  • NSAIDs are common offenders causing both prerenal AKI and AIN.

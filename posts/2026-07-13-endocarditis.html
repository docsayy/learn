Infective Endocarditis: The Complete Advanced Resident Guide

Added July 13, 2026 • Infectious Disease • ~35 min read

Infective endocarditis is an infection of the endocardial surface of the heart, usually involving a native or prosthetic valve, intracardiac device, or congenital cardiac lesion. It is a high-mortality disease that requires integration of blood cultures, multimodality imaging, organism-specific antimicrobial therapy, source control, evaluation for embolic and metastatic complications, and early surgical consultation when indicated.

Clinical Overview

Infective endocarditis is an infection involving the endocardial surface of the heart. The infection most commonly involves a cardiac valve but may also involve:

  • Prosthetic valve material
  • Pacemaker or defibrillator leads
  • Transcatheter valves
  • Septal closure devices
  • Congenital cardiac lesions
  • Mural endocardium
  • Previously damaged endocardial surfaces

The characteristic lesion is a vegetation composed of microorganisms, platelets, fibrin, inflammatory cells, and cellular debris.

Persistent Bacteremia + Endocardial Surface + Vegetation Formation = Infective Endocarditis

Why Infective Endocarditis Is Dangerous

  • Destruction of the valve can cause acute severe regurgitation and heart failure.
  • Extension beyond the valve can create abscesses, fistulas, and conduction abnormalities.
  • Vegetations can embolize to the brain, spleen, kidneys, bowel, coronary arteries, or extremities.
  • Persistent bacteremia can seed the spine, joints, and other distant tissues.
  • Prosthetic material and cardiac devices are difficult to sterilize medically.
  • Neurologic complications can substantially alter surgical timing and anticoagulation decisions.
Infective endocarditis is not managed with antibiotics alone. Every case requires assessment of valve function, embolic risk, invasive extension, prosthetic material, source control, and need for surgery.

The Four Questions for Every Suspected Case

  1. Is infective endocarditis likely or proven?
  2. Which organism is responsible?
  3. Has the infection caused structural or embolic complications?
  4. Can the patient be cured medically, or is surgery/device removal required?

Pathophysiology

Step 1: Endothelial Injury

Abnormal blood flow, valve degeneration, congenital lesions, prosthetic material, intracardiac devices, or direct bacterial invasion may damage the endocardium.

Step 2: Nonbacterial Thrombotic Material

Platelets and fibrin deposit on the injured surface, creating a sterile nidus that can later become infected.

Step 3: Transient or Sustained Bacteremia

Microorganisms enter the bloodstream through:

  • Skin infection
  • Intravascular catheters
  • Injection drug use
  • Oral or dental sources
  • Gastrointestinal or genitourinary sources
  • Surgery or invasive procedures
  • Healthcare-associated exposure

Step 4: Microbial Adherence

The organism adheres to damaged endocardium or prosthetic material. Continued deposition of fibrin and platelets protects microorganisms from host immune defenses.

Step 5: Local Destruction and Embolization

Vegetations may enlarge, destroy valve tissue, extend into adjacent structures, or fragment and embolize.

Vegetations are relatively avascular. This contributes to the need for prolonged bactericidal therapy because immune cells and antibiotics may penetrate imperfectly.

Why Certain Organisms Commonly Cause IE

Organism Important Virulence Feature
Staphylococcus aureus Can invade previously normal valves and adhere to endovascular tissue and foreign material
Viridans streptococci Adhere to platelet-fibrin matrices, especially on previously abnormal valves
Enterococci Persist in the bloodstream and may require synergistic bactericidal therapy
Coagulase-negative staphylococci Form biofilm on prosthetic valves and devices
Fungi Produce large vegetations, embolize, and are difficult to eradicate medically

Risk Factors

Cardiac Risk Factors

  • Previous infective endocarditis
  • Prosthetic heart valve
  • Transcatheter aortic valve replacement
  • Valve repair with prosthetic material
  • Rheumatic valve disease
  • Degenerative valve disease
  • Mitral valve prolapse with significant regurgitation
  • Bicuspid aortic valve
  • Congenital heart disease
  • Hypertrophic cardiomyopathy with obstruction
  • Pacemaker or ICD

Healthcare and Host Risk Factors

  • Hemodialysis
  • Long-term central venous catheter
  • Recent hospitalization
  • Recent cardiac surgery
  • Recent bacteremia
  • Immunosuppression
  • Older age
  • Diabetes mellitus
  • Chronic skin disease or wounds
  • Poor dentition

Behavioral and Exposure Risk Factors

  • Injection drug use
  • Nonsterile injection practices
  • Body piercing or tattooing under unsafe conditions
  • Poor dental hygiene
Modern infective endocarditis increasingly affects older patients, patients with prosthetic valves or cardiac devices, hemodialysis patients, and patients with healthcare-associated bacteremia.

HACEK Organisms

  • Haemophilus species
  • Aggregatibacter species
  • Cardiobacterium hominis
  • Eikenella corrodens
  • Kingella species
HACEK organisms are gram-negative oral flora associated with endocarditis. Modern automated blood-culture systems usually detect them without prolonged incubation.

Clinical Presentation

Common Symptoms

  • Fever
  • Chills or rigors
  • Fatigue
  • Weight loss
  • Night sweats
  • Dyspnea
  • New or worsening edema
  • Back pain
  • Joint pain
  • Neurologic symptoms
  • Pleuritic chest pain in right-sided IE

Cardiac Findings

  • New regurgitant murmur
  • Change in a known murmur
  • Acute heart failure
  • Pulmonary edema
  • New conduction abnormality
  • Prosthetic valve dysfunction
The absence of a murmur does not exclude infective endocarditis, particularly early in disease or with right-sided infection.

Peripheral Findings

Finding Description Mechanism
Janeway lesions Painless erythematous or hemorrhagic lesions on palms or soles Septic microemboli
Osler nodes Painful nodules on fingers or toes Immune-mediated phenomenon
Roth spots Retinal hemorrhages with pale centers Immune or embolic phenomenon
Splinter hemorrhages Linear nail-bed hemorrhages Microvascular injury; nonspecific
Palpable purpura Purpuric skin lesions Immune-complex vasculitis
Clubbing Chronic digital clubbing Seen in prolonged disease

Laboratory Findings

  • Normocytic anemia
  • Leukocytosis or normal WBC count
  • Elevated ESR and CRP
  • Microscopic hematuria
  • Proteinuria
  • Low complement in immune-complex disease
  • Elevated rheumatoid factor
  • Acute kidney injury
  • Abnormal liver enzymes in systemic illness
Back pain, focal joint pain, neurologic symptoms, or abdominal pain in a bacteremic patient should prompt evaluation for metastatic infection or embolization.

Major Complications

Cardiac Complications

  • Acute severe valve regurgitation
  • Heart failure
  • Perivalvular abscess
  • Valve perforation
  • Prosthetic valve dehiscence
  • Intracardiac fistula
  • Conduction block
  • Myocardial infarction from coronary embolism
  • Pericarditis

Neurologic Complications

  • Ischemic stroke
  • Intracranial hemorrhage
  • Mycotic aneurysm
  • Brain abscess
  • Meningitis
  • Seizure
  • Encephalopathy

Systemic Embolic Complications

  • Splenic infarct or abscess
  • Renal infarct
  • Mesenteric ischemia
  • Limb ischemia
  • Coronary embolism

Pulmonary Complications of Right-Sided IE

  • Septic pulmonary emboli
  • Cavitary pulmonary nodules
  • Pulmonary infarction
  • Empyema
  • Pneumothorax
  • Pulmonary abscess

Metastatic Infection

  • Vertebral osteomyelitis
  • Spinal epidural abscess
  • Septic arthritis
  • Psoas abscess
  • Prosthetic joint infection
  • Soft-tissue abscess

Renal Complications

  • Immune-complex glomerulonephritis
  • Renal infarction
  • Septic emboli
  • Antibiotic nephrotoxicity
  • Hemodynamic acute kidney injury
A new PR-interval prolongation, bundle-branch block, or complete heart block may indicate perivalvular abscess, especially with aortic valve endocarditis.

Initial Approach to Suspected Endocarditis

When to Suspect IE

  • Persistent bacteremia without a clear removable source
  • S. aureus bacteremia
  • Enterococcal bacteremia without a clear source
  • Viridans streptococcal bacteremia
  • Prosthetic valve with unexplained fever
  • Cardiac device with bacteremia
  • New murmur plus systemic infection
  • Multiple embolic events
  • Fever plus injection drug use
  • Fever plus new conduction abnormality
  • Culture-negative febrile illness with vascular or immunologic findings

Immediate Steps

  1. Assess hemodynamic stability and heart failure.
  2. Obtain three blood-culture sets before antibiotics when feasible.
  3. Perform a detailed examination for cardiac, embolic, neurologic, spinal, and device findings.
  4. Obtain ECG and baseline laboratory studies.
  5. Perform TTE promptly.
  6. Proceed to TEE when suspicion remains high or a high-risk feature exists.
  7. Begin empiric antibiotics after cultures if the patient is unstable or IE is strongly suspected.
  8. Obtain early infectious disease and cardiology consultation.
  9. Involve cardiac surgery early when complications are present.

Initial Laboratory Evaluation

Test Purpose
CBC Anemia, leukocytosis, thrombocytopenia
CMP Renal and hepatic function for antimicrobial planning
ESR and CRP Inflammatory baseline and trend
Urinalysis Microscopic hematuria, proteinuria, glomerulonephritis
Blood cultures Microbiologic diagnosis and clearance

Troponin If ischemia, coronary embolism, or myocarditis suspected

For a stable patient with suspected subacute IE, obtain adequate blood cultures before antibiotics. Prior antibiotic exposure is the most common reason for culture-negative endocarditis.

Blood Cultures

How Many Cultures?

Obtain three sets of blood cultures from separate venipuncture sites before antimicrobial therapy whenever the patient is stable enough to permit this.

Important Collection Principles

  • Use peripheral venipuncture whenever possible.
  • Label time and collection site.
  • Ensure adequate blood volume.
  • Do not rely solely on cultures from an existing catheter.
  • Do not delay urgent antibiotics in shock or severe sepsis.

Timing

In acute severe illness, obtain the sets rapidly over a short period and begin therapy. There is no need to delay treatment for hours solely to space cultures.

Repeat Cultures

  • Repeat cultures every 24–48 hours until bloodstream clearance is documented.
  • Persistent positivity should trigger reassessment for uncontrolled infection.
  • The first negative culture date is often used to anchor treatment duration.

Common Microbiologic Patterns Supporting IE

  • Typical organism in two separate blood-culture sets
  • Persistently positive cultures
  • Multiple cultures positive over time
  • Coxiella burnetii phase I IgG meeting diagnostic threshold
  • Organism identified by molecular testing from blood or valve tissue
One negative culture after antibiotics does not exclude infective endocarditis.

Diagnostic Criteria

The Duke framework integrates microbiology, imaging, surgical/pathologic findings, and clinical features. The criteria support diagnosis but do not replace clinical judgment.

Major Criteria

Major Criterion Examples
Positive microbiology Typical organisms from separate blood cultures, persistently positive cultures, or specific serologic/molecular evidence
Imaging evidence Vegetation, valve perforation, aneurysm, abscess, pseudoaneurysm, fistula, prosthetic dehiscence, or device involvement
Surgical evidence Direct intraoperative evidence of endocarditis in selected diagnostic frameworks

Minor Criteria

  • Predisposing cardiac condition or injection drug use
  • Fever ≥38°C
  • Vascular phenomena
  • Immunologic phenomena
  • Microbiologic evidence not meeting a major criterion
  • Selected imaging or physical examination findings not meeting major criteria

Vascular Phenomena

  • Major arterial embolus
  • Septic pulmonary infarct
  • Mycotic aneurysm
  • Intracranial hemorrhage
  • Conjunctival hemorrhage
  • Janeway lesions
  • Splenic or renal infarction

Immunologic Phenomena

  • Glomerulonephritis
  • Osler nodes
  • Roth spots
  • Positive rheumatoid factor

Clinical Classification

Classification Typical Combination
Definite IE Pathologic confirmation, two major criteria, or a qualifying combination of major and minor criteria
Possible IE Partial fulfillment of major and minor criteria
Rejected IE Alternative diagnosis, rapid resolution with short therapy, no evidence at surgery/autopsy, or criteria not met
A patient may have clinically important infective endocarditis even when formal criteria initially classify the disease as possible. Repeat imaging and further microbiologic testing may be necessary.

Echocardiography: TTE vs TEE

Transthoracic Echocardiography

TTE is the usual first imaging study because it is rapid, noninvasive, and provides information about valve function and hemodynamics.

TTE Can Evaluate

  • Vegetations
  • Valve regurgitation or stenosis
  • Ventricular function
  • Pulmonary pressure
  • Large abscesses
  • Pericardial effusion

Limitations

  • Lower sensitivity for small vegetations
  • Reduced sensitivity with prosthetic valves
  • Reduced sensitivity for cardiac-device leads
  • May miss perivalvular abscess
  • Image quality may be limited by body habitus or lung disease

Transesophageal Echocardiography

TEE has substantially greater sensitivity for vegetations, prosthetic valve infection, lead infection, abscess, perforation, and periannular extension.

TEE Is Strongly Indicated When:

  • TTE is negative but clinical suspicion remains high.
  • TTE is positive and complications must be defined.
  • A prosthetic valve is present.
  • A pacemaker or ICD is present.
  • S. aureus bacteremia has high-risk features.
  • Persistent bacteremia is present.
  • New conduction disease is present.
  • Perivalvular abscess is suspected.
  • Embolic complications are present.
  • Valve surgery is being considered.

When to Repeat Echocardiography

  • Initial study negative but suspicion remains high
  • Persistent fever or bacteremia
  • New murmur
  • New heart failure
  • New conduction abnormality
  • New embolic event
  • Clinical deterioration
A negative initial echocardiogram does not exclude early infective endocarditis. Repeat TEE is appropriate when clinical suspicion remains high.

High-Risk Echocardiographic Findings

  • Large or highly mobile vegetation
  • Severe acute regurgitation
  • Valve perforation
  • Perivalvular abscess
  • Prosthetic valve dehiscence
  • Intracardiac fistula
  • Increasing vegetation size despite therapy

Advanced Imaging

Cardiac CT

Cardiac CT may improve detection of:

  • Perivalvular abscess
  • Pseudoaneurysm
  • Fistula
  • Prosthetic valve complications
  • Structural anatomy before surgery

FDG PET/CT

FDG PET/CT is particularly useful in prosthetic valve and cardiac-device infection.

Potential Uses

  • Detect prosthetic valve infection
  • Identify cardiac-device pocket or lead infection
  • Detect extracardiac septic emboli
  • Find an occult source of bacteremia
  • Support diagnosis when echocardiography is equivocal

White Blood Cell SPECT/CT

Radiolabeled leukocyte imaging may assist in selected prosthetic valve or device infections when other imaging is inconclusive.

Brain Imaging

Obtain CT or MRI when there are:

  • Focal neurologic deficits
  • Seizure
  • Altered mental status
  • Severe headache
  • Concern for hemorrhage or mycotic aneurysm
  • Preoperative neurologic concerns

Whole-Body Imaging

Targeted CT, MRI, or PET/CT may identify:

  • Splenic infarct or abscess
  • Renal infarction
  • Vertebral osteomyelitis
  • Septic arthritis
  • Deep abscess
  • Vascular infection
Normal echocardiography does not exclude prosthetic valve endocarditis. Multimodality imaging is increasingly important in prosthetic and device-related disease.

Blood Culture–Negative Endocarditis

Blood culture–negative endocarditis is infective endocarditis in which routine blood cultures fail to identify an organism.

Most Common Cause

Antibiotics Given Before Blood Cultures

Other Causes

  • Fastidious organisms
  • Intracellular organisms
  • Fungal infection
  • Inadequate blood volume
  • Improper culture collection
  • Noninfectious endocarditis mimics

Important Organisms

Organism Exposure Clue Useful Testing
Coxiella burnetii Cattle, sheep, goats, farm exposure Phase I IgG serology and PCR
Bartonella henselae Cats Serology and molecular testing
Bartonella quintana Body lice, homelessness Serology and molecular testing
Brucella species Unpasteurized dairy, livestock, endemic travel Serology, prolonged culture, molecular testing
Tropheryma whipplei May lack classic GI symptoms PCR on blood or tissue
Fungi Prosthetic valve, immunosuppression, injection drug use Fungal cultures, biomarkers, tissue testing

Diagnostic Approach

  1. Confirm that adequate blood cultures were obtained.
  2. Review all antibiotics given before culture collection.
  3. Repeat cultures after discussion with infectious disease when appropriate.
  4. Obtain detailed animal, occupational, travel, housing, and dietary history.
  5. Send targeted serology and molecular testing.
  6. Use TEE and multimodality imaging.
  7. Send excised valve or embolic tissue for culture, histopathology, and molecular testing.
  8. Evaluate for noninfectious mimics.

Valve Tissue Testing

  • Histopathology
  • Gram stain and bacterial culture
  • Fungal stain and culture
  • Mycobacterial testing when indicated
  • Broad-range bacterial PCR
  • Organism-specific PCR
  • Metagenomic sequencing where available
Do not repeatedly broaden empiric antibiotics without pursuing a structured culture-negative endocarditis evaluation.

Differential Diagnosis

Noninfectious Endocardial Lesions

  • Nonbacterial thrombotic endocarditis
  • Libman-Sacks endocarditis
  • Cardiac tumor
  • Lambl excrescences
  • Thrombus on prosthetic material

Systemic Mimics

  • Vasculitis
  • Antiphospholipid syndrome
  • Atrial myxoma
  • Occult malignancy
  • Lymphoma
  • Drug fever
  • Adult-onset Still disease
  • Other causes of fever of unknown origin

Nonbacterial Thrombotic Endocarditis

Nonbacterial thrombotic endocarditis is associated with:

  • Advanced malignancy
  • Systemic lupus erythematosus
  • Antiphospholipid syndrome
  • Severe systemic inflammatory states

Clues Favoring NBTE

  • Repeated systemic emboli
  • Negative blood cultures
  • No infectious syndrome
  • Underlying malignancy or autoimmune disease
  • Valve vegetations without destruction
In culture-negative suspected endocarditis, always consider both fastidious infection and noninfectious thrombotic endocarditis.

Empiric Antimicrobial Therapy

Obtain adequate blood cultures before empiric therapy whenever the patient is stable. Empiric selection depends on:

  • Native vs prosthetic valve
  • Community vs healthcare acquisition
  • Acute vs subacute presentation
  • Previous antibiotics
  • Injection drug use
  • Cardiac device
  • Local resistance patterns
  • Renal function
  • Drug allergy

When to Start Immediately

  • Septic shock
  • Acute severe valve dysfunction
  • Rapidly progressive infection
  • Neurologic or embolic complication
  • High suspicion with systemic instability

Stable Subacute Disease

In a stable patient with subacute suspected endocarditis, it may be reasonable to obtain complete cultures and diagnostic testing before starting antibiotics.

Typical Empiric Coverage Goals

Clinical Setting Coverage Goals
Acute native valve IE MRSA, MSSA, streptococci, and enterococci
Subacute native valve IE Viridans streptococci, S. gallolyticus, enterococci, and staphylococci
Early prosthetic valve IE MRSA, coagulase-negative staphylococci, enterococci, and gram-negative organisms
Late prosthetic valve IE Staphylococci, streptococci, enterococci, and other community organisms
Healthcare-associated IE MRSA, enterococci, resistant gram-negative organisms

Common Empiric Concepts

  • Vancomycin is commonly used to cover MRSA and resistant gram-positive organisms.
  • A broad beta-lactam may be added for gram-negative and susceptible gram-positive coverage.
  • Regimens should be narrowed promptly when microbiology becomes available.
  • Aminoglycosides should not be added reflexively because of nephrotoxicity.
  • Empiric prosthetic valve regimens require specialist input.
Do not use a generic “endocarditis regimen” without considering valve type, timing, healthcare exposure, previous cultures, kidney function, and likely source.

Organism-Directed Therapy: High-Yield Overview

Definitive therapy should be based on organism identification, susceptibility, valve type, complications, renal function, and surgical plan.

Organism Common Preferred Therapy Typical Duration Framework
MSSA native valve Cefazolin, nafcillin, or oxacillin Usually 6 weeks for left-sided IE
MRSA native valve Vancomycin or high-dose daptomycin Usually 6 weeks
Penicillin-susceptible viridans streptococci Penicillin G or ceftriaxone Usually 4 weeks; selected uncomplicated cases may qualify for shorter combination therapy
S. gallolyticus Penicillin G or ceftriaxone when susceptible Usually 4 weeks for native valve
Enterococcus faecalis Ampicillin plus ceftriaxone or selected synergistic regimen Usually 6 weeks
HACEK Ceftriaxone Usually 4 weeks native valve and 6 weeks prosthetic valve
Fungal IE High-dose echinocandin or lipid amphotericin-based therapy plus surgery Prolonged; chronic suppression often required
Exact drug doses and combinations should be individualized with infectious disease, pharmacy, cardiology, and cardiac surgery.

Staphylococcal Endocarditis

MSSA Endocarditis

Preferred definitive therapy generally uses an antistaphylococcal beta-lactam:

  • Cefazolin
  • Nafcillin
  • Oxacillin
Vancomycin is not preferred definitive therapy for MSSA endocarditis when a beta-lactam can be safely used.

MRSA Endocarditis

Common options include:

  • Vancomycin with AUC-guided dosing
  • High-dose daptomycin
  • Specialist-directed salvage therapy for persistent bacteremia

Persistent MRSA Infection

  1. Confirm adequate dosing and susceptibility.
  2. Repeat cultures.
  3. Obtain or repeat TEE.
  4. Search for perivalvular abscess and metastatic infection.
  5. Remove infected devices.
  6. Obtain early surgical evaluation.
  7. Consider a daptomycin-based or combination salvage strategy.

Coagulase-Negative Staphylococci

These organisms are particularly associated with:

  • Prosthetic valve endocarditis
  • Cardiac-device infection
  • Vascular graft infection
  • Biofilm formation

Do not dismiss repeated coagulase-negative staphylococcal cultures as contamination in a patient with prosthetic material.

Rifampin

Rifampin may be included in selected staphylococcal prosthetic-material infections after bloodstream clearance and source-control planning. It should not be used as monotherapy because resistance develops rapidly.

Aminoglycosides

Routine aminoglycoside use is not recommended for native-valve staphylococcal endocarditis because nephrotoxicity outweighs proven benefit.

Streptococcal Endocarditis

Viridans Group Streptococci

Treatment depends on the penicillin MIC, valve type, renal function, and complications.

Common Options

  • Penicillin G
  • Ceftriaxone
  • Vancomycin when a severe beta-lactam allergy exists

Selected Two-Week Regimen

A two-week beta-lactam plus gentamicin regimen may be considered only in carefully selected patients with uncomplicated, highly susceptible native-valve infection and normal renal function.

Do Not Use the Short Regimen When:

  • Prosthetic valve is present
  • Renal dysfunction is present
  • Extracardiac abscess is present
  • Cardiac complication is present
  • Organism is not highly susceptible
  • Symptoms have been prolonged

Streptococcus gallolyticus

S. gallolyticus Endocarditis → Colon Evaluation

Patients should undergo evaluation for colorectal neoplasia, even if gastrointestinal symptoms are absent.

Abiotrophia and Granulicatella

Nutritionally variant streptococci may cause difficult-to-treat endocarditis with increased relapse risk. Therapy often requires prolonged treatment and specialist input.

Beta-Hemolytic Streptococci

Group A, B, C, and G streptococci can cause acute, destructive endocarditis. Evaluate for:

  • Skin or soft-tissue source
  • Diabetes
  • Older age
  • Injection drug use
  • Metastatic infection

Enterococcal Endocarditis

Why Enterococcus Is Difficult to Treat

Enterococci are relatively tolerant to beta-lactams and often require a combination strategy to achieve bactericidal activity.

Common Sources

  • Urinary tract
  • Gastrointestinal tract
  • Biliary tract
  • Healthcare-associated procedures
  • Unknown source in older adults

Enterococcus faecalis

Common Preferred Regimen

Ampicillin + Ceftriaxone

Advantages

  • Provides dual beta-lactam synergy
  • Avoids prolonged aminoglycoside nephrotoxicity
  • Useful with high-level aminoglycoside resistance
  • Often favored in older patients and renal dysfunction

Ampicillin Plus Gentamicin

This may be used in selected susceptible infections when renal function permits, but nephrotoxicity and ototoxicity limit prolonged use.

Enterococcus faecium

  • More frequently resistant to ampicillin
  • May be vancomycin resistant
  • May require linezolid or high-dose daptomycin-based treatment
  • Requires susceptibility-guided expert management

Colonic Evaluation

Some patients with E. faecalis endocarditis without a clear source may benefit from gastrointestinal evaluation, particularly when age and clinical context raise concern for colonic pathology.

Do not treat enterococcal endocarditis with ceftriaxone alone. Ceftriaxone is used with ampicillin for synergistic activity in selected E. faecalis disease.

HACEK and Non-HACEK Gram-Negative Endocarditis

HACEK Endocarditis

Ceftriaxone is a common preferred agent because beta-lactamase production may make ampicillin unreliable.

Typical Duration

  • Native valve: approximately 4 weeks
  • Prosthetic valve: approximately 6 weeks

Non-HACEK Gram-Negative IE

Non-HACEK gram-negative endocarditis is uncommon but associated with high morbidity and mortality.

Risk Factors

  • Healthcare exposure
  • Injection drug use
  • Prosthetic valves
  • Immunocompromise
  • Urinary or gastrointestinal source

Important Organisms

  • Pseudomonas aeruginosa
  • Serratia marcescens
  • Enterobacterales
  • Acinetobacter species

Management

  • Use susceptibility-guided bactericidal therapy.
  • Combination therapy may be considered in selected cases.
  • Obtain early surgical evaluation.
  • Remove infected devices or prosthetic material when feasible.
  • Expect prolonged therapy.
Non-HACEK gram-negative endocarditis should be managed with an Endocarditis Team because antimicrobial and surgical decisions are complex.

Fungal Endocarditis

Risk Factors

  • Prosthetic valve
  • Injection drug use
  • Prolonged central venous catheter
  • Total parenteral nutrition
  • Immunosuppression
  • Previous cardiac surgery
  • Prolonged broad-spectrum antibiotics

Common Organisms

  • Candida species
  • Aspergillus species
  • Other molds and yeasts

Clinical Features

  • Large vegetations
  • Frequent systemic embolization
  • Persistent fever
  • Negative routine cultures with Aspergillus
  • Prosthetic valve dysfunction
  • High relapse risk

Treatment Principles

  • Early cardiac surgery is generally recommended when feasible.
  • Candida IE may be treated initially with lipid amphotericin B-based therapy or a high-dose echinocandin.
  • Aspergillus IE often requires voriconazole-based therapy.
  • Therapy is prolonged.
  • Long-term or lifelong suppressive antifungal therapy may be required.
Aspergillus endocarditis frequently has negative blood cultures. Consider it with large vegetations, emboli, prosthetic valves, and immunosuppression.

Prosthetic Valve Endocarditis

Prosthetic valve endocarditis includes infection of surgically implanted valves, transcatheter valves, annuloplasty material, and valve-repair prosthetic material.

Why PVE Is High Risk

  • Biofilm formation
  • Perivalvular extension
  • Abscess formation
  • Valve dehiscence
  • Paravalvular leak
  • Difficult microbiologic eradication
  • Higher relapse and mortality

Clinical Clues

  • New prosthetic murmur
  • Heart failure
  • Persistent bacteremia
  • New conduction abnormality
  • Prosthetic instability
  • Perivalvular leak
  • Embolic disease

Diagnostic Imaging

  • TTE
  • TEE
  • Cardiac CT
  • FDG PET/CT
  • White blood cell SPECT/CT in selected cases

Common Organisms

  • S. aureus
  • Coagulase-negative staphylococci
  • Enterococci
  • Viridans streptococci
  • Gram-negative organisms
  • Fungi

Management Principles

  • Obtain early cardiac surgery consultation.
  • Use prolonged organism-specific antimicrobial therapy.
  • Assess for abscess, dehiscence, and obstruction.
  • Evaluate embolic complications.
  • Use multimodality imaging.
  • Remove infected prosthetic material when surgical indications are present.
A normal or equivocal TTE does not exclude prosthetic valve endocarditis. TEE and advanced imaging are often required.

Transcatheter Valve Endocarditis

Endocarditis after TAVR may be difficult to visualize because prosthetic material creates acoustic artifact. Enterococci and staphylococci are important organisms. Management should involve a valve center and multidisciplinary Endocarditis Team.

Cardiac Implantable Electronic Device Infection

Types of Infection

  • Generator-pocket infection
  • Lead infection
  • Device-associated endocarditis
  • Bloodstream infection with secondary device seeding

Pocket Infection Findings

  • Erythema
  • Swelling
  • Warmth
  • Pain
  • Drainage
  • Skin erosion
  • Exposed generator or lead
A normal device pocket does not exclude lead or valvular infection.

Diagnostic Evaluation

  • Multiple blood-culture sets
  • TTE
  • TEE
  • FDG PET/CT in selected cases
  • Assessment for septic pulmonary emboli
  • Evaluation for valvular involvement

Management

Definite Device Infection = Complete System Extraction
  • Remove the generator and all leads.
  • Do not remove only the generator while leaving infected leads.
  • Use organism-specific antimicrobial therapy.
  • Reimplant only after infection is controlled.
  • Reassess whether the patient still needs a device.
  • Consider an alternative implantation site or device type.

S. aureus Bacteremia with a Cardiac Device

A patient with a pacemaker or ICD and S. aureus bacteremia has a substantial risk of occult device infection. Obtain TEE and involve electrophysiology and infectious disease early.

Reimplantation

Timing depends on:

  • Organism
  • Blood-culture clearance
  • Presence of valvular infection
  • Pacing dependence
  • Need for ongoing device therapy

Right-Sided Endocarditis

Risk Factors

  • Injection drug use
  • Central venous catheter
  • Hemodialysis catheter
  • Pacemaker or ICD lead
  • Congenital heart disease

Most Common Valve

Tricuspid Valve

Common Presentation

  • Fever and bacteremia
  • Pleuritic chest pain
  • Dyspnea
  • Cough
  • Hemoptysis
  • Multiple peripheral pulmonary nodules
  • Cavitary lesions from septic pulmonary emboli

Common Organism

Staphylococcus aureus

Management Principles

  • Use organism-directed therapy.
  • Assess for persistent bacteremia.
  • Evaluate for pulmonary abscess or empyema.
  • Remove infected catheters and devices.
  • Offer evidence-based treatment for substance use disorder.
  • Consider surgery for refractory disease or severe valve dysfunction.

Potential Surgical Indications

  • Persistent bacteremia despite active therapy
  • Recurrent septic pulmonary emboli with large vegetation
  • Severe tricuspid regurgitation causing right heart failure
  • Difficult-to-eradicate organism
  • Valve destruction
Right-sided IE may have a less prominent murmur than left-sided disease. Pulmonary findings are often the diagnostic clue.

Surgical Indications

The three major reasons for surgery are:

Heart Failure • Uncontrolled Infection • Embolic Prevention

1. Heart Failure

  • Acute severe aortic or mitral regurgitation
  • Valve obstruction
  • Pulmonary edema
  • Cardiogenic shock
  • Intracardiac fistula causing hemodynamic compromise
  • Prosthetic valve dehiscence
Heart failure caused by valve destruction is the most common reason for urgent surgery and one of the strongest predictors of mortality.

2. Uncontrolled Infection

  • Perivalvular abscess
  • Pseudoaneurysm
  • Fistula
  • New conduction block
  • Persistent bacteremia despite active therapy
  • Increasing vegetation size
  • Fungal endocarditis
  • Highly resistant organism
  • Infected prosthetic material
  • Relapsing prosthetic valve endocarditis

3. Prevention of Embolism

  • Large mobile vegetation with previous embolic event
  • Persistent large vegetation despite active treatment
  • Very large vegetation with high embolic potential
  • Vegetation plus another surgical indication

Other Surgical Considerations

  • Prosthetic valve endocarditis with dysfunction
  • Cardiac-device infection requiring extraction
  • Right-sided IE with recurrent pulmonary emboli
  • Valve perforation
  • Coronary or systemic embolic complications

Timing Terminology

Timing Meaning
Emergency surgery Within approximately 24 hours when life-threatening hemodynamic compromise exists
Urgent surgery During the same hospitalization, often within days
Elective surgery After initial antimicrobial stabilization when immediate intervention is not required
Do not wait for completion of a six-week antibiotic course before consulting surgery. Surgical evaluation should occur as soon as an indication is recognized.

The Endocarditis Team

  • Infectious disease
  • Cardiology
  • Cardiac surgery
  • Cardiac imaging
  • Clinical microbiology
  • Neurology or neurosurgery when needed
  • Electrophysiology for device infection
  • Addiction medicine when relevant
  • Pharmacy

Neurologic Complications

Common Neurologic Events

  • Ischemic embolic stroke
  • Intracranial hemorrhage
  • Hemorrhagic conversion
  • Mycotic aneurysm
  • Brain abscess
  • Meningitis
  • Seizure
  • Encephalopathy

Imaging

  • Noncontrast CT for acute hemorrhage assessment
  • MRI for small embolic infarcts, abscess, and microhemorrhage
  • CTA or MRA when mycotic aneurysm is suspected
  • Catheter angiography in selected cases

Thrombolysis

IV thrombolysis is generally contraindicated in infective endocarditis–associated ischemic stroke because of the high risk of intracranial hemorrhage.

Mechanical Thrombectomy

Mechanical thrombectomy may be considered in selected patients with a large-vessel occlusion after multidisciplinary assessment.

Cardiac Surgery After Ischemic Stroke

When surgery is urgently indicated for heart failure, uncontrolled infection, or high embolic risk, an uncomplicated ischemic stroke without intracranial hemorrhage does not always require prolonged surgical delay.

Cardiac Surgery After Intracranial Hemorrhage

Intracranial hemorrhage often requires postponement of surgery when clinically possible, but timing must be individualized according to:

  • Hemodynamic status
  • Severity of infection
  • Size and stability of hemorrhage
  • Presence of mycotic aneurysm
  • Risk of ongoing embolization
  • Neurosurgical assessment

Mycotic Aneurysm

An infected intracranial aneurysm may rupture and cause subarachnoid or intracerebral hemorrhage. Management may require:

  • Targeted antibiotics
  • Endovascular intervention
  • Neurosurgery
  • Coordination with valve surgery
Neurologic complications do not automatically eliminate the possibility of cardiac surgery. They change the timing, imaging, and multidisciplinary risk assessment.

Anticoagulation and Antiplatelet Therapy

General Principle

Infective Endocarditis Is Not an Indication for Anticoagulation

Do not initiate anticoagulation solely to prevent embolization from a vegetation.

Existing Anticoagulation Indications

Patients may already require anticoagulation for:

  • Mechanical heart valve
  • Atrial fibrillation
  • Venous thromboembolism
  • Other established indications

Continuation or temporary interruption should be individualized based on:

  • Intracranial hemorrhage
  • Ischemic stroke
  • Planned surgery
  • Mechanical valve thrombosis risk
  • Bleeding risk

Mechanical Valve

Patients with a mechanical valve require careful multidisciplinary planning because stopping anticoagulation increases valve thrombosis risk, while continuing it may increase intracranial bleeding risk.

Antiplatelet Therapy

Do not initiate aspirin solely to prevent embolic events in active endocarditis. Continue an established antiplatelet indication only after considering bleeding and surgical risk.

Do not use anticoagulation or antiplatelet therapy as a substitute for antimicrobial treatment or surgery.

Monitoring Response to Therapy

Daily Clinical Monitoring

  • Temperature curve
  • Hemodynamics
  • Heart-failure symptoms
  • Cardiac examination
  • Neurologic examination
  • New focal pain
  • Device and catheter sites
  • Signs of embolization

Microbiologic Monitoring

  • Repeat blood cultures every 24–48 hours until negative.
  • Document the first negative culture date.
  • Investigate any recurrent positive culture.
  • Confirm organism identification and susceptibility.

Laboratory Monitoring

Test Reason
CBC Cytopenias and antibiotic toxicity
Creatinine Dose adjustment and nephrotoxicity
Liver enzymes Drug toxicity and systemic disease
CRP May help assess inflammatory trend
Vancomycin AUC Optimize exposure and reduce nephrotoxicity
CK Monitor daptomycin toxicity

When to Reimage

  • Persistent bacteremia
  • Persistent fever
  • New heart failure
  • New murmur
  • New conduction disease
  • New embolic event
  • Clinical deterioration
  • Before surgery when anatomy must be clarified

Persistent Fever Despite Negative Cultures

Consider:

  • Metastatic infection
  • Undrained abscess
  • Drug fever
  • Septic thrombophlebitis
  • Embolic tissue injury
  • Hospital-acquired infection
  • Inflammatory response that lags behind microbiologic clearance
Persistent fever is not synonymous with antimicrobial failure. Reassess diagnosis, complications, source control, and medication toxicity.

Duration of Therapy

Endocarditis generally requires prolonged therapy because of high bacterial burden, avascular vegetations, slow microbial killing, and risk of relapse.

Major Determinants

  • Organism
  • Native vs prosthetic valve
  • Susceptibility
  • Left- vs right-sided infection
  • Presence of abscess
  • Metastatic infection
  • Timing of culture clearance
  • Surgery and valve-culture results

Typical Framework

Infection Common Duration
Left-sided MSSA or MRSA native valve IE Usually 6 weeks
Penicillin-susceptible viridans native valve IE Usually 4 weeks
Selected uncomplicated viridans native valve IE Potential two-week synergistic regimen
Enterococcal IE Usually 6 weeks
HACEK native valve IE Usually 4 weeks
HACEK prosthetic valve IE Usually 6 weeks
Prosthetic valve IE Usually at least 6 weeks
Fungal IE Prolonged treatment plus possible chronic suppression

When Does Day 1 Begin?

Treatment is commonly counted from the first day of effective therapy after blood cultures have cleared. The treatment anchor should be explicitly documented.

Effect of Surgery on Duration

Postoperative duration depends on:

  • Preoperative treatment received
  • Valve-tissue culture result
  • Extent of infection
  • Presence of abscess
  • Organism
  • Residual infected material
Valve replacement does not automatically reset every patient to a completely new six-week course. Postoperative duration should be individualized with the Endocarditis Team.

OPAT and Partial Oral Therapy

Outpatient Parenteral Antimicrobial Therapy

OPAT may be appropriate after the critical phase when the patient is clinically stable and complications have been addressed.

OPAT Eligibility

  • Hemodynamic stability
  • Blood cultures cleared
  • No urgent surgical indication
  • Source controlled
  • Reliable vascular access
  • Stable renal and hepatic function
  • Safe home or infusion environment
  • Reliable follow-up
  • Defined monitoring plan

OPAT Is Unsafe When:

  • Persistent bacteremia
  • Uncontrolled heart failure
  • Perivalvular abscess without a plan
  • Unstable neurologic complication
  • Need for urgent surgery
  • Unreliable medication administration
  • No laboratory monitoring

Partial Oral Therapy

Selected clinically stable patients with left-sided endocarditis may complete treatment with carefully chosen high-bioavailability oral combinations after an initial IV phase.

Potential Eligibility

  • Stable clinical condition
  • Bloodstream clearance
  • Organism identified and susceptible
  • No uncontrolled abscess
  • No major malabsorption
  • Reliable adherence
  • Close specialist follow-up
  • Appropriate oral combination with pharmacokinetic support

Situations Requiring Caution or Continued IV Therapy

  • Persistent bacteremia
  • Fungal IE
  • Highly resistant organism
  • Uncontrolled prosthetic infection
  • CNS abscess
  • Severe GI absorption concerns
  • Poor adherence or follow-up
Partial oral therapy is not simply changing ceftriaxone to any oral antibiotic at discharge. It requires carefully selected patients, organism-specific multidrug regimens, and structured specialist follow-up.

Antibiotic Prophylaxis

Endocarditis prophylaxis is reserved for patients at the highest risk of a poor outcome who undergo selected high-risk dental procedures.

High-Risk Cardiac Conditions

  • Prosthetic cardiac valve, including transcatheter valve
  • Prosthetic material used for valve repair
  • Previous infective endocarditis
  • Selected unrepaired cyanotic congenital heart disease
  • Repaired congenital heart disease with residual shunt or valvular regurgitation adjacent to prosthetic material
  • Selected cardiac transplant recipients with structurally abnormal valve regurgitation

Dental Procedures Requiring Prophylaxis in High-Risk Patients

  • Manipulation of gingival tissue
  • Manipulation of the periapical region of teeth
  • Perforation of oral mucosa

Procedures That Generally Do Not Require Prophylaxis

  • Routine dental radiographs
  • Placement of removable orthodontic appliances
  • Adjustment of orthodontic appliances
  • Shedding of deciduous teeth
  • Bleeding from trauma to lips or oral mucosa
  • Routine GI or GU procedures without active infection

Standard Dental Prophylaxis

Situation Common Adult Regimen
Able to take oral medication Amoxicillin 2 g orally 30–60 minutes before procedure
Unable to take oral medication Ampicillin or selected IV/IM cephalosporin regimen
Non-anaphylactic penicillin allergy Cephalexin may be considered
Severe immediate beta-lactam allergy Azithromycin, clarithromycin, or doxycycline
Clindamycin is no longer preferred for routine dental endocarditis prophylaxis because of adverse-event risk.

Dental Health

Regular dental hygiene and treatment of chronic oral disease are more important for long-term prevention than episodic antibiotic prophylaxis.

Special Populations

People Who Inject Drugs

  • Screen for HIV, hepatitis B, and hepatitis C.
  • Evaluate for right-sided and left-sided IE.
  • Look for septic pulmonary emboli.
  • Offer medications for opioid use disorder.
  • Provide harm-reduction counseling and naloxone.
  • Do not deny surgery solely because of substance use.
  • Plan antimicrobial delivery based on individualized risk rather than stigma.

Hemodialysis

  • High risk for S. aureus bacteremia and endocarditis
  • Evaluate dialysis catheter or graft
  • Low threshold for TEE
  • Coordinate antimicrobial dosing with dialysis
  • Remove infected catheter when indicated

Pregnancy

  • Requires coordinated maternal-fetal, infectious disease, cardiology, and surgical care
  • Do not delay treatment of severe infection
  • Choose pregnancy-compatible antimicrobial therapy
  • Assess fetal and maternal hemodynamic risk
  • Surgery may be necessary for life-threatening maternal indications

Older Adults

  • May present without high fever
  • Enterococcus and healthcare-associated staphylococci are common
  • Degenerative valve disease and prosthetic valves increase risk
  • Renal dysfunction complicates antimicrobial therapy
  • Delirium may be the presenting symptom

Congenital Heart Disease

Management should involve adult congenital cardiology when complex anatomy or prosthetic material is present.

Daily Infective Endocarditis Checklist

  • What organism is causing the infection?
  • What are the susceptibility results?
  • Are blood cultures still positive?
  • What is the first negative culture date?
  • Is the antimicrobial regimen definitive and correctly dosed?
  • Does the patient have a native valve, prosthetic valve, or cardiac device?
  • Has TEE been completed?
  • Is advanced imaging needed?
  • Is there heart failure?
  • Is there a new conduction abnormality?
  • Is there evidence of abscess or valve destruction?
  • Has the patient developed a neurologic deficit?
  • Is there new back, joint, abdominal, or flank pain?
  • Has source control occurred?
  • Is cardiac surgery involved?
  • What is the planned treatment duration?
  • Is OPAT or partial oral therapy eventually appropriate?
  • What laboratory toxicity monitoring is required?

How to Present IE on Consult Rounds

A strong presentation should include:

  • Native valve, prosthetic valve, or device status
  • Organism and susceptibility
  • Dates of positive cultures
  • Date of first negative culture
  • TTE and TEE findings
  • Valve dysfunction
  • Vegetation size and location
  • Presence of abscess, perforation, fistula, or dehiscence
  • Embolic and metastatic evaluation
  • Neurologic status
  • Current antibiotic, dose, and start date
  • Surgical assessment
  • Planned duration and disposition
Avoid saying only, “TEE showed a vegetation.” State which valve is involved, vegetation size and mobility, severity of regurgitation, ventricular function, and whether an abscess or prosthetic complication is present.

Common Clinical Scenarios

Case 1: MSSA Bacteremia with Negative TTE

A hemodialysis patient has persistent MSSA bacteremia. TTE shows no vegetation.

A negative TTE does not exclude endocarditis. Obtain TEE, remove or evaluate dialysis access, repeat cultures, and search for metastatic infection.

Case 2: New PR Prolongation

A patient with aortic valve endocarditis develops progressive PR-interval prolongation.

Suspect perivalvular abscess. Obtain urgent TEE or cardiac CT and surgical evaluation.

Case 3: S. gallolyticus Endocarditis

Blood cultures grow Streptococcus gallolyticus, and TEE shows a mitral vegetation.

Treat the endocarditis and arrange colon evaluation for neoplasia.

Case 4: Enterococcus faecalis IE with CKD

An older patient with CKD has E. faecalis native-valve endocarditis.

Ampicillin plus ceftriaxone is often favored because it provides synergy while avoiding prolonged aminoglycoside nephrotoxicity.

Case 5: Stroke in Active IE

A patient with mitral valve endocarditis develops acute aphasia and a large-vessel occlusion.

Avoid routine IV thrombolysis. Urgently involve neurology and consider mechanical thrombectomy in an appropriate candidate.

Case 6: Pacemaker with S. aureus Bacteremia

The device pocket appears normal, but blood cultures repeatedly grow MRSA.

A normal pocket does not exclude lead infection. Obtain TEE and arrange electrophysiology and ID evaluation for complete extraction.

Case 7: Prosthetic Valve with Negative TEE

A patient with a prosthetic aortic valve has persistent fever and positive coagulase-negative staphylococcal cultures. TEE is equivocal.

Use multimodality imaging, including cardiac CT or FDG PET/CT, and treat repeated coagulase-negative staphylococcal cultures as clinically meaningful.

Case 8: Culture-Negative IE After Antibiotics

A stable patient received seven days of antibiotics before blood cultures were obtained. TEE shows a vegetation.

Prior antibiotics are the most likely cause of negative cultures. Pursue structured serologic, molecular, and tissue-based diagnosis rather than indefinitely broadening therapy.

Case 9: Tricuspid IE with Cavitary Nodules

A patient who injects drugs has fever, MRSA bacteremia, and multiple bilateral cavitary pulmonary nodules.

This is classic right-sided IE with septic pulmonary emboli. Evaluate the tricuspid valve and treat the substance use disorder alongside the infection.

Case 10: Acute Pulmonary Edema

A patient with aortic valve endocarditis develops acute severe aortic regurgitation and pulmonary edema.

This is a surgical emergency. Antibiotics alone will not correct acute valve failure.

Common Pitfalls

  • Ruling out IE because there is no murmur
  • Obtaining only one blood-culture set
  • Starting antibiotics before cultures in a stable subacute case
  • Relying on a negative TTE in a high-risk patient
  • Failing to repeat TEE when suspicion remains high
  • Ignoring new conduction disease
  • Failing to search for metastatic infection
  • Continuing vancomycin for MSSA endocarditis
  • Using gentamicin routinely for staphylococcal native-valve IE
  • Missing colonic neoplasia evaluation in S. gallolyticus disease
  • Using ceftriaxone alone for enterococcal endocarditis
  • Calling repeated coagulase-negative staphylococcal cultures contaminants in a prosthetic-valve patient
  • Leaving an infected cardiac device in place
  • Waiting until antibiotics are completed before consulting surgery
  • Initiating anticoagulation to prevent vegetation embolization
  • Giving IV thrombolysis for IE-associated stroke
  • Failing to consider culture-negative organisms
  • Assuming a negative blood culture after antibiotics excludes IE
  • Discharging to OPAT before blood-culture clearance
  • Using oral step-down without structured patient selection
  • Giving dental prophylaxis to low-risk patients
  • Failing to give prophylaxis to a high-risk patient undergoing an indicated dental procedure

Board and ID Consult Pearls

  • S. aureus bacteremia should always raise concern for endocarditis.
  • Three blood-culture sets should be obtained before antibiotics when feasible.
  • TEE is more sensitive than TTE, especially for prosthetic valves, devices, and abscesses.
  • A negative TTE does not exclude IE in a high-risk patient.
  • New conduction disease suggests perivalvular extension.
  • Heart failure is the most common major indication for surgery.
  • The major surgical categories are heart failure, uncontrolled infection, and embolic prevention.
  • MSSA IE should be treated with an antistaphylococcal beta-lactam when possible.
  • MRSA IE is commonly treated with vancomycin or high-dose daptomycin.
  • Routine gentamicin is not recommended for native-valve staphylococcal IE.
  • Viridans streptococcal IE is commonly treated with penicillin G or ceftriaxone.
  • S. gallolyticus requires colon evaluation.
  • E. faecalis IE is commonly treated with ampicillin plus ceftriaxone.
  • HACEK IE is commonly treated with ceftriaxone.
  • Culture-negative IE is most commonly caused by antibiotics given before cultures.
  • Coxiella, Bartonella, Brucella, and Tropheryma whipplei are key culture-negative pathogens.
  • Fungal IE usually requires surgery plus prolonged antifungal therapy.
  • Cardiac-device infection generally requires complete system extraction.
  • Right-sided IE often presents with septic pulmonary emboli.
  • IV thrombolysis is generally contraindicated in IE-associated ischemic stroke.
  • Mechanical thrombectomy may be considered for selected large-vessel occlusions.
  • IE itself is not an indication for anticoagulation.
  • FDG PET/CT is particularly useful in prosthetic valve and device infection.
  • Dental prophylaxis is reserved for high-risk cardiac patients undergoing selected dental procedures.
  • Amoxicillin 2 g orally 30–60 minutes before dental work is the standard adult prophylaxis regimen.
  • Clindamycin is no longer preferred for dental IE prophylaxis.
  • Good oral hygiene is a central preventive strategy.

Bottom Line

Infective endocarditis should be suspected in patients with persistent bacteremia, typical organisms, prosthetic valves or cardiac devices, unexplained embolic events, new valve dysfunction, or fever with injection drug use. Obtain multiple blood cultures before antibiotics when feasible, perform TTE followed by TEE when indicated, and use advanced imaging for prosthetic valve or device-related disease.

Treatment requires prolonged organism-directed bactericidal therapy, repeated blood cultures until clearance, evaluation for metastatic infection, and source control. MSSA requires a beta-lactam, MRSA generally requires vancomycin or high-dose daptomycin, viridans streptococci are commonly treated with penicillin or ceftriaxone, and E. faecalis often requires ampicillin plus ceftriaxone.

Early surgery is essential when infective endocarditis causes heart failure, uncontrolled infection, abscess, prosthetic dehiscence, persistent bacteremia, or major embolic risk. Device infection usually requires complete extraction. Neurologic events alter but do not automatically preclude surgery.

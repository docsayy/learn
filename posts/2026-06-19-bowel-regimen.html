Bowel Regimen: Practical Inpatient Approach to Constipation

Added June 19, 2026 • Hospital Medicine • ~10 min read

A practical inpatient approach to constipation management including first-line bowel regimens, escalation strategies, opioid-induced constipation, severe constipation, and common inpatient pitfalls.

Why Constipation Matters

Constipation is one of the most common inpatient problems. Hospitalized patients move less, receive opioid medications, eat differently, and often become dehydrated.

Nearly every hospitalized patient is at risk for constipation due to:

  • Reduced mobility
  • Opioid use
  • Dietary changes
  • Dehydration
  • Acute illness

Constipation can lead to:

  • Abdominal pain
  • Nausea and vomiting
  • Urinary retention
  • Delirium in elderly patients
  • Ileus
  • Prolonged hospitalization
Key point: Most admitted patients should be started on a bowel regimen proactively rather than waiting for constipation to develop.

First-Line Bowel Regimen

Miralax + Senna

The standard inpatient bowel regimen consists of:

Medication Typical Dose Mechanism
Polyethylene Glycol, also called Miralax 17 g daily Osmotic laxative
Senna, also called Senokot 17.2 mg nightly Stimulant laxative

This combination addresses constipation through two different mechanisms and is effective for most hospitalized patients.

Avoid Docusate

Avoid routine docusate: Docusate, also called Colace, is commonly prescribed but has poor evidence supporting efficacy and is generally considered low-value care.

Multiple reviews and hospital medicine recommendations suggest avoiding routine docusate use because it provides little benefit compared with placebo.

Second-Line Agents

If the patient has not responded to Miralax and Senna, consider escalation.

Medication Comments
Bisacodyl suppository Often the preferred next step
Oral bisacodyl, also called Dulcolax Alternative stimulant option
Milk of magnesia Avoid in renal dysfunction
Magnesium citrate Avoid in AKI, ESRD, heart failure, or volume overload
Practical point: Many clinicians prefer trying a bisacodyl suppository before moving to stronger osmotic agents or enemas.

Escalation Strategies

Lactulose

Lactulose is extremely effective when patients remain constipated despite standard therapy.

Medication Typical Dose
Lactulose 30 mL every 2 hours until bowel movement

The major limitation is bloating, which may worsen abdominal discomfort or nausea.

Enemas

If oral agents fail, enemas may be necessary.

Type Comments
Fleet enema Small-volume enema
Tap water enema Large-volume option
Lactulose enema Alternative large-volume option
Mineral oil enema Alternative option
Renal warning: Avoid sodium phosphate enemas in patients with AKI, CKD stage 5, or ESRD due to risk of severe electrolyte disturbances.

Last Resort: GoLYTELY

GoLYTELY is essentially a bowel preparation used before colonoscopy and can be very effective for severe refractory constipation.

  • Large-volume polyethylene glycol solution
  • Often used when multiple prior interventions have failed

Mechanisms of Action

Class Examples Common Side Effect
Osmotic Miralax, lactulose, GoLYTELY Bloating
Stimulant Senna, bisacodyl Cramping
Softener Docusate Minimal efficacy
Bulk forming Psyllium Bloating

Psyllium

Psyllium can be particularly useful in irritable bowel syndrome because it may improve both diarrhea and constipation.

Opioid-Induced Constipation

Opioid-induced constipation is extremely common in hospitalized patients.

Prevention

  • Start bowel regimen when initiating opioids
  • Encourage hydration when appropriate
  • Promote mobility whenever possible

Treatment Options

  • Reduce opioid dose if possible
  • Polyethylene glycol
  • Senna
  • Lactulose
  • Methylnaltrexone
  • Oral naloxone in selected situations
Key point: Every patient receiving scheduled opioids should also receive a bowel regimen unless a specific contraindication exists.

Ogilvie Syndrome

Ogilvie syndrome, also called acute colonic pseudo-obstruction, should be considered when the colon becomes massively dilated and fails to function normally.

Treatment

  • Correct electrolyte abnormalities
  • Correct hypokalemia
  • Stop offending medications
  • Neostigmine when appropriate

Avoid Neostigmine In

  • Bradycardia
  • Active bronchospasm
  • Severe asthma exacerbation

Clinical Pearls

  1. Most hospitalized patients should receive Miralax plus Senna.
  2. Docusate has poor evidence and should generally be avoided.
  3. Bisacodyl suppositories are excellent escalation options.
  4. Magnesium-containing laxatives should be avoided in significant renal dysfunction.
  5. Lactulose is highly effective but frequently causes bloating.
  6. GoLYTELY is a powerful rescue therapy for severe constipation.
  7. Always prescribe a bowel regimen alongside opioids.
  8. Constipation can paradoxically present as diarrhea due to overflow incontinence.
  9. Treating overflow diarrhea often requires aggressive bowel cleanout.
  10. Consider non-pharmacologic options such as walking, coffee, hydration, and prune juice when appropriate.

Bottom Line

For most hospitalized patients, start with Miralax 17 g daily and Senna 17.2 mg nightly. Escalate to bisacodyl, lactulose, enemas, or GoLYTELY when needed. Avoid routine docusate use, always provide bowel prophylaxis with opioids, and remember that apparent diarrhea may sometimes represent severe constipation with overflow incontinence.

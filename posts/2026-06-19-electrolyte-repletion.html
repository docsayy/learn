Electrolyte Repletion: Practical Guide to Potassium, Magnesium, Calcium, and Phosphate Replacement

June 19, 2026 Hospital Medicine

Electrolyte replacement is one of the most common tasks in hospital medicine. Potassium, magnesium, calcium, and phosphate are frequently replaced despite limited evidence supporting aggressive normalization in every patient. This guide reviews practical replacement strategies, common dosing regimens, important physiologic principles, and situations where replacement is most beneficial.

Electrolyte Replacement Overview

Many hospitals routinely target:

  • Potassium > 4.0 mEq/L
  • Magnesium > 2.0 mg/dL

These goals are particularly common in:

  • Heart failure patients
  • Acute coronary syndrome
  • Patients receiving aggressive diuresis
  • Patients at risk for arrhythmias
Although widely practiced, evidence supporting aggressive replacement to potassium >4 and magnesium >2 is relatively limited.

What Does the Evidence Suggest?

Observational studies suggest the highest risk occurs at electrolyte extremes:

  • Potassium < 3.5 mEq/L
  • Potassium > 4.5–5.0 mEq/L

Patients with potassium levels of 3.6–3.9 mEq/L and no arrhythmia risk factors are likely over-replaced in many hospitals.

Potassium Replacement

Quick Estimation Rule

10 mEq potassium generally raises serum potassium by approximately 0.1 mEq/L.
Current K Goal K Suggested Replacement
3.6 4.0 40 mEq PO
3.2 4.0 40 mEq PO + 40 mEq IV
<3.0 Variable Often requires larger replacement

Important Exception

Patients with AKI or CKD require substantially more cautious potassium replacement due to risk of hyperkalemia.

Preferred Route

Oral potassium is preferred whenever possible because:

  • Excellent gastrointestinal absorption
  • Less expensive
  • Avoids IV discomfort
  • Avoids unnecessary IV access use

Potassium Formulations

Formulation Advantages Disadvantages
KCl SR Tablet Commonly available Large tablet
KCl Oral Solution Rapid absorption Poor taste
KCl IV Useful when PO not possible Burning and discomfort
K-Lyte Effervescent, easier to take Less commonly stocked

IV Administration Rates

Access Type Maximum Rate
Peripheral IV 10 mEq/hr
Central Line 20 mEq/hr

Refractory Hypokalemia

One of the most common causes of persistent hypokalemia is magnesium deficiency.

Always check and replace magnesium before aggressively replacing potassium.

Manifestations of Hypokalemia

  • Muscle weakness
  • Fatigue
  • Paralysis
  • Cardiac arrhythmias
  • ECG abnormalities

Magnesium Replacement

Quick Estimation Rule

1 gram IV magnesium sulfate increases serum magnesium by approximately 0.1 mg/dL.

Approximately:

  • 400 mg magnesium oxide PO ≈ 2 grams IV magnesium

Replacement Options

Formulation Comments
IV Magnesium Sulfate Preferred inpatient option
Magnesium Oxide Common outpatient option

Typical Oral Dosing

  • 400 mg once
  • 400 mg daily
  • 400 mg twice daily

Major Limitation

Magnesium oxide commonly causes diarrhea.

Manifestations of Hypomagnesemia

  • Muscle cramps
  • Paresthesias
  • Hypokalemia
  • Hypocalcemia
  • Cardiac arrhythmias

Why Magnesium Matters

Magnesium inhibits potassium secretion channels in the collecting duct. Without adequate magnesium, the kidney continues wasting potassium regardless of replacement.

Calcium Replacement

Correct Calcium First

Always correct calcium for albumin before diagnosing hypocalcemia.

Corrected Calcium = Measured Calcium + 0.8 × (4.0 − Albumin)

Ionized calcium can also be measured directly and avoids the need for correction.

When to Give IV Calcium

  • Calcium < 7.5 mg/dL
  • Ionized calcium < 0.8 mmol/L
  • Symptomatic hypocalcemia

Calcium Formulations

Agent Typical Use
Calcium Chloride Cardiac arrest, central access preferred
Calcium Gluconate Most hospitalized patients
Calcium Carbonate Oral replacement
Calcium Citrate Alternative oral replacement

Calcium Chloride vs Calcium Gluconate

  • Calcium chloride contains approximately three times more elemental calcium.
  • Higher risk of tissue necrosis if extravasation occurs.
  • Generally reserved for central venous administration.

Refractory Hypocalcemia

Persistent hypocalcemia should prompt evaluation and treatment of hypomagnesemia.

Manifestations of Hypocalcemia

  • Perioral numbness
  • Paresthesias
  • Trousseau sign
  • Chvostek sign
  • Bronchospasm
  • Seizures
  • Prolonged QT interval
  • Arrhythmias

Phosphate Replacement

Phosphate replacement is dosed in mmol rather than mEq.

Normal Range

  • 2.4–5.0 mg/dL

IV Replacement Strategy

Phosphate Level Suggested Dose Infusion Time
2.0–2.5 15 mmol 4 hours
<2.0 30 mmol 6 hours
<1.5 45 mmol 8 hours

Which Formulation Should You Use?

  • Sodium phosphate if potassium is normal or elevated
  • Potassium phosphate if potassium is low

Important Fact

15 mmol potassium phosphate contains approximately 21 mEq potassium.

Oral Replacement

Mild hypophosphatemia (>2.0 mg/dL) can often be treated with oral K-Phos tablets.

Manifestations of Hypophosphatemia

  • Muscle weakness
  • Respiratory muscle weakness
  • Bone pain
  • Muscle cramps
  • Cardiac dysfunction
  • Neurologic symptoms

High-Yield Clinical Pearls

  • Potassium replacement is often overused in low-risk patients with K 3.6–3.9.
  • Always check magnesium in refractory hypokalemia.
  • Always check magnesium in refractory hypocalcemia.
  • PO potassium is preferred whenever possible.
  • Peripheral IV potassium should not exceed 10 mEq/hr.
  • Central IV potassium can be given up to 20 mEq/hr.
  • Magnesium oxide frequently causes diarrhea.
  • Correct calcium for albumin before replacing calcium.
  • Use sodium phosphate when potassium is normal and potassium phosphate when potassium is low.
  • Patients undergoing aggressive diuresis are among the highest-risk groups for electrolyte-related arrhythmias.

Bottom Line

The most important practical rule is to think of potassium, magnesium, and calcium as interconnected electrolytes. Persistent abnormalities in one are often caused by deficiencies in another. Replace thoughtfully, avoid unnecessary overcorrection, and prioritize oral replacement whenever feasible.

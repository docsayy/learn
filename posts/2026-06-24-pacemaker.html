Cardiac Pacemakers: The Complete Resident Guide

Added June 24, 2026 • Cardiology • ~14 min read

Cardiac pacemakers are among the most commonly implanted cardiac devices and are essential for the treatment of symptomatic bradycardia and conduction disease. Understanding pacemaker indications, modes, ECG interpretation, troubleshooting, and long-term management is critical for residents rotating through internal medicine, cardiology, emergency medicine, and critical care.

What Is a Pacemaker?

A cardiac pacemaker is an implantable device that delivers electrical impulses to the heart to maintain an adequate heart rate and rhythm.

  • Treats symptomatic bradycardia
  • Senses intrinsic cardiac activity
  • Provides pacing when native conduction fails
  • Can function as a backup or dominant rhythm source
Think of a pacemaker as an artificial electrical system that takes over when the heart's natural conduction system becomes unreliable.

Indications for Pacemaker Placement

High-Yield Principle

Symptoms + Bradycardia = Consider Pacemaker

Sinus Node Dysfunction

  • Symptomatic sinus bradycardia
  • Sinus pauses causing syncope
  • Tachy-brady syndrome

Atrioventricular Block

  • Mobitz II second-degree AV block
  • Third-degree complete heart block
  • High-grade AV block
Mobitz II and complete heart block generally require permanent pacing even when symptoms are minimal or absent.

Not Typical Indications

  • Asymptomatic sinus bradycardia
  • First-degree AV block
  • Asymptomatic Mobitz I / Wenckebach

Types of Pacemakers

By Chamber

Type Leads Typical Use
Single Chamber RA or RV AF with slow ventricular response
Dual Chamber RA + RV AV block, sinus node dysfunction
Biventricular / CRT RA + RV + LV Heart failure with dyssynchrony

Transvenous Pacemaker

  • Most common type
  • Leads inserted through subclavian or axillary vein
  • Can support single-chamber, dual-chamber, or CRT pacing

Leadless Pacemaker

  • Implanted directly into the right ventricle
  • No transvenous leads
  • No chest pocket
  • Lower risk of lead-related complications
  • Usually limited to ventricular pacing

Conduction System Pacing

  • His bundle pacing
  • Left bundle branch area pacing

Conduction system pacing provides more physiologic ventricular activation and may reduce the risk of pacing-induced cardiomyopathy compared with traditional right ventricular pacing.

Pacemaker Modes

Pacemaker modes use a standardized three-letter code.

Position Meaning
First Letter Chamber paced
Second Letter Chamber sensed
Third Letter Response to sensing

Common Modes

Mode Description Typical Use
VVI Ventricular pacing and sensing Atrial fibrillation with slow ventricular response
DDD Dual-chamber pacing and sensing Most common permanent pacemaker mode
AAI Atrial pacing and sensing Rarely used today
DDD pacing helps preserve AV synchrony, which improves hemodynamics in patients with intact atrial activity.

How Pacemakers Work

Sensing

The device detects intrinsic cardiac activity.

Pacing

If the heart rate falls below the programmed threshold, the pacemaker delivers an electrical impulse.

Capture

Capture means successful myocardial depolarization after a pacing stimulus.

Problem Definition
Failure to Capture Pacing spike without myocardial depolarization
Oversensing Device interprets noise as intrinsic cardiac activity
Undersensing Device misses intrinsic cardiac activity
Failure to Pace No pacing spike when pacing is needed

ECG Interpretation

Pacing Spikes

  • Spike before P wave → atrial pacing
  • Spike before QRS → ventricular pacing
  • Spike before both P wave and QRS → dual-chamber pacing

Typical ECG Patterns

  • Right ventricular pacing produces a left bundle branch block-like morphology.
  • CRT may produce a narrower QRS compared with RV-only pacing.

Recognizing Malfunction

ECG Finding Likely Diagnosis
Pacing spike without QRS Failure to capture
No pacing spike when needed Failure to pace
Pacing spikes occurring inappropriately Undersensing
Long pauses because device senses noise Oversensing

Implantation & Complications

Procedure Overview

  • Local anesthesia with sedation
  • Creation of generator pocket, usually in the upper chest
  • Lead placement in RA, RV, and/or coronary sinus
  • Device testing and programming

Complications

  • Pneumothorax
  • Lead dislodgement
  • Pocket hematoma
  • Infection
  • Cardiac perforation
  • Venous thrombosis
New chest pain, hypotension, pericardial effusion, or loss of capture shortly after implantation should raise concern for lead perforation.

Pacemaker Syndrome

Pacemaker syndrome occurs when AV synchrony is lost, resulting in reduced cardiac output.

Symptoms

  • Fatigue
  • Dizziness
  • Exercise intolerance
  • Hypotension
  • Palpitations
  • Cannon A waves

Classic Cause

VVI pacing in a patient who still has intact atrial activity.

Maintaining AV synchrony is one reason dual-chamber pacing is often preferred over isolated ventricular pacing.

Cardiac Resynchronization Therapy

Typical Indications

  • LVEF ≤ 35%
  • QRS ≥ 150 ms
  • Left bundle branch block pattern
  • Persistent heart failure symptoms despite guideline-directed medical therapy

Benefits

  • Improved survival
  • Improved symptoms
  • Reduced heart failure hospitalizations
  • Reverse ventricular remodeling
Right ventricular pacing alone can worsen ventricular dyssynchrony, whereas CRT restores more coordinated ventricular contraction.

Leadless Pacemakers

Advantages

  • No transvenous leads
  • No chest pocket
  • Reduced infection risk
  • Lower risk of lead-related complications

Limitations

  • Primarily ventricular pacing
  • Limited upgrade options
  • No traditional atrial lead
  • Not suitable for all patients

Follow-Up & Monitoring

Routine Follow-Up

  • Device interrogation
  • Remote monitoring when available
  • Annual or more frequent evaluations depending on device and patient status

What Is Checked?

  • Battery life
  • Lead integrity
  • Capture thresholds
  • Sensing performance
  • Arrhythmia burden
  • Percentage of pacing
Device interrogation is essential whenever pacemaker malfunction, syncope, unexplained dizziness, or device-related symptoms are suspected.

Perioperative Considerations

Electromagnetic Interference

Electrocautery, MRI scanners, and other electrical sources may interfere with device function.

Management Strategies

  • Determine device type and pacing dependence
  • Consider magnet placement when appropriate
  • Temporary reprogramming when needed
  • Device interrogation before and after selected procedures

MRI

Many modern pacemakers are MRI-conditional and can safely undergo MRI when appropriate protocols are followed.

Common Clinical Scenarios

Syncope + Complete Heart Block

Temporary pacing followed by permanent pacemaker placement.

Atrial Fibrillation + Slow Ventricular Response

Single-chamber VVI pacemaker.

Sinus Node Dysfunction + Intact AV Conduction

Dual-chamber DDD pacemaker.

Heart Failure + LBBB

Cardiac resynchronization therapy if criteria are met.

Pacemaker vs ICD

Feature Pacemaker ICD
Primary Purpose Treat bradycardia Prevent sudden cardiac death
Treats VT/VF No Yes
Can Deliver Shock No Yes
Can Pace Slow Rhythms Yes Often yes

Common Pitfalls

  • Implanting pacemakers for asymptomatic sinus bradycardia
  • Missing Mobitz II AV block
  • Ignoring pacemaker-induced cardiomyopathy
  • Failing to recognize loss of capture
  • Misinterpreting oversensing and undersensing on ECG
  • Overlooking CRT candidates with heart failure and wide QRS
  • Assuming all pacemakers are MRI-compatible
  • Failing to ask about device dependence before procedures

Clinical Pearls

  • Pacemakers primarily treat symptomatic bradycardia.
  • Mobitz II and complete heart block generally require pacing.
  • DDD pacing preserves AV synchrony.
  • VVI pacing is common in atrial fibrillation.
  • CRT improves outcomes in selected heart failure patients.
  • Always assess sensing, pacing, and capture.
  • RV pacing can worsen ventricular function over time.
  • Leadless pacemakers reduce lead-related complications.
  • Pacemaker spikes should always be interpreted in clinical context.
  • Device interrogation is essential for troubleshooting.

Bottom Line

Pacemaker management is about matching the patient's conduction abnormality to the correct device. Identify the rhythm problem, choose the appropriate pacing strategy, recognize device malfunction, and understand the long-term hemodynamic effects of pacing. Residents who understand sensing, capture, AV synchrony, CRT, and ECG interpretation will be far more comfortable managing patients with implanted cardiac devices.

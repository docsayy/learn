Asthma and Status Asthmaticus

Added July 15, 2026 • Pulmonary and Critical Care • ~30 min read

A resident-level guide to confirming asthma, selecting anti-inflammatory controller therapy, assessing severe exacerbations, bronchodilator delivery, systemic corticosteroids, magnesium, NIV, intubation, and avoiding dynamic hyperinflation and respiratory arrest.

Clinical Overview

Asthma is variable expiratory airflow limitation with airway inflammation and hyperresponsiveness. Every patient needs an anti-inflammatory strategy; short-acting bronchodilator-only treatment is unsafe because it does not address inflammation.

Confirm Variable Airflow Limitation + Control Inflammation + Reduce Future Risk

Confirming the Diagnosis

  • Typical variable symptoms: wheeze, dyspnea, chest tightness, cough.
  • Spirometry showing obstruction with significant bronchodilator reversibility or variability.
  • Peak-flow variability, bronchoprovocation, or response to anti-inflammatory therapy when spirometry is nondiagnostic.
  • Reconsider vocal-cord dysfunction, heart failure, COPD, bronchiectasis, PE, or central airway disease.

Controller Strategy

  • ICS-containing therapy is recommended for adults and adolescents with asthma.
  • ICS-formoterol can serve as reliever and maintenance in appropriate regimens.
  • Review inhaler technique, adherence, exposures, obesity, rhinitis, reflux, and smoking before escalating.
  • Refer severe asthma for phenotype assessment and biologic therapy consideration.

Assessing an Exacerbation

  • Speech, posture, agitation or drowsiness
  • Respiratory rate and accessory-muscle use
  • SpO₂ and peak expiratory flow when feasible
  • Silent chest or poor air movement
  • Prior intubation, ICU admission, or recent systemic steroid use
  • Response after the first treatment cycle
A normal or rising PaCO₂ in a severely distressed patient suggests fatigue and impending ventilatory failure.

Initial Severe-Exacerbation Treatment

  • Repeated or continuous inhaled short-acting beta agonist
  • Inhaled ipratropium in severe attacks
  • Systemic corticosteroid early
  • Controlled oxygen to target
  • IV magnesium for severe or poorly responsive exacerbation
  • Treat anaphylaxis, infection, pneumothorax, or other trigger when present

Epinephrine and Terbutaline

IM epinephrine is indicated for anaphylaxis and angioedema. Routine epinephrine is not required for standard asthma, but parenteral beta agonist may be considered when inhaled delivery is impossible or airflow is profoundly limited under specialist care.

NIV and HFNC

A closely monitored NIV trial may reduce work in selected alert patients, but evidence is weaker than in COPD. Do not use NIV in a tiring, obtunded, vomiting, or hemodynamically unstable patient. HFNC may improve oxygen delivery but does not relieve severe airflow obstruction as directly as ventilatory support.

Intubation and Ventilation

  • Use a skilled airway team and preoxygenate aggressively.
  • Anticipate hypotension from hyperinflation and induction.
  • Use low rate, high inspiratory flow, long expiratory time, and permissive hypercapnia.
  • Measure plateau and total PEEP.
  • Treat sudden hypotension with ventilator disconnection when dynamic hyperinflation is suspected, while excluding tension pneumothorax.

Refractory Status

  • Continuous bronchodilator and corticosteroid
  • Brief neuromuscular blockade for dangerous dyssynchrony
  • Ketamine or inhaled anesthetic in selected expert settings
  • Bronchoscopy only when mucus plugging is strongly suspected and risk is acceptable
  • ECMO referral for refractory gas-exchange failure

Discharge and Prevention

  • Provide ICS-containing controller and reliever plan.
  • Teach inhaler and spacer technique.
  • Provide written action plan.
  • Arrange early follow-up.
  • Address smoking, occupational triggers, and access barriers.

Common Pitfalls

  • SABA-only discharge
  • Reassurance from disappearance of wheeze in a silent chest
  • Delaying steroids
  • Routine antibiotics without infection
  • Normalizing PaCO₂ with injurious ventilation
  • Missing anaphylaxis or pneumothorax

Bottom Line

Treat asthma inflammation long term and airway obstruction urgently during exacerbation. Severe fatigue, silent chest, altered mentation, or rising PaCO₂ requires immediate escalation and a ventilator strategy that prioritizes exhalation time.

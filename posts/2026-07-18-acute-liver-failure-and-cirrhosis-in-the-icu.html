Liver Failure Spectrum, Metabolic Disease, and Renal Complications in the ICU

Clinical Overview

Abnormal liver enzymes, acute liver injury, acute liver failure, decompensated cirrhosis, and acute-on-chronic liver failure are related but clinically distinct conditions. The most important task is to determine whether the patient has isolated hepatocellular injury or true loss of hepatic function.

Aminotransferase elevation is a marker of hepatocyte injury. It does not, by itself, establish liver failure. Clinically important liver dysfunction is suggested by impaired synthetic function, hypoglycemia, hyperammonemia, altered mental status, worsening lactate clearance, or development of multiorgan failure.

Core principle: Transaminitis identifies hepatocyte injury. Acute liver failure requires hepatic encephalopathy and impaired synthetic function in a patient without established cirrhosis.

Normal Liver Function

Understanding normal hepatic physiology helps explain why severe liver dysfunction affects nearly every organ system.

Function Normal Role Consequence of Failure
Detoxification Converts ammonia to urea and clears endogenous toxins. Hyperammonemia, cerebral edema, and hepatic encephalopathy.
Synthetic function Produces coagulation factors, anticoagulant proteins, albumin, and transport proteins. Rising INR, altered hemostasis, low oncotic pressure, and edema.
Drug metabolism Metabolizes medications through hepatic enzymes and conjugation pathways. Medication accumulation, prolonged sedation, and toxicity.
Glucose regulation Stores glycogen, performs gluconeogenesis, and maintains fasting glucose. Hypoglycemia, especially in advanced acute liver failure.
Lipid metabolism Processes fatty acids, cholesterol, and lipoproteins. Steatosis, altered lipid handling, and metabolic dysfunction.
Bile formation Excretes bilirubin, cholesterol, drugs, and toxins. Jaundice, cholestasis, pruritus, and impaired fat absorption.
Clinical pearl: Albumin has a relatively long half-life and may remain normal early in acute liver failure. INR, glucose, lactate, and neurologic status are more useful markers of rapidly evolving hepatic dysfunction.

Acute Liver Failure

The key transition from biochemical injury to organ failure is the loss of hepatic synthetic and detoxification capacity. This is reflected by worsening coagulopathy, hypoglycemia, hyperammonemia, encephalopathy, and impaired lactate clearance.

The absolute aminotransferase value does not reliably determine severity. AST and ALT may fall because the patient is improving, but they may also fall because there are fewer viable hepatocytes remaining. Trends must therefore be interpreted alongside INR, bilirubin, lactate, glucose, renal function, hemodynamics, and mental status.

Transaminitis vs Acute Liver Injury vs Acute Liver Failure

Condition AST/ALT INR Encephalopathy Clinical Meaning
Transaminitis Elevated Usually normal Absent Biochemical marker of hepatocyte injury, not a syndrome of liver failure.
Acute liver injury Often markedly elevated May be ≥1.5 Absent Acute hepatic dysfunction without hepatic encephalopathy.
Acute liver failure Variable ≥1.5 Present Acute hepatic failure in a patient without pre-existing cirrhosis.

Transaminitis

  • Refers to elevation of AST and ALT.
  • Indicates hepatocellular injury but does not measure functional reserve.
  • Can occur with infection, ischemia, medications, toxins, muscle injury, metabolic disease, and biliary obstruction.
  • Does not necessarily require ICU admission unless accompanied by organ dysfunction or an unstable underlying cause.

Acute Liver Injury

  • Represents acute hepatocellular injury with evidence of impaired hepatic function.
  • Coagulopathy may be present, but hepatic encephalopathy is absent.
  • May progress rapidly to acute liver failure.
  • Requires close neurologic, laboratory, and hemodynamic monitoring.

Acute Liver Failure

Acute liver failure is generally defined by:

  • Acute hepatic injury.
  • INR of at least 1.5.
  • Any degree of hepatic encephalopathy.
  • No established pre-existing cirrhosis.
Acute liver failure is an ICU emergency. Early transfer or consultation with a liver-transplant center should occur before irreversible multiorgan failure develops.

Causes of Acute Liver Failure

Etiology strongly influences prognosis, treatment, transplant urgency, and likelihood of spontaneous recovery. A broad differential should be evaluated rapidly and simultaneously.

Clinical pearl: Obtain an acetaminophen level in unexplained acute liver injury even when overdose is denied. Delayed presentation, repeated supratherapeutic dosing, and occult combination-product use are common.

Initial Evaluation of Acute Liver Failure

Immediate History

  • Acetaminophen use, including combination cold, pain, and sleep medications.
  • Prescription medications, antibiotics, antiseizure drugs, supplements, and herbal products.
  • Alcohol and recreational drug exposure.
  • Recent travel, sick contacts, sexual exposures, and food or water exposures.
  • Pregnancy status.
  • History of autoimmune disease.
  • Personal or family history of liver disease.
  • Episodes of hypotension, cardiac arrest, sepsis, or hypoxemia.
  • Possible mushroom or occupational toxin exposure.

Laboratory Assessment

  • AST, ALT, alkaline phosphatase, total and direct bilirubin.
  • PT/INR and aPTT.
  • Basic metabolic panel, magnesium, phosphorus, and ionized calcium.
  • Glucose with frequent bedside monitoring.
  • Arterial or venous blood gas and lactate.
  • Complete blood count.
  • Acetaminophen level and toxicology testing as appropriate.
  • Ammonia, interpreted in clinical context.
  • Hepatitis A, B, and E testing as appropriate.
  • HSV, CMV, EBV, or adenovirus testing in selected patients.
  • ANA, anti-smooth muscle antibody, immunoglobulin G, and other autoimmune testing.
  • Ceruloplasmin, hemolysis studies, and evaluation for Wilson disease in younger patients.
  • Pregnancy testing when relevant.
  • Blood, urine, respiratory, and other cultures when infection is possible.

Imaging

  • Right upper quadrant ultrasound with Doppler to assess hepatic and portal flow.
  • Evaluation for biliary obstruction, vascular thrombosis, hepatic congestion, or Budd–Chiari syndrome.
  • Head CT when intracranial pathology must be excluded, although a normal scan does not exclude intracranial hypertension.
  • Additional CT or MRI imaging based on suspected etiology and hemodynamic stability.

Initial Management of Acute Liver Failure

1. Contact a Transplant Center Early

Do not wait for severe encephalopathy, refractory shock, or advanced kidney failure. Patients may deteriorate rapidly, and transfer becomes more difficult after intubation, escalating vasopressor requirements, or severe cerebral edema.

2. Administer N-Acetylcysteine

  • Give promptly for suspected or confirmed acetaminophen toxicity.
  • Do not delay treatment while awaiting confirmatory testing when suspicion is meaningful.
  • N-acetylcysteine may also be considered in selected non-acetaminophen acute liver failure, particularly early in the course and in consultation with hepatology or toxicology.
  • Continue therapy according to the clinical course, laboratory trends, and specialist guidance rather than stopping solely because a standard infusion duration has elapsed.

3. Monitor for Rapid Deterioration

  • Frequent neurologic examinations.
  • Serial INR, glucose, lactate, creatinine, electrolytes, and liver tests.
  • Continuous hemodynamic and oxygen saturation monitoring.
  • Close urine-output measurement.
  • Early recognition of infection, cerebral edema, kidney failure, hypoglycemia, and worsening acidosis.

4. Prevent Secondary Brain Injury

  • Avoid hypoxemia.
  • Avoid hypotension.
  • Avoid fever.
  • Avoid hyponatremia.
  • Avoid severe hypercapnia.
  • Minimize unnecessary stimulation in patients with suspected intracranial hypertension.

5. Manage Hypoglycemia

Hepatic glycogen depletion and impaired gluconeogenesis can produce recurrent hypoglycemia. Frequent glucose checks and continuous dextrose infusion may be required.

Hepatic Encephalopathy and Cerebral Edema in ALF

Hepatic encephalopathy in acute liver failure is fundamentally different from the typical encephalopathy of chronic cirrhosis. Patients with ALF are at substantial risk of cerebral edema, intracranial hypertension, seizures, herniation, and death.

Grading Encephalopathy

Grade Typical Findings
I Altered sleep, impaired concentration, anxiety, subtle behavior change.
II Disorientation, lethargy, asterixis, inappropriate behavior.
III Marked confusion, somnolence, response to stimuli, inability to protect airway reliably.
IV Coma with variable response to painful stimuli.

Airway Management

  • Intubate for advanced encephalopathy, loss of airway reflexes, severe agitation that prevents safe care, or respiratory failure.
  • Prepare carefully because induction-related hypotension may worsen cerebral perfusion.
  • Use short-acting sedatives when possible to preserve neurologic reassessment.
  • Avoid prolonged accumulation of benzodiazepines or other sedatives unless specifically indicated.

Cerebral-Edema Prevention

  • Elevate the head of the bed approximately 30 degrees.
  • Keep the neck in a neutral position and avoid venous outflow obstruction.
  • Avoid unnecessary endotracheal suctioning and stimulation.
  • Treat fever aggressively.
  • Maintain adequate oxygenation and perfusion.
  • Avoid hypotonic fluids and hyponatremia.
  • Consider a higher-normal serum sodium target under specialist guidance in patients at high risk for intracranial hypertension.

Treatment of Suspected Intracranial Hypertension

  • Hypertonic saline may be used to increase serum sodium and reduce cerebral edema.
  • Mannitol may be used when renal function and hemodynamics permit.
  • Brief controlled hyperventilation may be used only as a temporary rescue measure for impending herniation.
  • Continuous renal replacement therapy may help control ammonia, volume, sodium, and acid-base status in selected patients.
  • Intracranial-pressure monitoring is reserved for selected patients at experienced centers because it carries substantial bleeding and procedural risks.
Important: Lactulose is routinely used for hepatic encephalopathy in cirrhosis, but it does not replace cerebral-edema management in acute liver failure. Excessive enteral administration may also cause aspiration, abdominal distention, dehydration, or electrolyte abnormalities.

MASLD, MASH, and Metabolic Syndrome

Metabolic dysfunction-associated steatotic liver disease, previously called nonalcoholic fatty liver disease, is characterized by hepatic steatosis in association with metabolic risk factors and without another dominant cause of steatosis.

Terminology has evolved:

  • MASLD: Metabolic dysfunction-associated steatotic liver disease.
  • MASH: Metabolic dysfunction-associated steatohepatitis, previously called NASH.

Core Pathophysiology

Insulin resistance increases adipose-tissue lipolysis and delivery of free fatty acids to the liver. Hepatic lipid accumulation contributes to oxidative stress, mitochondrial dysfunction, inflammation, cellular injury, and fibrosis.

MASLD is commonly viewed as the hepatic manifestation of systemic metabolic dysfunction.

Traditional Metabolic Syndrome Criteria

Metabolic syndrome is generally identified when at least three of the following five abnormalities are present:

Criterion Traditional Threshold
Waist circumference At least 102 cm in men or 88 cm in women, with ethnicity-specific thresholds often preferred.
Triglycerides At least 150 mg/dL or treatment for hypertriglyceridemia.
HDL cholesterol Below 40 mg/dL in men or below 50 mg/dL in women, or treatment for low HDL.
Blood pressure At least 130/85 mmHg or treatment for hypertension.
Fasting glucose At least 100 mg/dL or treatment for hyperglycemia.

ICU Relevance

  • Patients may present with previously unrecognized advanced fibrosis or cirrhosis.
  • Obesity and diabetes increase risks related to infection, ventilation, thromboembolism, and cardiovascular disease.
  • Portal hypertension can be present even when aminotransferase elevations are mild.
  • Normal or mildly abnormal AST and ALT do not exclude advanced cirrhosis.
  • Acute infection, surgery, GI bleeding, medication exposure, or hemodynamic stress may precipitate decompensation.

Decompensated Cirrhosis and Acute-on-Chronic Liver Failure

Decompensated Cirrhosis

Decompensation occurs when a patient with cirrhosis develops a major complication such as:

  • Ascites.
  • Variceal hemorrhage.
  • Hepatic encephalopathy.
  • Spontaneous bacterial peritonitis.
  • Hepatorenal syndrome.
  • Jaundice or worsening synthetic dysfunction.

Acute-on-Chronic Liver Failure

Acute-on-chronic liver failure is a syndrome in which an acute insult in a patient with chronic liver disease produces hepatic decompensation together with one or more organ failures and high short-term mortality.

Common Precipitants

  • Bacterial infection.
  • GI bleeding.
  • Alcohol-associated hepatitis.
  • Medication or toxin exposure.
  • Acute viral hepatitis.
  • Portal or hepatic vein thrombosis.
  • Surgery or procedural stress.
  • Volume depletion or overdiuresis.
  • Constipation, sedatives, opioids, or electrolyte abnormalities.
  • No clearly identifiable trigger in some patients.

Why Infection Is Easily Missed

Patients with cirrhosis may not develop prominent fever or leukocytosis. New encephalopathy, worsening kidney function, hypotension, ileus, hypothermia, or increased bilirubin may be the only clues.

Do not wait for abdominal pain: Hospitalized patients with cirrhosis, ascites, and clinical deterioration should generally undergo prompt diagnostic paracentesis unless there is a compelling contraindication.

Hepatic Encephalopathy in Cirrhosis

Hepatic encephalopathy in cirrhosis is a diagnosis of exclusion. Hyperammonemia contributes to its pathophysiology, but the serum ammonia concentration alone does not establish severity, guide treatment intensity, or exclude alternative neurologic disease.

Common Precipitants

  • Infection, including SBP.
  • GI bleeding.
  • Constipation.
  • Dehydration or overdiuresis.
  • Hypokalemia or metabolic alkalosis.
  • Hyponatremia.
  • Renal failure.
  • Opioids, benzodiazepines, gabapentinoids, and other sedating medications.
  • Urinary retention.
  • Portal-systemic shunting.
  • Nonadherence to lactulose or rifaximin.
  • Intracranial bleeding, seizure, stroke, meningitis, hypoglycemia, or hypercapnia.

Lactulose

  • Titrate to approximately two to three soft bowel movements per day.
  • Initial frequent dosing may be used in significant overt encephalopathy.
  • Avoid excessive diarrhea, dehydration, hypernatremia, hypokalemia, and worsening kidney injury.
  • Rectal administration may be considered when enteral therapy is unsafe or impossible.

Rifaximin

  • Frequently added for recurrent or persistent overt hepatic encephalopathy.
  • Usually used with lactulose rather than as a substitute for correcting precipitating factors.
Clinical pearl: Worsening mental status should never be treated with lactulose alone. Simultaneously search for infection, bleeding, hypoglycemia, hypercapnia, medications, renal failure, electrolyte abnormalities, and structural neurologic disease.

Acute Kidney Injury in Cirrhosis

Kidney dysfunction in cirrhosis may be caused by simple volume depletion, infection-related hypoperfusion, acute tubular injury, nephrotoxins, obstruction, abdominal compartment physiology, glomerular disease, or hepatorenal syndrome.

Common AKI Definition

AKI is generally recognized by either:

  • An increase in serum creatinine of at least 0.3 mg/dL within 48 hours.
  • An increase to at least 1.5 times baseline within the preceding seven days.
  • Reduced urine output, when reliable measurements are available.

Major Categories

Category Examples
Pre-renal or volume responsive Overdiuresis, diarrhea, poor intake, bleeding, large-volume paracentesis without appropriate albumin, systemic vasodilation.
Acute tubular injury Sepsis, prolonged hypotension, ischemia, nephrotoxins, pigment injury.
Post-renal Urinary retention, obstruction, pelvic or retroperitoneal disease.
Hepatorenal syndrome Functional kidney failure caused by advanced circulatory dysfunction in cirrhosis.
Other intrinsic kidney disease Glomerulonephritis, interstitial nephritis, bile-cast nephropathy, thrombotic microangiopathy.

Initial Evaluation

  • Review baseline creatinine and urine output.
  • Assess volume status and recent fluid losses.
  • Review diuretics, NSAIDs, ACE inhibitors, ARBs, aminoglycosides, contrast exposure, and other nephrotoxins.
  • Obtain urinalysis and urine microscopy.
  • Evaluate for infection and shock.
  • Assess for GI bleeding.
  • Perform bladder scan or renal ultrasound when obstruction is possible.
  • Consider intra-abdominal hypertension in tense ascites or oliguria.
  • Perform diagnostic paracentesis when ascites and decompensation are present.

Primary problem Structural tubular injury Functional renal hypoperfusion from severe circulatory dysfunction
Typical setting Shock, sepsis, nephrotoxins, prolonged ischemia Advanced cirrhosis with ascites and marked splanchnic vasodilation

Urine sodium, fractional excretion of sodium, and fractional excretion of urea may be difficult to interpret in cirrhosis, especially after diuretic exposure. No single urine index reliably distinguishes HRS from acute tubular injury.

Hepatorenal Syndrome–AKI

HRS-AKI is a functional form of kidney failure caused by advanced circulatory dysfunction in cirrhosis. Severe splanchnic vasodilation reduces effective arterial blood volume, triggering activation of the sympathetic nervous system, renin–angiotensin–aldosterone system, and vasopressin. The resulting renal vasoconstriction reduces renal blood flow and glomerular filtration.

Features Supporting HRS-AKI

  • Cirrhosis with ascites.
  • Acute kidney injury.
  • No sustained improvement after appropriate volume assessment and withdrawal of contributing medications.
  • No shock as the primary explanation.
  • No current nephrotoxic exposure sufficient to explain the kidney injury.
  • No strong evidence of structural kidney disease.

Features Suggesting Another Diagnosis

  • Persistent septic or hemorrhagic shock.
  • Marked hematuria or proteinuria.
  • Active urinary sediment.
  • Abnormal renal imaging.
  • Recent severe hypotension or prolonged vasopressor-dependent shock.
  • Clear nephrotoxin exposure.
  • Rapid improvement with simple volume replacement.
Clinical pearl: HRS is a diagnosis made after evaluating and treating reversible causes of AKI. It should not be used as a default label for every creatinine rise in cirrhosis.

Mechanism-Based Treatment of HRS-AKI

Step 1: Remove Precipitants

  • Treat infection promptly.
  • Control GI bleeding.
  • Stop NSAIDs and other nephrotoxins.
  • Hold or reduce diuretics during significant AKI.
  • Review ACE inhibitors, ARBs, beta blockers, and other medications that may worsen hypotension in the individual patient.
  • Correct excessive gastrointestinal losses.
  • Evaluate for abdominal compartment physiology or obstruction.

Step 2: Albumin

Albumin expands effective circulating volume and may help identify volume-responsive kidney injury. A commonly used initial regimen is:

  • Albumin approximately 1 g/kg per day, up to 100 g per day, for up to two days during initial evaluation.
  • Subsequent dosing should be individualized according to hemodynamics, urine output, respiratory status, serum albumin, and the vasoconstrictor strategy.
Albumin is not harmless. Monitor for pulmonary edema, increasing oxygen requirement, elevated filling pressures, tense ascites, and other evidence of volume overload.

Step 3: Vasoconstrictor Therapy

Therapy Typical Role Important Considerations
Terlipressin Preferred vasoconstrictor in many suitable patients. Monitor oxygenation, ischemic complications, volume status, and respiratory deterioration.
Norepinephrine Effective ICU alternative. Requires close hemodynamic monitoring and usually central venous access.
Midodrine plus octreotide Alternative when preferred therapies are unavailable or unsuitable. Generally less effective than terlipressin or norepinephrine.

Why Vasoconstrictors Improve Kidney Function

The primary circulatory problem in HRS is not generalized excessive vasoconstriction. It is marked splanchnic vasodilation with pooling of blood in the abdominal circulation and a reduction in effective arterial volume.

Splanchnic vasoconstriction reduces this pooling, improves effective arterial filling, decreases maladaptive neurohormonal activation, and may improve renal perfusion and GFR.

Terlipressin Mechanism

  • Acts predominantly through vasopressin V1 receptors.
  • Constrains the splanchnic circulation.
  • Reduces portal venous pooling.
  • Improves effective arterial volume.
  • May reduce intense compensatory renal vasoconstriction.
Although terlipressin is a vasoconstrictor, it may improve kidney function by correcting the upstream splanchnic vasodilation that drives renal hypoperfusion.

Renal Replacement Therapy

Dialysis or continuous renal replacement therapy may be used for conventional indications, including:

  • Refractory hyperkalemia.
  • Severe metabolic acidosis.
  • Refractory volume overload.
  • Uremic complications.
  • Selected cases of severe hyperammonemia.
  • Bridge to liver transplantation or recovery when clinically appropriate.

Renal replacement therapy does not reverse the underlying circulatory disorder and should be considered within the broader context of transplant candidacy, prognosis, and goals of care.

Spontaneous Bacterial Peritonitis

SBP is infection of ascitic fluid without a surgically treatable intra-abdominal source. Presentation may be subtle and may consist only of encephalopathy, kidney injury, hypotension, ileus, or worsening liver function.

Diagnosis

An ascitic-fluid polymorphonuclear leukocyte count of at least 250 cells/mm³ supports the diagnosis of SBP and warrants treatment.

Who Should Receive Diagnostic Paracentesis?

  • Patients admitted with cirrhosis and ascites.
  • New or worsening encephalopathy.
  • Acute kidney injury.
  • Fever, hypothermia, leukocytosis, or leukopenia.
  • Hypotension or shock.
  • Abdominal pain, tenderness, vomiting, or ileus.
  • GI bleeding.
  • Unexplained clinical deterioration.

Ascitic Studies

  • Cell count with differential.
  • Albumin and total protein.
  • Culture inoculated directly into blood-culture bottles at the bedside.
  • Glucose, LDH, Gram stain, or additional studies when secondary peritonitis is suspected.

Treatment

  • Start antibiotics promptly after obtaining cultures when possible.
  • Select therapy based on community versus healthcare-associated infection, prior resistant organisms, recent antibiotics, local susceptibility patterns, and illness severity.
  • Use broader empiric therapy in septic shock or when multidrug-resistant infection is likely.
  • Narrow therapy when microbiologic data become available.

Albumin in SBP

Albumin is commonly administered to patients at increased risk of renal failure. A traditional regimen is:

  • 1.5 g/kg on day 1.
  • 1 g/kg on day 3.

The decision should account for renal function, bilirubin, hemodynamics, respiratory status, and risk of volume overload.

When to Repeat Paracentesis

  • Clinical response is inadequate.
  • The diagnosis remains uncertain.
  • Resistant organisms are possible.
  • Secondary bacterial peritonitis is suspected.

Features Suggesting Secondary Peritonitis

  • Polymicrobial culture.
  • Very high ascitic PMN count.
  • Low ascitic glucose.
  • High ascitic LDH.
  • High ascitic protein.
  • Persistent pain, fever, shock, or failure to improve with appropriate antibiotics.

Acute Variceal Hemorrhage

Variceal bleeding is a time-sensitive emergency requiring coordinated resuscitation, pharmacologic therapy, antibiotics, endoscopy, and early assessment for rescue or preemptive portal-decompression strategies.

Immediate Management

  • Establish adequate IV access.
  • Type and crossmatch blood.
  • Assess airway protection, especially in active hematemesis or encephalopathy.
  • Begin vasoactive therapy as soon as variceal bleeding is suspected.
  • Administer antibiotic prophylaxis early.
  • Arrange urgent endoscopy after initial stabilization.

Transfusion Strategy

A restrictive red-blood-cell transfusion strategy is appropriate for many patients, often targeting a hemoglobin near 7 g/dL, while individualizing for:

  • Active exsanguination.
  • Hemodynamic instability.
  • Myocardial ischemia.
  • Severe hypoxemia.
  • Other specific comorbidities.

Overtransfusion can increase portal pressure and may worsen bleeding.

Vasoactive Therapy

  • Octreotide is commonly used in the United States.
  • Terlipressin or somatostatin may be used depending on local availability and clinical context.
  • Continue vasoactive therapy after endoscopic control according to bleeding risk and specialist guidance.

Antibiotic Prophylaxis

Patients with cirrhosis and upper GI bleeding are at high risk for bacterial infection, recurrent bleeding, and mortality. Antibiotic prophylaxis should be started early, with the agent selected according to severity, local resistance, and prior colonization.

Endoscopic Therapy

  • Endoscopic variceal ligation is preferred for esophageal varices.
  • Gastric varices may require different endoscopic, radiologic, or obliteration techniques.
  • Coordination with hepatology, interventional radiology, and transplant services may be necessary.

Early or Preemptive TIPS

Selected patients at high risk of treatment failure may benefit from early transjugular intrahepatic portosystemic shunt placement. Selection requires consideration of liver severity, active bleeding, encephalopathy risk, cardiac function, pulmonary hypertension, infection, and overall transplant strategy.

Bridge Therapy for Uncontrolled Bleeding

  • Balloon tamponade may provide temporary control but has major aspiration, ischemia, perforation, and airway risks.
  • A covered esophageal stent may be used in selected cases.
  • These are bridges to definitive endoscopic or radiologic therapy, not definitive management.

Coagulation and Procedures in Cirrhosis

Cirrhosis produces a rebalanced but fragile hemostatic state. Both procoagulant and anticoagulant factors are reduced. Standard laboratory tests measure only part of this balance.

Why INR Is Misleading in Cirrhosis

  • INR reflects selected procoagulant pathways.
  • It does not measure reduced anticoagulant proteins.
  • It does not account for elevated von Willebrand factor or endothelial changes.
  • It does not reliably predict procedural bleeding.
  • It should not be interpreted as equivalent to warfarin-associated anticoagulation.
Avoid routine plasma transfusion solely to normalize an elevated INR in a nonbleeding patient with cirrhosis. Plasma may add substantial volume, increase portal pressure, and fail to meaningfully improve hemostasis.

Before a Procedure, Consider

  • Whether the procedure is low or high bleeding risk.
  • Whether bleeding is active.
  • Platelet count.
  • Fibrinogen concentration.
  • Renal failure and uremic platelet dysfunction.
  • Sepsis and disseminated intravascular coagulation.
  • Medication-related anticoagulation or antiplatelet therapy.
  • Viscoelastic testing when available and clinically appropriate.

Thrombosis Still Occurs

An elevated INR does not protect patients from venous thromboembolism, portal-vein thrombosis, catheter thrombosis, or pulmonary embolism. Pharmacologic VTE prophylaxis should be assessed based on the total bleeding and thrombotic risk rather than INR alone.

General ICU Support

Hemodynamics

  • Evaluate fluid responsiveness rather than administering repeated unselected boluses.
  • Balanced crystalloids are often preferred when crystalloid is required.
  • Use albumin for established indications and selected resuscitation scenarios.
  • Initiate vasopressors promptly when hypotension persists despite appropriate volume management.
  • Norepinephrine is commonly used as the initial vasopressor in distributive shock.

Mechanical Ventilation

  • Use lung-protective ventilation when acute respiratory distress syndrome is present.
  • Avoid severe hypercapnia in acute liver failure with concern for intracranial hypertension.
  • Use sedation strategies that permit neurologic assessment when possible.
  • Consider aspiration risk in encephalopathy or active GI bleeding.

Renal Protection

  • Avoid NSAIDs and unnecessary nephrotoxins.
  • Adjust medication doses for changing renal and hepatic function.
  • Avoid excessive chloride and unselected fluid loading.
  • Monitor urine output and creatinine trends closely.
  • Evaluate early for HRS, acute tubular injury, infection, and obstruction.

Nutrition

  • Avoid prolonged fasting.
  • Provide adequate calories and protein unless a specific temporary contraindication exists.
  • Routine severe protein restriction is generally inappropriate and may worsen sarcopenia.
  • Use enteral nutrition when feasible.
  • Consider smaller, frequent meals and a late-evening snack after stabilization in chronic liver disease.

Medication Considerations

  • Reduce or avoid unnecessary benzodiazepines, opioids, and other sedatives.
  • Review hepatically metabolized medications for accumulation.
  • Avoid NSAIDs in decompensated cirrhosis.
  • Review beta blockers individually during shock, severe AKI, or profound hypotension.
  • Do not automatically stop every chronic medication without assessing risks and benefits.

Infection Management

  • Obtain cultures promptly when infection is suspected.
  • Start empiric antibiotics early in sepsis or septic shock.
  • Account for healthcare exposure, prior resistant organisms, and recent antibiotic use.
  • Reassess daily and narrow or stop therapy when appropriate.

Prognosis and Transplantation

  • Contact transplant and hepatology teams early.
  • Use severity scores as aids, not substitutes for clinical judgment.
  • Discuss candidacy, reversible barriers, and expected trajectory.
  • Clarify goals of care when transplantation is not an option or multiorgan failure progresses despite treatment.

ICU Pearls

  • Transaminitis is a marker of injury, not liver failure.
  • Acute liver failure requires coagulopathy and hepatic encephalopathy without established cirrhosis.
  • Always obtain an acetaminophen level in unexplained acute liver injury.
  • Falling AST and ALT do not always mean recovery. Interpret them with INR, lactate, glucose, bilirubin, renal function, and neurologic status.
  • Hepatic encephalopathy in ALF raises concern for cerebral edema.
  • Hepatic encephalopathy in cirrhosis usually represents decompensation and requires a search for triggers.
  • ACLF is decompensation accompanied by organ failure and high short-term mortality.
  • Albumin can be diagnostic and therapeutic in selected patients with cirrhosis and AKI.
  • HRS is functional circulatory kidney failure; ATN is structural tubular injury.
  • HRS treatment requires more than albumin alone. Vasoconstrictor therapy is usually needed.
  • INR is useful for acute hepatic synthetic dysfunction but does not predict procedural bleeding reliably in cirrhosis.
  • Do not miss SBP because fever or abdominal pain is absent.
  • Early transplant-center involvement is a treatment, not an administrative step.

Common Pitfalls

  • Calling severe transaminitis “liver failure” despite normal synthetic function and no encephalopathy.
  • Failing to recognize ALF because AST and ALT are already falling.
  • Waiting for the acetaminophen level before administering N-acetylcysteine in a high-risk presentation.
  • Delaying transplant-center referral until multiorgan failure is advanced.
  • Using lactulose as the only response to worsening mental status.
  • Missing infection because fever and leukocytosis are absent.
  • Delaying diagnostic paracentesis in a hospitalized patient with ascites.
  • Assuming every AKI in cirrhosis is HRS.
  • Diagnosing HRS without evaluating shock, nephrotoxins, obstruction, infection, and structural kidney disease.
  • Continuing repeated albumin doses despite worsening pulmonary edema.
  • Using midodrine and octreotide in a critically ill patient when more effective ICU vasopressor therapy is available.
  • Correcting INR routinely with plasma in a nonbleeding patient.
  • Overtransfusing during variceal hemorrhage.
  • Administering large unselected crystalloid boluses in advanced cirrhosis.
  • Severely restricting protein and worsening malnutrition or sarcopenia.
  • Assuming an elevated INR protects against venous thromboembolism.

Bottom Line

The liver failure spectrum progresses from hepatocyte injury to loss of synthetic and detoxification function. Transaminitis alone is not liver failure. Acute liver injury includes impaired hepatic function without encephalopathy, while acute liver failure requires coagulopathy and hepatic encephalopathy in a patient without established cirrhosis.

In cirrhosis, hepatic encephalopathy signals decompensation and may be part of acute-on-chronic liver failure when accompanied by renal, circulatory, respiratory, neurologic, or coagulation failure. Acute kidney injury requires a structured evaluation because volume depletion, sepsis, acute tubular injury, obstruction, and HRS frequently overlap.

HRS-AKI is a functional hemodynamic disorder caused by profound splanchnic vasodilation and reduced effective arterial volume. Treatment is mechanism-based: remove precipitants, use albumin appropriately, and initiate vasoconstrictor therapy in suitable patients. Throughout the course, prioritize early infection recognition, cautious resuscitation, brain and kidney protection, and early transplant-center involvement.

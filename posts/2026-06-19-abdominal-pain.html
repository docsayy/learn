Abdominal Pain: Practical Inpatient Approach to GERD, Gastritis, Gas, and Dyspepsia

Added June 19, 2026 • Hospital Medicine / Gastroenterology • ~8 min read

A practical approach to treating low-risk inpatient abdominal discomfort when symptoms sound most consistent with GERD, gastritis, esophagitis, gas, bloating, or dyspepsia. This guide focuses on common bedside options such as Maalox, Tums, sucralfate, simethicone, famotidine, pantoprazole, and the GI cocktail.

Overview

Abdominal pain is common in hospitalized patients. This article focuses on patients without concerning features whose symptoms sound most consistent with gastritis, esophagitis, GERD, dyspepsia, bloating, or gas-related discomfort.

Key idea: These options are for low-risk abdominal discomfort. If the history, exam, vitals, or labs are concerning, evaluate for dangerous causes first.

Rule Out Dangerous Causes

Before ordering symptomatic treatment, make sure there are no red flags suggesting a more serious diagnosis.

Concerning Features

  • Peritonitis or rebound tenderness
  • Severe or worsening abdominal pain
  • Hypotension or shock
  • Fever with concerning abdominal exam
  • GI bleeding
  • Persistent vomiting
  • New severe pain after procedure or surgery
  • Marked leukocytosis or lactic acidosis
  • Concern for obstruction, perforation, ischemia, pancreatitis, cholecystitis, appendicitis, or ACS
Do not treat and ignore: If the patient looks sick or has focal concerning findings, escalate evaluation rather than only giving antacids.

Aluminum and Magnesium Hydroxide: Maalox

Maalox is a liquid antacid that can be useful for GERD-like symptoms, dyspepsia, gastritis-type discomfort, or esophageal irritation.

Medication Typical Dose Use
Aluminum and magnesium hydroxide 15 mL every 6 hours PRN or with meals GERD, dyspepsia, gastritis-type symptoms

Side Effects

  • Aluminum can cause constipation
  • Magnesium can cause diarrhea
  • Together they may balance each other out, but GI side effects can still occur
Memory trick: Aluminum = “minimum” stool = constipation. Magnesium = “must go” = diarrhea.

Calcium Carbonate: Tums

Calcium carbonate is a simple PRN option for GERD-like symptoms.

Medication Typical Dose Notes
Calcium carbonate 1–4 tablets of 500 mg PRN Good for GERD-type symptoms
Caution: Avoid or use carefully in patients with hypercalcemia. Excess calcium carbonate can contribute to milk-alkali syndrome.

Sucralfate: Carafate

Sucralfate helps form a protective lining over irritated gastric or duodenal mucosa.

When It May Help

  • Peptic ulcer disease symptoms
  • Gastritis-type discomfort
  • Mucosal irritation
  • Patients who need a non-acid-suppressing option
Practical point: Sucralfate can interfere with absorption of other medications, so timing matters.

Simethicone: Gas-X

Simethicone is useful for patients reporting gas, bloating, or crampy discomfort related to gas.

Medication Use Mechanism
Simethicone Bloating and gas discomfort Changes surface tension of gas bubbles, helping them combine and pass more easily
Practical point: Simethicone is low-risk and often appreciated by patients with bloating or gassiness.

Famotidine: Pepcid

Famotidine is an H2 blocker. It reduces acid production but is generally less potent than a proton pump inhibitor.

When to Consider

  • Mild GERD symptoms
  • Dyspepsia
  • When you want something stronger than antacids but do not want to start a PPI
Limitation: H2 blockers can develop tachyphylaxis, meaning the effect may wear off over a few weeks.

Pantoprazole and PPIs

Pantoprazole is a proton pump inhibitor. PPIs are more potent acid suppressants than H2 blockers and are commonly used for GERD, gastritis, esophagitis, peptic ulcer disease, and selected stress ulcer prophylaxis situations.

Stress Ulcer Prophylaxis: Common ICU Indications

  • Mechanical ventilation for more than 48 hours
  • Coagulopathy such as INR >1.5, platelets <50, or PTT >2 times upper limit of normal in ICU patients

Relative Indications

  • History of gastric ulcer or GI bleeding in the past 12 months
  • Trauma such as traumatic brain injury, spinal cord injury, or burns
  • Two or more risk factors such as ICU stay longer than 1 week, occult GI bleeding, or high-dose steroids

Long-Term Risks

  • Malabsorption concerns
  • Increased infection risk
  • Increased risk of C. difficile infection
  • Increased pneumonia risk in ventilated ICU patients
Avoid unnecessary PPIs: PPIs are useful when indicated, but long-term continuation without a clear reason is common and can cause harm.

GI Cocktail

The classic GI cocktail is commonly used in emergency departments and some inpatient settings for dyspepsia or esophageal irritation.

Component Typical Dose
Maalox 30 mL
Viscous lidocaine 2% 10 mL
Donnatal 5 mL

Evidence suggests that the GI cocktail may not be more effective than plain liquid antacid alone. The viscous lidocaine and anticholinergic components may add side effects without consistently improving outcomes.

Practical approach: Start with plain liquid antacid for typical dyspepsia or GERD symptoms. Consider viscous lidocaine selectively if the patient has prominent esophageal or mucosal irritation.

Medication Review

When a hospitalized patient develops abdominal pain, review medications that may be contributing.

Common Culprits

  • NSAIDs causing gastritis or ulcer irritation
  • Stimulant laxatives such as senna or bisacodyl causing cramping
  • Osmotic laxatives such as lactulose causing bloating
  • Iron causing constipation or dyspepsia
  • Opioids causing constipation or ileus
  • Antibiotics causing nausea, diarrhea, or C. difficile infection
Do not forget medication causes: Abdominal pain may improve simply by stopping NSAIDs or backing off an overly aggressive bowel regimen.

Clinical Pearls

  1. First rule out dangerous causes of abdominal pain.
  2. Maalox is a useful first-line PRN option for GERD-like or dyspeptic symptoms.
  3. Aluminum can constipate; magnesium can cause diarrhea.
  4. Tums can help GERD symptoms but should be avoided in hypercalcemia.
  5. Sucralfate may help ulcer-type symptoms by coating the mucosa.
  6. Simethicone is useful and low-risk for gas and bloating.
  7. Famotidine is a reasonable middle-ground option between antacids and PPIs.
  8. PPIs are useful when indicated but should not be continued indefinitely without a reason.
  9. The GI cocktail is not clearly better than plain liquid antacid.
  10. Review medications, especially NSAIDs and bowel regimen agents, when abdominal pain develops.

Bottom Line

For low-risk abdominal discomfort that sounds like GERD, gastritis, esophagitis, dyspepsia, gas, or bloating, start with simple targeted therapy: Maalox or Tums for reflux-type symptoms, simethicone for gas, sucralfate for ulcer-type irritation, famotidine for mild acid suppression, and pantoprazole when a PPI is truly indicated. Always screen for red flags first and review medications that may be causing symptoms.

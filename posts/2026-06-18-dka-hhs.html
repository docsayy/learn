DKA and HHS: A Practical Guide for Internal Medicine Residents

Added June 18, 2026 • Endocrinology • ~13 min read

A practical resident-level review of diabetic ketoacidosis and hyperosmolar hyperglycemic state, including how to distinguish them, how to evaluate triggers, and how to treat with fluids, insulin, potassium, bicarbonate, and transition planning.

Overview

Whenever a patient presents with extremely elevated glucose, think about diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA) and hyperosmolar hyperglycemic state (HHS).

Both conditions are caused by a relative deficiency of insulin and are associated with osmotic diuresis, profound dehydration, and metabolic stress.

Classic symptoms of DKA and HHS include polyuria, polydipsia, and profound volume depletion.

DKA vs HHS

Feature DKA HHS
Typical glucose Often 350–500; usually < 800 Often > 1000
Diabetes type More common in type 1 diabetes More common in type 2 diabetes
Age group Often younger Often older
Onset Rapid, over hours Insidious, over days
Ketones Present Absent or minimal
Beta-hydroxybutyrate Positive Usually negative or minimal
Anion gap Elevated Usually normal or mildly elevated
pH Low Usually normal
Dominant symptoms Abdominal pain, nausea, vomiting, shortness of breath Neurologic symptoms, stupor, coma

Euglycemic DKA

DKA can occur with glucose less than 250 mg/dL, especially in patients taking SGLT2 inhibitors.

The biggest risk factor for euglycemic DKA is SGLT2 inhibitor use.

Clinical Presentation

Shared Features

  • Polyuria
  • Polydipsia
  • Dehydration
  • Tachycardia
  • Weakness
  • Possible altered mental status

DKA-Predominant Features

  • Abdominal pain
  • Nausea and vomiting
  • Kussmaul respirations or shortness of breath
  • Rapid onset over hours
  • Anion gap metabolic acidosis

HHS-Predominant Features

  • Very high glucose, often above 1000 mg/dL
  • Profound dehydration
  • Altered mental status
  • Stupor or coma
  • Insidious onset over several days
Up to 25–50% of patients with HHS may be obtunded or comatose.

Key Laboratory Differences

DKA Labs

  • Elevated glucose, though may be normal or mildly elevated in euglycemic DKA
  • Positive urine ketones
  • Positive serum beta-hydroxybutyrate
  • Elevated anion gap
  • Low bicarbonate
  • Low pH

HHS Labs

  • Markedly elevated glucose
  • Very high serum osmolality
  • Minimal or absent ketones
  • No major ketoacidosis
  • Usually normal pH
  • Anion gap usually normal or only mildly elevated

Initial Workup

The initial workup should confirm the diagnosis, assess severity, identify complications, and search for a trigger.

Test Reason
Basic metabolic panel Glucose, sodium, potassium, bicarbonate, creatinine, anion gap
VBG or ABG Assess pH and degree of acidosis
Serum beta-hydroxybutyrate Assess ketosis
Urinalysis Ketones and possible infection
Serum osmolality Especially important in HHS
EKG Ischemia and potassium effects
Troponin Evaluate myocardial infarction as trigger
Lipase Evaluate pancreatitis as trigger
Chest X-ray Evaluate infection
Blood cultures If infection or sepsis is suspected

Precipitating Triggers

DKA and HHS often have a precipitating event. Identifying the trigger is part of treatment.

Common Triggers

  • Infection
  • Missed insulin or inadequate insulin
  • Myocardial infarction
  • Stroke
  • Pancreatitis
  • Major acute illness
  • Compromised water intake, especially in elderly patients
  • SGLT2 inhibitors in euglycemic DKA
Acute illness increases counter-regulatory hormones such as cortisol and epinephrine, worsening relative insulin deficiency.

Treatment Step 1: Fluids

Fluids are the first and most important treatment because patients are profoundly volume depleted from osmotic diuresis.

Fluid Choice

Corrected Sodium Fluid Choice
Low sodium Normal saline
Normal or high sodium Half-normal saline may be considered

Once glucose falls below approximately 250 mg/dL, dextrose-containing fluids are added so insulin can continue safely while the anion gap closes.

Do not stop insulin just because glucose improves. Dextrose is added so insulin can continue until ketoacidosis resolves.

Treatment Step 2: Insulin

Insulin corrects the underlying pathophysiology by suppressing ketogenesis and lowering serum glucose.

Insulin Drip

  • Commonly used for severe DKA
  • Usually requires ICU or stepdown depending on institutional policy
  • Allows rapid titration
  • Requires frequent glucose and electrolyte monitoring

Subcutaneous DKA Protocols

Some institutions use subcutaneous insulin protocols for mild to moderate DKA.

Mild to moderate DKA may be eligible for subcutaneous DKA protocols depending on institutional policy and patient stability.

Treatment Step 3: Potassium

Patients with DKA and HHS are often total-body potassium depleted due to osmotic diuresis.

Insulin shifts potassium into cells and can dangerously lower serum potassium.

Potassium Action
K < 3.3 Hold insulin and replete potassium first
K 3.3–5.2 Give potassium with fluids per protocol
K > 5.2 Hold potassium but monitor closely
If potassium is less than 3.3, do not start insulin until potassium is corrected.

Treatment Step 4: Bicarbonate

Bicarbonate is not routinely indicated in DKA.

Consider bicarbonate only for severe acidemia, typically pH less than 6.9.

If pH is above 6.9, bicarbonate is generally not recommended.

DKA Severity

Severity Typical Features Usual Treatment Location
Mild Less severe acidosis, alert mental status Floor or stepdown depending on protocol
Moderate More significant acidosis but not severe Stepdown or ICU depending on protocol
Severe pH < 7.0, stupor, coma, severe metabolic derangement ICU-level care
Severe DKA generally requires ICU-level care and insulin drip therapy.

Monitoring During Treatment

  • Point-of-care glucose every 1 hour while on insulin drip
  • Basic metabolic panel every 4 hours or per protocol
  • Monitor anion gap
  • Monitor potassium closely
  • Monitor volume status
  • Assess for trigger and clinical improvement

When Is DKA Resolved?

A practical approach is to continue therapy until the anion gap closes on two consecutive checks.

Do not transition off insulin drip after only one closed anion gap. Confirm closure on repeat BMP.

Transition Off Insulin Drip

Once the anion gap has closed twice and the patient can tolerate subcutaneous insulin, transition to basal-bolus insulin.

Critical Rule

Overlap the insulin drip with the first long-acting subcutaneous insulin dose by approximately 2 hours.

This prevents a gap in insulin coverage and reduces the risk of recurrent DKA.

Basal-Bolus Regimen

  • Basal insulin: long-acting insulin such as glargine
  • Bolus insulin: rapid-acting insulin with meals
  • Correctional insulin: sliding scale as needed

Insulin Dose Calculation Example

One practical way to calculate the subcutaneous insulin regimen is based on the last 6 hours of insulin drip use.

Step-by-Step Method

  1. Add the insulin used over the last 6 hours.
  2. Multiply by 4 to estimate a 24-hour total daily dose.
  3. Multiply by 80% to be conservative.
  4. Give 50% as basal insulin.
  5. Divide the remaining 50% as prandial insulin across meals.

Example

Hour Insulin Used
3 PM–4 PM 2 units
4 PM–5 PM 1.5 units
5 PM–6 PM 1.5 units
6 PM–7 PM 1 unit
7 PM–8 PM 1 unit
8 PM–9 PM 1 unit
Last 6 hours = 8 units
Estimated 24-hour use = 8 × 4 = 32 units
Conservative total daily dose = 32 × 0.8 ≈ 26 units
Component Dose
Basal insulin Glargine 13 units daily
Prandial insulin Aspart 4 units with meals
Correctional insulin Add sliding scale as needed
This is a practical starting estimate. Adjust based on oral intake, renal function, hypoglycemia risk, home insulin regimen, and institutional protocol.

High-Yield Pearls

Corrected Sodium

Severe hyperglycemia can cause hypertonic hyponatremia. Sodium may appear low because glucose pulls water into the extracellular space.

Always interpret sodium in DKA/HHS after correcting for hyperglycemia.

Leukocytosis

DKA can cause stress leukocytosis. Mild to moderate leukocytosis may correlate with ketonemia and stress, but infection still must be considered clinically.

Can Patients Eat While on an Insulin Drip?

Sometimes patients may want to eat while still on an insulin drip.

If a patient eats while on an insulin drip, continue the drip if still needed and add subcutaneous rapid-acting insulin to cover meals.

Transition Safety

  • Confirm anion gap closure twice.
  • Give basal insulin before stopping drip.
  • Overlap drip and basal insulin by about 2 hours.
  • Add meal-time insulin if eating.
  • Continue close glucose checks after transition.

Take-Home Pearls

  • DKA and HHS are both due to relative insulin deficiency.
  • DKA usually has ketosis, anion gap metabolic acidosis, and lower glucose than HHS.
  • HHS usually has very high glucose, severe dehydration, and neurologic symptoms.
  • SGLT2 inhibitors are a major risk factor for euglycemic DKA.
  • Always search for triggers such as infection, missed insulin, MI, stroke, and pancreatitis.
  • Fluids are the first and most important treatment.
  • If potassium is less than 3.3, hold insulin and replete potassium first.
  • Add dextrose-containing fluids once glucose drops below about 250 so insulin can continue.
  • Bicarbonate is generally reserved for pH less than 6.9.
  • Continue insulin until the anion gap closes on two consecutive checks.
  • Overlap insulin drip with long-acting subcutaneous insulin by about 2 hours.
  • Calculate transition insulin dose from recent drip requirements and adjust clinically.

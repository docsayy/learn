Immediate Post-Intubation Priorities

Waveform Capnography → Secure ETT → Analgesia + Sedation → Ventilator Setup → Hemodynamic Reassessment
  • Confirm tracheal placement with continuous waveform capnography; do not rely on auscultation alone.
  • Start analgesia and an amnestic sedative immediately after intubation, especially after rocuronium.
  • Confirm ETT depth clinically and with chest imaging when indicated; the calculator gives only a starting estimate.
  • Use predicted body weight—not actual body weight—to calculate tidal volume.
  • Reassess blood pressure within minutes; post-intubation hypotension is common and clinically important.
  • Document a RASS goal and use the lightest effective ongoing sedation unless a specific indication requires deep sedation.

ETT and Ventilator Explanation

Predicted body weight

Tidal volume is based on height and sex because lung size correlates more closely with height than actual body mass.

Male PBW = 50 + 0.91 × (height cm − 152.4)
Female PBW = 45.5 + 0.91 × (height cm − 152.4)

Tidal volume

  • ARDS: begin near 6 mL/kg PBW and reduce toward 4 mL/kg PBW when needed to limit plateau pressure.
  • Most critically ill adults without ARDS: a lung-protective starting range of approximately 6–8 mL/kg PBW is commonly used.
  • Obstructive physiology: use a lower respiratory rate and longer expiratory time; monitor auto-PEEP rather than increasing rate reflexively.
  • Severe metabolic acidosis: avoid an abrupt fall in minute ventilation; compare with the patient’s pre-intubation respiratory compensation and obtain early blood gas reassessment.

ETT size and depth

  • Common adult starting sizes are approximately 7.0–7.5 mm for many women and 7.5–8.0 mm for many men, modified by airway anatomy and procedural needs.
  • The traditional starting-depth reminder is approximately 21 cm at the teeth for an average woman and 23 cm for an average man.
  • Confirm depth using waveform capnography, bilateral examination, tube marking, and chest radiography when appropriate. Head movement can change tip position.

Key Safety Pitfalls

  • Never equate paralysis with sedation. A paralyzed patient may be awake and in pain.
  • Do not use dexmedetomidine alone for immediate deep sedation while a long-acting paralytic remains active.
  • Do not use full-dose propofol induction in a patient at high risk of peri-intubation hypotension.
  • Do not use succinylcholine when important hyperkalemia or malignant-hyperthermia risk exists.
  • Do not calculate tidal volume from actual weight in obesity.
  • Do not accept the 21/23 rule without confirming tube position.
  • In severe asthma/COPD, avoid excessive respiratory rate and monitor for dynamic hyperinflation.
  • In severe metabolic acidosis, inadequate post-intubation minute ventilation can precipitate rapid decompensation.

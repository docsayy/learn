Travel Medicine: The Complete Advanced Resident Guide

Added July 14, 2026 • Infectious Disease • ~58 min read

Travel medicine combines infectious-disease prevention, vaccination, antimicrobial pharmacology, environmental medicine, chronic-disease planning, emergency preparation, and post-travel diagnosis. Recommendations must be individualized according to the exact itinerary, season, duration, accommodations, activities, traveler health, pregnancy and immune status, local outbreaks, access to medical care, and country entry requirements. This guide covers the complete pretravel consultation, travel vaccines, malaria prevention, travelers’ diarrhea, vector and animal exposures, altitude illness, air travel, special populations, travel health kits, and the urgent evaluation of fever after return.

Clinical Overview

Travel risk is determined by much more than the country name. Two travelers visiting the same country may have completely different risks depending on whether they remain in a major city, stay with relatives in a rural community, work in a refugee camp, climb at high altitude, handle animals, receive medical care, or travel during an outbreak.

Traveler Factors + Exact Itinerary + Activities + Season + Duration + Local Epidemiology

Major Travel-Related Risks

  • Motor-vehicle and motorcycle injury
  • Foodborne and waterborne infection
  • Malaria and other vector-borne diseases
  • Respiratory infections
  • Animal bites and rabies
  • Sexually transmitted infections
  • Altitude, heat, cold, and water hazards
  • Venous thromboembolism during prolonged travel
  • Interruption of chronic medications or medical care
  • Exposure to resistant organisms through healthcare abroad

Prevention Categories

  1. Routine vaccination
  2. Destination-specific vaccination
  3. Malaria chemoprophylaxis
  4. Food, water, vector, and environmental precautions
  5. Medication and emergency planning
  6. Post-travel recognition of illness
In many destinations, injuries and road traffic incidents are a greater overall threat to travelers than rare tropical infections.

The Pretravel Consultation

Core Components

  • Review exact itinerary and transit points.
  • Assess traveler health and immune status.
  • Review all current medications and allergies.
  • Update routine immunizations.
  • Administer indicated travel vaccines.
  • Select malaria prophylaxis when needed.
  • Discuss food, water, insects, animals, and environmental hazards.
  • Prescribe self-treatment medications when appropriate.
  • Develop an emergency and medical-evacuation plan.

Information to Obtain

  • Every country and subnational destination
  • Dates and duration
  • Urban, rural, jungle, coastal, or high-altitude exposure
  • Hotel, cruise, hostel, camping, or local-home accommodations
  • Reason for travel
  • Planned activities
  • Medical-care availability
  • Previous international travel experience

Traveler Medical Factors

  • Age
  • Pregnancy or breastfeeding
  • Immune suppression
  • Chronic cardiac or pulmonary disease
  • Renal or hepatic dysfunction
  • History of seizures or psychiatric illness
  • Medication interactions
  • Previous vaccine reactions
  • G6PD status when relevant

Timing of the Pretravel Consultation

Preferred Timing

Ideally 4–6 Weeks Before Departure

Early consultation allows time to:

  • Complete multidose vaccine series
  • Assess vaccine response when necessary
  • Start mefloquine early enough to assess tolerance
  • Arrange yellow fever vaccination
  • Coordinate specialist review for complex disease
  • Obtain travel insurance and medication supplies

Do Not Refuse Last-Minute Care

Even on the day before travel, clinicians can:

  • Give indicated single-dose vaccines.
  • Start malaria prophylaxis with an appropriate rapid-start agent.
  • Provide travelers’ diarrhea treatment.
  • Review rabies and injury precautions.
  • Identify travel that should be postponed.
Incomplete protection is usually better than no protection, but the traveler must understand when immunity will begin and which precautions remain essential.

Individual Risk Assessment

Highest-Risk Traveler Groups

  • Travelers visiting friends and relatives
  • Long-term travelers and expatriates
  • Humanitarian and healthcare workers
  • Adventure travelers
  • Pregnant travelers
  • Infants and young children
  • Older adults
  • Immunocompromised travelers
  • Travelers with unstable chronic disease

Risk Is Not Uniform Within a Country

Malaria, yellow fever, Japanese encephalitis, altitude illness, and many other risks vary by:

  • Province or district
  • Elevation
  • Urban versus rural setting
  • Rainy versus dry season
  • Time of year
  • Current outbreak activity

Prioritize When Cost Is Limited

  1. Routine vaccines, especially measles
  2. Malaria prevention when risk is meaningful
  3. Required vaccines
  4. Hepatitis A
  5. High-consequence exposure-specific vaccines
  6. Travel health kit and evacuation planning

Detailed Itinerary Assessment

Ask About Activities

  • Freshwater swimming or rafting
  • Camping and hiking
  • Caving
  • Animal contact
  • Medical work
  • Sexual exposures
  • High-altitude trekking
  • Motorcycle or scooter use
  • Religious mass gatherings
  • Remote travel without medical access

Transit Can Matter

Yellow fever documentation requirements can depend on:

  • Countries visited before the final destination
  • Airport layovers
  • Duration of transit
  • Whether the traveler leaves the airport
  • Sequence of cruise-ship ports

Check Current Sources

  • CDC destination pages
  • CDC Travel Health Notices
  • WHO travel guidance
  • Embassy or consular requirements
  • Airline and cruise requirements

Travelers Visiting Friends and Relatives

A traveler visiting friends and relatives may underestimate risk because the destination is familiar or because they previously lived there.

Why Risk Is Higher

  • Longer duration of stay
  • Rural travel
  • Local-home accommodations
  • Consumption of untreated food and water
  • Reduced use of mosquito precautions
  • Limited pretravel consultation
  • Belief that previous residence provides immunity

Important Risks

  • Malaria
  • Typhoid
  • Hepatitis A
  • Tuberculosis
  • Rabies
  • Measles
  • Travelers’ diarrhea
Partial immunity to malaria declines after leaving an endemic area. A former resident returning years later may have the same or greater risk of severe malaria as another nonimmune traveler.

Last-Minute Travelers

Useful Rapid Interventions

  • Hepatitis A vaccine
  • Injectable typhoid vaccine
  • Yellow fever vaccine when indicated and enough time remains for certificate validity
  • Meningococcal vaccine
  • Polio booster
  • Rabies vaccine days 0 and 7 when departure timing permits
  • Atovaquone-proguanil or doxycycline for malaria

Accelerated Vaccine Schedules

Some vaccines have accelerated schedules, but clinicians should:

  • Follow product-specific age rules.
  • Document the need for later doses.
  • Explain when protection is incomplete.
  • Avoid improvising unvalidated schedules.

Consider Postponing Travel When:

  • A live vaccine is required but contraindicated.
  • A severe chronic illness is unstable.
  • Pregnancy creates an unacceptable infectious risk.
  • Essential medical care will be unavailable.
  • The traveler cannot safely obtain malaria prevention.

Travel Documents, Medications, and Insurance

Carry:

  • Medication list using generic names
  • Allergy list
  • Relevant medical summary
  • Vaccine record
  • International Certificate of Vaccination or Prophylaxis when required
  • Insurance and evacuation information
  • Emergency contact information

Medication Transport

  • Keep medications in original labeled containers.
  • Carry them in hand luggage.
  • Bring extra supply for delays.
  • Confirm whether controlled medications are legal at the destination.
  • Carry a clinician letter for injectable or controlled medication.

Insurance

Standard U.S. insurance may not cover:

  • International hospitalization
  • Medical evacuation
  • Repatriation
  • Preexisting conditions abroad
Medical-evacuation insurance can be more important than carrying an extensive supply of medications when travel is remote.

Travel Vaccination Strategy

Three Categories

Category Examples
Routine vaccines MMR, influenza, COVID-19, Tdap, varicella, pneumococcal, shingles
Recommended travel vaccines Hepatitis A, typhoid, rabies, Japanese encephalitis, chikungunya, TBE
Required vaccines Yellow fever or meningococcal vaccine for selected itineraries and entry rules

Recommendation vs Requirement

A vaccine may be:

  • Medically recommended but not required for entry
  • Required for entry but not medically recommended for the traveler’s actual exposure
  • Both recommended and required

Review Before Administration

  • Age indication
  • Pregnancy
  • Immune status
  • Previous vaccine doses
  • Drug interactions
  • Time before departure
  • Current destination recommendation

Routine Vaccines

Measles, Mumps, and Rubella

All international travelers should have adequate measles immunity before departure.

Measles can be acquired:

  • At the destination
  • In airports
  • During flights
  • At mass gatherings

Other Routine Vaccines

  • Influenza
  • COVID-19
  • Tetanus, diphtheria, and pertussis
  • Varicella
  • Pneumococcal vaccination
  • Recombinant zoster vaccine
  • Routine hepatitis B vaccination

Influenza Season Differs by Hemisphere

Influenza may circulate:

  • During the opposite season in the Southern Hemisphere
  • Year-round in tropical regions
  • During cruise travel and mass gatherings

Live-Vaccine Principles

Common Live Travel-Relevant Vaccines

  • Yellow fever
  • Oral typhoid Ty21a
  • Oral cholera vaccine
  • MMR
  • Varicella

General Rule

Injectable live vaccines should be administered:

  • On the same day
  • Or separated by at least 28 days

Yellow Fever and MMR

When feasible, an interval of approximately 30 days may be considered between yellow fever and MMR because simultaneous administration may reduce immune responses to selected antigens.

Contraindications

Live vaccines may be contraindicated with:

  • Severe cellular immune suppression
  • Pregnancy
  • Recent transplant
  • Selected biologic immunosuppressive therapies
Do not assume all immune suppression is equivalent. Determine the exact medication, dose, duration, immune defect, and vaccine involved.

Hepatitis A Vaccine

Who Should Receive It?

  • Unvaccinated travelers going to areas with hepatitis A risk
  • Travelers visiting friends and relatives
  • Travelers with chronic liver disease
  • Persons with HIV
  • Persons who use drugs
  • Persons experiencing homelessness

Last-Minute Travel

Give the first vaccine dose even when departure is imminent.

Vaccine Plus Immune Globulin

For selected travelers leaving within two weeks who are at increased risk for severe disease or poor vaccine response, consider:

  • Hepatitis A vaccine
  • Plus immune globulin at a separate site

Complete the Series

The second dose provides durable long-term protection and should be administered according to the product schedule.

Hepatitis B Vaccine

Travel-Related Exposures

  • Sexual contact
  • Medical or dental care
  • Tattooing or piercing
  • Injection-drug use
  • Healthcare work
  • Long-term residence

Vaccination

  • Use routine universal adult recommendations.
  • Accelerated combination schedules may be used when appropriate.
  • Complete all remaining doses after return if the series is unfinished.

Post-Vaccination Serology

Routine titer testing is not required for most healthy travelers but is indicated for selected groups, including:

  • Healthcare personnel
  • Hemodialysis patients
  • Immunocompromised persons
  • Sex partners of HBsAg-positive persons

Typhoid Vaccine

Who Is at Risk?

  • Travelers to South Asia
  • Travelers visiting friends and relatives
  • Travelers staying in smaller cities or rural areas
  • Travelers with prolonged food and water exposure

Injectable Vi Vaccine

  • One IM dose
  • Age at least 2 years
  • Give at least two weeks before exposure when possible
  • Booster every two years if risk continues

Oral Ty21a Vaccine

  • Four capsules on alternating days
  • Age at least 6 years
  • Complete at least one week before exposure
  • Booster every five years if risk continues

Important Limitations

  • Neither vaccine is fully protective.
  • Food and water precautions remain necessary.
  • Vaccines do not reliably protect against paratyphoid fever.
  • Oral Ty21a is a live vaccine.

Interactions

Systemic antibiotics can reduce oral Ty21a vaccine effectiveness. Coordinate timing with:

  • Doxycycline malaria prophylaxis
  • Other antibiotics
  • Atovaquone-proguanil

Yellow Fever Vaccine

Indications

  • Travel to yellow fever risk areas in Africa or South America
  • Country entry requirement based on itinerary

Standard Administration

  • One 0.5 mL subcutaneous dose
  • Generally for travelers aged at least 9 months
  • Administer only at an authorized yellow fever vaccination center

Certificate Validity

International Certificate Becomes Valid 10 Days After Primary Vaccination

Under international regulations, a properly completed certificate is generally valid for life.

Booster Dose

A booster is not needed for most travelers but may be considered or recommended for selected people, including:

  • Travel to a higher-risk setting at least 10 years after vaccination
  • Persons vaccinated during pregnancy
  • Persons living with HIV
  • Laboratory workers handling yellow fever virus
  • Persons vaccinated before hematopoietic stem-cell transplantation

Contraindications

  • Age younger than 6 months
  • Severe immune suppression
  • Symptomatic thymus disorder associated with immune dysfunction
  • Severe allergy to a vaccine component

Precautions

  • Age 6–8 months
  • Age 60 years or older
  • Pregnancy
  • Breastfeeding
  • Selected moderate immune abnormalities

Risk-Benefit Decision

Yellow fever vaccine can rarely cause severe neurotropic or viscerotropic disease. Vaccinate only when exposure risk or entry requirements justify the vaccine risk.

Medical Waiver

When vaccination is medically contraindicated but documentation is required:

  • Issue an official waiver through an authorized center.
  • Explain that the destination may decline to accept it.
  • Emphasize meticulous mosquito precautions.

Meningococcal Vaccine

Meningitis Belt

MenACWY vaccine is recommended for travelers visiting or residing in the meningitis belt of sub-Saharan Africa during the dry-season risk period, generally December through June.

Hajj and Umrah

Saudi Arabia requires documentation of quadrivalent meningococcal vaccination for pilgrims. Requirements can change annually.

Verify the current Saudi visa and vaccination requirements immediately before travel rather than relying on a previous year’s rules.

Timing

  • Administer at least 7–10 days before travel when feasible.
  • For Hajj and Umrah, the certificate must satisfy the current minimum and maximum timing requirements.

Boosters

Travelers with ongoing risk require boosters according to age at initial vaccination and the current MenACWY schedule.

Polio Vaccination

Routine Series

  • Ensure complete age-appropriate IPV vaccination.

Adult Booster

A fully vaccinated adult traveling to a country with increased poliovirus exposure risk may receive one lifetime IPV booster.

Long-Term Travelers

Some countries affected by poliovirus may require documented vaccination within a specified period before departure from that country.

Documentation

  • Use the International Certificate of Vaccination or Prophylaxis when required.
  • Check current country-specific outbreak and exit requirements.

Japanese Encephalitis Vaccine

Risk Setting

  • Rural agricultural areas of Asia and the western Pacific
  • Rice-growing and pig-farming regions
  • Evening and nighttime mosquito exposure

Recommend Vaccine For:

  • Travel of approximately one month or longer in an endemic area during transmission season
  • Long-term residence
  • Frequent travel to endemic regions
  • Laboratory exposure

Consider Vaccine For Shorter Travel With:

  • Extensive rural outdoor exposure
  • Camping or hiking
  • Uncertain itinerary
  • Accommodations without screens or air conditioning

Usually Not Recommended For:

  • Short urban-only travel
  • Travel outside transmission season without rural exposure
  • Very low-risk itineraries

Schedule

  • Two-dose primary series
  • Complete the final dose at least one week before travel
  • Adults aged 18–65 may use an accelerated interval when needed
  • Booster after at least one year when ongoing exposure risk continues

Rabies Pre-Exposure Vaccination

Who Should Be Considered?

  • Travelers with likely animal contact
  • Veterinarians and animal workers
  • Cavers with bat exposure
  • Children in high-risk destinations
  • Long-term travelers
  • Travelers to remote areas with limited PEP access

Primary Series

Rabies Vaccine on Days 0 and 7

Travelers With Sustained Risk Beyond Three Years

Either:

  • Obtain a rabies antibody titer 1–3 years after the primary series.
  • Or receive a third vaccine dose between day 21 and year 3.

Important Limitation

Pre-exposure vaccination does not eliminate the need for urgent post-exposure treatment. It simplifies PEP and removes the need for rabies immune globulin.

Cholera Vaccine

Routine Travelers

Cholera vaccine is not routinely recommended for most U.S. travelers.

Indication

  • Travelers aged 2–64 visiting an area of active cholera transmission

Vaccine

  • Live oral CVD 103-HgR vaccine
  • Single dose
  • Administer at least 10 days before potential exposure

Important Points

  • No country requires cholera vaccination for entry.
  • Food and water precautions remain essential.
  • Routine antibiotic prophylaxis is not recommended.
  • Avoid administration within 14 days after systemic antibiotics.

Age and Immune Limitations

The vaccine is not licensed for:

  • Children younger than 2 years
  • Adults aged 65 years or older

Data are limited for pregnancy, breastfeeding, and immune suppression.

Chikungunya Vaccine

Current U.S. Travel Vaccine

  • VIMKUNYA
  • Single intramuscular dose
  • Approved for persons aged 12 years or older

Recommended For:

  • Travel to a country or territory experiencing a chikungunya outbreak

May Be Considered For:

  • Travel or residence for approximately six months or longer in a location with elevated chikungunya risk

Risk Discussion

Consider:

  • Destination outbreak activity
  • Trip duration
  • Mosquito exposure
  • Age and comorbidities
  • Traveler preferences

Booster

  • No booster recommendation currently exists.

Tick-Borne Encephalitis Vaccine

Risk Areas

  • Parts of Europe
  • Russia
  • Central and eastern Asia

Exposure Settings

  • Forested areas
  • Grasslands
  • Hiking and camping
  • Spring through autumn tick activity
  • Unpasteurized dairy exposure in selected regions

Recommended For:

  • Travelers who will have extensive tick exposure in an endemic area

May Be Considered For:

  • Travelers with possible tick exposure based on itinerary, age, trip duration, and risk tolerance

Vaccine

  • TICOVAC
  • Licensed from age 1 year
  • Three-dose primary series
  • Schedule varies by age
Outdoor activity pattern is often more important than total trip duration when determining TBE risk.

Other Travel-Relevant Vaccines

Mpox

JYNNEOS vaccination may be indicated according to current outbreak and behavioral risk recommendations.

Influenza and COVID-19

  • Update before cruises, conferences, mass gatherings, and international travel.
  • Consider destination seasonality.

Dengue Vaccine

The U.S.-licensed dengue vaccine is not a general travel vaccine and is not recommended for adult travelers simply because they are visiting a dengue-endemic country.

Special Occupational Vaccines

Selected laboratory, military, or occupational travelers may require vaccines not routinely offered to tourists.

Malaria Prevention

Core Strategy

Destination-Specific Chemoprophylaxis + Mosquito Avoidance + Rapid Evaluation of Fever

Why Malaria Matters

  • Plasmodium falciparum can progress rapidly to severe disease and death.
  • Chemoprophylaxis is highly effective but not 100% protective.
  • Travelers visiting friends and relatives are disproportionately affected.
  • Symptoms may begin after the traveler returns.

Species

  • P. falciparum
  • P. vivax
  • P. ovale
  • P. malariae
  • P. knowlesi

No Prophylaxis Regimen Replaces Bite Prevention

Mosquito avoidance also protects against:

  • Dengue
  • Chikungunya
  • Zika
  • Yellow fever
  • Japanese encephalitis

Assessing Malaria Risk

Determine:

  • Exact country and region
  • Elevation
  • Urban versus rural travel
  • Season
  • Duration
  • Nighttime exposure
  • Local resistance patterns

Some Itineraries Need No Medication

A country may have malaria transmission only in selected:

  • Rural districts
  • Border regions
  • Low-elevation areas
  • Seasonal zones

Check the Current Destination Table

Do not select malaria prophylaxis based solely on the continent or country name. Recommendations may differ within the same country.

Mosquito-Bite Prevention

Use:

  • EPA-registered insect repellent
  • Long sleeves and long pants
  • Permethrin-treated clothing and gear
  • Screened or air-conditioned accommodations
  • Bed nets when sleeping areas are exposed

Time of Biting

  • Anopheles mosquitoes transmitting malaria generally bite from evening through morning.
  • Aedes mosquitoes transmitting dengue, chikungunya, yellow fever, and Zika often bite during the day.
Travelers in tropical regions need mosquito protection throughout the day and night.

  • Destination resistance
  • Trip duration
  • Pregnancy
  • Renal function
  • Psychiatric and seizure history
  • Drug interactions
  • Daily versus weekly preference
  • Cost
  • Adherence after return

Atovaquone-Proguanil

Adult Prophylaxis

One Adult Tablet Daily

Schedule

  • Begin 1–2 days before entering the malaria area.
  • Take daily during exposure.
  • Continue for 7 days after leaving.
  • Take with food or a milky drink.

Advantages

  • Well tolerated
  • Short post-travel course
  • Useful for last-minute travelers
  • Daily schedule

Avoid or Use Caution With:

  • Creatinine clearance below 30 mL/min
  • Pregnancy
  • Breastfeeding an infant weighing below 5 kg
  • Child weighing below 5 kg
  • Warfarin interaction

Common Adverse Effects

  • Abdominal discomfort
  • Nausea
  • Vomiting
  • Headache

Doxycycline for Malaria Prevention

Adult Dose

Doxycycline 100 mg Orally Daily

Schedule

  • Begin 1–2 days before entering the malaria area.
  • Take daily during exposure.
  • Continue for four weeks after leaving.

Advantages

  • Useful for last-minute travel
  • Effective in most malaria areas
  • Additional activity against selected rickettsial and leptospiral infections
  • Low cost

Adverse Effects

  • Photosensitivity
  • Esophagitis
  • Nausea
  • Vaginal candidiasis

Administration

  • Take with a full glass of water.
  • Remain upright for at least 30 minutes.
  • Do not take immediately before bed.
  • Use sun protection.

Contraindications

  • Pregnancy
  • Age younger than 8 years
  • Severe tetracycline allergy

Mefloquine

Adult Dose

  • 250 mg salt orally once weekly

Schedule

  • Begin at least two weeks before exposure.
  • Take on the same day each week.
  • Continue for four weeks after leaving.

Advantages

  • Weekly dosing
  • Can be used during pregnancy in chloroquine-resistant areas
  • Can be used in children with weight-based dosing

Contraindications

  • Active or recent depression
  • Generalized anxiety disorder
  • Psychosis or major psychiatric disorder
  • Seizure disorder
  • Known hypersensitivity to related compounds

Avoid With:

  • Cardiac conduction abnormalities
  • Travel to known mefloquine-resistant areas

Possible Adverse Effects

  • Abnormal dreams
  • Insomnia
  • Anxiety
  • Dizziness
  • Mood changes
  • Rare persistent neuropsychiatric effects
Do not prescribe mefloquine without a careful psychiatric, seizure, and cardiac history.

Chloroquine

Use

  • Only for destinations where malaria remains chloroquine sensitive

Schedule

  • Begin 1–2 weeks before travel.
  • Take once weekly.
  • Continue for four weeks after leaving.

Advantages

  • Weekly dosing
  • Can be used during pregnancy
  • Can be used in children

Adverse Effects

  • Pruritus
  • GI discomfort
  • Headache
  • Blurred vision
  • Possible psoriasis exacerbation
Chloroquine resistance is widespread. Confirm that the exact destination remains chloroquine sensitive before prescribing it.

Primaquine and Tafenoquine

G6PD Testing

Document a Normal Quantitative G6PD Level Before Either Drug

Primaquine

  • Particularly effective where P. vivax predominates
  • Begin 1–2 days before exposure
  • Take daily during travel
  • Continue seven days after leaving
  • Take with food

Primaquine Terminal Prophylaxis

Presumptive antirelapse therapy may be considered after prolonged exposure to P. vivax or P. ovale regions.

Tafenoquine

  • Adults only
  • Daily loading for three days
  • Weekly dosing during exposure
  • One final weekly dose after leaving

Do Not Use With:

  • G6PD deficiency
  • Unknown G6PD status
  • Pregnancy
  • Breastfeeding when maternal or infant G6PD status is abnormal or unknown

Additional Tafenoquine Limitation

  • Avoid in persons with a history of psychotic disorder.
Qualitative G6PD screening is insufficient when prescribing primaquine or tafenoquine. Use a quantitative test.

Malaria Prevention During Pregnancy

Preferred Advice

Pregnant travelers should avoid or postpone travel to malaria-endemic areas whenever possible because malaria increases maternal and fetal morbidity and mortality.

Chloroquine-Sensitive Area

  • Chloroquine is recommended.

Chloroquine-Resistant Area

  • Mefloquine is the recommended U.S. chemoprophylaxis option.

Avoid During Pregnancy

  • Doxycycline
  • Primaquine
  • Tafenoquine
  • Atovaquone-proguanil as routine prophylaxis because safety data are insufficient

Mosquito Avoidance

Repellents such as DEET and picaridin can be used during pregnancy according to label directions.

Malaria Prevention in Children

Medication Options

  • Atovaquone-proguanil from 5 kg with weight-based dosing
  • Chloroquine in sensitive regions
  • Mefloquine with weight-based dosing
  • Doxycycline from age 8 years
  • Primaquine after quantitative G6PD testing

Practical Issues

  • Pediatric doses must not exceed the adult dose.
  • Tablets may require pharmacy compounding.
  • Store medication in child-resistant containers.
  • Vomiting after a dose may reduce protection.

Children and Mosquito Nets

  • Use bed nets over cribs and carriers.
  • Use age-appropriate repellent according to product labeling.
  • Do not apply repellent to a young child’s hands.

Breakthrough Malaria

Chemoprophylaxis Is Not Perfect

Malaria remains possible because of:

  • Missed doses
  • Vomiting or malabsorption
  • Incorrect drug selection
  • Drug resistance
  • Rare prophylaxis failure despite adherence

Traveler Counseling

Any Fever During Travel or Within 1 Year After Malaria Exposure Requires Urgent Malaria Testing

Continue Medication During Evaluation

A traveler who becomes febrile while still in a malaria-endemic region should:

  • Seek urgent medical care.
  • Continue chemoprophylaxis until a diagnosis is established.
  • Avoid relying solely on self-diagnosis.

Travelers’ Diarrhea

Common Pathogens

  • Enterotoxigenic and other diarrheagenic E. coli
  • Campylobacter
  • Shigella
  • Salmonella
  • Norovirus
  • Giardia
  • Cryptosporidium
  • Cyclospora

Typical Timing

  • Bacterial and viral illness often begins during travel or shortly afterward.
  • Protozoal infection more often causes delayed or persistent diarrhea.

Major Clinical Risk

  • Dehydration
  • Electrolyte abnormalities
  • Inability to continue travel
  • Postinfectious irritable bowel syndrome
  • Colonization with resistant organisms after antibiotic exposure

Food and Water Precautions

Lower-Risk Choices

  • Food cooked thoroughly and served hot
  • Fruit peeled by the traveler
  • Pasteurized dairy
  • Commercially bottled or appropriately treated water
  • Dry packaged foods

Higher-Risk Choices

  • Raw or undercooked meat and seafood
  • Unpasteurized dairy
  • Raw sprouts
  • Unpeeled produce
  • Food held at room temperature
  • Ice made from unsafe water
  • Street food with poor handling

Water Treatment

  • Boiling is highly reliable.
  • Appropriate filtration and chemical treatment may be used.
  • Carbon filters alone do not reliably remove pathogens.

Limitations

Even travelers who follow food and water precautions can develop diarrhea because contamination may occur during food preparation.

Prevention of Travelers’ Diarrhea

Hand Hygiene

  • Wash with soap and water before eating.
  • Use alcohol-based sanitizer when soap and water are unavailable.

Bismuth Subsalicylate

Bismuth can reduce the incidence of travelers’ diarrhea but is limited by:

  • Frequent dosing
  • Black tongue and stool
  • Salicylate toxicity
  • Drug interactions

Avoid Bismuth With:

  • Salicylate allergy
  • Anticoagulant use
  • Gout
  • Significant renal disease
  • Pregnancy
  • Children at risk for Reye syndrome

Antibiotic Prophylaxis

Routine antibiotic prophylaxis is not recommended for most travelers because risks include adverse effects, C. difficile infection, and acquisition of resistant organisms.

Rare exceptions may involve travelers in whom even brief diarrhea would create a severe medical consequence.

Dysentery

  • Visible blood in stool

Travelers’ Diarrhea Self-Treatment

Rehydration

  • Use oral rehydration solution for substantial fluid loss.
  • Commercial sports drinks may not contain an optimal sodium-glucose balance for severe diarrhea.
  • Children and older adults dehydrate more rapidly.

Options include:

High-Altitude Travel

Risk Begins With Rapid Ascent

Altitude illness commonly occurs after ascent above approximately:

2,500 m or 8,200 ft

Risk Factors

  • Previous altitude illness
  • Rapid ascent
  • High sleeping altitude
  • Strenuous exercise immediately after arrival
  • No acclimatization

Fitness Does Not Protect

Excellent physical fitness does not prevent altitude illness and may increase risk if it encourages rapid ascent or overexertion.

Prevention

  • Ascend gradually.
  • Avoid increasing sleeping altitude by more than approximately 500 m per day above 3,000 m when possible.
  • Add an acclimatization day for every approximately 1,000 m gained.
  • Avoid heavy exertion and alcohol during the first days.
  • Never ascend with symptoms.

Acute Mountain Sickness

Symptoms

  • Headache
  • Nausea
  • Dizziness
  • Fatigue
  • Sleep disturbance

Management

  • Stop ascent.
  • Rest at the current altitude.
  • Use nonopioid analgesics and antiemetics.
  • Use acetazolamide when appropriate.
  • Descend at least approximately 300 m if symptoms worsen.

Do Not Ascend With Mild Symptoms

A traveler with any symptoms of altitude illness should not sleep at a higher altitude until symptoms have resolved.

Clinical Features

  • Ataxia
  • Confusion

  • Drowsiness

Immediate Descent + Oxygen + Dexamethasone

Dexamethasone

  • 8 mg initially
  • Then 4 mg every six hours

When Descent Is Impossible

  • Supplemental oxygen

  • Viral hemorrhagic fever in the appropriate epidemiologic setting

Initial Evaluation

  • CBC with differential
  • Metabolic and liver profile
  • Coagulation testing when severe
  • Blood cultures
  • Urgent malaria testing
  • Targeted arboviral or rickettsial testing
  • HIV testing
  • Respiratory testing when indicated

Laboratory Clues

  • Thrombocytopenia: malaria, dengue, severe sepsis
  • Leukopenia: dengue, malaria, enteric fever, acute HIV
  • Eosinophilia: helminths, drug reaction, allergy
  • Transaminitis: dengue, viral hepatitis, malaria, leptospirosis

Malaria Is a Medical Emergency

Rule out malaria in every patient with fever after travel to a malaria-endemic area during the preceding year, regardless of prophylaxis use.

Testing

  • Thick and thin blood smears
  • Rapid diagnostic test when it provides a faster result
  • Species identification
  • Parasitemia quantification

Timing

Results Should Be Available Within Hours—not Sent to a Slow Reference Laboratory

Initial Negative Test

When suspicion remains and no alternative diagnosis exists:

  • Repeat conventional malaria testing two or three times during the first 24–48 hours.

Severe Malaria Features

  • Altered consciousness
  • Seizures
  • Shock
  • Severe anemia
  • Acute kidney injury
  • Acidosis
  • Pulmonary edema or ARDS
  • Hypoglycemia
  • High parasitemia

Empiric Treatment

Empiric antimalarial treatment is generally avoided because smears guide therapy, except when:

  • Severe malaria is strongly suspected.
  • Immediate testing is unavailable.
  • Waiting would create dangerous delay.

Common Clinical Scenarios

Case 1: Two-Week Hotel Trip to a Major City

A healthy traveler will remain in an urban hotel and has no rural activities.

Recommendations may be limited to routine vaccines, food and water precautions, selected destination vaccines, and no malaria prophylaxis if the city has no transmission.

Case 2: Visiting Family in Rural West Africa

The traveler believes childhood residence provides malaria immunity.

Treat the traveler as nonimmune. Provide destination-appropriate malaria prophylaxis, mosquito precautions, and indicated vaccines.

Case 3: Departure in Three Days

A traveler has not received vaccines and plans a high-risk itinerary.

Provide all useful immediate interventions. Last-minute consultation still permits vaccines, rapid-start malaria medication, diarrhea treatment, and critical counseling.

Case 4: Yellow Fever Vaccine in an Older Adult

A 72-year-old plans travel to an area with low potential for yellow fever exposure.

Balance destination risk against increased vaccine adverse-event risk. Vaccination is not automatically appropriate merely because part of the country has yellow fever transmission.

Case 5: Pregnancy and Malaria Travel

A pregnant patient plans travel to a chloroquine-resistant malaria area.

Recommend postponing travel when possible. If unavoidable, use mefloquine and meticulous bite prevention after individualized assessment.

Case 6: Traveler With Depression Requesting Mefloquine

A traveler prefers weekly dosing but has active major depression.

Mefloquine is contraindicated. Select another destination-appropriate medication.

Case 7: Traveler With G6PD Status Unknown

A traveler requests tafenoquine because of weekly dosing.

Do not prescribe tafenoquine until normal quantitative G6PD activity is documented.

Case 8: Bloody Travelers’ Diarrhea

A traveler develops fever and bloody stool in Southeast Asia.

Use azithromycin and oral hydration. Do not use loperamide alone.

Case 9: Monkey Bite

A traveler is bitten by a macaque at a tourist site.

Irrigate immediately and seek urgent evaluation for rabies PEP, bacterial infection, tetanus, and possible B-virus prophylaxis.

Case 10: Headache at 3,500 m

A traveler develops headache, nausea, and fatigue after rapid ascent.

Stop ascent and treat acute mountain sickness. Do not continue to a higher sleeping altitude.

Case 11: Ataxia at Altitude

A climber develops confusion and cannot walk in a straight line.

Treat as HACE with immediate descent, oxygen, and dexamethasone.

Case 12: Dyspnea at Rest at Altitude

A trekker develops cough, rales, and severe hypoxemia.

Treat as HAPE. Minimize exertion, provide oxygen, and descend urgently.

Case 13: Fever After Returning From Ghana

A patient presents with fever two weeks after return despite reporting full adherence to atovaquone-proguanil.

Obtain immediate malaria testing. Chemoprophylaxis does not exclude malaria.

Case 14: First Malaria Smear Is Negative

The patient remains febrile without another diagnosis.

Repeat conventional malaria testing two or three times over the first 24–48 hours.

Case 15: Persistent Diarrhea for Three Weeks

A traveler received ciprofloxacin twice without improvement.

Evaluate for Giardia, Cryptosporidium, Cyclospora, C. difficile, and noninfectious postinfectious disease rather than prescribing another blind antibacterial course.

Case 16: Fever and Bleeding After Outbreak Exposure

A healthcare worker returns from an outbreak region with fever and mucosal bleeding.

Isolate immediately and notify infection prevention and public health before routine phlebotomy or transport.

Common Pitfalls

  • Using the country name without reviewing the subnational itinerary
  • Ignoring transit and layover requirements
  • Failing to update routine vaccines
  • Assuming previous residence provides lasting malaria immunity
  • Refusing consultation because departure is imminent
  • Confusing vaccine recommendations with country entry requirements
  • Administering yellow fever vaccine without assessing contraindications
  • Assuming the yellow fever certificate is immediately valid
  • Using oral live typhoid vaccine in a severely immunocompromised traveler
  • Forgetting antibiotic interactions with oral typhoid or cholera vaccines
  • Prescribing malaria medication based only on the country
  • Using chloroquine in a resistant region
  • Prescribing mefloquine with psychiatric disease or seizures
  • Prescribing primaquine or tafenoquine without quantitative G6PD testing
  • Using doxycycline during pregnancy
  • Stopping malaria medication immediately after returning
  • Assuming prophylaxis excludes malaria
  • Failing to give written travelers’ diarrhea instructions
  • Using routine antibiotic prophylaxis for diarrhea
  • Using loperamide alone for dysentery or febrile diarrhea
  • Using rifaximin for invasive diarrhea
  • Repeatedly treating persistent diarrhea with broad antibiotics
  • Ignoring daytime mosquito exposure
  • Applying permethrin directly to skin
  • Assuming rabies pre-exposure vaccine eliminates the need for PEP
  • Failing to infiltrate rabies immune globulin into the wound
  • Delaying rabies PEP while attempting to locate an animal abroad
  • Allowing ascent despite mild altitude symptoms
  • Using medication instead of descent for HACE or HAPE
  • Recommending aspirin routinely for flight-associated VTE prevention
  • Failing to arrange adequate medication supply and evacuation insurance
  • Overlooking medical tourism as a source of resistant infection
  • Failing to ask a sick patient about travel
  • Sending urgent malaria testing to a laboratory with multiday turnaround
  • Stopping after one negative malaria smear
  • Searching only for exotic diseases and missing common pneumonia, UTI, or PE
  • Failing to isolate a traveler with a potential high-consequence infection

Board and ID Consult Pearls

  • The exact itinerary is more important than the country name alone.
  • Schedule pretravel care ideally 4–6 weeks before departure, but provide useful care even at the last minute.
  • Travelers visiting friends and relatives are at increased risk for malaria, typhoid, hepatitis A, and other infections.
  • All international travelers should have adequate measles immunity.
  • Travel vaccines may be routine, recommended, required, or a combination.
  • Yellow fever vaccine is administered through authorized centers.
  • A primary yellow fever certificate becomes valid 10 days after vaccination.
  • Yellow fever vaccination is generally valid for life under international regulations.
  • A yellow fever booster is not required for most travelers.
  • Yellow fever vaccine risk increases in some older adults and immunocompromised patients.
  • Typhoid vaccine is not fully protective and does not replace food precautions.
  • Japanese encephalitis risk depends on rural exposure, season, duration, and nighttime mosquito contact.
  • Current rabies pre-exposure vaccination uses doses on days 0 and 7.
  • Previously vaccinated travelers still require rabies vaccine on days 0 and 3 after exposure.
  • Previously vaccinated travelers do not receive rabies immune globulin.
  • Unvaccinated rabies PEP includes immune globulin infiltrated into the wound plus vaccine.
  • Cholera vaccine is reserved for travel to areas of active transmission and is not routinely required.
  • Chikungunya vaccine is recommended for travelers to active outbreak areas.
  • TBE vaccine is recommended for extensive tick exposure in endemic regions.
  • Malaria prevention requires both chemoprophylaxis and mosquito avoidance.
  • Atovaquone-proguanil starts 1–2 days before travel and continues 7 days after.
  • Doxycycline starts 1–2 days before travel and continues 4 weeks after.
  • Mefloquine begins at least 2 weeks before travel and continues 4 weeks after.
  • Mefloquine is contraindicated with major psychiatric disease or seizures.
  • Chloroquine is used only where malaria remains chloroquine sensitive.
  • Primaquine and tafenoquine require quantitative G6PD testing.
  • Pregnant travelers should avoid malaria regions when possible.
  • Mefloquine is the recommended U.S. prophylaxis option during pregnancy for chloroquine-resistant malaria areas.
  • Travelers’ diarrhea severity is defined by functional impact rather than stool frequency alone.
  • Mild travelers’ diarrhea does not require antibiotics.
  • Azithromycin is preferred for dysentery, febrile diarrhea, and much of South and Southeast Asia.
  • Loperamide should not be used alone with bloody or febrile diarrhea.
  • Persistent post-travel diarrhea suggests protozoa, C. difficile, or postinfectious disease.
  • DEET and picaridin are effective mosquito repellents.
  • Permethrin is applied to clothing and equipment, not directly to skin.
  • Dengue warning signs often emerge as fever resolves.
  • Avoid NSAIDs until dengue is excluded.
  • At altitude, never ascend with symptoms.
  • Ataxia or confusion at altitude indicates HACE until proven otherwise.
  • Dyspnea at rest at altitude indicates possible HAPE.
  • Descent and oxygen are the definitive treatments for severe altitude illness.
  • Acetazolamide prophylaxis is commonly 125 mg twice daily.
  • Prolonged travel beginning around four hours increases VTE risk.
  • Frequent ambulation and calf exercises are reasonable VTE precautions.
  • Fever after travel to a malaria region requires immediate malaria testing.
  • Malaria prophylaxis does not exclude malaria.
  • Repeat malaria testing when initial conventional testing is negative and suspicion persists.
  • Falciparum malaria is a medical emergency.
  • Travel-associated eosinophilia suggests helminth infection or a noninfectious cause rather than most protozoal disease.
  • Isolate first when a high-consequence transmissible infection is possible.

Bottom Line

Travel medicine begins with a detailed assessment of the traveler, exact itinerary, season, duration, accommodations, activities, transit points, medical access, pregnancy status, immune status, and chronic disease. Country-level advice alone is insufficient because many risks vary substantially within national borders.

Update routine immunizations first, especially measles, influenza, COVID-19, tetanus-pertussis, varicella, and hepatitis B. Then add itinerary-specific vaccines such as hepatitis A, typhoid, yellow fever, meningococcal, Japanese encephalitis, rabies, cholera, chikungunya, tick-borne encephalitis, or polio booster. Distinguish medical recommendations from entry requirements and verify rapidly changing regulations immediately before departure.

Malaria prevention requires destination-specific chemoprophylaxis, daytime and nighttime mosquito precautions, and explicit counseling that prophylaxis is not completely protective. Select medication according to local resistance, pregnancy, age, renal function, psychiatric history, adherence, and G6PD status. Fever during travel or within one year after exposure requires urgent malaria testing even when prophylaxis was taken correctly.

Travelers’ diarrhea should be managed according to functional severity. Mild disease requires hydration and symptom control without antibiotics. Antibiotics may be used for moderate disease and are advised for severe disease. Azithromycin is preferred for dysentery, febrile diarrhea, and many Asian destinations. Loperamide should not be used alone for bloody or febrile diarrhea.

Prevent vector, animal, freshwater, soil, respiratory, sexual, road, and environmental exposures through practical behavioral counseling. Rabies exposure requires immediate wound washing and urgent PEP. High-altitude illness is prevented by gradual ascent; travelers must never ascend with symptoms, and HACE or HAPE requires immediate descent and oxygen whenever possible.

In the ill returned traveler, obtain the exact itinerary and exposure timeline, evaluate common infections alongside tropical disease, and immediately rule out malaria after fever in anyone returning from an endemic area. Repeat malaria testing when the first conventional test is negative and suspicion persists. Travelers with a potential high-consequence transmissible infection should be isolated promptly with early infection-prevention and public-health involvement.

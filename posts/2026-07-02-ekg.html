ECG Stepwise Guide

Added July 2, 2026 • Cardiology • ECG

Overview

Use the same sequence every time you look at a tracing. That keeps you from jumping straight to “STEMI or not?” and missing rhythm, AV block, bundle branch block, axis deviation, hypertrophy, QT prolongation, or electrolyte patterns.

  • Steps 1–2: rhythm and rate
  • Steps 3–5: P waves, PR interval, AV blocks, and axis
  • Steps 6–8: QRS width, bundle branch blocks, rotation, voltage, and hypertrophy
  • Steps 9–10: Q waves, infarct localization, ST segment, and T waves
  • Step 11: QT interval, U waves, and electrolyte patterns
Green = normal Red = do not miss Blue = pattern recognition

1 Rhythm and Sinus Check

Start by deciding whether the rhythm is sinus.

  • Is there a P wave before each QRS?
  • Are P waves upright in leads I and II?
  • Are PR and RR intervals reasonably regular?

If yes, this is likely sinus rhythm. If not, consider atrial fibrillation, atrial flutter, junctional rhythm, ventricular rhythm, or paced rhythm.

Sinus: P → QRS → T Irregularly irregular: think AF

2 Heart Rate

For a regular rhythm, use the big-box method.

  • Heart rate ≈ 300 divided by the number of large boxes between QRS peaks.
  • 1 large box ≈ 300 bpm
  • 2 large boxes ≈ 150 bpm
  • 3 large boxes ≈ 100 bpm
  • 4 large boxes ≈ 75 bpm
  • 5 large boxes ≈ 60 bpm
  • 6 large boxes ≈ 50 bpm

For irregular rhythms, count QRS complexes in 10 seconds and multiply by 6.

Steps 3–5: P Waves, PR Interval, AV Blocks, and Axis

3 P Waves and Atrial Size

Look carefully at leads II, III, aVF, and V1.

  • Right atrial enlargement: tall, peaked P wave greater than 2.5 mm in II, III, or aVF. This is sometimes called P pulmonale.
  • Left atrial enlargement: broad, notched P wave in lead II, sometimes called P mitrale.
  • In V1, left atrial enlargement may show a biphasic P wave with a deep or wide terminal negative component.

4 PR Interval and AV Blocks

Measure from the beginning of the P wave to the beginning of the QRS complex. Normal PR interval is approximately 0.12–0.20 seconds, or 3–5 small boxes.

  • PR > 0.20 sec: first-degree AV block if every P conducts.
  • PR < 0.12 sec: consider pre-excitation, junctional rhythm, or ectopic atrial rhythm.
  • Short PR + delta wave: Wolff-Parkinson-White pattern.

AV Block Pattern Recognition

AV Block Memory Aid

  • Mobitz I: “Longer, longer, longer, drop.”
  • Mobitz II: “If some Ps don’t get through, Mobitz II may kill you.”
  • Complete heart block: “P waves and QRS complexes are divorced.”
Mobitz II, high-grade AV block, and complete heart block are pacing rhythms until proven otherwise, especially if symptomatic, post-MI, or associated with a wide QRS escape.

AV Block Localization Pearls

AV Nodal Blocks

  • Usually narrow QRS.
  • Often vagal, medication-related, or inferior MI related.
  • Mobitz I is usually AV nodal.
  • Atropine often works if symptomatic.

Infranodal Blocks

  • Often wide QRS.
  • Usually His-Purkinje disease.
  • Mobitz II is usually infranodal.
  • Atropine is unreliable.
  • Progression to complete heart block is a major concern.

Escape Rhythm in Complete Heart Block

Escape QRS Rate Meaning
Junctional escape Narrow 40–60 bpm More stable, usually higher escape focus
Ventricular escape Wide 20–40 bpm Less reliable, higher risk, often needs urgent pacing
Atropine works “upstairs” at the SA and AV node. It does not reliably fix infranodal disease such as Mobitz II or wide-complex complete heart block.

5 QRS Axis

Use the two-lead quadrant method first. Look at QRS polarity in lead I and aVF. Then use lead II to refine the result.

Axis Memory Aid

  • Lead I positive: the axis points leftward.
  • aVF positive: the axis points downward.
  • Both positive: down and left = normal quadrant.
  • Both negative: neither left nor down = extreme / northwest axis.

Refine With Lead II

Lead I aVF Lead II Refined Interpretation
Positive Positive Positive Axis approximately 0° to +60°: clearly normal
Positive Positive Negative Axis approximately +60° to +90°: borderline rightward but still within adult normal range
Positive Negative Positive Axis approximately 0° to −30°: borderline or mild leftward axis
Positive Negative Negative Axis approximately −30° to −90°: true left axis deviation; consider left anterior fascicular block when pattern fits

Fascicular Block Thresholds

  • Left anterior fascicular block: axis −45° to −90° with qR pattern in aVL and rS pattern in II, III, and aVF.
  • Left posterior fascicular block: axis +90° to +180° with rS pattern in I and aVL, and qR pattern in III and aVF.
Do not rely only on the automated computer axis. Confirm visually using leads I, aVF, and lead II. Computer algorithms can make obvious axis errors that are easy to catch by inspection.

Axis Categories

Normal Axis

Adult normal QRS axis is approximately −30° to +90°.

Left Axis Deviation

Seen with LVH, left anterior fascicular block, inferior MI, paced rhythm, or conduction disease.

Right Axis Deviation

Seen with RVH, pulmonary hypertension, PE, lateral MI, or left posterior fascicular block.

Extreme Axis Deviation

Also called northwest axis. Think ventricular rhythm, severe conduction disease, hyperkalemia, sodium-channel blockade, or limb lead reversal.

Clinical Interpretation Pearls

  • Always interpret axis with QRS duration and morphology.
  • RBBB plus left axis deviation suggests bifascicular block: RBBB + left anterior fascicular block.
  • Right axis deviation with tall R in V1 supports RVH or pulmonary hypertension.
  • Left axis deviation with high voltage and lateral ST-T changes supports LVH with secondary repolarization abnormality.
  • Extreme axis should prompt a quick check for limb lead reversal before calling severe pathology.

Steps 6–8: QRS Duration, Bundle Branch Blocks, Rotation, Voltage, and Hypertrophy

6 QRS Duration and Bundle Branch Blocks

  • Narrow QRS: less than 0.10 seconds.
  • Borderline QRS: 0.10–0.12 seconds, possible incomplete bundle branch block or nonspecific intraventricular conduction delay.
  • Wide QRS: at least 0.12 seconds.

Right Bundle Branch Block

  • QRS duration ≥120 ms for complete RBBB.
  • rSR′, rsR′, or “rabbit ears” pattern in V1–V2.
  • Wide terminal S wave in lateral leads I, aVL, V5, and V6.
  • Secondary ST depression/T-wave inversion in V1–V3 may be expected.

Left Bundle Branch Block

  • QRS duration ≥120 ms.
  • Broad, notched, or slurred R waves in I, aVL, V5, and V6.
  • Deep S wave or QS pattern in V1.
  • Absent normal septal Q waves in lateral leads.
  • Discordant ST-T changes are expected.

BBB Memory Aid

  • WiLLiaM: LBBB has W in V1 and M in V6.
  • MaRRoW: RBBB has M in V1 and W in V6.
A wide QRS changes how you interpret ST-T abnormalities because bundle branch blocks create secondary repolarization changes.

Incomplete BBB, Fascicular Blocks, and Bifascicular Block

Incomplete RBBB

  • RBBB-like pattern with QRS duration 100–119 ms.
  • Often benign, especially in young healthy patients.
  • Interpret in context: pulmonary disease, RV strain, congenital heart disease, or Brugada pattern can mimic right precordial abnormalities.

Incomplete LBBB

  • LBBB-like morphology with QRS duration 110–119 ms.
  • May suggest evolving conduction disease or structural heart disease.

Left Anterior Fascicular Block

  • Left axis deviation, often −45° to −90°.
  • qR in aVL.
  • rS in II, III, and aVF.
  • Usually narrow or mildly widened QRS.

Left Posterior Fascicular Block

  • Right axis deviation, usually +90° to +180°.
  • rS in I and aVL.
  • qR in III and aVF.
  • Diagnose only after excluding RVH, lateral MI, pulmonary disease, and lead reversal.

Bifascicular and Trifascicular Disease

Bifascicular Block

  • Most common: RBBB + left anterior fascicular block.
  • RBBB + left posterior fascicular block is less common but often more concerning.
  • Can progress to high-grade AV block, especially with syncope or PR prolongation.

“Trifascicular Block” Nuance

The term “trifascicular block” is often used loosely. True disease of all three fascicles usually presents as alternating bundle branch block or high-grade AV block. RBBB + fascicular block + first-degree AV block may suggest diffuse conduction disease, but the prolonged PR interval may still be AV nodal rather than His-Purkinje.

Alternating RBBB and LBBB, alternating fascicular block, syncope with bifascicular block, Mobitz II, high-grade AV block, or complete heart block should prompt urgent cardiology/electrophysiology evaluation.

BBB and STEMI Interpretation

  • RBBB usually does not prevent STEMI diagnosis, but anterior ST-T changes can be harder.
  • LBBB and ventricular pacing cause secondary ST-T changes that can mask or mimic MI.
  • Use original or modified Sgarbossa criteria when ischemia is suspected in LBBB or paced rhythm.
  • New LBBB alone is no longer automatically treated as STEMI equivalent without supportive clinical/ECG findings.

7 Precordial Transition and Rotation

  • Normal R/S transition occurs around V3–V4.
  • Late transition at V5 or later suggests clockwise rotation or poor R-wave progression.
  • Early transition at V2 or earlier suggests counterclockwise rotation.

QRS Duration and LVH Nuance

  • LVH can mildly prolong QRS duration and produce secondary ST-T abnormalities.
  • In LBBB, voltage criteria become harder to apply.
  • When LBBB is present, very wide QRS duration, especially greater than 155 ms, can support associated LVH with high specificity, but ECG alone should not be treated as definitive.
  • Low voltage in the limb leads should prompt consideration of pericardial effusion, COPD, obesity, infiltrative cardiomyopathy, hypothyroidism, or diffuse myocardial disease.

8 Voltage and Ventricular Hypertrophy

LVH and RVH are ECG pattern diagnoses, not single-number diagnoses. No single voltage criterion is perfect. Voltage must be interpreted with age, sex, race, body habitus, QRS duration, axis, and ST-T pattern.

Left Ventricular Hypertrophy Criteria

Criterion Threshold Key Feature
Sokolow-Lyon SV1 + RV5 or RV6 > 35 mm Most common; low sensitivity but high specificity
Cornell voltage RaVL + SV3 > 28 mm men; > 20 mm women Sex-specific
Cornell product (RaVL + SV3) × QRS duration > 2436 mm·ms Combines voltage and QRS duration
Peguero-Lo Presti Deepest S wave in any lead + SV4 ≥ 28 mm men; ≥ 23 mm women Higher sensitivity, lower specificity
Romhilt-Estes ≥5 points definite; ≥4 probable Uses voltage, ST-T, P-wave, axis, QRS duration

Supporting LVH Clues

  • Left axis deviation.
  • Left atrial abnormality.
  • Prolonged QRS duration.
  • Secondary ST-T abnormalities, formerly called “strain.”
  • Lateral downsloping ST depression and asymmetric T-wave inversion in I, aVL, V5, or V6.

Right Ventricular Hypertrophy Criteria

RVH ECG criteria are usually highly specific but poorly sensitive, especially for mild RVH. In most cases, RVH should have both rightward axis and prominent anterior forces in the right precordial leads.

Criterion Threshold Context
Tall R in V1 R wave > 6 mm Specific, especially in congenital heart disease or marked RV pressure load
R:S ratio in V1 > 1.0 Suggests right-sided pressure overload pattern
Sokolow-Lyon RVH RV1 + SV5 or SV6 > 10.5 mm Voltage-based RVH criterion
Deep S in V5 > 10 mm Reflects rightward QRS shift
R peak time in V1 > 0.035 sec when QRS < 0.12 sec Delayed right precordial activation

Supporting RVH Clues

  • Right axis deviation greater than +90°.
  • Tall R wave in V1.
  • R:S ratio greater than 1 in V1.
  • Deep S waves in V5–V6.
  • Right atrial enlargement may coexist.
  • Secondary ST-T changes in right precordial leads can occur with RV strain.
In COPD, low voltage and altered chest anatomy can distort the usual RVH pattern. Do not diagnose RVH from one criterion alone; look for a coherent pattern.

When Voltage Matters

  • LVH with strain suggests chronic pressure load such as hypertension or aortic stenosis.
  • RVH with right axis deviation suggests pulmonary hypertension, chronic lung disease, pulmonary embolism, or congenital heart disease.
  • Low voltage plus tachycardia should raise concern for pericardial effusion or tamponade, especially if the clinical picture fits.

Steps 9–10: Q Waves, Infarction, ST Segment, and T Waves

9 Infarction Signs and Localization

  • Look for pathologic Q waves, poor R-wave progression, or QS complexes.
  • Inferior territory: II, III, aVF.
  • Anterior territory: V1–V4.
  • Lateral territory: I, aVL, V5–V6.
  • Posterior MI: ST depression in V1–V3 with tall R waves, representing a mirror image pattern.

10 ST Segment and T Waves

  • Localized convex ST elevation in a coronary territory suggests STEMI.
  • ST depression and T-wave inversion suggest subendocardial ischemia, unstable angina, or NSTEMI depending on clinical context and biomarkers.
  • Diffuse concave ST elevation with PR depression suggests pericarditis.
  • LVH and bundle branch blocks can create secondary repolarization changes that make ischemia interpretation harder.

Sgarbossa Concepts for LBBB or Paced Rhythm

  • Concordant ST elevation in a lead with positive QRS is concerning.
  • Concordant ST depression in V1–V3 is concerning.
  • Excessively discordant ST elevation is concerning, especially when proportional to the preceding S wave.
In LBBB or ventricular paced rhythm, do not call ischemia based only on ordinary ST elevation. Use concordance, proportional discordance, symptoms, serial ECGs, troponins, and cardiology input.

Step 11: QT Interval, U Waves, and Electrolytes

11 QT Interval and U Waves

  • Visual trick: if the T wave ends before half of the RR interval, QT is probably acceptable.
  • If the T wave extends beyond half the RR interval, verify the QTc and review medications and electrolytes.
  • Hyperkalemia: tall peaked T waves, loss of P waves, QRS widening, and possible sine-wave pattern.
  • Hypokalemia: flattened T waves, ST depression, and prominent U waves.
  • Hypocalcemia prolongs QT.
  • Hypercalcemia shortens QT.

Putting It Together

At the end of your ECG review, you should be able to produce two clean lines.

Rhythm line: “Sinus rhythm at 82 bpm with normal axis and normal intervals.”

Summary line: “LVH with lateral strain; no definite acute ischemic changes.”

Examples

  • “Sinus rhythm with first-degree AV block, left axis deviation, and RBBB consistent with bifascicular disease plus PR prolongation.”
  • “Sinus rhythm with complete LBBB and expected discordant ST-T abnormalities; no Sgarbossa-positive ischemic pattern.”
  • “Complete heart block with wide ventricular escape rhythm at 32 bpm.”

Clinical Pearls

  • Use the same ECG sequence every time.
  • Start with rhythm before looking for ischemia.
  • Irregularly irregular rhythm should immediately raise concern for atrial fibrillation.
  • Short PR plus delta wave suggests WPW pattern.
  • First-degree AV block is PR prolongation with 1:1 conduction.
  • Mobitz I has progressive PR prolongation before a dropped QRS.
  • Mobitz II has sudden dropped QRS complexes with fixed PR intervals.
  • 2:1 AV block is ambiguous; wide QRS or symptoms should make you treat it as high risk.
  • Complete heart block means P waves and QRS complexes march independently.
  • Use leads I and aVF first for axis, then lead II to refine borderline left or rightward patterns.
  • Lead I positive + aVF negative + lead II negative = true left axis deviation.
  • RBBB plus left axis deviation suggests bifascicular block.
  • Alternating bundle branch block is high-risk conduction disease.
  • LBBB and LVH can make ST-T interpretation difficult.
  • Use Sgarbossa or modified Sgarbossa logic when evaluating MI in LBBB or paced rhythm.
  • Sokolow-Lyon, Cornell, Cornell product, Peguero-Lo Presti, and Romhilt-Estes are all LVH tools; no single criterion is perfect.
  • RVH criteria are specific but insensitive. Look for right axis deviation plus prominent anterior forces.
  • Posterior MI can present as ST depression in V1–V3 with tall R waves.
  • Hyperkalemia progresses from peaked T waves to loss of P waves to QRS widening.
  • Hypokalemia can cause prominent U waves.
  • Always interpret the ECG in clinical context.

Bottom Line

A safe ECG interpretation follows a consistent order: rhythm, rate, P waves, PR interval and AV block, axis, QRS width and bundle branch block, precordial transition, voltage, infarction patterns, ST-T changes, QT interval, and electrolyte clues. For axis, start with lead I and aVF, then refine with lead II. For AV block, distinguish nodal patterns from dangerous infranodal disease. For wide QRS, separate RBBB, LBBB, fascicular block, bifascicular disease, pacing, and nonspecific conduction delay. This prevents premature closure and helps you identify rhythm disorders, conduction disease, hypertrophy, ischemia, infarction, and dangerous electrolyte abnormalities.

Wide Complex Tachycardia: VT vs SVT With Aberrancy

Added June 18, 2026 • Cardiology • ~15 min read

A practical resident-level guide to evaluating wide complex tachycardias, differentiating ventricular tachycardia from supraventricular tachycardia with aberrancy, understanding Brugada criteria, and recognizing high-yield ECG findings.

A Framework for Evaluating Tachycardia

When evaluating a tachyarrhythmia, start by asking two simple questions:

  1. Is the QRS narrow or wide?
  2. Is the rhythm regular or irregular?
Rhythm Type Common Diagnoses
Regular Narrow Complex Sinus tachycardia, AVNRT, AVRT, atrial tachycardia, atrial flutter, junctional tachycardia
Irregular Narrow Complex Atrial fibrillation, multifocal atrial tachycardia, atrial flutter with variable conduction
Regular Wide Complex VT, SVT with aberrancy, pacemaker-mediated tachycardia
Irregular Wide Complex Polymorphic VT (torsades), ventricular fibrillation
Most of the diagnostic challenge occurs in regular wide complex tachycardia where the key question becomes: VT or SVT with aberrancy?

What Is SVT With Aberrancy?

SVT with aberrancy refers to a supraventricular rhythm conducted through abnormal ventricular conduction pathways, most commonly due to a pre-existing bundle branch block.

  • AVNRT with bundle branch block
  • AVRT with bundle branch block
  • Baseline right bundle branch block
  • Baseline left bundle branch block

Because ventricular conduction is delayed, the QRS becomes wide and may resemble ventricular tachycardia.

The Golden Rule

If the patient is unstable, do not spend time trying to distinguish VT from SVT with aberrancy. Follow ACLS and treat immediately.

If you are unsure whether the rhythm is VT or SVT with aberrancy, it is generally safest to assume VT.

  • VT accounts for roughly 80–90% of regular wide complex tachycardias.
  • Missing VT is generally more dangerous than misclassifying SVT with aberrancy.
  • Treatment pathways often favor assuming VT when uncertainty exists.

Clinical Clues from the Patient

Features Favoring VT

  • Age greater than 35 years
  • Prior myocardial infarction
  • Known coronary artery disease
  • Structural heart disease
  • Cardiomyopathy
  • Implantable cardioverter-defibrillator (ICD)

Features Favoring SVT With Aberrancy

  • Younger patient
  • No structural heart disease
  • No ischemic history
  • Known history of SVT
Clinical history alone cannot establish the diagnosis, but it significantly changes pre-test probability.

AV Dissociation

AV dissociation occurs when atrial activity and ventricular activity are independent of one another.

The atria continue firing from the sinus node while the ventricles are driven by a ventricular focus.

AV dissociation is highly specific for ventricular tachycardia.

How to Recognize It

  • P waves march through independently.
  • No consistent relationship between P waves and QRS complexes.
  • PR intervals vary continuously.

Capture Beats and Fusion Beats

Capture Beats

A capture beat occurs when a sinus impulse briefly captures the ventricle during VT and produces a nearly normal QRS complex.

A normal-looking QRS appearing within a run of VT is a capture beat and strongly supports VT.

Fusion Beats

A fusion beat occurs when simultaneous activation from both the sinus node and ventricular focus create a hybrid QRS complex.

  • Not fully normal.
  • Not fully ventricular in appearance.
  • Intermediate morphology.
Fusion beats are highly suggestive of VT.

Positive and Negative Concordance

Concordance refers to all precordial leads (V1–V6) pointing in the same direction.

Positive Concordance

  • All QRS complexes predominantly upright.
  • No RS complexes present.

Negative Concordance

  • All QRS complexes predominantly negative.
  • No RS complexes present.
Either positive or negative concordance strongly favors VT.

Extreme Axis Deviation

One classic VT finding is a "northwest axis."

Lead Finding
Lead I Negative
aVL Negative
aVR Positive
Inferior Leads Often negative

This extreme axis deviation strongly favors ventricular tachycardia.

The Brugada Criteria

The Brugada algorithm is one of the most widely taught approaches for distinguishing VT from SVT with aberrancy.

If any step is positive, VT is diagnosed.

Step 1: Absence of RS Complexes

Look across all precordial leads (V1–V6).

  • No RS complexes present → VT.
  • Equivalent to positive or negative concordance.

Step 2: Brugada Sign

Measure from the beginning of the R wave to the nadir of the S wave.

RS Interval > 100 ms = VT

On standard ECG paper this is greater than approximately 2.5 small boxes.

Step 3: AV Dissociation

If AV dissociation is present, VT is diagnosed.

Step 4: Morphologic Criteria

Evaluate QRS morphology in V1 and V6 according to right bundle branch-like or left bundle branch-like patterns.

Most internists will not routinely use the full morphologic criteria, but understanding the overall framework is valuable.

Morphologic Criteria

Right Bundle Branch Block-Like Morphology

Dominant positive QRS in V1.

Features favoring VT include:

  • Tall left rabbit ear
  • Monophasic R wave
  • QR complex
  • R/S ratio less than 1 in V6

Left Bundle Branch Block-Like Morphology

Dominant negative QRS in V1.

Features favoring VT include:

  • Initial R wave >30–40 ms
  • Josephson sign
  • RS interval >60–70 ms
  • QR complex in V6

Josephson Sign

The Josephson sign is a notch or slurring near the nadir of the S wave.

The Josephson sign is another classic ECG finding supporting VT.

Pacemaker-Mediated Tachycardia

Not every wide complex tachycardia is VT.

Pacemaker-mediated tachycardia should be considered when pacemaker spikes precede each QRS complex.

  • Wide paced QRS complexes
  • Visible pacing spikes
  • Regular rhythm
  • Often resembles VT at first glance
Always inspect the ECG carefully for pacing spikes before diagnosing VT.

Take-Home Pearls

  • Most regular wide complex tachycardias are VT.
  • If the patient is unstable, shock first and diagnose later.
  • If uncertain, assume VT.
  • AV dissociation is highly specific for VT.
  • Capture beats and fusion beats strongly favor VT.
  • Positive or negative concordance is highly suggestive of VT.
  • QRS duration >160 ms favors VT.
  • Extreme axis deviation favors VT.
  • Brugada criteria remain one of the most useful ECG algorithms for VT diagnosis.
  • Always remember pacemaker-mediated tachycardia in the differential.

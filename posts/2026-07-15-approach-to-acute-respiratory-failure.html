Approach to Acute Respiratory Failure

Added July 15, 2026 • Pulmonary and Critical Care • ~55 min read

Acute respiratory failure is not a single diagnosis. It is the failure of oxygenation, ventilation, respiratory mechanics, airway protection, or a combination of these processes. The first bedside task is to determine whether the patient is failing now, identify the dominant physiology, provide the correct level of respiratory support, and treat the cause without delaying intubation when noninvasive support is failing. This guide provides a practical framework for hypoxemic, hypercapnic, and mixed respiratory failure, including arterial blood gas interpretation, mechanism-based differential diagnosis, oxygen devices, high-flow nasal cannula, noninvasive ventilation, airway escalation, post-intubation care, and common diagnostic traps.

Clinical Overview

Acute respiratory failure occurs when the respiratory system cannot maintain adequate gas exchange, meet the patient’s ventilatory demand, protect the airway, or sustain the work required to breathe. The patient may initially compensate with tachypnea, increased tidal volume, accessory-muscle use, and sympathetic activation. Respiratory arrest often occurs only after these compensatory mechanisms fail.

Respiratory Failure = Oxygenation Failure, Ventilatory Failure, Mechanical Failure, Airway Failure, or a Combination

The Immediate Questions

  1. Is the airway patent and protected?
  2. Is oxygenation adequate?
  3. Is ventilation adequate?
  4. Can the patient sustain the current work of breathing?
  5. What is the most likely reversible cause?
  6. Is noninvasive support appropriate, or is intubation safer?

Respiratory Support Is a Bridge

Oxygen, high-flow nasal cannula, noninvasive ventilation, and invasive ventilation support physiology while the underlying cause is treated. They do not replace:

  • Antibiotics and source control for infection
  • Diuresis and afterload reduction for cardiogenic pulmonary edema
  • Bronchodilation for obstructive disease
  • Anticoagulation or reperfusion for pulmonary embolism
  • Chest decompression for tension pneumothorax
  • Antidotes for toxicologic respiratory depression
Do not allow an improving oxygen saturation to obscure worsening mental status, exhaustion, hypercapnia, shock, or inability to protect the airway.

Definitions

Acute Hypoxemic Respiratory Failure

Acute hypoxemic respiratory failure is failure to maintain adequate arterial oxygenation. Common practical markers include:

  • PaO₂ below approximately 60 mm Hg
  • SpO₂ below approximately 90% on room air
  • Need for substantial supplemental oxygen to maintain an acceptable saturation
  • Abnormally low PaO₂/FiO₂ or SpO₂/FiO₂ ratio

Acute Hypercapnic Respiratory Failure

Acute hypercapnic respiratory failure is inadequate alveolar ventilation producing elevated PaCO₂ with acidemia or acute clinical deterioration.

Acute Hypercapnic Failure = Elevated PaCO₂ + pH Below the Patient’s Expected Baseline

Acute-on-Chronic Hypercapnic Failure

A patient with chronic CO₂ retention may have a high PaCO₂ at baseline. Acute failure is suggested by:

  • Lower-than-expected pH
  • New somnolence or confusion
  • Increasing work of breathing
  • Rising PaCO₂ from a known baseline
  • Inadequate chronic bicarbonate compensation

Airway Failure

A patient may require intubation despite acceptable oxygen and carbon-dioxide values because of:

  • Inability to protect the airway
  • Progressive upper-airway obstruction
  • Recurrent aspiration
  • Copious secretions with ineffective clearance
  • Severe encephalopathy

Types of Respiratory Failure

Type Dominant Problem Common Causes
Type 1 Hypoxemia Pneumonia, ARDS, edema, PE, shunt, atelectasis
Type 2 Hypercapnia COPD, asthma, obesity hypoventilation, neuromuscular weakness, sedatives
Mixed Hypoxemia and hypercapnia Severe COPD, fatigue during pneumonia, obesity hypoventilation with edema
Mechanical Unsustainable work of breathing Severe obstruction, chest-wall restriction, metabolic acidosis
Airway Patency or protection failure Angioedema, anaphylaxis, tumor, coma, seizure

Classification Is Useful but Not Absolute

Patients commonly move between categories. A patient with pneumonia may begin with pure hypoxemia and later develop hypercapnia as respiratory muscles fatigue. A patient with COPD may have chronic hypercapnia but present primarily because of acute hypoxemia from pneumonia or pulmonary edema.

Recognizing Impending Respiratory Failure

Early Warning Signs

  • Tachypnea
  • Accessory-muscle use
  • Inability to speak full sentences
  • Tripod positioning
  • Paradoxical abdominal movement
  • Diaphoresis
  • Agitation or anxiety
  • Tachycardia

Late and Dangerous Signs

  • Decreasing respiratory rate despite distress
  • Shallow breathing
  • Bradycardia
  • Somnolence
  • Silent chest in severe asthma
  • Inability to clear secretions
  • New hypotension
  • Severe acidemia
  • Refractory hypoxemia
A patient who becomes quieter after prolonged respiratory distress may be exhausting rather than improving.

Normal Numbers Can Mislead

A normal SpO₂ may coexist with severe ventilatory failure when supplemental oxygen is being delivered. A normal PaCO₂ can be ominous in a patient with severe asthma or metabolic acidosis who should be markedly hypocapnic.

The First Five Minutes

Immediate Actions

  1. Call for additional help and move to a monitored setting.
  2. Position the patient upright unless contraindicated.
  3. Apply continuous pulse oximetry and cardiac monitoring.
  4. Assess airway patency and mental status.
  5. Start appropriate oxygen or ventilatory support.
  6. Obtain IV access.
  7. Prepare suction, airway equipment, and emergency medications.
  8. Treat immediately reversible causes.

Immediately Reversible Threats

  • Tension pneumothorax
  • Upper-airway obstruction
  • Opioid toxicity
  • Anaphylaxis
  • Severe bronchospasm
  • Flash pulmonary edema
  • Mucus plugging

Parallel Rather Than Sequential Care

Support Breathing + Diagnose Cause + Prepare for Escalation

Do not wait for a chest radiograph, arterial blood gas, or CT scan before treating obvious severe bronchospasm, pulmonary edema, pneumothorax, or opioid-induced hypoventilation.

Core Gas-Exchange Physiology

Oxygenation

Oxygenation depends on:

  • Inspired oxygen concentration
  • Alveolar ventilation
  • Ventilation-perfusion matching
  • Diffusion
  • Shunt fraction
  • Cardiac output and hemoglobin concentration

Ventilation

PaCO₂ Is Inversely Related to Alveolar Ventilation

Carbon-dioxide elimination depends on effective alveolar ventilation, not simply respiratory rate. Rapid shallow breathing may produce high minute ventilation but poor alveolar ventilation because a large fraction of each breath ventilates dead space.

Oxygen Content

CaO₂ ≈ 1.34 × Hemoglobin × SaO₂ + 0.003 × PaO₂

Most oxygen is carried by hemoglobin. A patient with severe anemia may have a normal saturation but inadequate oxygen content and delivery.

Oxygen Delivery

DO₂ = Cardiac Output × Arterial Oxygen Content

Respiratory care must therefore consider hemoglobin, cardiac output, perfusion, and shock—not SpO₂ alone.

Mechanisms of Hypoxemia

Mechanism Examples Response to Oxygen
Low inspired oxygen High altitude, equipment failure Usually strong
Hypoventilation Opioids, neuromuscular weakness Usually improves, but ventilation remains untreated
V/Q mismatch COPD, asthma, pneumonia, edema, PE Usually improves
Diffusion limitation Interstitial lung disease Often improves
Right-to-left shunt ARDS, dense consolidation, intracardiac shunt Limited response

V/Q Mismatch

V/Q mismatch is the most common mechanism of hypoxemia. Some lung units receive perfusion but inadequate ventilation, while others are ventilated more than they are perfused.

Shunt Physiology

In true shunt, blood passes through nonventilated lung units or bypasses the lungs entirely. Increasing FiO₂ may have limited effect. Positive pressure and recruitment may be required when alveolar collapse or flooding is present.

Pulmonary Embolism

Pulmonary embolism increases dead-space ventilation and causes V/Q mismatch. Severe hypoxemia may also reflect low cardiac output, atelectasis, intracardiac shunting, or concurrent lung disease.

Mechanisms of Hypercapnia

Reduced Respiratory Drive

  • Opioids
  • Sedative-hypnotics
  • Brainstem injury
  • Severe hypothyroidism

Increased Mechanical Load

  • COPD
  • Severe asthma
  • Obesity hypoventilation
  • Chest-wall deformity
  • Massive ascites

Neuromuscular Weakness

  • Myasthenic crisis
  • Guillain–Barré syndrome
  • ALS
  • High cervical spinal-cord injury
  • Critical hypophosphatemia

Increased CO₂ Production

  • Fever
  • Sepsis
  • Seizure
  • Overfeeding
  • Severe agitation

Increased Dead Space

  • Pulmonary embolism
  • Emphysema
  • ARDS
  • Excessive ventilator circuit dead space
Hypercapnia reflects failure to meet ventilatory demand. The problem may be low drive, weak muscles, excessive load, increased dead space, or a combination.

Work of Breathing and Respiratory-Muscle Fatigue

Work Increases With:

  • High airway resistance
  • Low lung compliance
  • Intrinsic PEEP
  • High minute-ventilation demand
  • Metabolic acidosis
  • Pain and agitation
  • Weak respiratory muscles

Signs of Fatigue

  • Paradoxical abdominal movement
  • Alternating abdominal and rib-cage breathing
  • Decreasing tidal volume
  • Worsening hypercapnia
  • Inability to speak
  • Reduced mental status

Respiratory Rate Alone Is Inadequate

A patient with a respiratory rate of 35/min who is alert and generating reasonable tidal volumes may be compensating. A patient with a respiratory rate of 18/min after prolonged distress may be failing.

Arterial Blood Gas Approach

Step 1: Assess pH

  • Acidemia: pH below 7.35
  • Alkalemia: pH above 7.45

Step 2: Determine the Primary Process

  • High PaCO₂ with acidemia suggests respiratory acidosis.
  • Low PaCO₂ with alkalemia suggests respiratory alkalosis.
  • Do not ignore concurrent metabolic disorders.

Step 3: Assess Compensation

Condition Expected Compensation
Acute respiratory acidosis HCO₃⁻ rises about 1 mEq/L per 10 mm Hg PaCO₂ increase
Chronic respiratory acidosis HCO₃⁻ rises about 3.5–4 mEq/L per 10 mm Hg PaCO₂ increase
Acute respiratory alkalosis HCO₃⁻ falls about 2 mEq/L per 10 mm Hg PaCO₂ decrease
Chronic respiratory alkalosis HCO₃⁻ falls about 4–5 mEq/L per 10 mm Hg PaCO₂ decrease

Step 4: Assess Oxygenation in Context

A PaO₂ value is uninterpretable without knowing:

  • FiO₂
  • Respiratory device
  • PEEP or positive pressure
  • Altitude
  • Hemodynamic state

Venous Blood Gas

A VBG is often adequate for rapid pH assessment and for following trends in many stable patients. It is less reliable for exact PaCO₂ and cannot assess arterial oxygenation.

Do not delay urgent ventilatory support while waiting for an arterial blood gas in an obviously deteriorating patient.

Oxygenation Indices

PaO₂/FiO₂ Ratio

P/F Ratio = PaO₂ ÷ FiO₂

Example: PaO₂ 80 mm Hg on FiO₂ 0.50 gives a P/F ratio of 160.

SpO₂/FiO₂ Ratio

S/F Ratio = SpO₂ ÷ FiO₂

The S/F ratio can provide a noninvasive estimate of oxygenation when saturation is not near the flat upper portion of the oxyhemoglobin dissociation curve.

Interpret the Trend

A worsening ratio despite escalating support suggests increasing shunt, alveolar flooding, collapse, or disease progression.

The Alveolar–Arterial Oxygen Gradient

Alveolar Gas Equation

PAO₂ ≈ FiO₂ × (Atmospheric Pressure − Water Vapor Pressure) − PaCO₂ ÷ Respiratory Quotient

At sea level on room air, a simplified estimate is:

PAO₂ ≈ 150 − PaCO₂ ÷ 0.8

A–a Gradient

A–a Gradient = PAO₂ − PaO₂

Clinical Use

  • Normal or near-normal gradient: low inspired oxygen or pure hypoventilation
  • Elevated gradient: V/Q mismatch, diffusion limitation, or shunt

Limitations

  • Less useful on uncertain FiO₂
  • Changes with age
  • Changes with altitude
  • Does not identify a specific diagnosis

P/F Ratio, S/F Ratio, and ARDS Recognition

The 2024 global ARDS definition expands recognition beyond intubated patients and can include patients supported with high-flow nasal oxygen at flows of at least 30 L/min or noninvasive ventilation. It also permits use of the S/F ratio when SpO₂ is 97% or lower and includes lung ultrasound as an imaging option.

Global ARDS Concept

  • Acute onset or worsening within one week
  • Bilateral pulmonary opacities
  • Edema not primarily explained by cardiogenic pulmonary edema or fluid overload
  • Objective hypoxemia by P/F or S/F ratio

Practical Point

Do not wait for intubation before recognizing severe inflammatory hypoxemic respiratory failure or beginning lung-protective thinking.

Pulse Oximetry Limitations

False or Unreliable Readings

  • Poor peripheral perfusion
  • Shock or vasoconstriction
  • Motion artifact
  • Nail products
  • Dyshemoglobinemia
  • Venous pulsation
  • Device or probe malposition

Skin Pigmentation

Pulse oximetry may overestimate arterial oxygen saturation in some patients with darker skin pigmentation, creating occult hypoxemia. When the reading conflicts with clinical findings, obtain an arterial blood gas or co-oximetry rather than dismissing the examination.

Carbon Monoxide Poisoning

Standard pulse oximetry cannot distinguish oxyhemoglobin from carboxyhemoglobin and may appear normal. Use co-oximetry.

Methemoglobinemia

Pulse oximetry often trends toward approximately 85% regardless of the true oxygen tension. Obtain co-oximetry when suspected.

Bedside Assessment

Assess Simultaneously

  • Airway patency
  • Respiratory rate and pattern
  • Speech
  • Accessory-muscle use
  • Breath sounds
  • Mental status
  • Perfusion and blood pressure
  • Secretion burden

Position and Appearance

  • Tripod position suggests severe mechanical load.
  • Inability to lie flat may suggest pulmonary edema or diaphragmatic weakness.
  • Unilateral reduced movement suggests pneumothorax, effusion, or obstruction.
  • Stridor suggests upper-airway narrowing.

Do Not Separate Respiratory and Hemodynamic Assessment

Shock worsens respiratory failure by increasing lactate and ventilatory demand. Positive pressure may reduce venous return and precipitate collapse in hypovolemia, right-ventricular failure, or tamponade.

Focused History

Time Course

  • Seconds to minutes: pneumothorax, PE, aspiration, anaphylaxis, upper-airway obstruction
  • Hours: flash pulmonary edema, asthma, COPD, toxin exposure
  • Days: pneumonia, ARDS, heart failure, neuromuscular deterioration

Key Symptoms

  • Fever and sputum
  • Chest pain
  • Orthopnea
  • Wheeze
  • Hemoptysis
  • Dysphagia
  • Weakness
  • Medication or substance exposure

Important Background

  • COPD, asthma, ILD, heart failure
  • Obesity hypoventilation or sleep apnea
  • Neuromuscular disease
  • Thromboembolism risk
  • Recent surgery or hospitalization
  • Home oxygen or PAP settings
  • Baseline ABG and bicarbonate
  • Goals of care and prior intubation experience

Focused Examination

Finding Possible Interpretation
Diffuse wheeze Asthma, COPD, cardiogenic edema, airway compression
Silent chest Life-threatening airflow obstruction
Diffuse crackles Edema, pneumonia, ARDS, ILD
Unilateral absent breath sounds Pneumothorax, mainstem obstruction, large effusion
Stridor Upper-airway obstruction
JVD with hypotension RV failure, PE, tamponade, tension pneumothorax
Peripheral edema and orthopnea Heart failure, but does not prove cardiogenic edema
Ptosis or bulbar weakness Neuromuscular respiratory failure

Point-of-Care Ultrasound

Lung Ultrasound

  • Diffuse bilateral B-lines: interstitial syndrome
  • Focal B-lines or consolidation: pneumonia or focal lung injury
  • Absent sliding with lung point: pneumothorax
  • Anechoic dependent fluid: pleural effusion
  • Dynamic air bronchograms: pneumonia

Cardiac Ultrasound

  • LV systolic function
  • RV size and function
  • Pericardial effusion
  • Gross valvular pathology
  • Volume and filling clues

Integrated Interpretation

Diffuse B-lines are not synonymous with cardiogenic pulmonary edema. They may occur with ARDS, pneumonia, ILD, and other interstitial processes. Integrate the cardiac examination and clinical context.

Diaphragm and Neuromuscular Assessment

Ultrasound may demonstrate reduced diaphragmatic excursion or paradoxical movement, but it should complement rather than replace serial vital capacity, inspiratory-force assessment, and clinical examination.

Initial Diagnostics

Common Initial Tests

  • Portable chest radiograph
  • ECG
  • ABG or VBG
  • CBC and metabolic panel
  • Lactate when shock or high work is present
  • Respiratory viral testing when relevant
  • Blood and respiratory cultures when infection is suspected

Selected Tests

  • Troponin
  • BNP or NT-proBNP
  • D-dimer in appropriately selected low- or intermediate-risk patients
  • CT pulmonary angiography
  • CT chest
  • Co-oximetry
  • Neuromuscular testing

BNP and Troponin Are Contextual

BNP may be elevated with renal dysfunction, RV strain, sepsis, and chronic heart disease. Troponin may rise with PE, myocarditis, tachyarrhythmia, and critical illness. Neither test independently establishes cardiogenic pulmonary edema or acute coronary syndrome.

Mechanism-Based Differential Diagnosis

Alveolar Filling or Collapse

  • Pneumonia
  • ARDS
  • Cardiogenic edema
  • Aspiration
  • Alveolar hemorrhage
  • Atelectasis

Airway Obstruction

  • Asthma
  • COPD
  • Mucus plug
  • Foreign body
  • Upper-airway edema or tumor

Pulmonary Vascular

  • Pulmonary embolism
  • Acute pulmonary hypertension
  • RV infarction or failure

Extrapulmonary Ventilatory Failure

  • Opioid or sedative toxicity
  • Neuromuscular disease
  • Obesity hypoventilation
  • Chest-wall restriction
  • Severe metabolic demand

Do Not Anchor on One Abnormality

A patient may have multiple causes, such as COPD plus pneumonia, heart failure plus PE, obesity hypoventilation plus sedative exposure, or sepsis plus metabolic acidosis and ARDS.

Oxygen Targets

Most Acutely Ill Adults

A practical target is often an SpO₂ in the low-to-mid 90s, avoiding both persistent hypoxemia and unnecessary hyperoxia. Current sepsis guidance allows individualized targets roughly within 90%–96%, depending on patient factors and resources.

Risk of Hypercapnic Failure

Common Initial Target: SpO₂ 88%–92%

This target is often used initially for patients with known or suspected chronic CO₂ retention, including severe COPD or obesity hypoventilation, until blood gases clarify the physiology.

Exceptions Requiring Different Targets

  • Carbon monoxide poisoning
  • Pregnancy
  • Severe anemia or shock
  • Post-cardiac arrest or neurologic injury
  • Specific congenital heart disease

Oxygen-Induced Hypercapnia

Hypercapnia after excessive oxygen in COPD is driven mainly by worsening V/Q mismatch and the Haldane effect, not simply loss of “hypoxic drive.” Oxygen should not be withheld from a dangerously hypoxemic patient; it should be titrated and ventilation supported when necessary.

Oxygen-Delivery Devices

Device Typical Use Key Limitation
Nasal cannula Mild oxygen requirement FiO₂ varies with inspiratory flow and mouth breathing
Simple face mask Moderate oxygen requirement Requires adequate minimum flow to prevent CO₂ rebreathing
Venturi mask More controlled FiO₂ Limited maximum support
Nonrebreather mask Immediate severe hypoxemia or preoxygenation FiO₂ falls with mask leak or inadequate reservoir flow
HFNC Acute hypoxemic failure Does not guarantee ventilation or airway protection
NIV Ventilatory failure or cardiogenic edema Requires cooperation and close monitoring

Escalate Based on Physiology

Do not progress through devices mechanically. A patient with acute COPD and acidemia may need NIV rather than a prolonged trial of HFNC. A patient with severe hypoxemic pneumonia may benefit from HFNC rather than repeated low-flow-mask adjustments.

Nasal Cannula and Face Masks

Standard Nasal Cannula

  • Comfortable
  • Allows eating and speaking
  • Best for mild oxygen needs
  • FiO₂ is imprecise

Venturi Mask

A Venturi mask provides a more predictable oxygen concentration and may be useful when controlled oxygen delivery is important.

Nonrebreather Mask

  • Use high flow sufficient to keep the reservoir inflated.
  • Ensure a good facial seal.
  • Use as a bridge while preparing HFNC, NIV, or intubation.

Dual Oxygen During Preoxygenation

A nasal cannula may be placed under a nonrebreather mask to increase delivered oxygen and permit apneic oxygenation during airway preparation.

High-Flow Nasal Cannula

Physiologic Benefits

  • Heated humidified gas
  • High and more stable FiO₂
  • Meeting high inspiratory flow demand
  • Washout of upper-airway dead space
  • Small amount of flow-dependent positive airway pressure
  • Reduced work of breathing

Strongest Use Case

Acute Hypoxemic Respiratory Failure Without Immediate Need for Intubation

ERS guidance supports HFNC over conventional oxygen for hypoxemic acute respiratory failure and supports its use over NIV in many patients with de novo hypoxemic failure. The 2026 sepsis guideline also suggests HFNC over conventional oxygen and over NIV as initial support in sepsis-associated acute hypoxemic respiratory failure.

Typical Initial Setup

  • Flow approximately 40–60 L/min in a severely distressed adult
  • FiO₂ initially high enough to rapidly achieve the oxygen target
  • Titrate FiO₂ down as oxygenation improves
  • Adjust flow to reduce respiratory effort and improve comfort

Not the Best Primary Modality For:

  • Severe acute hypercapnic acidosis requiring pressure support
  • Immediate airway protection
  • Progressive upper-airway obstruction
  • Cardiac or respiratory arrest

HFNC Monitoring and Failure

Reassess Within Minutes and Repeatedly

  • Respiratory rate
  • Work of breathing
  • Mental status
  • Oxygen requirement
  • Hemodynamics
  • ABG when hypercapnia or severe disease is possible

Signs of Success

  • Reduced respiratory rate
  • Reduced accessory-muscle use
  • Improved comfort
  • Stable or decreasing FiO₂ requirement
  • Improved gas exchange

Signs of Failure

  • Persistent severe tachypnea
  • Worsening work of breathing
  • Increasing FiO₂ requirement
  • Hemodynamic instability
  • Altered mental status
  • Rising PaCO₂ or worsening acidemia
  • Inability to clear secretions

ROX Index

ROX = (SpO₂ ÷ FiO₂) ÷ Respiratory Rate

The ROX index can support trend assessment in selected HFNC patients, but it should not override obvious clinical deterioration or delay intubation.

The most dangerous HFNC complication is delayed recognition of failure.

Awake Proning

Potential Role

Awake prone positioning may improve oxygenation in selected nonintubated patients with acute hypoxemic respiratory failure, particularly while receiving HFNC.

Requirements

  • Awake and cooperative patient
  • Ability to reposition safely
  • Continuous monitoring
  • No immediate indication for intubation

Limitations

  • Improved saturation does not prove disease improvement.
  • Do not sedate solely to force tolerance.
  • Do not delay intubation in a failing patient.

Noninvasive Ventilation

Noninvasive ventilation provides positive pressure without an endotracheal tube. It may reduce work of breathing, improve alveolar ventilation, recruit alveoli, and reduce cardiac preload and afterload.

Best-Supported Acute Indications

  • COPD exacerbation with acute or acute-on-chronic respiratory acidosis
  • Acute cardiogenic pulmonary edema
  • Selected post-extubation prevention in high-risk patients

Use With Caution

  • De novo hypoxemic respiratory failure
  • Severe pneumonia
  • ARDS
  • Asthma
  • Severe immunocompromise

These patients may fail because NIV cannot reliably offset severe shunt, high respiratory drive, or progressive disease. Close monitoring and early controlled intubation are essential.

CPAP vs BiPAP

CPAP

Continuous positive airway pressure provides one pressure throughout the respiratory cycle.

Primary Effects

  • Alveolar recruitment
  • Improved oxygenation
  • Reduced preload and LV afterload

Common Use

  • Acute cardiogenic pulmonary edema
  • Predominantly oxygenation failure

BiPAP or Bilevel NIV

Bilevel ventilation provides inspiratory positive airway pressure and expiratory positive airway pressure.

Pressure Support = IPAP − EPAP

Primary Effects

  • EPAP improves oxygenation and counters airway collapse.
  • Pressure support increases tidal volume and reduces PaCO₂.

Common Use

  • COPD with hypercapnic acidosis
  • Obesity hypoventilation
  • Selected neuromuscular or chest-wall failure

Strong NIV Indications

COPD Exacerbation With Acidosis

ERS/ATS guidance strongly supports bilevel NIV when acute or acute-on-chronic respiratory acidosis is present. Benefits include lower intubation rates and improved outcomes when patients are appropriately selected.

Acute Cardiogenic Pulmonary Edema

CPAP or bilevel NIV can rapidly improve dyspnea and gas exchange while nitrates, diuresis, and treatment of the precipitating cardiac problem are initiated.

Postoperative Respiratory Failure

NIV may be used in selected postoperative patients when airway protection, surgical anatomy, hemodynamics, and secretion management are favorable.

Prevention After Extubation

Preventive NIV is useful in selected high-risk patients, particularly those with chronic hypercapnic respiratory disease. NIV used only after established post-extubation failure can delay needed reintubation in unselected patients.

NIV Contraindications and Poor Candidates

Absolute or Near-Absolute Contraindications

  • Cardiac or respiratory arrest
  • Inability to protect the airway
  • Severe uncontrolled vomiting
  • Complete upper-airway obstruction
  • Major facial trauma preventing interface seal
  • Immediate need for intubation

Relative Contraindications

  • Severe agitation
  • Copious secretions
  • Hemodynamic instability
  • Severe hypoxemia
  • Recent upper GI or facial surgery
  • High aspiration risk

Do Not Use Sedation to Hide Failure

Small carefully monitored doses may occasionally improve interface tolerance, but deep sedation can worsen airway protection, hypoventilation, and delayed intubation.

Starting NIV

General Bilevel Starting Framework

  • IPAP approximately 10–12 cm H₂O
  • EPAP approximately 5 cm H₂O
  • Increase pressure support to improve tidal volume, work of breathing, and PaCO₂
  • Increase EPAP and FiO₂ to improve oxygenation

COPD

  • Start tolerably and increase IPAP promptly if ventilation remains inadequate.
  • Avoid excessive EPAP that worsens hyperinflation unless needed to counter intrinsic PEEP.
  • Reassess pH and PaCO₂ within approximately 1–2 hours or earlier if deteriorating.

Cardiogenic Pulmonary Edema

  • CPAP approximately 8–12 cm H₂O is commonly effective.
  • Bilevel support is useful when hypercapnia or high work of breathing is present.
  • Treat hypertension and congestion simultaneously.

Interface

  • Oronasal mask is commonly used initially.
  • Helmet NIV may improve tolerance in experienced centers.
  • Prevent pressure injury and excessive leak.

Recognizing NIV Failure

Failure Signs

  • Worsening pH or rising PaCO₂
  • Persistent severe tachypnea
  • Increasing accessory-muscle use
  • Refractory hypoxemia
  • New hemodynamic instability
  • Worsening encephalopathy
  • Inability to tolerate the interface
  • Inability to manage secretions

Expected Early Response in COPD

Within the first one to two hours, successful NIV commonly produces:

  • Lower respiratory rate
  • Reduced dyspnea
  • Improved pH
  • Falling PaCO₂
  • Improved mental status
Do not continue failing NIV because the patient “has not yet had enough time.” Worsening physiology is an indication to intubate, not to extend the trial.

When to Intubate

Immediate Indications

  • Cardiac arrest or apnea
  • Inability to protect the airway
  • Progressive upper-airway obstruction
  • Refractory hypoxemia
  • Severe hemodynamic instability
  • Failure of HFNC or NIV
  • Severe exhaustion
  • Life-threatening acidemia from ventilatory failure

No Single Threshold Is Absolute

Intubation should integrate:

  • Trajectory
  • Work of breathing
  • Mental status
  • Gas exchange
  • Hemodynamics
  • Anticipated disease course
  • Airway difficulty

Controlled Intubation Is Safer

Intubate before the patient reaches peri-arrest physiology whenever failure is becoming clear.

The Physiologically Difficult Airway

An anatomically easy airway can still be physiologically dangerous. Intubation removes spontaneous respiratory effort, introduces apnea, and exposes the patient to sedative-induced vasodilation and positive-pressure effects.

High-Risk Physiologies

  • Severe hypoxemia
  • Shock
  • Metabolic acidosis
  • Right-ventricular failure
  • Severe obstructive lung disease

Before Induction

  • Optimize preoxygenation.
  • Position appropriately.
  • Prepare vasopressors.
  • Treat hypovolemia when present.
  • Use the most experienced operator.
  • Have a backup airway plan.
  • Plan initial ventilator settings before medications are given.

Severe Metabolic Acidosis

A patient with profound metabolic acidosis may be maintaining pH through extreme minute ventilation. Even brief apnea or inadequate post-intubation ventilation can cause cardiac arrest.

Preoxygenation

Goals

  • Maximize end-expiratory oxygen reserve
  • Reduce desaturation during apnea
  • Correct recruitable atelectasis

Methods

  • Nonrebreather mask plus nasal cannula
  • HFNC
  • NIV with high FiO₂ for severe shunt or obesity
  • Head-up positioning

Apneic Oxygenation

Continue nasal oxygen during laryngoscopy when feasible. Apneic oxygenation does not remove CO₂ and does not replace timely airway placement.

Delayed Sequence Intubation

In selected severely agitated patients, carefully titrated dissociative sedation may permit effective preoxygenation before paralytic administration. This requires an experienced airway team and full preparation for immediate intubation.

Immediate Post-Intubation Care

Confirm Tube Position

  • Continuous waveform capnography
  • Bilateral chest movement and breath sounds
  • Appropriate tube depth
  • Chest imaging after stabilization

Start Lung-Protective Ventilation

  • Tidal volume approximately 6–8 mL/kg predicted body weight
  • Lower tidal volume when ARDS is present
  • Adjust respiratory rate for pH and ventilatory demand
  • Use sufficient PEEP for oxygenation and recruitment
  • Measure plateau pressure

Predicted Body Weight

Ventilator tidal volume is based on height and sex—not actual body weight.

Treat Post-Intubation Hypotension

  • Evaluate sedative effect.
  • Assess for hypovolemia.
  • Consider tension pneumothorax.
  • Consider excessive PEEP or dynamic hyperinflation.
  • Use vasopressors promptly when needed.

Provide Analgesia and Sedation

Begin post-intubation analgesia and sedation immediately. Do not leave a paralyzed patient awake because medications were focused only on the procedure.

Cause-Directed Management

Respiratory support must be paired with treatment of the underlying process. The same oxygen device may be used for very different diseases, but the definitive treatment differs dramatically.

Cause Immediate Disease-Directed Action
Pneumonia or sepsis Antibiotics, cultures, source control, hemodynamic support
Cardiogenic edema Nitrates when hypertensive, diuresis, treat ischemia or valve disease
COPD or asthma Bronchodilators, corticosteroids, treat trigger
Pulmonary embolism Anticoagulation, risk stratification, reperfusion when indicated
Pneumothorax Immediate decompression when tension physiology is present
Opioid toxicity Ventilatory support and titrated naloxone

Pneumonia, Sepsis, and ARDS

Initial Support

  • Conventional oxygen for mild disease
  • HFNC for significant acute hypoxemia
  • Early intubation for worsening work, shock, encephalopathy, or refractory hypoxemia

NIV Caution

NIV failure is common in severe de novo hypoxemic respiratory failure. Large unsupported tidal volumes during vigorous spontaneous breathing may worsen lung stress. Use only in carefully selected patients with continuous observation and rapid escalation capability.

ARDS Recognition

  • Acute inflammatory hypoxemia
  • Bilateral opacities
  • Not primarily cardiogenic
  • Reduced P/F or S/F ratio

After Intubation

  • Low tidal volume ventilation
  • Plateau pressure no higher than approximately 30 cm H₂O
  • Appropriate PEEP
  • Prone positioning for moderate-to-severe ARDS

Acute Cardiogenic Pulmonary Edema

Clinical Clues

  • Orthopnea
  • Diffuse B-lines
  • Hypertension
  • Elevated filling-pressure signs
  • Known heart or valve disease

Respiratory Support

  • CPAP or bilevel NIV when severe distress is present
  • HFNC when positive pressure is not tolerated and hypercapnia is not dominant
  • Intubation for shock, exhaustion, or NIV failure

Treat the Hemodynamics

  • Nitrates for hypertensive pulmonary edema when appropriate
  • Loop diuretics for congestion
  • Treat acute ischemia, arrhythmia, or valvular catastrophe
In flash pulmonary edema with severe hypertension, rapid afterload reduction may improve respiratory failure faster than diuresis alone.

COPD Exacerbation With Respiratory Failure

Oxygen

  • Initially target SpO₂ approximately 88%–92% when hypercapnic risk is present.
  • Obtain a blood gas when severe illness, somnolence, or high oxygen need is present.

Noninvasive Ventilation

Acute Respiratory Acidosis From COPD → Bilevel NIV Unless Contraindicated

Medical Treatment

  • Short-acting bronchodilators
  • Systemic corticosteroids
  • Antibiotics when indicated
  • Treat pneumonia, edema, PE, or arrhythmia when present

Intubation

Intubate for worsening acidemia, exhaustion, inability to protect the airway, hemodynamic instability, refractory hypoxemia, or failed NIV.

After Intubation

  • Use lower respiratory rates.
  • Allow prolonged expiration.
  • Monitor auto-PEEP.
  • Accept permissive hypercapnia when appropriate.

Severe Asthma and Status Asthmaticus

Danger Signs

  • Silent chest
  • Exhaustion
  • Altered mental status
  • Rising or normalizing PaCO₂ despite severe distress
  • Bradycardia or hypotension

Immediate Therapy

  • Repeated or continuous inhaled beta-agonist
  • Inhaled anticholinergic
  • Systemic corticosteroid
  • IV magnesium in severe disease
  • Consider epinephrine when anaphylaxis or severe refractory bronchospasm is present

NIV

Evidence is less established than in COPD. A short closely monitored trial may be considered in a cooperative patient without imminent failure.

Intubation

  • Use an experienced airway team.
  • Expect hypotension and dynamic hyperinflation.
  • Use low respiratory rate and long expiratory time.
  • Avoid excessive minute ventilation.

Pulmonary Embolism

Respiratory Findings

  • Tachypnea
  • Hypoxemia
  • Respiratory alkalosis
  • Increased dead space

High-Risk Features

  • Hypotension
  • Syncope
  • RV dilation or dysfunction
  • Elevated lactate
  • Severe hypoxemia

Intubation Risk

Intubation can precipitate cardiovascular collapse in severe RV failure by reducing preload and increasing pulmonary vascular resistance.

Before Intubation

  • Optimize blood pressure.
  • Prepare vasopressors.
  • Avoid excessive PEEP.
  • Use the lowest effective airway pressure.
  • Coordinate urgent reperfusion when indicated.

Pneumothorax

Clinical Clues

  • Sudden dyspnea or pleuritic pain
  • Unilateral absent breath sounds
  • Hypotension
  • JVD
  • Acute deterioration after positive-pressure ventilation

Tension Pneumothorax

Clinical Diagnosis + Hemodynamic Compromise → Immediate Decompression

Do not wait for imaging when the clinical diagnosis is compelling and the patient is unstable.

Ultrasound

  • Absent lung sliding is not specific.
  • A lung point strongly supports pneumothorax.
  • Severe bullous disease can complicate interpretation.

Upper-Airway Obstruction

Causes

  • Angioedema
  • Anaphylaxis
  • Epiglottitis
  • Foreign body
  • Tumor
  • Post-extubation laryngeal edema

Warning Signs

  • Stridor
  • Drooling
  • Voice change
  • Inability to handle secretions
  • Progressive facial or tongue swelling

Management Principle

Secure a threatened airway early with the most experienced team. A previously straightforward airway can become impossible after complete obstruction develops.

Anaphylaxis

  • IM epinephrine is first-line.
  • Airway planning and hemodynamic treatment occur simultaneously.

Neuromuscular Respiratory Failure

Potential Causes

  • Myasthenic crisis
  • Guillain–Barré syndrome
  • ALS
  • Botulism
  • Spinal-cord disease
  • Critical electrolyte abnormalities

Symptoms

  • Weak cough
  • Orthopnea
  • Dysphagia
  • Bulbar weakness
  • Paradoxical breathing
  • Morning headache

Monitoring

  • Serial forced vital capacity
  • Negative inspiratory force
  • Bulbar function
  • Secretion clearance
  • ABG or VBG trends

Do Not Wait for Hypercapnia

Hypercapnia is often a late finding in neuromuscular respiratory failure. Worsening bulbar function and declining vital capacity may require intubation before the blood gas becomes dramatic.

Respiratory Failure in Severe Metabolic Acidosis

Examples

  • Diabetic ketoacidosis
  • Septic lactic acidosis
  • Salicylate toxicity
  • Renal failure
  • Toxic alcohols

Compensatory Ventilation

The patient may require extremely high minute ventilation to maintain pH. Respiratory muscle fatigue can cause abrupt deterioration.

Intubation Hazard

  • Preoxygenate thoroughly.
  • Minimize apnea time.
  • Match or exceed pre-intubation minute ventilation rapidly.
  • Obtain an early post-intubation blood gas.
  • Treat the metabolic cause immediately.
A “normal” post-intubation respiratory rate may be catastrophically inadequate for a patient who was breathing 35–45 times per minute to compensate for severe metabolic acidosis.

Toxicologic Causes

Opioids

  • Support ventilation.
  • Use titrated naloxone to restore adequate breathing rather than necessarily full arousal.
  • Observe for recurrent toxicity from long-acting agents.

Sedative-Hypnotics

  • Support airway and ventilation.
  • Avoid routine flumazenil in mixed or chronic benzodiazepine exposure because of seizure risk.

Salicylates

  • Respiratory alkalosis and metabolic acidosis are typical.
  • Loss of compensatory hyperventilation after intubation can be fatal.
  • Urgently alkalinize and evaluate for dialysis.

Carbon Monoxide

  • Give high-concentration oxygen.
  • Use co-oximetry.
  • Consider hyperbaric consultation based on severity and patient factors.

Methemoglobinemia

  • Consider with cyanosis and a saturation gap.
  • Confirm with co-oximetry.
  • Treat severe symptomatic disease with methylene blue when appropriate.

Reassessment

After Every Intervention

  • Respiratory rate and pattern
  • Work of breathing
  • Mental status
  • SpO₂ and oxygen device
  • Hemodynamics
  • Gas-exchange trend
  • Secretion burden

Set a Time-Limited Trial

When initiating HFNC or NIV, define:

  • Expected response
  • Reassessment interval
  • Failure criteria
  • Airway plan

Trajectory Matters More Than One Snapshot

Improving Support Requirement + Improving Work + Stable Mentation = Likely Success
Escalating Support + Persistent Work + Worsening Mentation or Hemodynamics = Failure

Documentation

Document the Syndrome

  • Acute hypoxemic respiratory failure
  • Acute hypercapnic respiratory failure
  • Acute-on-chronic respiratory failure
  • Airway-protection failure

Document Objective Evidence

  • Oxygen saturation and device
  • FiO₂ or flow
  • ABG findings
  • Work of breathing
  • Baseline oxygen requirement
  • Response to treatment

Example

Acute hypoxemic respiratory failure due to multifocal pneumonia, with respiratory rate 34/min, marked accessory-muscle use, and SpO₂ 84% on room air. Improved to 94% on HFNC 50 L/min at FiO₂ 0.60, but remains tachypneic. Blood cultures and respiratory testing obtained; antibiotics started. ICU and airway teams notified. Will reassess work of breathing, FiO₂ requirement, mental status, and ABG within 30 minutes, with intubation for worsening distress, shock, encephalopathy, or escalating oxygen requirement.

Acute Respiratory Failure Checklist

  • Is the airway patent and protected?
  • Is this primarily hypoxemic, hypercapnic, mixed, mechanical, or airway failure?
  • Is the patient compensating or exhausting?
  • What oxygen device and FiO₂ are currently being used?
  • What is the target saturation?
  • Does the patient need HFNC, NIV, or immediate intubation?
  • Is NIV strongly indicated for COPD acidosis or cardiogenic edema?
  • Are there contraindications to NIV?
  • Has a time-limited support trial and failure criteria been defined?
  • What does the ABG show?
  • Is the compensation appropriate?
  • What is the P/F or S/F ratio?
  • Could pulse oximetry be inaccurate?
  • What does lung and cardiac POCUS show?
  • Could this be pneumothorax, PE, airway obstruction, or toxin exposure?
  • Has the underlying cause been treated?
  • Is the airway team aware of possible deterioration?
  • Are vasopressors and post-intubation sedation prepared?
  • Were post-intubation ventilator settings chosen using predicted body weight?
  • Has the patient been reassessed after every intervention?

Common Clinical Scenarios

Case 1: Severe Pneumonia

A patient with bilateral infiltrates has SpO₂ 86% on a nonrebreather mask, respiratory rate 36/min, and marked accessory-muscle use.

Initiate maximal monitored respiratory support while preparing for intubation. Severe persistent work and failure of a properly functioning nonrebreather suggest that a prolonged low-flow oxygen trial is unsafe.

Case 2: COPD With pH 7.25

A patient with COPD has PaCO₂ 78 mm Hg, pH 7.25, and remains awake and cooperative.

Start bilevel NIV, bronchodilators, corticosteroids, controlled oxygen, and trigger-directed therapy. Reassess pH, PaCO₂, work, and mental status within one to two hours.

Case 3: Flash Pulmonary Edema

A severely hypertensive patient has diffuse B-lines, frothy sputum, and extreme respiratory distress.

Use CPAP or bilevel NIV promptly and treat the hypertension with rapid afterload reduction while evaluating the cardiac trigger.

Case 4: Quiet Asthmatic

A patient with severe asthma becomes less tachypneic, speaks less, and has minimal wheeze after prolonged distress.

Assume exhaustion and critically reduced airflow rather than improvement. Prepare for controlled intubation while continuing aggressive bronchodilator therapy.

Case 5: Normal SpO₂ With Somnolence

A patient receiving high-flow oxygen has SpO₂ 97% but becomes somnolent with shallow breathing.

Oxygenation does not assess ventilation. Obtain a blood gas and support ventilation immediately; consider hypercapnia, opioid effect, or neuromuscular failure.

Case 6: Severe Metabolic Acidosis

A patient with DKA has pH 6.95 and respiratory rate 42/min.

Avoid intubation unless necessary. If required, minimize apnea and immediately deliver a minute ventilation capable of maintaining compensation while definitive DKA treatment proceeds.

Case 7: Pulmonary Embolism With RV Failure

A hypotensive patient with massive PE is tiring and requires intubation.

Prepare for peri-intubation collapse with vasopressors, careful induction, low airway pressures, and urgent reperfusion planning.

Case 8: Neuromuscular Weakness

A patient with myasthenia has worsening dysphagia and a declining vital capacity but normal oxygen saturation.

Do not wait for hypoxemia or hypercapnia. Bulbar dysfunction and declining respiratory mechanics may require early intubation.

Case 9: HFNC Improves Saturation Only

SpO₂ improves from 84% to 94% on HFNC, but respiratory rate remains 40/min with worsening diaphoresis.

This is not adequate clinical improvement. Persistent high work despite corrected saturation suggests HFNC failure and need for escalation.

Case 10: One-Sided Breath Sounds After Intubation

A patient becomes hypotensive and hypoxemic immediately after intubation with absent left-sided breath sounds.

Immediately evaluate mainstem intubation and tension pneumothorax. Do not assume the underlying pneumonia simply worsened at the exact moment of intubation.

Common Pitfalls

  • Using SpO₂ alone to assess respiratory failure
  • Missing hypercapnia in a patient receiving supplemental oxygen
  • Assuming a normal PaCO₂ is reassuring in severe asthma or metabolic acidosis
  • Waiting for ABG or imaging before treating obvious life-threatening physiology
  • Failing to recognize decreasing respiratory effort as exhaustion
  • Treating all hypoxemia as pneumonia
  • Assuming diffuse B-lines prove cardiogenic edema
  • Ignoring pulmonary embolism because the chest radiograph is abnormal
  • Using uncertain FiO₂ to overinterpret a P/F ratio
  • Ignoring pulse-oximeter limitations in shock or darker skin pigmentation
  • Withholding oxygen from a severely hypoxemic COPD patient
  • Targeting 100% saturation without a specific reason
  • Using HFNC for severe hypercapnic acidosis when NIV is indicated
  • Using NIV in a patient who cannot protect the airway
  • Continuing NIV despite worsening pH, work, or mental status
  • Allowing HFNC or NIV to delay intubation
  • Intubating severe metabolic acidosis without matching pre-intubation minute ventilation
  • Intubating massive PE or RV failure without preparing for hemodynamic collapse
  • Using actual body weight for ventilator tidal volume
  • Failing to provide immediate post-intubation analgesia and sedation
  • Missing tension pneumothorax or auto-PEEP after sudden post-intubation hypotension
  • Failing to reassess the patient after changing respiratory support

Board and Bedside Pearls

  • Acute respiratory failure may reflect oxygenation, ventilation, mechanics, airway protection, or several processes simultaneously.
  • Oxygen saturation does not assess ventilation.
  • PaCO₂ is inversely related to alveolar ventilation.
  • Most arterial oxygen content is carried by hemoglobin rather than dissolved in plasma.
  • V/Q mismatch is the most common mechanism of hypoxemia.
  • Shunt physiology responds incompletely to increasing FiO₂.
  • Hypercapnia may result from reduced drive, increased load, muscle weakness, dead space, or increased CO₂ production.
  • A falling respiratory rate after prolonged distress may indicate fatigue.
  • Interpret every PaO₂ in relation to FiO₂ and respiratory support.
  • A VBG can assess pH in many patients but cannot evaluate arterial oxygenation.
  • The 2024 global ARDS definition includes selected nonintubated patients on HFNC or NIV.
  • The S/F ratio is less useful when SpO₂ is above 97%.
  • Pulse oximetry may miss occult hypoxemia and is unreliable in dyshemoglobinemia.
  • HFNC is favored for many patients with acute hypoxemic respiratory failure.
  • HFNC should not delay intubation when work of breathing or oxygen needs worsen.
  • Bilevel NIV is strongly supported for COPD exacerbation with acute respiratory acidosis.
  • CPAP or bilevel NIV is strongly supported for acute cardiogenic pulmonary edema.
  • NIV requires an alert patient with a protectable airway and close reassessment.
  • Improving pH and respiratory rate within one to two hours support NIV success in COPD.
  • A normalizing PaCO₂ in severe asthma may signal respiratory-muscle failure.
  • Neuromuscular respiratory failure may require intubation before hypercapnia appears.
  • Intubation of severe metabolic acidosis can cause arrest if minute ventilation falls.
  • Positive pressure can cause collapse in severe RV failure or hypovolemia.
  • Tidal volume is set using predicted body weight.
  • Sudden post-intubation deterioration requires evaluation for displacement, obstruction, pneumothorax, equipment failure, and hemodynamic effects.

Bottom Line

Acute respiratory failure is a physiologic syndrome rather than a diagnosis. Determine whether the dominant problem is hypoxemia, hypercapnia, excessive work of breathing, neuromuscular weakness, airway obstruction, impaired airway protection, or a mixed process. Treat immediately reversible threats while simultaneously identifying the cause.

Assess the whole patient rather than one saturation value. Respiratory rate, breathing pattern, accessory-muscle use, speech, mental status, hemodynamics, secretion burden, blood gases, and trajectory determine severity. A patient who becomes quieter after prolonged distress may be exhausting.

Use oxygen deliberately. Conventional devices are appropriate for mild disease, HFNC is preferred for many patients with acute hypoxemic failure, and NIV is particularly effective for COPD exacerbation with respiratory acidosis and acute cardiogenic pulmonary edema. Each noninvasive trial needs explicit response targets, frequent reassessment, and predetermined failure criteria.

Intubate when oxygenation, ventilation, airway protection, hemodynamics, or work of breathing cannot be safely sustained. Prepare for physiologic collapse in severe hypoxemia, shock, metabolic acidosis, right-ventricular failure, and obstructive lung disease. After intubation, confirm tube placement, provide immediate analgesia and sedation, use predicted-body-weight ventilation, and reassess for hypotension, auto-PEEP, pneumothorax, or equipment problems.

The safest approach is early recognition, mechanism-based support, treatment of the underlying cause, and escalation before the patient reaches respiratory or cardiac arrest.

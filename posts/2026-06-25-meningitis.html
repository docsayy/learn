Tuberculous Meningitis: A Clinical Reasoning Case

Added June 25, 2026 • Infectious Disease • ~18 min read

A middle-aged woman with diabetes and advanced chronic kidney disease presented with fever, cough, nausea, vomiting, and a new severe headache. Her case evolved from a broad fever-and-headache differential into disseminated tuberculosis with tuberculous meningitis. This article reviews fever frameworks, headache red flags, CSF interpretation, pulmonary miliary nodules, antibiotic failure, and high-yield CNS tuberculosis management.

Case Presentation

A 50-year-old woman presented with cough, nausea, vomiting, and fevers for one week. She initially developed a dry cough with subjective fevers and chills. She also reported nausea with multiple episodes of non-bloody, non-bilious vomiting, headache, decreased oral intake, and lightheadedness when getting out of bed.

Negative Symptoms

  • No abdominal pain
  • No dysuria
  • No diarrhea
  • No constipation
  • No recent sick contacts
The patient’s chief complaint included cough, but the key diagnostic clue was the combination of fever plus new severe headache.

Approach to Fever

A practical fever differential can be organized using the I-MADE framework.

Letter Category Examples
I Infection Pneumonia, meningitis, UTI, endocarditis, TB, fungal disease
M Malignancy Lymphoma, leukemia, solid tumors
A Autoimmune Vasculitis, lupus, adult-onset Still disease
D Drugs Drug fever, serotonin syndrome, NMS
E Endocrinopathy Thyroid storm, adrenal crisis
Infection is the most common cause of fever, but malignancy, autoimmune disease, medications, and endocrinopathy should remain in the background when the presentation does not fit a simple infection.

Headache Red Flags

The headache history was concerning because it was severe, new, associated with systemic symptoms, and accompanied by neck stiffness.

Headache Details

  • Severe bilateral throbbing headache
  • Photophobia
  • Phonophobia
  • Neck stiffness
  • Worse with upright posture
  • No visual changes
  • No prior similar headaches

SNOOP Red Flags

Feature Meaning Present in This Case?
Systemic symptoms Fever, weight loss, malignancy, immunosuppression Yes: fever
Neurologic signs Focal deficit, altered mental status, seizure Initially no, later worsened mental status
Onset Sudden onset or onset after age 50 Yes: new headache at age 50
Positional Worse upright or supine Yes: worse upright
Progressive Increasing severity or frequency Concern developed during admission
Papilledema / Pregnancy Elevated ICP or pregnancy-related causes Needed assessment
Fever plus new headache plus neck stiffness should trigger evaluation for meningitis, even if classic meningeal signs are absent on exam.

Past History and Medication Clues

Past Medical History

  • Type 2 diabetes mellitus with A1C 6.2%
  • Chronic kidney disease secondary to diabetic nephropathy
  • History of peripartum cardiomyopathy with improved ejection fraction

Medication List

  • Non-adherent to carvedilol, furosemide, and hydralazine
  • Taking sevelamer
  • Taking sodium bicarbonate
  • Taking insulin glargine
  • Taking citalopram
Sevelamer plus sodium bicarbonate plus avoidance of metformin/SGLT2 inhibitors suggests advanced chronic kidney disease.

Diabetes Pearl

An A1C of 6.2% is generally considered controlled for most non-pregnant adults. For many patients, the usual goal is less than 7%, while older patients or those with significant comorbidities may have a more lenient goal.

A good problem representation distills the case into the features that activate the right illness scripts.

A middle-aged woman with type 2 diabetes and advanced CKD presenting with acute fever, new severe headache, vomiting, and mild cough.

Although cough was part of the chief complaint, the key diagnostic syndrome was fever plus new headache in a patient with mild immunocompromising comorbidities.

Relative Bradycardia / Pulse-Temperature Dissociation

With fever, heart rate usually rises. A heart rate that is lower than expected for the degree of fever is called relative bradycardia or pulse-temperature dissociation.

High-Yield Associations

  • Legionella
  • Typhoid fever
  • Q fever
  • Some viral infections
  • Drug fever
A temperature of 39°C with heart rate only 90 bpm can be a subtle clue toward pulse-temperature dissociation.

Initial Labs

Important Findings

  • WBC 8.6
  • Hemoglobin 10.5
  • Platelets 216
  • Neutrophil predominance
  • Lactate 1.2
  • Metabolic acidosis with low bicarbonate
  • Elevated anion gap
  • Low albumin

Albumin as a Chronicity Clue

Albumin can behave as a negative acute phase reactant and may decrease during chronic inflammatory illness, malnutrition, liver disease, nephrotic syndrome, or prolonged infection.

A low albumin can suggest that the illness may be more chronic than the presenting symptoms initially imply.

Acid-Base Pearls

Anion Gap

Anion Gap = Na − Cl − HCO₃

The patient had a high anion gap metabolic acidosis. In advanced CKD, uremia is a common cause.

Delta-Delta

The delta-delta helps determine whether a high anion gap metabolic acidosis is isolated or mixed with a non-anion gap metabolic acidosis or metabolic alkalosis.

Delta-Delta = (Measured AG − Normal AG) / (Normal HCO₃ − Measured HCO₃)
Ratio Interpretation
<0.8 High anion gap metabolic acidosis + non-anion gap metabolic acidosis
0.8–2 Pure high anion gap metabolic acidosis
>2 High anion gap metabolic acidosis + metabolic alkalosis

Chest Imaging

Chest radiography showed multifocal opacities. CT chest further demonstrated diffuse innumerable randomly distributed pulmonary nodules with a left upper lobe consolidation abutting the pleura, pancreatic atrophy and calcifications compatible with chronic pancreatitis, a right hilar calcified lymph node, and splenic calcified granuloma.

Important Imaging Clues

  • Diffuse innumerable pulmonary nodules
  • Random distribution
  • Left upper lobe consolidation
  • Calcified hilar lymph node
  • Splenic calcified granuloma
Diffuse innumerable randomly distributed pulmonary nodules should immediately trigger the concept of a miliary pattern.

Miliary Pulmonary Nodules

A miliary pattern refers to diffuse tiny nodules spread throughout the lungs, often from hematogenous dissemination.

Major Causes

The CSF was inflammatory with elevated white blood cells and elevated protein. The glucose was borderline low and the differential was lymphocyte-predominant.

This pattern can resemble viral meningitis, but TB and fungal meningitis remain important considerations, especially with miliary pulmonary nodules.

Empiric Meningitis Treatment

Because bacterial meningitis cannot be missed, empiric therapy is often started while diagnostic testing is pending.

Typical Empiric Coverage in Adults Older Than 50

  • Vancomycin
  • Ceftriaxone
  • Ampicillin for Listeria coverage
  • Acyclovir if HSV encephalitis is possible
In suspected bacterial meningitis, do not delay antibiotics while waiting for lumbar puncture if LP is delayed.

Steroid Pearl

Dexamethasone is often recommended when bacterial meningitis is suspected, especially pneumococcal meningitis, and steroids are also important in tuberculous meningitis.

Clinical Worsening

After admission, the patient had episodes of transient loss of consciousness when rising from a sitting position. EEG showed diffuse slowing. MRI brain showed a focus of increased FLAIR signal in the inferior left frontal lobe near the posterior left insular cortex.

Antibiotic Failure Framework

When a patient worsens despite antibiotics, use a simple framework.

Cause Meaning
Wrong Bug The true organism is not covered by the current regimen
Wrong Drug The organism is known but the selected therapy is ineffective or has poor penetration
No Source Control Abscess, obstruction, infected device, or undrained focus remains
Not an Infection Autoimmune, malignancy, drug fever, inflammatory disease
Natural Disease Course Expected progression despite early therapy
The most common antibiotic failure questions are: wrong bug, wrong drug, or source control problem.

Final Diagnosis

The diagnosis was disseminated tuberculosis with tuberculous meningitis.

Diagnostic Clues

  • Patient born in El Salvador
  • Fever and new headache
  • Lymphocytic-predominant CSF early in course
  • Borderline-low CSF glucose
  • Elevated CSF protein
  • Diffuse pulmonary nodules with miliary pattern
  • Progressive mental status changes
  • Elevated opening pressure on repeat LP
  • CSF MTB PCR positive
  • CSF culture later grew MTB complex
  • BAL eventually supported disseminated TB
The combination of miliary pulmonary nodules and lymphocytic meningitis should strongly suggest disseminated TB until proven otherwise.

CNS Tuberculosis

Central nervous system tuberculosis includes tuberculous meningitis, intracranial tuberculoma, and spinal tuberculous arachnoiditis.

High-Yield Features

  • Occurs in a minority of patients with TB but carries high morbidity
  • Often associated with inflammation at the base of the brain
  • Can cause cranial neuropathies
  • Can cause stroke from vasculitis
  • Can cause hydrocephalus and elevated intracranial pressure
  • May require external ventricular drain if hydrocephalus develops

Complications

Complication Clinical Significance
Hydrocephalus Common and may require CSF diversion
Stroke Can occur due to vasculitis or basal inflammation
Tuberculoma Mass-like inflammatory lesion
Elevated intracranial pressure Can cause lethargy, vomiting, papilledema, or herniation risk
Drug toxicity TB regimens require close monitoring

Treatment Pearls

Standard TB Treatment Concept

RIPE = Rifampin + Isoniazid + Pyrazinamide + Ethambutol

For pulmonary TB, the classic framework is four drugs for two months, then two drugs for four months. CNS tuberculosis generally requires a longer continuation phase.

CNS TB Treatment Principles

  • Start multi-drug anti-tuberculous therapy promptly.
  • Use pyridoxine with isoniazid to reduce neuropathy risk.
  • Use adjunctive corticosteroids such as dexamethasone or prednisone.
  • Monitor for hydrocephalus and elevated intracranial pressure.
  • Evaluate for drug resistance and adjust regimen based on susceptibility testing.
  • Monitor liver enzymes, renal function, vision symptoms, and drug interactions.

HIV Pearl

If HIV is present, do not start antiretroviral therapy before TB treatment in suspected CNS TB because of concern for immune reconstitution inflammatory syndrome.

Cryptococcal meningitis is another important condition where ART timing must be handled carefully.

Common Pitfalls

  • Anchoring on cough when the key syndrome is fever plus new headache.
  • Ruling out meningitis because the neck is supple.
  • Forgetting that Kernig and Brudzinski signs have poor sensitivity.
  • Assuming lymphocytic CSF always means viral meningitis.
  • Missing TB or fungal meningitis when CSF glucose is low or borderline low.
  • Ignoring miliary pulmonary nodules on imaging.
  • Not asking about birth country, travel, incarceration, homelessness, or TB exposure risk.
  • Not measuring opening pressure when chronic meningitis is suspected.
  • Continuing the same antibiotics despite clinical worsening without reassessing the diagnosis.
  • Forgetting steroids in suspected TB meningitis.

Clinical Pearls

  • Use I-MADE for fever: infection, malignancy, autoimmune, drugs, endocrinopathy.
  • Fever plus headache plus neck stiffness should trigger meningitis evaluation.
  • New headache after age 50 is a red flag.
  • A supple neck does not rule out meningitis.
  • Brudzinski and Kernig signs are specific when present but poorly sensitive.
  • Relative bradycardia can be seen with Legionella, typhoid fever, Q fever, and drug fever.
  • Low albumin can suggest chronic inflammation or chronic illness.
  • Diffuse innumerable randomly distributed pulmonary nodules suggest a miliary pattern.
  • Miliary nodules should trigger consideration of TB, endemic fungi, and malignancy.
  • TB meningitis often has lymphocytic CSF, high protein, and low or borderline-low glucose.
  • Cryptococcal meningitis classically causes very high opening pressure.
  • Elevated protein with normal cells is albuminocytologic dissociation.
  • Antibiotic failure usually means wrong bug, wrong drug, or source control problem.
  • CNS TB can cause hydrocephalus, stroke, tuberculoma, and elevated ICP.
  • Treat TB meningitis with multi-drug TB therapy plus adjunctive steroids.

Bottom Line

This case began with cough, fever, vomiting, and headache, but the key syndrome was fever plus new severe headache in a patient with diabetes and advanced CKD. Headache red flags, abnormal CSF, and diffuse miliary pulmonary nodules shifted the differential away from simple viral illness and toward chronic meningitis from TB or fungal disease. The final diagnosis was disseminated tuberculosis with tuberculous meningitis. In similar cases, do not be reassured by a supple neck, do not assume lymphocytic CSF is always viral, and always connect pulmonary miliary nodules with CNS symptoms. Worsening despite empiric antibiotics should prompt reassessment for wrong bug, wrong drug, or source control failure.

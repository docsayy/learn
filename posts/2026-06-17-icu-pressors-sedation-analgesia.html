ICU Pressors, Sedation, Analgesia, and Paralysis: The Complete Resident Guide

Added June 17, 2026 • Critical Care • ~16 min read

A practical ICU guide for residents covering vasopressors, inotropes, sedatives, analgesics, RASS targets, and paralytics. Focuses on bedside medication selection, common doses, side effects, and high-yield ICU decision-making.

Overview

Pressors, sedation, analgesia, and paralysis are core ICU medications. A practical way to learn them is to group them by what you are trying to accomplish: raise blood pressure, improve cardiac output, sedate the patient, treat pain, or improve ventilator synchrony.

Simple ICU starting framework: for many newly intubated ICU patients, the common initial medication trio is norepinephrine for shock, propofol for sedation, and fentanyl for analgesia.

Common ICU Pressors

The most common pressors and vasoactive agents used in the ICU include norepinephrine, vasopressin, phenylephrine, epinephrine, dopamine, dobutamine, and milrinone. Less commonly used agents include angiotensin II and methylene blue.

Medication Common Role Key Concept
Norepinephrine First-line pressor for most shock Strong alpha with some beta activity
Vasopressin Second-line adjunct V1 receptor-mediated vasoconstriction
Phenylephrine Pure vasoconstrictor Pure alpha agonist
Epinephrine Shock with bradycardia or need for inotropy Beta effects first, alpha at higher doses
Dopamine Less favored due to arrhythmias Dose-dependent beta and alpha effects
Dobutamine Inotrope Beta-1 and beta-2 agonist
Milrinone Inodilator Phosphodiesterase inhibitor

Pressor Mechanisms

Understanding receptor activity helps you predict what each medication will do at the bedside.

  • Alpha stimulation: vasoconstriction and increased systemic vascular resistance.
  • Beta-1 stimulation: increased heart rate and contractility.
  • Beta-2 stimulation: some vasodilation and bronchodilation.
  • V1 stimulation: vasoconstriction through vasopressin receptors.
Why norepinephrine works so well: it gives strong alpha-mediated vasoconstriction while still providing some beta support for cardiac output.

Norepinephrine

Norepinephrine, also called Levophed, is the first-line vasopressor for most forms of shock, including septic shock and many cases of cardiogenic shock when blood pressure support is needed.

Common Dose

0.01–0.5 mcg/kg/min

As norepinephrine requirements rise, the incremental blood pressure response often becomes smaller. At higher doses, the risk of peripheral and digital ischemia increases.

Practical Bedside Points

  • Consider central access when doses rise, especially around or above 0.1 mcg/kg/min.
  • Peripheral norepinephrine may be used temporarily in some settings, but extravasation can cause tissue injury.
  • Consider holding tube feeds when pressor needs are rising because mesenteric perfusion may be compromised.
Important: escalating pressor requirement should prompt reassessment of shock type, source control, volume status, cardiac function, bleeding, obstruction, and medication strategy.

Vasopressin

Vasopressin is commonly added after norepinephrine when shock remains persistent. It acts on V1 receptors in blood vessels and causes vasoconstriction through a mechanism different from catecholamines.

Common Dose

0.03–0.04 units/min

Vasopressin is usually not titrated. Higher titrated doses have been associated with ischemic complications, including mesenteric ischemia.

Advantages

  • Less dependent on pH than catecholamine pressors.
  • May remain effective in severe acidemia.
  • Often has a norepinephrine-sparing effect.

Phenylephrine and Epinephrine

Phenylephrine

Phenylephrine is a pure alpha agonist. It increases systemic vascular resistance but can also increase afterload and cause reflex bradycardia.

  • Can be useful when tachyarrhythmia limits other pressors.
  • Avoid or use caution in bradycardic patients.
  • Avoid in obstructive shock when increased afterload may worsen hemodynamics.

Epinephrine

Epinephrine provides beta activity at lower doses and more alpha activity at higher doses. It can increase heart rate, contractility, and vascular tone.

  • Useful when shock is associated with bradycardia.
  • Can support both cardiac output and vascular tone.
  • May increase lactate and arrhythmia risk.

Inotropes: Dopamine, Dobutamine, and Milrinone

Inotropes are used when the main problem is inadequate cardiac output rather than isolated vasodilation. They are especially relevant in cardiogenic shock or mixed shock states.

Agent Best Use Major Limitation
Dopamine Low SVR with need for beta and alpha effect More arrhythmias
Dobutamine Low cardiac output with normal SVR Arrhythmogenic; can lower BP
Milrinone Low cardiac output with high SVR Renally cleared; can cause hypotension

Dobutamine has a shorter half-life and faster onset than milrinone, but it can be more arrhythmogenic. Milrinone has more vasodilatory effect and should be used cautiously or avoided in significant renal failure because it is renally cleared.

Practical selection: low SVR may favor dopamine or norepinephrine-based support, normal SVR may favor dobutamine, and high SVR with poor cardiac output may favor milrinone if renal function allows.

Stress-Dose Steroids in Refractory Shock

When a patient is requiring multiple pressors, stress-dose steroids are often considered. This is commonly used in refractory septic shock or vasopressor-dependent shock.

Common Regimens

  • Hydrocortisone 50 mg IV every 6 hours
  • Hydrocortisone 100 mg IV every 8 hours
  • Some protocols add fludrocortisone

Steroids may shorten the duration of shock in some patients, although mortality benefit is less clear.

ICU Sedation

Common ICU sedatives include propofol, dexmedetomidine, midazolam, and ketamine. The goal is not simply to make the patient unconscious, but to target the right depth of sedation for safety, comfort, ventilator synchrony, and liberation from mechanical ventilation.

Sedative Common Dose Main Side Effect
Propofol 5–50 mcg/kg/min; sometimes higher Hypotension
Dexmedetomidine 0.2–1.4 mcg/kg/hr Bradycardia
Midazolam 2–20 mg/hr ICU delirium and accumulation
Ketamine Often 8–25 mg/hr infusion Emergence reactions; increased HR/BP

Propofol

Propofol is commonly first-line for ICU sedation because it has rapid onset, rapid offset, and is easy to titrate. When stopped, patients often begin waking quickly compared with benzodiazepines.

Important Side Effects

  • Hypotension
  • Hypertriglyceridemia
  • Propofol infusion syndrome
  • Green urine, which can occur and is usually benign
Propofol infusion syndrome: watch for metabolic acidosis, rhabdomyolysis, bradycardia, cardiovascular collapse, and rising triglycerides. Many ICUs monitor triglycerides every few days while patients are on prolonged propofol.

Dexmedetomidine

Dexmedetomidine, also called Precedex, is useful for lighter sedation, agitation, and ICU delirium. It causes less respiratory depression than many other sedatives, so patients can sometimes remain on it during extubation or even after extubation.

Advantages

  • Less respiratory depression
  • Useful for agitation and delirium
  • Can be continued while weaning from the ventilator

Limitation

Dexmedetomidine is often weaker than propofol when deep sedation is required and may cause bradycardia.

Midazolam and Ketamine

Midazolam

Midazolam is generally avoided when possible because it is associated with ICU delirium, tolerance, and prolonged accumulation, especially after several days of use. It may be useful when blood pressure neutrality is important.

  • Patients can develop tolerance after 48–72 hours.
  • It accumulates in fat stores.
  • Wake-up can be delayed for days after prolonged infusion.

Ketamine

Ketamine provides both sedation and analgesia. It can increase heart rate and blood pressure transiently, and it may cause emergence reactions such as hallucinations.

  • Useful as an adjunct sedative and analgesic.
  • May preserve respiratory drive better than some alternatives.
  • Can be considered during ventilator weaning in selected patients.

RASS Scale

Sedation is commonly titrated using the Richmond Agitation-Sedation Scale, or RASS. The scale ranges from +4, which is combative, to -5, which is unarousable.

RASS Range Meaning Clinical Use
+1 to +4 Agitated to combative May need more sedation or pain control
0 Alert and calm Often ideal if clinically appropriate
-1 to -2 Drowsy to light sedation Common ICU sedation target
-3 to -4 Moderate to deep sedation May be used for severe ventilator dyssynchrony or paralysis
-5 Unarousable Deepest sedation
Common target: many ventilated patients are targeted to RASS 0 to -1 or 0 to -2. Paralyzed patients usually require deeper sedation before neuromuscular blockade.

Analgesia

Pain control is a core part of ICU care. Common analgesic drips include fentanyl, hydromorphone, and morphine.

Analgesic Typical Use Key Point
Fentanyl First-line ICU analgesia Relatively neutral blood pressure effect
Hydromorphone Opioid-tolerant or resistant patients Hepatically cleared
Morphine Air hunger, comfort care, end of life Renally cleared; can worsen hypotension

Fentanyl

Fentanyl is commonly first-line because it is potent, titratable, and tends to have less hypotension compared with morphine or hydromorphone.

Typical infusion: 25–200 mcg/hr

Hydromorphone

Hydromorphone can be useful when patients are opioid tolerant or when fentanyl is not providing adequate analgesia.

Morphine

Morphine has anxiolytic properties and is particularly useful for air hunger in comfort-focused care. Use caution in acute kidney injury because morphine metabolites are renally cleared.

Do not forget the bowel regimen: opioid-induced constipation is common in ICU patients. Severe cases may require agents such as methylnaltrexone.

Paralysis in the ICU

Neuromuscular blockade can improve ventilator synchrony in selected patients, especially those with severe ventilator dyssynchrony or severe ARDS physiology. Paralysis allows the ventilator to fully control breathing and may reduce high airway pressures caused by patient-ventilator fighting.

Critical rule: never paralyze an inadequately sedated patient. Ensure deep sedation before and during neuromuscular blockade.

Monitoring Paralysis

  • Train-of-four: peripheral nerve stimulation used to estimate depth of paralysis.
  • Typical goal: often around 2 twitches, suggesting significant neuromuscular blockade.
  • Bispectral index: may be used in some hospitals to estimate depth of sedation.

Common Paralytics

Medication Common Use Typical Dose Mentioned
Cisatracurium Common ICU paralytic infusion 0.15–0.2 mg/kg/hr
Rocuronium Shorter-acting push ~0.6 mg/kg push
Vecuronium Alternative paralytic Institution dependent

Major Risk

The major downside of prolonged paralysis is ICU-acquired weakness and myopathy. Recovery can be prolonged, especially in patients who are critically ill for extended periods.

Evidence pearl: paralysis has been studied in severe ARDS with mixed mortality findings. It remains useful for ventilator synchrony, but should be used thoughtfully and reassessed frequently.

Practical Summary

A simple way to remember the basic ICU medication structure is to divide therapies by problem:

  • Low blood pressure: norepinephrine first, then add vasopressin.
  • Low cardiac output: consider inotropes such as dobutamine or milrinone depending on SVR and renal function.
  • Need sedation: propofol first-line for many patients; dexmedetomidine for lighter sedation or delirium.
  • Need pain control: fentanyl first-line, hydromorphone for opioid tolerance, morphine for air hunger or comfort care.
  • Ventilator dyssynchrony despite sedation: consider paralysis only after adequate sedation.
High-yield ICU starter set: norepinephrine, propofol, and fentanyl are common first-line medications for a newly intubated, unstable ICU patient.

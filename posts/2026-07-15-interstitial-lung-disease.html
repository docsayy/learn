Interstitial Lung Disease: Diagnostic Framework and Acute Deterioration

Added July 15, 2026 • Pulmonary and Critical Care • ~30 min read

A structured approach to ILD patterns, exposure and autoimmune history, HRCT interpretation, PFTs, serology, multidisciplinary diagnosis, IPF and progressive pulmonary fibrosis, antifibrotic therapy, immunosuppression, oxygen, transplant referral, and acute exacerbation.

Clinical Overview

Interstitial lung disease includes many disorders affecting alveoli, interstitium, small airways, and pulmonary vessels. The diagnosis is built from clinical context, HRCT pattern, serology, exposure history, and multidisciplinary review—not from one antibody or imaging phrase.

High-Yield History

  • Bird, mold, farming, hot-tub, and occupational exposure
  • Medication and radiation history
  • Connective-tissue symptoms: Raynaud, rash, arthritis, myositis, sicca
  • Smoking and vaping
  • Family history of fibrosis or premature graying
  • Tempo of symptoms and oxygen requirement

HRCT Pattern Recognition

PatternTypical Clues
UIPBasal subpleural reticulation, traction bronchiectasis, honeycombing
NSIPGround glass and fine reticulation, often with subpleural sparing
Organizing pneumoniaPeripheral or peribronchovascular consolidation
Hypersensitivity pneumonitisMosaic attenuation, air trapping, centrilobular nodules, fibrosis
SarcoidosisPerilymphatic nodules and hilar/mediastinal adenopathy

Diagnostic Testing

  • Full PFTs with DLCO
  • Rest and exertional oximetry
  • Autoimmune serologies guided by phenotype
  • HRCT with expiratory images when air trapping is relevant
  • Bronchoscopy or biopsy selectively
  • Multidisciplinary discussion with pulmonology, radiology, pathology, and rheumatology

Idiopathic Pulmonary Fibrosis

  • Occurs mainly in older adults with a UIP pattern and no alternative cause.
  • Antifibrotics slow decline but do not reverse fibrosis.
  • Avoid chronic prednisone-based immunosuppression for IPF.
  • Refer early for transplant when appropriate.

Progressive Pulmonary Fibrosis

Non-IPF fibrotic ILD can develop progressive symptoms, physiologic decline, or radiologic worsening despite management. Reassess antigen removal, inflammatory treatment, antifibrotic therapy, comorbidity, and transplant candidacy.

Connective-Tissue Disease ILD

  • Treatment depends on inflammatory activity, fibrotic burden, and underlying disease.
  • Immunomodulatory agents may include mycophenolate, rituximab, cyclophosphamide, or others in selected conditions.
  • Coordinate with rheumatology and monitor infection and drug toxicity.

Acute Deterioration

  • Infection
  • Pulmonary edema
  • PE
  • Pneumothorax
  • Diffuse alveolar hemorrhage
  • Drug toxicity
  • Acute exacerbation of ILD

Acute exacerbation is a diagnosis of exclusion with new bilateral ground-glass opacity or consolidation on chronic fibrotic lung disease. Prognosis is poor, especially when invasive ventilation is required.

Supportive Care

  • Supplemental oxygen and ambulatory assessment
  • Pulmonary rehabilitation
  • Vaccination
  • Reflux and sleep-disordered-breathing assessment when relevant
  • Palliative symptom management
  • Early goals-of-care and transplant discussions

Common Pitfalls

  • Calling every reticular CT pattern IPF
  • Ignoring exposures
  • Ordering broad serology without clinical interpretation
  • Treating IPF with chronic steroids
  • Delaying transplant referral
  • Missing PE or infection during acute worsening

Bottom Line

Classify ILD by clinical context and HRCT pattern through multidisciplinary review. Separate inflammatory disease that may respond to immunomodulation from fibrotic progression, and plan oxygen, rehabilitation, antifibrotic therapy, transplant, and goals of care early.

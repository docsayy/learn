Pneumonia: The Complete Advanced Resident Guide

Added July 13, 2026 • Infectious Disease • ~38 min read

Pneumonia is one of the most common infectious diseases encountered in the emergency department, hospital wards, intensive care unit, and outpatient clinic. This guide reviews community-acquired pneumonia, hospital-acquired pneumonia, ventilator-associated pneumonia, aspiration syndromes, diagnostic testing, severity assessment, antimicrobial selection, resistant-pathogen risk, pleural complications, immunocompromised hosts, nonresolving pneumonia, and high-yield ABIM and infectious disease consult pearls.

Clinical Overview

Pneumonia is an acute infection of the pulmonary parenchyma. The diagnosis requires a compatible clinical syndrome together with radiographic or ultrasonographic evidence of a new pulmonary infiltrate.

Compatible Respiratory Syndrome + New Pulmonary Infiltrate = Pneumonia

A cough and fever without an infiltrate may represent bronchitis, influenza, COVID-19, COPD exacerbation, or another respiratory illness. An infiltrate without infectious symptoms may represent atelectasis, pulmonary edema, hemorrhage, malignancy, infarction, or inflammatory lung disease.

The Major Questions in Pneumonia

  1. Does the patient truly have pneumonia?
  2. Is the infection community acquired, hospital acquired, or ventilator associated?
  3. How severe is the illness?
  4. Does the patient require MRSA or antipseudomonal coverage?
  5. Is there a viral diagnosis or atypical pathogen?
  6. Is there a complication requiring drainage or another procedure?
  7. When can antibiotics be narrowed, changed to oral therapy, or stopped?
Pneumonia management begins with syndrome confirmation and severity assessment, not with choosing the broadest possible antibiotic.

Major Causes of Poor Outcome

  • Delayed recognition of severe pneumonia
  • Inadequate initial antimicrobial therapy in septic shock
  • Failure to recognize resistant-pathogen risk
  • Empyema or lung abscess without source control
  • Delayed recognition of influenza or other treatable viral infection
  • Incorrect diagnosis, such as pulmonary edema or malignancy
  • Immunocompromised host with an opportunistic infection
  • Failure to reassess a nonresponding patient

Definitions and Classification

Category Definition Clinical Importance
Community-acquired pneumonia Pneumonia acquired outside the hospital or recognized early after admission Usually caused by community respiratory pathogens
Hospital-acquired pneumonia Pneumonia developing at least approximately 48 hours after admission and not incubating at admission Greater risk for resistant gram-negative organisms and MRSA
Ventilator-associated pneumonia Pneumonia developing more than approximately 48 hours after endotracheal intubation Requires attention to local ICU resistance patterns and respiratory cultures
Aspiration pneumonitis Chemical lung injury after aspiration of sterile gastric contents Supportive treatment; antibiotics are not automatically required
Aspiration pneumonia Infectious pneumonia after aspiration of colonized oropharyngeal or gastric material Requires antibiotics targeting likely respiratory flora
Necrotizing pneumonia Pulmonary infection causing parenchymal necrosis and multiple small cavities Often severe and may require prolonged treatment
Lung abscess Localized cavitary infection containing pus and necrotic tissue Requires prolonged therapy and assessment for obstruction or aspiration
Parapneumonic effusion Pleural effusion associated with pneumonia May require thoracentesis or drainage
Empyema Frank pus or infected fluid in the pleural space Requires drainage and antibiotics

Healthcare-Associated Pneumonia

“Healthcare-associated pneumonia,” or HCAP, is no longer considered a useful independent pneumonia category.

Residence in a nursing facility, recent hospitalization, dialysis, or other healthcare contact does not automatically require vancomycin and two antipseudomonal antibiotics. Resistant-pathogen coverage should be based on patient-specific risk factors, prior cultures, recent parenteral antibiotic exposure, local epidemiology, and severity.

Pathophysiology

Routes of Infection

  • Microaspiration of colonized oropharyngeal secretions
  • Inhalation of infectious droplets or aerosols
  • Macroaspiration of gastric or oral contents
  • Hematogenous spread
  • Direct extension from adjacent infection

Host Defense Mechanisms

  • Cough reflex
  • Glottic closure
  • Mucociliary clearance
  • Alveolar macrophages
  • Humoral immunity
  • Cell-mediated immunity
  • Normal airway microbiome

Factors That Impair Defense

  • Altered mental status
  • Sedatives and opioids
  • Endotracheal intubation
  • Dysphagia
  • Stroke or neuromuscular disease
  • Poor dentition
  • Smoking
  • COPD or structural lung disease
  • Neutropenia
  • Humoral or cellular immunodeficiency
  • Recent broad-spectrum antibiotics

Inflammatory Response

Infection triggers alveolar inflammation, capillary leak, and accumulation of neutrophils, protein, and fluid within the alveoli. This creates consolidation and impairs gas exchange.

Mechanisms of Hypoxemia

  • Ventilation-perfusion mismatch
  • Intrapulmonary shunt
  • Alveolar filling
  • Reduced ventilation
  • Acute respiratory distress syndrome

Common Organisms

Community-Acquired Pneumonia

Group Common Pathogens

Atypical bacteria Mycoplasma pneumoniae, Chlamydia pneumoniae, Legionella species
Respiratory viruses Influenza, SARS-CoV-2, RSV, human metapneumovirus, parainfluenza, adenovirus, rhinovirus
Selected exposures Endemic fungi, Chlamydia psittaci, Coxiella burnetii, tularemia, plague

Hospital-Acquired and Ventilator-Associated Pneumonia

  • Pseudomonas aeruginosa
  • Enterobacterales
  • ESBL-producing organisms
  • AmpC-producing organisms
  • Acinetobacter species
  • Stenotrophomonas maltophilia
  • MRSA
  • MSSA

Immunocompromised Hosts

  • Pneumocystis jirovecii
  • Aspergillus and other molds
  • Cryptococcus
  • Endemic fungi
  • CMV
  • Nocardia
  • Mycobacteria
  • Strongyloides hyperinfection
  • Routine bacterial and viral pathogens
Immunocompromised patients still develop ordinary bacterial and viral pneumonia. Do not focus exclusively on opportunistic organisms.

Risk Factors

Risk Factors for Pneumonia

  • Advanced age
  • Smoking
  • Alcohol use disorder
  • COPD
  • Heart failure
  • Diabetes
  • Chronic kidney or liver disease
  • Neurologic disease
  • Dysphagia
  • Immunosuppression
  • Poor oral hygiene
  • Recent viral respiratory infection
  • Recent hospitalization or antibiotics

Risk Factors for Aspiration

  • Stroke
  • Dementia
  • Seizure
  • Intoxication
  • Sedation
  • Neuromuscular disease
  • Esophageal disease
  • Vomiting
  • Tube feeding
  • Poor dentition
  • Impaired cough reflex

Risk Factors for MRSA Pneumonia

  • Previous respiratory isolation of MRSA
  • Recent MRSA infection or colonization
  • Recent hospitalization with parenteral antibiotics
  • Severe necrotizing or cavitary pneumonia
  • Post-influenza pneumonia
  • Hemodialysis or substantial healthcare exposure in the appropriate clinical setting
  • Local validated risk factors

Risk Factors for Pseudomonas

  • Previous respiratory isolation of Pseudomonas
  • Recent hospitalization with parenteral antibiotics
  • Bronchiectasis
  • Advanced structural lung disease
  • Repeated broad-spectrum antibiotic exposure
  • Tracheostomy or chronic ventilatory support
  • Local validated risk factors
The strongest predictors of resistant pneumonia pathogens are previous patient-specific respiratory cultures and recent parenteral antibiotic exposure.

Clinical Presentation

Typical Symptoms

  • Cough
  • Purulent or rust-colored sputum
  • Fever or chills
  • Dyspnea
  • Pleuritic chest pain
  • Fatigue
  • Myalgias
  • Confusion in older adults

Physical Examination Findings

  • Tachypnea
  • Hypoxemia
  • Fever or hypothermia
  • Crackles
  • Bronchial breath sounds
  • Egophony
  • Dullness to percussion
  • Increased tactile fremitus
  • Reduced breath sounds over an effusion

Atypical Presentations

Older, frail, or immunocompromised patients may present with:

  • Delirium
  • Weakness
  • Falls
  • Poor oral intake
  • Hypothermia
  • Minimal leukocytosis
  • Worsening functional status
The absence of fever or leukocytosis does not exclude pneumonia in older or immunocompromised patients.

Initial Approach

Step 1: Stabilize the Patient

  • Assess airway and work of breathing.
  • Measure oxygen saturation.
  • Provide supplemental oxygen.
  • Assess for shock and organ dysfunction.
  • Begin sepsis management when indicated.
  • Consider high-flow oxygen, noninvasive ventilation, or intubation.

Step 2: Confirm the Syndrome

Look for:

  • New respiratory symptoms
  • Systemic signs of infection
  • New infiltrate on chest imaging or lung ultrasound
  • Alternative diagnoses

Step 3: Determine the Pneumonia Category

  • Community acquired
  • Hospital acquired
  • Ventilator associated
  • Aspiration related
  • Immunocompromised-host pneumonia

Step 4: Assess Severity

  • PSI or CURB-65
  • ATS/IDSA severe CAP criteria
  • Oxygen requirement
  • Hemodynamics
  • Mental status
  • Organ dysfunction

Step 5: Choose Diagnostic Tests

Diagnostic testing should become more extensive as disease severity, resistant-pathogen risk, or immune compromise increases.

Step 6: Start Appropriate Therapy

  • Do not delay antibiotics in severe bacterial pneumonia.
  • Obtain cultures first when this does not cause dangerous delay.
  • Include antiviral therapy when indicated.
  • Plan early reassessment and de-escalation.

Imaging

Chest Radiograph

Chest radiography remains the most common initial imaging test.

Potential Findings

  • Lobar consolidation
  • Patchy bronchopneumonia
  • Interstitial infiltrates
  • Multilobar disease
  • Pleural effusion
  • Cavitation

Lung Ultrasound

Lung ultrasound is an acceptable alternative to chest radiography in centers with appropriate expertise.

Potential Findings

  • Subpleural consolidation
  • Dynamic air bronchograms
  • Focal B-lines
  • Pleural line abnormalities
  • Pleural effusion

Chest CT

CT is more sensitive than chest radiography but should not be routine for every uncomplicated case.

Consider CT When:

  • Chest radiograph is negative but clinical suspicion remains high.
  • Pneumonia is not improving.
  • Cavitation or abscess is suspected.
  • Empyema or loculated effusion is suspected.
  • Malignancy or airway obstruction is possible.
  • Pulmonary embolism is in the differential.
  • The patient is immunocompromised.
  • An atypical or fungal infection is suspected.

Imaging Patterns Are Not Organism-Specific

Lobar consolidation, bilateral ground-glass opacity, cavitation, and interstitial infiltrates can each have multiple infectious and noninfectious causes. Imaging should guide the differential, not identify the organism by itself.

Diagnostic Testing

Routine Laboratory Studies

Test Purpose
CBC Leukocytosis, leukopenia, thrombocytopenia, anemia
BMP/CMP Renal function, sodium, hepatic function, dosing
Lactate Assess tissue hypoperfusion in sepsis
ABG Severe hypoxemia, hypercapnia, or ventilatory failure
Viral PCR Influenza, SARS-CoV-2, RSV, or broader respiratory viral testing
HIV testing When risk factors or an opportunistic pattern exists

When More Extensive Testing Is Appropriate

  • Severe CAP
  • ICU admission
  • MRSA or Pseudomonas risk
  • Immunocompromised host
  • Treatment failure
  • Cavitary disease
  • Outbreak or epidemiologic concern
  • Unusual exposure or travel

Cultures and Microbiology

Blood Cultures in CAP

Blood cultures are not required for every mild CAP patient.

Obtain Blood Cultures When:

  • Severe CAP
  • Empiric MRSA coverage is started
  • Empiric antipseudomonal therapy is started
  • Previous MRSA or Pseudomonas infection exists
  • Recent hospitalization with parenteral antibiotics
  • Sepsis or shock
  • Immunocompromised host
  • Parapneumonic effusion or empyema is suspected

Sputum Cultures

Consider Sputum Culture When:

  • Severe CAP
  • Intubation
  • MRSA or Pseudomonas risk
  • Recent hospitalization with parenteral antibiotics
  • Treatment failure
  • Cavitary disease
  • Immunocompromised host

Assess Specimen Quality

  • Lower respiratory material rather than saliva
  • Abundant inflammatory cells
  • Limited squamous epithelial contamination
  • Collection before antibiotics when feasible

Endotracheal Aspirate

For suspected VAP, noninvasive endotracheal sampling with semiquantitative culture is generally preferred over routine invasive quantitative bronchoscopy.

Legionella Urinary Antigen

Consider When:

  • Severe CAP
  • Epidemiologic exposure
  • Outbreak concern
  • Recent travel or hotel exposure
  • Failure of standard beta-lactam treatment
The standard Legionella urinary antigen primarily detects Legionella pneumophila serogroup 1 and does not exclude other Legionella species or serogroups.

Pneumococcal Urinary Antigen

Consider in severe CAP. A positive result may support narrowing in an appropriate clinical setting.

Bronchoscopy

Consider bronchoscopy when:

  • The patient is immunocompromised.
  • Noninvasive tests are nondiagnostic.
  • An opportunistic infection is suspected.
  • Pneumonia is not responding.
  • Airway obstruction or malignancy is possible.
  • Diffuse alveolar hemorrhage is in the differential.

Biomarkers

Procalcitonin

Procalcitonin may support antibiotic discontinuation in selected improving patients but should not be used as the sole reason to withhold initial antibiotics in a patient with radiographically confirmed pneumonia and meaningful concern for bacterial infection.

Limitations

  • May be low early in infection
  • May be low in localized pneumonia
  • May be elevated after trauma, surgery, burns, or shock
  • May be elevated in renal dysfunction
  • Cannot reliably identify the organism

CRP

CRP may support inflammatory trend assessment but lacks specificity for bacterial pneumonia.

Beta-D-Glucan

May support a diagnosis of PCP or invasive fungal infection in the correct host but is nonspecific and can be falsely positive.

No biomarker replaces clinical assessment, imaging, microbiology, and response to treatment.

Severity Assessment

Pneumonia Severity Index

The Pneumonia Severity Index is generally preferred for determining hospitalization risk because it has stronger validation for mortality prediction.

CURB-65

Feature Point
Confusion 1
Urea / BUN elevation 1
Respiratory rate ≥30/min 1
Low blood pressure 1
Age ≥65 1
PSI and CURB-65 support decision-making but do not account fully for oxygen need, social support, ability to take oral medication, or rapid clinical deterioration.

ATS/IDSA Severe CAP Criteria

Major Criteria

  • Septic shock requiring vasopressors
  • Respiratory failure requiring mechanical ventilation

Minor Criteria

  • Respiratory rate ≥30/min
  • PaO₂/FiO₂ ratio ≤250
  • Multilobar infiltrates
  • Confusion or disorientation
  • BUN ≥20 mg/dL
  • Leukopenia
  • Thrombocytopenia
  • Hypothermia
  • Hypotension requiring aggressive fluid resuscitation
1 Major Criterion or ≥3 Minor Criteria = Severe CAP

Other ICU Warning Signs

  • Rapidly escalating oxygen requirement
  • High-flow nasal cannula requirement
  • Worsening hypercapnia
  • Persistent lactate elevation
  • Progressive mental-status change
  • Impending respiratory fatigue

Site of Care

Outpatient Treatment May Be Appropriate When:

  • Low mortality risk
  • No hypoxemia
  • No hemodynamic instability
  • Oral medication can be tolerated
  • Reliable follow-up exists
  • Safe home environment

Hospitalization Should Be Considered When:

  • Hypoxemia
  • Hemodynamic instability
  • Significant comorbidity
  • Altered mental status
  • Unable to take oral medication
  • Failure of outpatient therapy
  • Multilobar disease
  • Unsafe outpatient situation
  • High PSI or CURB-65 score

Direct ICU Admission

  • Septic shock requiring vasopressors
  • Mechanical ventilation
  • Rapidly worsening oxygenation
  • Three or more severe CAP minor criteria
  • Need for close respiratory monitoring

Outpatient CAP Treatment

Previously Healthy Without Major Comorbidities

Option Typical Adult Regimen
Amoxicillin 1 g orally three times daily
Doxycycline 100 mg orally twice daily
Macrolide Only when local pneumococcal resistance is sufficiently low

Comorbidities

Relevant comorbidities include chronic heart, lung, liver, or kidney disease; diabetes; alcohol use disorder; malignancy; or asplenia.

Combination Therapy

  • Amoxicillin-clavulanate plus azithromycin or doxycycline
  • Cefuroxime plus azithromycin or doxycycline
  • Cefpodoxime plus azithromycin or doxycycline

Monotherapy Alternative

  • Levofloxacin
  • Moxifloxacin
Respiratory fluoroquinolones are effective but should be used thoughtfully because of tendinopathy, neuropathy, dysglycemia, QT prolongation, C. difficile, and other adverse effects.

Antibiotic Selection Factors

  • Recent antibiotic use
  • Drug allergy
  • QT interval
  • Renal and hepatic function
  • Pregnancy
  • Drug interactions
  • Previous cultures
  • Local resistance

Inpatient CAP Treatment

Nonsevere Inpatient CAP

Preferred Strategy

Beta-Lactam + Atypical Coverage
Beta-Lactam Options Atypical Coverage
Ceftriaxone Azithromycin
Ampicillin-sulbactam Azithromycin
Cefotaxime Doxycycline when macrolide is unsuitable

Alternative Monotherapy

  • Levofloxacin
  • Moxifloxacin

Common Regimens

  • Ceftriaxone 1–2 g IV daily plus azithromycin 500 mg daily
  • Ampicillin-sulbactam 1.5–3 g IV every 6 hours plus azithromycin
  • Levofloxacin 750 mg IV or orally daily in selected patients

When Doxycycline Is Useful

  • QT prolongation
  • Macrolide intolerance
  • Important macrolide drug interaction
  • Need for reliable atypical coverage without fluoroquinolone
Adjust all antimicrobial doses for renal function, weight, severity, and local formulary recommendations.

Severe Community-Acquired Pneumonia

Recommended Regimen Structure

  • Beta-lactam plus macrolide
  • Beta-lactam plus respiratory fluoroquinolone
Respiratory fluoroquinolone monotherapy is not the preferred regimen for severe CAP.

Common Beta-Lactams

  • Ceftriaxone
  • Ampicillin-sulbactam
  • Cefotaxime
  • Ceftaroline

If Pseudomonas Risk Exists

  • Piperacillin-tazobactam
  • Cefepime
  • Ceftazidime
  • Meropenem
  • Imipenem-cilastatin
  • Aztreonam in selected severe beta-lactam allergy

Severe CAP Management Bundle

  • Blood cultures
  • High-quality respiratory culture
  • Viral PCR
  • Legionella urinary antigen
  • Pneumococcal urinary antigen
  • Assess pleural space
  • Early critical-care evaluation
  • MRSA/Pseudomonas risk assessment
  • Reassessment within 24–48 hours

MRSA and Pseudomonas Risk

When to Add MRSA Coverage

  • Previous MRSA respiratory isolation
  • Recent MRSA infection or colonization with severe pneumonia
  • Recent hospitalization and parenteral antibiotics with locally validated risk
  • Post-influenza necrotizing pneumonia
  • Cavitation or rapidly progressive disease
  • Septic shock with meaningful MRSA risk

MRSA Agents

Agent Important Features
Vancomycin Use AUC-guided dosing when feasible; monitor kidney function
Linezolid Excellent lung penetration; monitor platelets and drug interactions
Daptomycin should not be used for pneumonia because pulmonary surfactant inactivates it.

MRSA Nasal PCR

  • A negative result has strong value for ruling out MRSA pneumonia in many settings.
  • A positive result indicates colonization and does not prove MRSA pneumonia.
  • Use the result with cultures and clinical context.

When to Add Antipseudomonal Coverage

  • Previous respiratory isolation of Pseudomonas
  • Advanced structural lung disease
  • Recent hospitalization with parenteral antibiotics
  • Severe pneumonia with locally validated risk factors

Dual Antipseudomonal Therapy

Empiric treatment with two antipseudomonal agents from different classes may be appropriate in:

  • Septic shock
  • Very high resistance risk
  • High local resistance to potential monotherapy
  • Previous highly resistant Pseudomonas
Once susceptibility confirms an active beta-lactam and the patient is improving, continued dual antipseudomonal therapy is usually unnecessary.

Viral Pneumonia

Common Viral Causes

  • Influenza
  • SARS-CoV-2
  • RSV
  • Human metapneumovirus
  • Parainfluenza
  • Adenovirus
  • Rhinovirus

Positive Viral Testing

A positive viral PCR does not always exclude bacterial coinfection. The decision to prescribe antibiotics depends on:

  • Severity
  • Comorbidities
  • Imaging pattern
  • Bacterial culture data
  • Procalcitonin trend
  • Clinical trajectory
  • Evidence of lobar bacterial consolidation

When Antibiotics May Be Deferred

In selected otherwise healthy outpatients with clinical and imaging evidence of CAP, a confirmed respiratory virus, and low suspicion for bacterial coinfection, empiric antibiotics may be deferred with reliable follow-up.

Hospitalized Patients

Hospitalized or severe CAP patients with a positive viral test generally require more cautious assessment because bacterial coinfection is more consequential.

Influenza

  • Start oseltamivir promptly in hospitalized or high-risk patients.
  • Do not delay treatment while awaiting confirmatory testing when suspicion is high.
  • Consider secondary bacterial pneumonia if the patient worsens after initial improvement.

Post-Influenza Bacterial Pneumonia

  • Staphylococcus aureus
  • Streptococcus pneumoniae
  • Group A Streptococcus
  • Haemophilus influenzae
Rapid deterioration, hemoptysis, leukopenia, shock, or cavitary infiltrates after influenza should raise concern for necrotizing MRSA pneumonia.

Corticosteroids in CAP

Nonsevere CAP

Routine systemic corticosteroids are not recommended for nonsevere community-acquired pneumonia.

Severe CAP

Current ATS guidance supports considering systemic corticosteroids in selected hospitalized adults with severe CAP.

Potential Benefits

  • Reduced progression of inflammatory lung injury
  • Reduced vasopressor duration in selected patients
  • Potential mortality benefit in severe CAP

Potential Harms

  • Hyperglycemia
  • Delirium
  • Secondary infection
  • GI bleeding
  • Neuromuscular weakness

Use Caution When:

  • Influenza pneumonia is the primary diagnosis
  • Uncontrolled fungal infection is possible
  • There is active GI bleeding
  • Severe immunosuppression exists
Steroids remain indicated for other established reasons, including refractory septic shock, COPD exacerbation, asthma exacerbation, or adrenal insufficiency.

Aspiration Syndromes

Aspiration Pneumonitis

Aspiration pneumonitis is a chemical injury caused by aspiration of gastric contents.

Typical Features

  • Witnessed large-volume aspiration
  • Abrupt hypoxemia
  • Rapid respiratory distress
  • Dependent infiltrates
  • Potential improvement within 24–48 hours

Management

  • Airway support
  • Oxygen
  • Bronchial suctioning when indicated
  • Ventilatory support
  • Observation
Antibiotics are not routinely required for uncomplicated aspiration pneumonitis.

Aspiration Pneumonia

Aspiration pneumonia is an infectious process caused by aspiration of colonized oral or gastric contents.

Features Favoring Infection

  • Persistent fever
  • Purulent sputum
  • Leukocytosis
  • Progressive infiltrate
  • Symptoms developing after the aspiration event rather than immediately resolving
  • Lung abscess or empyema

Dependent Lung Segments

Patient Position Common Distribution
Upright Lower lobes, especially basal segments
Supine Posterior upper lobes and superior lower-lobe segments
Right-sided anatomy Right lung is commonly involved because of the more vertical right main bronchus

Anaerobic Coverage

Routine additional anaerobic coverage is not required for every aspiration pneumonia.

Consider Anaerobic Coverage When:

  • Lung abscess
  • Empyema
  • Necrotizing infection
  • Severe periodontal disease
  • Putrid sputum
  • Clear aspiration-associated polymicrobial infection

Potential Regimens

  • Ampicillin-sulbactam
  • Amoxicillin-clavulanate
  • Piperacillin-tazobactam for healthcare-associated severe disease
  • Carbapenem when resistant gram-negative risk is high
Do not routinely add metronidazole to piperacillin-tazobactam or a carbapenem. These agents already provide substantial anaerobic activity.

Hospital-Acquired and Ventilator-Associated Pneumonia

Diagnostic Features

  • New or progressive infiltrate
  • Fever or hypothermia
  • Purulent respiratory secretions
  • Leukocytosis or leukopenia
  • Worsening oxygenation
  • Increased ventilator requirement

Why Diagnosis Is Difficult

Hospitalized and ventilated patients frequently have noninfectious pulmonary abnormalities, including:

  • Atelectasis
  • Pulmonary edema
  • ARDS
  • Pulmonary hemorrhage
  • Mucus plugging
  • Pulmonary embolism
  • Drug toxicity
  • Ventilator-associated tracheobronchitis

Respiratory Colonization

Endotracheal aspirates commonly grow colonizing gram-negative organisms. A positive respiratory culture alone does not prove VAP.

HCAP Has Been Removed

The former HCAP category led to excessive broad-spectrum therapy. Current practice emphasizes patient-specific resistance risk and local antibiograms.

Common HAP/VAP Pathogens

  • Pseudomonas aeruginosa
  • MRSA
  • MSSA
  • Enterobacterales
  • ESBL- and AmpC-producing organisms
  • Acinetobacter
  • Stenotrophomonas maltophilia

Obtain Respiratory Cultures

  • Before antibiotics when feasible
  • Endotracheal aspirate for VAP
  • Expectorated sputum for HAP when adequate
  • Bronchoscopy when diagnosis is unclear or host factors require it

HAP/VAP Treatment

Empiric Coverage Goals

  • S. aureus
  • Pseudomonas aeruginosa
  • Other gram-negative bacilli

When to Include MRSA Coverage

  • Prior IV antibiotics within 90 days
  • Known MRSA colonization or infection
  • High unit-level MRSA prevalence
  • Unknown MRSA prevalence
  • Septic shock
  • Need for ventilatory support due to HAP

MRSA Therapy

  • Vancomycin
  • Linezolid

Common Antipseudomonal Beta-Lactams

  • Piperacillin-tazobactam
  • Cefepime
  • Ceftazidime
  • Meropenem
  • Imipenem-cilastatin
  • Aztreonam in selected beta-lactam allergy

When to Use Two Antipseudomonal Agents

  • Prior IV antibiotics within 90 days
  • Septic shock
  • High risk of mortality
  • Bronchiectasis or cystic fibrosis
  • High unit resistance to the proposed monotherapy

Second Antipseudomonal Classes

  • Fluoroquinolone
  • Aminoglycoside
  • Selected non-beta-lactam active agent
Avoid aminoglycoside monotherapy for pneumonia because lung penetration is poor and toxicity is substantial.

De-Escalation

  • Review cultures within 48–72 hours.
  • Stop MRSA therapy if unsupported.
  • Stop the second antipseudomonal agent once susceptibilities are known.
  • Narrow to the most focused active agent.
  • Reconsider the diagnosis if cultures and clinical course do not support pneumonia.

Duration

Most HAP/VAP: Approximately 7 Days

Longer courses may be required for:

  • Slow response
  • Empyema
  • Lung abscess
  • Necrotizing pneumonia
  • Inadequate source control
  • Severe immune compromise

Complications of Pneumonia

Pulmonary Complications

  • Parapneumonic effusion
  • Empyema
  • Lung abscess
  • Necrotizing pneumonia
  • Acute respiratory distress syndrome
  • Bronchopleural fistula
  • Pneumothorax
  • Organizing pneumonia

Systemic Complications

  • Sepsis and septic shock
  • Bacteremia
  • Endocarditis
  • Metastatic infection
  • Acute kidney injury
  • Myocardial injury
  • Delirium
  • Venous thromboembolism

When to Suspect a Complication

  • Persistent fever after 48–72 hours
  • Worsening oxygenation
  • Persistent bacteremia
  • New pleuritic pain
  • Increasing pleural fluid
  • Hemoptysis
  • Cavitation
  • Failure of leukocytosis to improve with clinical deterioration

Parapneumonic Effusion and Empyema

When to Perform Thoracentesis

  • More than minimal free-flowing effusion
  • Moderate or large effusion
  • Loculated fluid
  • Persistent fever or sepsis
  • Concern for empyema
  • Failure to improve

Pleural Fluid Tests

  • pH
  • Glucose
  • LDH
  • Protein
  • Cell count and differential
  • Gram stain
  • Aerobic and anaerobic cultures

Classification

Type Typical Findings Management
Uncomplicated parapneumonic effusion Free-flowing, pH above approximately 7.20, negative culture Antibiotics alone may be sufficient
Complicated parapneumonic effusion Low pH, low glucose, high LDH, positive culture, or loculation Chest-tube drainage plus antibiotics
Empyema Frank pus in pleural space Immediate drainage plus antibiotics
Pleural Fluid pH ≤7.20 → Strongly Consider Drainage

Drainage Options

  • Image-guided chest tube
  • Intrapleural fibrinolytic and DNase therapy in selected loculated effusions
  • Video-assisted thoracoscopic surgery
  • Open surgical decortication in advanced disease
Antibiotics alone are inadequate treatment for empyema.

Lung Abscess and Necrotizing Pneumonia

Lung Abscess

A lung abscess is a localized cavitary infection containing necrotic tissue and pus.

Risk Factors

  • Aspiration
  • Poor dentition
  • Altered mental status
  • Airway obstruction
  • Malignancy
  • Septic emboli
  • Immunosuppression

Common Organisms

  • Oral anaerobes
  • Streptococci
  • S. aureus
  • Gram-negative bacilli
  • Nocardia
  • Mycobacteria
  • Fungi

Treatment

  • Ampicillin-sulbactam
  • Amoxicillin-clavulanate for oral continuation
  • Broader therapy when resistant or healthcare pathogens are suspected
  • Prolonged therapy until clinical and radiographic improvement

When to Consider Bronchoscopy

  • Concern for obstructing malignancy
  • Foreign body
  • Failure to improve
  • Atypical organism
  • Need for microbiologic diagnosis

Necrotizing Pneumonia

Necrotizing pneumonia causes multiple areas of parenchymal destruction and may be associated with:

  • MRSA
  • Pneumococcus
  • Klebsiella
  • Group A Streptococcus
  • Anaerobic polymicrobial infection
Cavitary or necrotizing pneumonia after influenza should prompt immediate consideration of MRSA.

Pneumonia in the Immunocompromised Host

Host Factors Determine the Differential

Immune Defect Important Pathogens
Neutropenia Gram-negative bacteria, MRSA, Aspergillus, other molds
T-cell dysfunction PCP, CMV, Cryptococcus, mycobacteria
Humoral deficiency Pneumococcus, H. influenzae, Giardia, encapsulated bacteria
Chronic corticosteroids PCP, Nocardia, TB, fungal infection, Strongyloides
Solid-organ transplant Time-dependent bacterial, viral, and fungal infections
Hematopoietic stem-cell transplant Bacterial infection, molds, CMV, PCP, respiratory viruses

Diagnostic Strategy

  • Chest CT early
  • Blood cultures
  • Respiratory viral PCR
  • Sputum bacterial and fungal cultures
  • PCP PCR or direct testing
  • Beta-D-glucan
  • Galactomannan when mold is suspected
  • Urinary or serum fungal antigens when appropriate
  • Bronchoscopy with BAL when noninvasive testing is insufficient

PCP Clues

  • Subacute dyspnea
  • Dry cough
  • Fever
  • Diffuse bilateral ground-glass opacities
  • Elevated beta-D-glucan
  • Exertional hypoxemia
  • HIV with CD4 below 200 or significant cellular immunosuppression

Nocardia Clues

  • Chronic corticosteroid use
  • Transplantation
  • Cavitary or nodular lung disease
  • Brain abscess
Pulmonary Nocardia should prompt brain imaging because clinically silent CNS dissemination may be present.

Invasive Aspergillosis Clues

  • Prolonged neutropenia
  • Transplantation
  • High-dose corticosteroids
  • Nodules with halo sign
  • Cavitation
  • Hemoptysis

Nonresolving Pneumonia

Expected Response

Most patients should show some clinical improvement within approximately 48–72 hours, although radiographic resolution often takes much longer.

Reasons for Failure to Improve

Incorrect Diagnosis

  • Pulmonary edema
  • Pulmonary embolism
  • Malignancy
  • Organizing pneumonia
  • Diffuse alveolar hemorrhage
  • Drug-induced pneumonitis
  • Vasculitis
  • Eosinophilic pneumonia

Incorrect Organism or Resistance

  • MRSA
  • Pseudomonas
  • ESBL organism
  • TB
  • Fungal infection
  • PCP
  • Nocardia
  • Viral infection

Complication

  • Empyema
  • Lung abscess
  • Necrotizing pneumonia
  • Endocarditis
  • Metastatic infection

Host or Treatment Issue

  • Incorrect dose
  • Drug interaction
  • Malabsorption
  • Severe immune deficiency
  • Nonadherence
  • Ongoing aspiration

Evaluation of Nonresponse

  1. Reconfirm the diagnosis.
  2. Review cultures and antimicrobial exposure.
  3. Repeat chest imaging, often with CT.
  4. Assess the pleural space.
  5. Search for abscess or obstruction.
  6. Review immune status and exposures.
  7. Consider bronchoscopy.
  8. Consider noninfectious mimics.
Do not respond to every nonresolving pneumonia by simply adding vancomycin, a carbapenem, and antifungal therapy.

Treatment Duration

Community-Acquired Pneumonia

Duration should be guided by severity, clinical stability, organism, complications, and immune status.

Selected Nonsevere CAP

For selected outpatients or nonsevere inpatients who achieve clinical stability rapidly, a course shorter than five days, with a minimum of approximately three days, may be reasonable.

Traditional Standard

At least five days remains appropriate for many patients, particularly when stability is not achieved by day 3.

Longer Treatment May Be Required For:

  • Severe CAP with delayed response
  • MRSA pneumonia
  • Pseudomonas pneumonia
  • Legionella in an immunocompromised patient
  • Bacteremia with metastatic infection
  • Lung abscess
  • Empyema
  • Necrotizing pneumonia
  • Immunocompromised host

HAP/VAP

Approximately seven days is appropriate for most patients who respond and have no uncontrolled complication.

Do not extend antibiotics solely because the chest radiograph remains abnormal. Radiographic resolution often lags behind clinical recovery.

Clinical Stability and IV-to-Oral Conversion

Clinical Stability Criteria

  • Improving temperature
  • Heart rate improving
  • Respiratory rate improving
  • Blood pressure stable without vasopressors
  • Oxygenation stable on baseline or low support
  • Mental status returned to baseline
  • Able to eat and take oral medication

IV-to-Oral Conversion

Switch when:

  • Hemodynamically stable
  • Clinically improving
  • Functional GI tract
  • Active oral regimen available
  • No complication requiring IV therapy

Oral Step-Down Options

  • Amoxicillin-clavulanate
  • Amoxicillin
  • Cefuroxime
  • Cefpodoxime
  • Doxycycline
  • Azithromycin as part of a selected regimen
  • Respiratory fluoroquinolone when appropriate
  • Linezolid for selected MRSA pneumonia
Patients do not need to remain hospitalized solely to complete IV antibiotics if an appropriate oral regimen exists.

Follow-Up Imaging

Routine Imaging

Routine repeat imaging is not required for every patient who improves normally.

Consider Follow-Up Chest Imaging When:

  • Symptoms persist
  • Clinical response is incomplete
  • Recurrent pneumonia occurs in the same location
  • Patient is older with meaningful smoking history
  • Malignancy or airway obstruction is suspected
  • Cavitation was present
  • Complicated pneumonia occurred

Radiographic Resolution

Infiltrates may take weeks to resolve, especially in:

  • Older adults
  • Multilobar pneumonia
  • Severe disease
  • Underlying lung disease
  • Gram-negative or staphylococcal infection

Prevention

Vaccination

  • Pneumococcal vaccination
  • Annual influenza vaccination
  • COVID-19 vaccination
  • RSV vaccination for eligible adults
  • Pertussis vaccination

Risk-Factor Modification

  • Smoking cessation
  • Alcohol-use treatment
  • Dental care
  • Dysphagia assessment
  • Aspiration precautions
  • Mobility
  • Good control of chronic disease

VAP Prevention

  • Head-of-bed elevation
  • Minimize sedation
  • Daily assessment for extubation
  • Oral care
  • Subglottic secretion drainage in selected patients
  • Avoid unnecessary intubation
  • Early mobility

Daily Inpatient Pneumonia Checklist

  • Does the patient still appear to have pneumonia?
  • Is the pneumonia CAP, HAP, VAP, aspiration related, or opportunistic?
  • Is oxygenation improving?
  • Is the patient clinically stable?
  • What cultures and viral tests are available?
  • Does MRSA coverage remain necessary?
  • Does antipseudomonal coverage remain necessary?
  • Can the second antipseudomonal agent stop?
  • Is anaerobic coverage truly indicated?
  • Is the antibiotic dose correct for renal function?
  • Can treatment change to oral therapy?
  • Is there an effusion requiring thoracentesis?
  • Is there cavitation, abscess, or necrosis?
  • Is the patient aspirating repeatedly?
  • What is the planned treatment stop date?
  • Are vaccines and smoking cessation addressed before discharge?

How to Present Pneumonia on ID Rounds

  • Pneumonia category
  • Day of illness and hospital day
  • Current oxygen requirement
  • Imaging distribution and complications
  • Severity criteria
  • Microbiology and viral testing
  • Prior resistant organisms
  • Current antibiotic, dose, and start date
  • Reason for MRSA or Pseudomonas coverage
  • Clinical stability
  • Planned duration and oral step-down
Avoid saying only, “The patient has bilateral pneumonia and is on vancomycin and Zosyn.” Explain the syndrome, severity, risk factors, microbiology, and plan for de-escalation.

Common Clinical Scenarios

Case 1: Stable Outpatient Without Comorbidities

A healthy 35-year-old has fever, cough, focal infiltrate, normal oxygen saturation, and reliable follow-up.

Outpatient treatment with high-dose amoxicillin or doxycycline is appropriate. Broad gram-negative and MRSA coverage is unnecessary.

Case 2: Nursing-Home Resident

An older nursing-home resident presents with CAP but has no previous resistant organism, no recent IV antibiotics, and no shock.

Nursing-home residence alone does not mandate vancomycin plus an antipseudomonal regimen.

Case 3: Post-Influenza Shock and Cavitation

A patient initially improved from influenza and then develops shock, hemoptysis, leukopenia, and cavitary infiltrates.

Suspect necrotizing MRSA pneumonia. Begin MRSA-active therapy promptly and obtain respiratory and blood cultures.

Case 4: Witnessed Aspiration

An intoxicated patient aspirates, becomes acutely hypoxemic, and develops dependent infiltrates within hours.

This may be aspiration pneumonitis. Provide supportive care and reassess rather than automatically committing to prolonged antibiotics.

Case 5: Pneumonia with Pleural pH 7.05

A patient with pneumonia has a loculated pleural effusion with pH 7.05.

This is a complicated parapneumonic effusion requiring drainage in addition to antibiotics.

Case 6: Negative MRSA Nasal PCR

A patient receiving vancomycin for pneumonia has a negative MRSA nasal PCR, no MRSA culture growth, and is improving.

Stop vancomycin unless another strong reason for MRSA treatment exists.

Case 7: VAP with Susceptible Pseudomonas

A ventilated patient was started on cefepime plus levofloxacin. Culture shows Pseudomonas susceptible to cefepime.

Continue a single active antipseudomonal beta-lactam and stop unnecessary combination therapy.

Case 8: Diffuse Ground-Glass Opacities in HIV

A patient with untreated HIV, CD4 count 48, subacute dyspnea, dry cough, and diffuse bilateral ground-glass opacities presents with exertional hypoxemia.

Suspect PCP. Obtain targeted testing and begin TMP-SMX when clinical suspicion is high.

Case 9: Nonresolving Right Upper-Lobe Pneumonia

A smoker has recurrent right upper-lobe pneumonia despite multiple antibiotic courses.

Evaluate for obstructing malignancy with CT and bronchoscopy rather than prescribing another empiric antibiotic course.

Case 10: Legionella Syndrome

A traveler develops severe pneumonia, diarrhea, confusion, hyponatremia, and elevated liver enzymes.

Obtain Legionella testing and ensure the regimen includes effective intracellular therapy such as azithromycin or levofloxacin.

Common Pitfalls

  • Diagnosing pneumonia without a compatible infiltrate
  • Treating every infiltrate as infection
  • Using the obsolete HCAP category
  • Giving vancomycin solely because the patient lives in a nursing home
  • Giving antipseudomonal therapy without meaningful risk factors
  • Failing to obtain cultures in severe CAP
  • Continuing vancomycin despite negative MRSA testing
  • Continuing two antipseudomonal agents after susceptibilities return
  • Using daptomycin for pneumonia
  • Using aminoglycoside monotherapy for pneumonia
  • Withholding initial antibiotics solely because procalcitonin is low
  • Giving routine steroids for nonsevere CAP
  • Giving antibiotics automatically for aspiration pneumonitis
  • Adding routine anaerobic coverage to every aspiration case
  • Missing empyema and failing to drain it
  • Extending therapy because the chest radiograph remains abnormal
  • Failing to consider TB or fungal disease in nonresolving pneumonia
  • Failing to recognize post-influenza MRSA
  • Failing to assess immune status
  • Broadening therapy instead of reconsidering the diagnosis

Board and ID Consult Pearls

  • Pneumonia requires a compatible syndrome plus a new infiltrate.
  • Healthcare-associated pneumonia is no longer a recommended category.
  • PSI is generally preferred over CURB-65 for hospitalization decisions.
  • Severe CAP is defined by one major or at least three minor ATS/IDSA criteria.
  • Nonsevere inpatient CAP is commonly treated with a beta-lactam plus azithromycin.
  • Severe CAP should receive a beta-lactam plus macrolide or respiratory fluoroquinolone.
  • Macrolide monotherapy is appropriate only where pneumococcal resistance is sufficiently low.
  • Previous respiratory isolation is a major risk factor for MRSA or Pseudomonas.
  • A negative MRSA nasal PCR supports stopping MRSA therapy.
  • A positive MRSA nasal PCR does not prove MRSA pneumonia.
  • Daptomycin cannot treat pneumonia.
  • Aminoglycosides should not be used as pneumonia monotherapy.
  • Procalcitonin should not be the sole reason to withhold initial CAP therapy.
  • Routine steroids are not recommended for nonsevere CAP.
  • Steroids may benefit selected patients with severe CAP.
  • Influenza should be treated promptly in hospitalized patients.
  • Post-influenza cavitary pneumonia suggests MRSA.
  • Aspiration pneumonitis is chemical injury and may not require antibiotics.
  • Routine anaerobic coverage is not required for every aspiration pneumonia.
  • Empyema requires drainage.
  • Pleural fluid pH at or below approximately 7.20 supports chest-tube drainage.
  • Most HAP/VAP can be treated for approximately seven days.
  • Many selected nonsevere CAP patients may receive a three- to five-day course if rapidly stable.
  • Radiographic resolution lags behind clinical resolution.
  • Nonresolving pneumonia should trigger diagnostic reassessment rather than automatic broadening.
  • Nocardia pneumonia should prompt brain imaging.
  • The Legionella urinary antigen does not detect all Legionella infections.
  • Recurrent pneumonia in the same lobe suggests obstruction.

Bottom Line

Pneumonia management begins by confirming a compatible clinical syndrome and a new pulmonary infiltrate. Determine whether the infection is community acquired, hospital acquired, ventilator associated, aspiration related, or occurring in an immunocompromised host. Assess severity early using clinical judgment, oxygen requirement, PSI, and ATS/IDSA severe CAP criteria.

Most community-acquired pneumonia does not require MRSA or Pseudomonas coverage. Add these agents only when prior cultures, recent parenteral antibiotics, structural lung disease, severe illness, or locally validated risk factors support their use. Obtain cultures in severe disease and de-escalate promptly.

Differentiate aspiration pneumonitis from infectious aspiration pneumonia. Do not provide routine additional anaerobic coverage unless abscess, empyema, necrosis, or another strong indication exists. Drain infected pleural fluid, investigate cavitary disease, and reconsider the diagnosis when the patient fails to improve.

Use the shortest effective treatment duration, convert to oral therapy once stable, and do not extend antibiotics solely because radiographic abnormalities remain.

Overview

Heme/Onc becomes much easier when organized by pattern recognition: anemia by MCV, bleeding by platelet versus clotting factor pattern, hemolysis by intra- versus extravascular destruction, and malignancy by cell lineage.

Core workflow: CBC → MCV → reticulocyte response → smear → iron studies or hemolysis labs → targeted confirmatory testing.

Microcytic Anemia

Microcytic anemia means low MCV, usually less than 80 fL. The most important causes are iron deficiency anemia, thalassemia, anemia of chronic disease, sideroblastic anemia, and lead poisoning.

Cause Iron Ferritin Key Clue
Iron deficiency anemia Low Low High RDW, pale RBCs
Thalassemia Normal Normal Target cells, no response to iron
Anemia of chronic disease Low Normal or high Chronic inflammation, CKD, autoimmune disease, malignancy
Sideroblastic anemia High High Ring sideroblasts
Lead poisoning Variable Variable Basophilic stippling, neuro symptoms

Hemoglobin Low Low

Ferritin Low Normal
RDW High Normal or low
Blood smear Pale RBCs Target cells

Microcytic anemia that does not improve with iron supplementation should make you think of thalassemia.

Beta-Thalassemia

  • Decreased beta-globin chain production.
  • Hemoglobin electrophoresis shows increased HbA2 and HbF.
  • Beta-thalassemia major presents in children with severe anemia, hepatosplenomegaly, and extramedullary hematopoiesis.

Alpha-Thalassemia

  • Decreased alpha-globin chain production.
  • Hemoglobin electrophoresis may be normal in mild adult disease.
  • Three alpha mutations causes HbH disease.
  • Four alpha mutations causes Hb Barts and is usually fatal in utero.

Anemia of Chronic Disease

Anemia of chronic disease occurs with chronic inflammation, kidney disease, autoimmune disease, chronic infection, hepatitis, HIV, and malignancy.

Lab Typical Finding
Serum iron Low
Ferritin Normal or high
TIBC / transferrin Low or normal
MCV Usually normal, sometimes low
Mechanism: Inflammation increases hepcidin, which decreases gut iron absorption and traps iron in storage sites.

If anemia of chronic disease is due to chronic kidney disease, erythropoietin can be used. Otherwise, treatment is directed at the underlying condition.

Macrocytosis

Macrocytosis means MCV greater than 100 fL.

Cause Mechanism Clue
B12 deficiency Impaired DNA synthesis Neurologic findings, elevated methylmalonic acid
Folate deficiency Impaired DNA synthesis No neurologic findings
Alcohol Direct marrow toxicity, liver disease, nutritional deficiency High MCV with normal B12/folate possible
Orotic aciduria Impaired pyrimidine synthesis Rare cause
Hypersegmented neutrophils suggest megaloblastic anemia from B12 or folate deficiency.

Heme Synthesis Disorders

Disorder Defect Classic Finding
Sideroblastic anemia ALA synthase problem or impaired heme synthesis Ring sideroblasts, high iron
Lead poisoning Inhibits ALA dehydratase and ferrochelatase Basophilic stippling, abdominal pain, neuropathy
Acute intermittent porphyria Porphobilinogen deaminase deficiency Abdominal pain, neuro symptoms, port-wine urine
Porphyria cutanea tarda Uroporphyrinogen decarboxylase deficiency Photosensitivity, blistering, tea-colored urine
Heme synthesis starts with succinyl-CoA + glycine.

Hemolysis Overview

Type Location Key Findings
Intravascular hemolysis Bloodstream Hemoglobinuria, low haptoglobin
Extravascular hemolysis Spleen and liver macrophages No hemoglobinuria, haptoglobin usually preserved

Intravascular Examples

  • G6PD deficiency
  • PNH
  • DIC
  • HUS
  • TTP
  • Mechanical hemolysis from prosthetic valves

Extravascular Examples

  • Hereditary spherocytosis
  • Thalassemia
  • Warm and cold autoimmune hemolytic anemia

Hereditary Spherocytosis

Hereditary spherocytosis is usually autosomal dominant and caused by defects in RBC membrane or cytoskeletal proteins such as ankyrin, spectrin, or band proteins.

  • Spherocytes lack central pallor.
  • Coombs test is negative.
  • MCHC may be increased.
  • Can cause pigment gallstones.
  • Diagnosis: osmotic fragility test or eosin-5-maleimide test.
  • Treatment: splenectomy in moderate to severe disease.
Spherocytes with a positive Coombs test suggest autoimmune hemolytic anemia, not hereditary spherocytosis.

G6PD and Pyruvate Kinase Deficiency

G6PD Deficiency

  • X-linked recessive.
  • Decreased NADPH production.
  • RBCs become vulnerable to oxidative stress.
  • Triggers include sulfa drugs, dapsone, primaquine, fava beans, and infection.
  • Smear: bite cells and Heinz bodies.

Pyruvate Kinase Deficiency

  • Decreased ATP production in RBCs.
  • Na/K ATPase function decreases.
  • RBC swelling and hemolysis occur.

Sickle Cell Disease

Sickle cell disease is autosomal recessive and caused by a missense mutation in the beta-globin chain.

Sickle cell mutation = glutamic acid → valine

Major Clinical Features

  • Pain crises
  • Acute chest syndrome
  • Dactylitis
  • Autosplenectomy
  • Increased risk of encapsulated organisms
  • Salmonella osteomyelitis
  • Pigment gallstones
  • Renal papillary necrosis

Management Pearls

  • Hydroxyurea increases HbF and reduces recurrent crises.
  • Children require pneumococcal prophylaxis and vaccination.
  • Functional asplenia increases risk of infection with encapsulated organisms.

Plasma Cell Disorders

Multiple Myeloma

Multiple myeloma is a plasma cell malignancy classically presenting in older adults with bone pain, anemia, renal dysfunction, and hypercalcemia.

CRAB = HyperCalcemia, Renal failure, Anemia, Bone lesions
  • SPEP shows monoclonal spike.
  • Urine may show Bence Jones proteins.
  • Bone marrow biopsy confirms increased plasma cells.
  • Smear can show rouleaux formation.
  • Light chains can cause amyloidosis.

MGUS

MGUS has monoclonal protein but fewer plasma cells and no CRAB features. It carries a small annual risk of progression to multiple myeloma.

Waldenström Macroglobulinemia

  • Plasmacytoid cell malignancy.
  • IgM monoclonal spike.
  • Causes hyperviscosity: headache, blurry vision, tinnitus, Raynaud phenomenon.
  • No lytic lesions, hypercalcemia, or Bence Jones proteinuria.

Polycythemia and Myeloproliferative Disease

Disorder Key Finding Mechanism
Polycythemia vera High RBCs with high WBCs and/or platelets JAK2 mutation, low EPO
Secondary polycythemia Isolated high RBCs High EPO from hypoxia, RCC, or exogenous EPO
Essential thrombocythemia Very high platelets, thrombosis symptoms Usually JAK2 mutation
Myelofibrosis Dry tap, teardrop cells, massive splenomegaly Marrow fibrosis from abnormal megakaryocytes
Pruritus after a hot shower is a classic clue for polycythemia vera.

DIC, HUS, and TTP

DIC

DIC is widespread activation of coagulation with consumption of platelets and clotting factors.

  • High PT, PTT, bleeding time, D-dimer
  • Low platelets, fibrinogen, clotting factors
  • Schistocytes on smear
  • Bleeding from IV or catheter sites is a classic clue

HUS

HUS = thrombocytopenia + hemolytic anemia with schistocytes + renal failure

Classically follows bloody diarrhea from EHEC or Shigella toxin.

TTP

TTP = HUS triad + fever + neurologic symptoms

TTP is caused by ADAMTS13 deficiency or antibodies against ADAMTS13. Treatment is plasmapheresis.

Neutropenia

Neutropenia commonly presents with fever, mouth ulcers, and sore throat.

Important Causes

  • Chemotherapy or radiation
  • Viral infection
  • Parvovirus B19
  • Clozapine
  • Methotrexate
  • PTU or methimazole
  • TMP-SMX
  • Cyclic neutropenia
Febrile neutropenia is a medical emergency. Treat immediately with IV broad-spectrum antibiotics.

Leukemias

Leukemia Typical Patient Key Finding

APL AML subtype t(15;17), treated with all-trans retinoic acid

CML shows a mixture of myeloid cells at different stages. Leukemoid reaction usually shows mature neutrophilia and high leukocyte alkaline phosphatase.

Lymphomas

Hodgkin Lymphoma

  • Reed-Sternberg cells
  • CD15 and CD30 positive
  • Painless neck mass
  • May have mediastinal mass or B symptoms
  • Nodular sclerosis subtype is common in young women

High-Yield Pearls

  1. High RDW strongly favors iron deficiency anemia.
  2. Target cells strongly suggest thalassemia on exams.
  3. Thalassemia does not improve with iron supplementation.
  4. Beta-thalassemia has increased HbA2 and HbF.
  5. Anemia of chronic disease has low iron but normal or high ferritin.
  6. CKD-related anemia of chronic disease can be treated with erythropoietin.
  7. Hypersegmented neutrophils suggest B12 or folate deficiency.
  8. Lead inhibits ALA dehydratase and ferrochelatase.
  9. Acute intermittent porphyria causes abdominal pain and port-wine urine.
  10. PNH causes morning red urine from complement-mediated hemolysis.
  11. Hereditary spherocytosis is Coombs negative.
  12. Autoimmune hemolytic anemia is Coombs positive.
  13. G6PD deficiency causes bite cells and Heinz bodies.
  14. Hydroxyurea increases HbF in sickle cell disease.
  15. Multiple myeloma causes CRAB findings and monoclonal spike.
  16. Waldenström causes IgM hyperviscosity without lytic lesions.
  17. Polycythemia vera has low EPO and often JAK2 mutation.
  18. Myelofibrosis causes dry tap, teardrop cells, and massive splenomegaly.
  19. ITP causes low platelets with normal PT/PTT.
  20. vWD causes platelet-type and clotting-factor-type bleeding.
  21. Hemophilia causes isolated elevated PTT and hemarthrosis.
  22. DIC causes high PT, PTT, bleeding time, D-dimer, and low fibrinogen.
  23. HUS is thrombocytopenia, schistocytes, and renal failure after bloody diarrhea.
  24. TTP is HUS plus fever and neurologic findings.
  25. Febrile neutropenia requires immediate IV broad-spectrum antibiotics.
  26. ALL is the classic leukemia of children.
  27. CLL causes smudge cells in older adults.
  28. AML has Auer rods.
  29. CML has Philadelphia chromosome t(9;22) and BCR-ABL.
  30. Hodgkin lymphoma has Reed-Sternberg cells that are CD15 and CD30 positive.
  31. Burkitt lymphoma has t(8;14), c-MYC, and starry-sky histology.
  32. TRALI occurs within 6 hours of transfusion.
  33. TACO is volume overload, especially after 6 hours or in patients with heart disease.

Bottom Line

Heme/Onc is pattern recognition. Microcytic anemia is separated by iron studies and smear findings. Platelet disorders cause mucocutaneous bleeding, while clotting factor disorders cause deep bleeding and hemarthrosis. Hemolysis is divided into intravascular versus extravascular patterns. Plasma cell disorders cause monoclonal spikes, bone disease, renal injury, and hyperviscosity syndromes. Leukemias and lymphomas are identified by age, cell type, smear, immunophenotype, and translocations. For pharmacology, focus on mechanism, classic toxicity, and the antidote or protective agent.

Pain Management in Hospital Medicine: A Practical Guide for Internal Medicine Residents

June 17, 2026 • Hospital Medicine • 15–18 min read

Pain is one of the most common reasons residents receive pages overnight. Effective pain management requires understanding different pain mechanisms, selecting appropriate medications, minimizing opioid exposure when possible, and recognizing special situations such as neuropathic pain, opioid tolerance, and cancer-related pain.

Principles of Pain Management

Pain is one of the most frequent complaints encountered in hospitalized patients. Before prescribing medications, determine whether the pain is:

  • Nociceptive pain
  • Neuropathic pain
  • Cancer-related pain
  • Musculoskeletal pain
  • Procedure-related pain
  • End-of-life discomfort
Neuropathic pain responds poorly to opioids and often requires medications such as gabapentin, duloxetine, venlafaxine, or tricyclic antidepressants.

Acetaminophen (Tylenol)

Acetaminophen should be considered first-line therapy for mild to moderate pain in most hospitalized patients.

Regimen Dose
PRN 650 mg every 4 hours as needed
Scheduled 1000 mg every 6 hours
Maximum Daily Dose 4 grams/day
Cirrhosis/Liver Disease 2 grams/day
For patients requiring frequent PRN doses, scheduled acetaminophen often provides superior pain control.
Avoid the abbreviation "APAP." Use "acetaminophen" or "Tylenol" to reduce medication errors.

Routes of Administration

  • Oral
  • Rectal
  • Intravenous (more expensive)

NSAIDs

NSAIDs are highly effective analgesics but should be used cautiously due to risks of acute kidney injury and gastrointestinal bleeding.

Medication Typical Dose Key Pearl
Ibuprofen 400–600 mg every 6–8 hours 400 mg often provides similar analgesia with fewer side effects
Naproxen Every 12 hours Longer duration but increased GI bleeding risk
Ketorolac (Toradol) IV or IM Excellent for renal colic but highest GI bleed risk
Indomethacin Variable Commonly used for gout flares
Celecoxib Variable May have lower GI toxicity than traditional NSAIDs
Avoid NSAIDs in patients with active GI bleeding, advanced CKD, severe AKI, or high bleeding risk unless benefits clearly outweigh risks.

Topical Therapies

Lidocaine Patches

  • Useful for localized musculoskeletal pain
  • Up to three patches may be used simultaneously
  • Typically changed every 24 hours
  • Frequently used for rib fractures and back pain

Capsaicin Cream

Capsaicin initially stimulates nociceptors and may cause burning, followed by prolonged desensitization and reduced pain signaling.

Condition Concentration
Osteoarthritis 0.025%
Neuropathic Pain 0.075%
Warn patients that capsaicin commonly causes burning or redness during the first several days of therapy.

Diclofenac Gel

  • Topical NSAID
  • 1% gel used for pain
  • Low systemic absorption
  • Useful when oral NSAIDs are undesirable

Opioids

Opioids remain important for moderate to severe pain but require careful monitoring and adverse-effect prevention.

Medication Typical Dose Important Pearl
Tramadol 50 mg every 4–6 hours Lowers seizure threshold
Oxycodone 5 mg PO Start 2.5 mg in elderly or frail patients
Hydromorphone (Dilaudid) Variable Rapid onset and potent analgesia
Morphine Variable Excellent for air hunger and hospice care
Meperidine (Demerol) Rarely used May be used for rigors

Common Outpatient Combination Products

  • Norco (Hydrocodone/Acetaminophen)
  • Percocet (Oxycodone/Acetaminophen)
  • Vicodin

Opioid Pearls

Every patient receiving opioids should have naloxone available for overdose reversal.

Bowel Regimen

Constipation is one of the most predictable opioid side effects.

  • Polyethylene glycol (Miralax)
  • Senna
  • Escalate therapy when needed
Patients on chronic opioids should almost always receive a bowel regimen unless contraindicated.

Organ Dysfunction Considerations

Medication Avoid In
Hydromorphone Severe liver dysfunction
Morphine Advanced renal dysfunction

Neuropathic Pain

Burning, tingling, electric, and shooting pain is typically neuropathic and often responds poorly to opioids.

Class Examples
SNRI Duloxetine, Venlafaxine
Gabapentinoid Gabapentin
TCA Amitriptyline, Nortriptyline
Gabapentin is commonly used inpatient, while SNRIs often have stronger evidence for chronic neuropathic pain.

Methadone

  • Used for opioid use disorder and chronic pain
  • Very long half-life
  • Requires careful dose titration
  • IV formulation may prolong QT interval
Methadone dosing can be complex. Consider consultation with pain management or addiction medicine.

Ketamine

Ketamine is increasingly used as an opioid-sparing analgesic in patients with severe or difficult-to-control pain.

  • Useful in opioid tolerance
  • Can reduce opioid requirements
  • Often managed with pain pharmacy support
  • Increasingly utilized in inpatient settings

Buprenorphine

Buprenorphine is a partial mu-opioid receptor agonist increasingly used for opioid use disorder and chronic pain management.

  • Lower risk of respiratory depression
  • Useful for opioid use disorder
  • May reduce misuse of full opioid agonists
  • Becoming increasingly common in hospital medicine

Miscellaneous Pain Management Options

Oral Pain

  • Lidocaine swish-and-swallow
  • Useful for mucositis and oral ulcers

Foley Catheter Pain

  • Lidocaine gel (Uro-Jet)

Localized Swelling and Musculoskeletal Pain

  • Warm compresses
  • Heating pads (K-pad)

Muscle Relaxants

Medication Pearl
Baclofen Often used for central spasticity
Cyclobenzaprine Sedating
Tizanidine Sedating
Watch for baclofen withdrawal and gabapentin withdrawal in hospitalized patients who suddenly stop chronic therapy.

Consult Services

  • Pain Pharmacy
  • Palliative Care
  • Wound Care
  • Addiction Medicine

Key Takeaways

  • Acetaminophen remains first-line therapy for many hospitalized patients.
  • NSAIDs are effective but limited by AKI and GI bleeding risk.
  • Topical therapies are underutilized and often effective.
  • Always prescribe bowel regimens with opioids.
  • Neuropathic pain requires different treatment than nociceptive pain.
  • Gabapentin and SNRIs are common neuropathic pain treatments.
  • Methadone, ketamine, and buprenorphine are increasingly important in modern pain management.
  • Consult pain specialists early for difficult pain syndromes.

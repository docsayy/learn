Fever in the Hospitalized Patient: The Complete Advanced Resident Guide

Added July 14, 2026 • Infectious Disease • ~48 min read

New or persistent fever in a hospitalized patient may represent infection, inflammation, tissue injury, medication toxicity, thrombosis, transfusion reaction, malignancy, neurologic injury, or environmental hyperthermia. The correct response is not automatic broad-spectrum antibiotics or indiscriminate cultures. This guide provides a structured bedside approach to temperature confirmation, sepsis recognition, culture selection, device review, postoperative fever, drug fever, fever despite antibiotics, occult infection, central fever, thromboembolism, transfusion reactions, diagnostic imaging, antimicrobial decisions, and reassessment after 72 hours.

Clinical Overview

Fever is a physiologic signal rather than a diagnosis. It may be the first sign of serious infection, but it is also common after surgery, transfusion, tissue injury, thrombosis, medication exposure, and acute neurologic disease.

Confirm the Temperature → Assess Stability → Localize the Syndrome → Obtain Targeted Tests → Treat the Patient, Not the Number

The Major Clinical Questions

  1. Is the temperature measurement accurate?
  2. Is the patient unstable, septic, or immunocompromised?
  3. Is there a localizing symptom, examination finding, or device?
  4. What changed immediately before the fever began?
  5. Which cultures or imaging studies could meaningfully change management?
  6. Does the patient need immediate empiric antibiotics?
  7. Is there a source requiring drainage, removal, extraction, or surgery?
  8. Could the fever be noninfectious?

Fever Is Not Synonymous With Infection

Potential categories include:

  • Bacterial, viral, fungal, or parasitic infection
  • Postoperative inflammation
  • Drug fever or drug hypersensitivity
  • Venous thromboembolism
  • Transfusion reaction
  • Acute neurologic injury
  • Pancreatitis or tissue necrosis
  • Autoimmune or autoinflammatory disease
  • Malignancy
  • Endocrine emergency
  • Environmental or medication-induced hyperthermia
The diagnostic value of fever depends on the patient’s clinical context, immune status, timing, temperature pattern, and associated organ dysfunction.

Defining Fever

Critically Ill Adults

A Single Temperature ≥38.3°C (101°F) Is Commonly Used to Define Fever in the ICU

This threshold is useful for standardization, but clinical investigation should not depend on a single number alone.

General-Ward Patients

There is no single universal threshold appropriate for every inpatient. A new temperature elevation becomes more concerning when it is:

  • At least 38.0–38.3°C
  • Repeated or sustained
  • Associated with rigors
  • Accompanied by hypotension, tachypnea, hypoxemia, confusion, or organ dysfunction
  • Unexpected for the clinical context

High Fever

Temperatures above approximately 39.5°C should prompt particular attention to:

  • Severe infection
  • Drug reaction
  • Transfusion reaction
  • Central fever
  • Malignant hyperthermia
  • Serotonin syndrome
  • Neuroleptic malignant syndrome
  • Heat illness

Hypothermia

Severe infection may present with hypothermia rather than fever, particularly in older adults, patients with advanced liver disease, and those with severe shock or immune suppression.

Temperature Measurement

Preferred Methods in Critically Ill Patients

When already present, accurate central measurements may be obtained from:

  • Pulmonary-artery catheter thermistor
  • Bladder catheter thermistor
  • Esophageal thermistor

Without a Central Device

Oral or rectal measurements are generally more reliable than:

  • Axillary temperature
  • Noninvasive temporal-artery measurement
  • Chemical-dot thermometer
  • Poorly positioned tympanic measurement

Before Starting a Major Workup

  • Repeat an unexpected reading.
  • Confirm the measurement site.
  • Review recent hot or cold oral intake.
  • Review use of warming or cooling devices.
  • Compare with the previous temperature trend.
  • Determine whether the device is calibrated and functioning.

Rectal Temperature Cautions

Avoid rectal temperature measurement when possible in:

  • Severe neutropenia
  • Thrombocytopenia
  • Rectal surgery or disease
  • Severe diarrhea
  • Perianal infection
A single isolated low-quality temperature reading should not automatically trigger cultures, imaging, and broad antibiotics in an otherwise stable patient.

Fever vs Hyperthermia

Fever

Fever results from an increased hypothalamic temperature set point, usually mediated by inflammatory cytokines and prostaglandins.

Hyperthermia

Hyperthermia results from excessive heat production or impaired heat dissipation without an elevated hypothalamic set point.

Important Hyperthermia Syndromes

  • Heat stroke
  • Malignant hyperthermia
  • Serotonin syndrome
  • Neuroleptic malignant syndrome
  • Anticholinergic toxicity
  • Severe sympathomimetic intoxication
  • Thyroid storm

Clinical Clues to Hyperthermia

  • Temperature often above 40°C
  • Altered mental status
  • Rigidity, clonus, or marked agitation
  • Recent anesthesia or medication exposure
  • Elevated creatine kinase
  • Metabolic acidosis
  • Multiorgan injury
Hyperthermia is a medical emergency. Antipyretic medications are ineffective because the hypothalamic set point is not the primary problem. Rapid physical cooling and syndrome-specific treatment are required.

Blunted or Absent Fever Response

Patients May Have Serious Infection Without Fever

  • Older adults
  • Neutropenic patients
  • Patients taking corticosteroids
  • Patients receiving antipyretics
  • Advanced cirrhosis
  • Chronic kidney disease
  • Severe malnutrition
  • Spinal-cord injury
  • Profound sepsis or shock

Alternative Warning Signs

  • New confusion
  • Functional decline
  • Hypothermia
  • Unexplained tachypnea
  • Hypotension
  • Acute kidney injury
  • Hyperglycemia
  • Reduced urine output
  • New oxygen requirement
Absence of fever should not provide reassurance when the patient has new organ dysfunction or a convincing infectious syndrome.

Immediate Priorities

First Determine Whether the Patient Is Unstable

  • Hypotension
  • Altered mental status
  • Respiratory distress
  • New hypoxemia
  • Oliguria
  • Rising lactate
  • Mottled skin
  • Rapidly progressive rash or tissue injury

If Sepsis or Shock Is Possible

  1. Obtain appropriate cultures promptly.
  2. Measure lactate when organ hypoperfusion is possible.
  3. Begin hemodynamic resuscitation.
  4. Administer empiric antimicrobials without unnecessary delay.
  5. Identify a source requiring urgent control.
  6. Escalate monitoring or level of care.

Antibiotic Timing

  • Septic shock or high-probability sepsis: treat immediately.
  • Possible sepsis without shock: perform a rapid focused assessment.
  • Low likelihood of infection and stable physiology: close observation without immediate antibiotics may be appropriate.
Cultures should ideally precede antibiotics, but culture collection should not substantially delay life-saving antimicrobial treatment in a patient with sepsis or shock.

Focused History

Timing

  • When did the fever begin?
  • Was it present on admission?
  • Did it begin after surgery, transfusion, procedure, medication, or device insertion?
  • Is it isolated, intermittent, sustained, or progressive?

Symptoms

  • Headache or neck stiffness
  • Cough or dyspnea
  • Chest pain
  • Abdominal or flank pain
  • Diarrhea
  • Dysuria or urinary obstruction
  • Back or joint pain
  • Rash or mucosal symptoms
  • Line or wound pain

Exposure Review

  • Roommate or visitor illness
  • Hospital respiratory-virus activity
  • Travel before admission
  • Animal, food, water, or occupational exposure
  • Previous multidrug-resistant organisms

Medication Review

Review every new medication, including:

  • Antibiotics
  • Anticonvulsants
  • Anticoagulants
  • Allopurinol
  • Heparin
  • Biologic agents
  • Blood products
  • Contrast agents
  • PRN medications
  • Medication infusions

Procedure and Device Review

  • Surgery
  • Endoscopy
  • Central-line placement
  • Urinary catheter
  • Intubation
  • Thoracentesis or paracentesis
  • Drain placement
  • Dialysis access

Focused Physical Examination

General and Perfusion

  • Mental status
  • Work of breathing
  • Skin temperature and mottling
  • Capillary refill
  • Volume status

Skin

  • Rash
  • Petechiae
  • Pressure wounds
  • IV sites
  • Drug-injection sites
  • Intertriginous areas
  • Perineum
  • Signs of necrotizing infection

Devices

  • Central-line insertion site and tunnel
  • Peripheral IV sites
  • Urinary catheter
  • Tracheostomy
  • Chest tubes
  • Surgical drains
  • Pacemaker or port pocket

Cardiopulmonary Examination

  • New murmur
  • Signs of emboli
  • Focal lung findings
  • Pleural rub or effusion
  • New oxygen or ventilatory requirement

Abdomen and Pelvis

  • Distention
  • Focal tenderness
  • Guarding or rigidity
  • Murphy sign
  • Costovertebral-angle tenderness
  • Postoperative wounds
  • Rectal or pelvic examination when indicated

Musculoskeletal and Neurologic Examination

  • Spinal tenderness
  • Joint effusion
  • Focal weakness
  • Neck stiffness
  • Cranial neuropathy
  • Autonomic instability
  • Rigidity or clonus
A complete inspection of the skin, lines, wounds, back, sacrum, perineum, and oral cavity often yields more information than indiscriminate laboratory testing.

Differential Diagnosis

Infectious

  • Bloodstream or line infection
  • Pneumonia or empyema
  • Urinary infection or obstruction
  • Clostridioides difficile infection
  • Intra-abdominal abscess or leak
  • Cholangitis or cholecystitis
  • Endocarditis
  • Skin, wound, or surgical-site infection
  • Septic arthritis or osteomyelitis
  • Meningitis
  • Viral respiratory infection
  • Fungal infection

Noninfectious

  • Drug fever
  • Transfusion reaction
  • DVT or pulmonary embolism
  • Pancreatitis
  • Tissue ischemia or infarction
  • Postoperative inflammation
  • Central fever
  • Autoimmune disease
  • Malignancy
  • Adrenal crisis
  • Thyroid storm
  • Hyperthermic drug syndrome
  • Hematoma
  • Gout or pseudogout

Use the Hospital Timeline

Construct a One-Line Chronology

For example:

Hospital Day 6, Postoperative Day 3, Central-Line Day 5, Urinary-Catheter Day 6, Cefepime Day 4

Timeline Questions

  • Which devices were inserted?
  • Which antibiotics were started or stopped?
  • Was there a transfusion?
  • Was the patient intubated?
  • Did diarrhea begin after antibiotics?
  • Did fever begin after a new anticonvulsant?
  • Did the patient aspirate?
  • Was a drain manipulated?

Temporal Association Is Not Proof

A medication or procedure may precede fever without causing it. The timeline helps generate and prioritize hypotheses but does not establish causality.

When to Obtain Cultures

Culture When Bacteremia or Invasive Infection Is Plausible

  • Sepsis or septic shock
  • Rigors
  • Suspected endocarditis
  • Central-line infection
  • Severe pneumonia
  • Ascending cholangitis
  • Severe intra-abdominal infection
  • Neutropenic fever
  • Immunocompromised host with systemic illness
  • Persistent unexplained fever

Fever Alone Does Not Mandate Every Culture

Avoid automatic:

  • Blood cultures for every low-grade temperature
  • Urine cultures without urinary or systemic findings
  • Sputum cultures without a pneumonia syndrome
  • Wound cultures from an uninfected incision
  • Stool cultures without diarrhea

Culture Before Antibiotics

When feasible, obtain meaningful cultures before antimicrobial therapy, but do not delay treatment in an unstable patient.

Blood-Culture Strategy

Standard Collection

At Least 2 Sets From Separate Anatomic Sites

For critically ill adults, the preferred total collection is approximately 60 mL of blood, typically distributed among properly filled aerobic and anaerobic bottles.

Collection Principles

  • Use meticulous skin antisepsis.
  • Collect adequate blood volume.
  • Obtain sets sequentially without an unnecessary waiting interval.
  • Prefer peripheral venipuncture for routine cultures.
  • Label collection sites clearly.

Central-Line Evaluation

When catheter infection is suspected:

  • Obtain peripheral cultures.
  • Obtain simultaneous cultures from the central catheter.
  • Sample relevant catheter lumens.
  • Use differential time to positivity when available.

Interpreting Skin Flora

Possible contaminants include:

  • Coagulase-negative staphylococci
  • Corynebacterium species
  • Cutibacterium acnes
  • Bacillus species other than anthracis

True infection is more likely with:

  • Multiple positive sets
  • Rapid time to positivity
  • Intravascular device
  • Prosthetic material
  • Immunocompromise
  • Persistent positivity
Blood-culture contamination may lead to unnecessary vancomycin, echocardiography, line removal, prolonged hospitalization, and incorrect diagnoses.

When to Repeat Blood Cultures

Repeat Until Clearance With:

  • Staphylococcus aureus bacteremia
  • Candidemia
  • Endocarditis
  • Persistent fever or instability with initial bacteremia
  • Suspected infected vascular catheter that remains in place
  • Multidrug-resistant organism when clearance affects management
  • Endovascular prosthetic infection

Repeat Cultures Are Often Unnecessary With:

  • Uncomplicated improving gram-negative bacteremia
  • Source-controlled urinary bacteremia
  • Source-controlled biliary infection
  • Clinically improving cellulitis without endovascular concern

Persistent Positivity

Ongoing positive cultures should prompt evaluation for:

  • Endocarditis
  • Retained infected catheter
  • Suppurative thrombophlebitis
  • Undrained abscess
  • Infected prosthetic material
  • Vertebral or joint infection
  • Incorrect or inactive antibiotics

Urine Cultures

When Appropriate

  • Dysuria, urgency, or frequency
  • Suprapubic pain
  • Flank pain
  • Costovertebral-angle tenderness
  • Sepsis without another source
  • Urinary obstruction
  • Compatible catheter-associated infection

When Usually Inappropriate

  • Cloudy urine alone
  • Odorous urine alone
  • Pyuria alone
  • Delirium without urinary or systemic features
  • Routine fever workup without urinary suspicion

Catheterized Patients

When UTI is suspected and pyuria is present:

  • Replace an old urinary catheter when feasible.
  • Obtain urine from the newly placed catheter.
  • Never collect from the drainage bag.
Bacteriuria is extremely common with long-term urinary catheters and frequently distracts clinicians from the actual fever source.

Respiratory Testing

Test for Pneumonia When There Is:

  • New respiratory symptoms
  • New oxygen requirement
  • Purulent secretions
  • Worsening ventilator parameters
  • New or progressive pulmonary infiltrate

Potential Testing

  • Chest radiograph
  • Sputum or endotracheal aspirate culture
  • Respiratory viral NAAT
  • Blood cultures in severe pneumonia
  • CT or lung ultrasound when complications are suspected

Respiratory Viral Testing

Use viral NAAT when:

  • Cough or upper-respiratory symptoms occur
  • Pneumonia is suspected
  • Hospital transmission is possible
  • Seasonal epidemiology supports testing
  • Results affect isolation or antiviral therapy

Colonization

A positive endotracheal aspirate or tracheostomy culture does not diagnose pneumonia without a compatible respiratory and radiographic syndrome.

Stool Testing

C. difficile Testing

Test patients with:

  • New clinically significant unformed stools
  • No clear alternative explanation
  • Relevant antibiotic or healthcare exposure

Before Testing

  • Review laxatives.
  • Confirm stool consistency.
  • Review tube feeds and medications.
  • Review previous C. difficile results.

Other Stool Testing

Consider enteric testing with:

  • Bloody diarrhea
  • Severe abdominal pain
  • Outbreak concern
  • Immunocompromise
  • Persistent diarrhea
  • Travel or food exposure

Do Not Test:

  • Formed stool
  • Asymptomatic patients
  • For C. difficile cure
  • Repeatedly during the same diarrheal episode without a major change

Wound, Drain, and Device Cultures

Preferred Specimens

  • Aspirated pus
  • Deep tissue
  • Operative specimens
  • Freshly drained sterile-space fluid

Low-Value Specimens

  • Superficial chronic-wound swabs
  • Swabs from an intact postoperative incision
  • Culture from a long-standing drain
  • Culture of pressure injury without clinical infection

Why Chronic Drains Mislead

Drains become colonized with:

  • Gram-negative bacilli
  • Enterococcus
  • Staphylococci
  • Candida

Growth may represent the drain’s biofilm rather than the organisms within the original infected space.

Imaging Strategy

Chest Imaging

A chest radiograph is reasonable in most critically ill patients with new fever, especially with:

  • Respiratory symptoms
  • New hypoxemia
  • Mechanical ventilation
  • No obvious alternative source

Postoperative Imaging

After recent thoracic, abdominal, or pelvic surgery, obtain CT in collaboration with the surgical service when the initial evaluation does not identify the fever source.

Abdominal Imaging

Targeted abdominal ultrasound is appropriate with:

  • Right-upper-quadrant findings
  • Abnormal bilirubin or cholestatic enzymes
  • Recent abdominal surgery
  • Suspected biliary source

Routine abdominal ultrasound without abdominal symptoms, abnormal liver tests, recent surgery, or clinical suspicion has low value.

CT Abdomen and Pelvis

Consider with:

  • Persistent unexplained fever
  • Abdominal pain or distention
  • Postoperative ileus or tachycardia
  • Suspected abscess or leak
  • Neutropenic enterocolitis
  • Occult malignancy or inflammation

PET/CT

FDG-PET/CT may be useful for prolonged unexplained fever after standard evaluation, particularly when considering:

  • Endovascular infection
  • Vascular-graft infection
  • Malignancy
  • Large-vessel vasculitis
  • Occult inflammatory disease

Procalcitonin, CRP, and Other Biomarkers

Procalcitonin

Procalcitonin may support assessment when the probability of bacterial infection is low to intermediate and no clear focus is present.

Do Not Use a Low Procalcitonin to Exclude:

  • High-probability bacterial infection
  • Endocarditis
  • Localized abscess
  • Early infection
  • Severe immunocompromise
  • Selected atypical infections

Potential Noninfectious Elevation

  • Major surgery
  • Severe trauma
  • Burns
  • Cardiogenic shock
  • Renal dysfunction
  • Prolonged resuscitation

CRP and ESR

These are nonspecific and may remain elevated because of:

  • Infection
  • Surgery
  • Inflammatory disease
  • Malignancy
  • Tissue injury

Best Use

Biomarkers complement the bedside assessment. They should not override a convincing clinical syndrome or substitute for source control.

When to Start Empiric Antibiotics

Start Promptly With:

  • Septic shock
  • High-probability sepsis
  • Neutropenic fever
  • Suspected bacterial meningitis
  • Ascending cholangitis
  • Necrotizing infection
  • Severe hospital-acquired pneumonia
  • Unstable postoperative intra-abdominal infection
  • Severe infection in a profoundly immunocompromised patient

Empiric Selection Should Reflect:

  • Likely source
  • Severity
  • Previous cultures
  • Recent antibiotics
  • Local susceptibility
  • Immune status
  • Renal and hepatic function
  • Device or prosthetic material

Broad Therapy Is Not Automatically Better

Avoid unnecessary:

  • MRSA coverage
  • Dual antipseudomonal therapy
  • Anaerobic coverage
  • Empiric antifungal treatment
  • Prolonged antibiotic duration

Reassess Daily

Does Infection Still Seem Likely? → Can Therapy Be Narrowed or Stopped?

When Observation Without Immediate Antibiotics Is Reasonable

A time-limited diagnostic evaluation may be appropriate when:

  • The patient is hemodynamically stable.
  • There is no new organ dysfunction.
  • The likelihood of bacterial infection is low.
  • The fever is isolated or low grade.
  • A noninfectious explanation is plausible.
  • Close monitoring is available.

Examples

  • Single early postoperative temperature elevation
  • Suspected uncomplicated drug fever
  • Stable viral syndrome
  • Fever temporally related to transfusion after serious reactions are excluded
  • Inflammatory fever in a known rheumatologic disorder
Observation is not appropriate when the patient is unstable, neutropenic, profoundly immunocompromised, or has a high-risk focal syndrome.

Source Control

Potential Interventions

  • Remove an infected vascular catheter.
  • Replace or remove an infected urinary catheter.
  • Drain an abscess.
  • Debride necrotic tissue.
  • Perform ERCP for obstructed cholangitis.
  • Repair an anastomotic leak.
  • Extract infected cardiac hardware.
  • Wash out a septic joint.

Persistent Fever Is Often an Anatomy Problem

Active Antibiotics + Persistent Sepsis = Reassess Source Control

Do Not Delay

Patients with sepsis or shock and a source requiring intervention should receive source control as early as feasible.

Device-Associated Fever

Review Every Device Daily

  • Central venous catheter
  • Peripheral IV
  • Urinary catheter
  • Endotracheal tube
  • Tracheostomy
  • Chest tube
  • Surgical drain
  • External ventricular drain
  • Pacemaker or ICD
  • Vascular graft
  • Orthopedic hardware

Ask:

  • Is it still needed?
  • When was it inserted?
  • Has it been manipulated?
  • Is it functioning?
  • Is there pain, erythema, drainage, or thrombosis?
  • Can it be removed safely?
Removal of an unnecessary device is both a diagnostic and therapeutic intervention.

Central Venous Catheters

Clues to Catheter Infection

  • Rigors during infusion
  • Unexplained bacteremia
  • Exit-site purulence
  • Tunnel tenderness
  • Port-pocket inflammation
  • Persistent bacteremia
  • No alternative source

Evaluation

  • Peripheral blood cultures
  • Central-line cultures
  • Sampling of relevant lumens
  • Differential time to positivity
  • Ultrasound for septic thrombophlebitis when indicated

Removal Is Strongly Favored With:

  • S. aureus
  • Pseudomonas
  • Candida
  • Mycobacteria
  • Severe sepsis
  • Tunnel or pocket infection
  • Persistent positive cultures

Urinary Catheters

Common Problem

Bacteriuria develops progressively with catheter duration and frequently leads to false attribution of fever to the urinary tract.

Assess for True Infection

  • Flank pain
  • Costovertebral-angle tenderness
  • Pelvic or suprapubic discomfort
  • Acute hematuria
  • Sepsis without another source

Management

  • Remove the catheter if unnecessary.
  • Replace an older catheter when infection is suspected and continued drainage is needed.
  • Obtain urine from the new catheter.
  • Relieve obstruction.

Endotracheal Tubes and Tracheostomies

Colonization Is Expected

Airway devices become colonized rapidly. Purulent-appearing secretions and positive cultures may occur without pneumonia.

Diagnose Pneumonia Using the Entire Syndrome

  • New infiltrate
  • New fever or inflammatory change
  • Worsening oxygenation
  • Purulent secretions
  • Compatible microbiology

Other Airway-Related Fever Sources

  • Tracheostomy-site infection
  • Sinusitis with prolonged nasal instrumentation
  • Aspiration
  • Empyema
  • Ventilator-associated tracheobronchitis

Surgical Drains and Implanted Hardware

Drains

Inspect for:

  • Purulent output
  • Enteric or biliary output
  • Loss of output suggesting obstruction
  • Dislodgement
  • Local inflammation
  • Residual undrained collection

Implanted Devices

Persistent fever or bacteremia may arise from:

  • Pacemaker or ICD leads
  • Prosthetic valves
  • Vascular grafts
  • Prosthetic joints
  • CSF shunts

Biofilm

When foreign material is truly infected, antibiotics alone may suppress but often cannot eradicate biofilm. Extraction or revision may be required.

Postoperative Fever

First Principle

The probability of infection depends more on the patient’s physiology and operative course than on a memorized postoperative-day list.

Important Questions

  • Was the operation clean, contaminated, or emergent?
  • Was there bowel perforation or infected tissue?
  • Were prosthetic materials placed?
  • Were there intraoperative complications?
  • Is recovery proceeding as expected?
  • Is there persistent tachycardia, ileus, hypotension, or increasing pain?

Do Not Use the “Five Ws” as a Diagnostic Rule

The traditional day-based “wind, water, wound, walking, wonder drugs” framework is oversimplified. Serious infection may occur early, and noninfectious fever may occur late.

Atelectasis

Atelectasis commonly occurs after surgery but is not a reliable explanation for significant postoperative fever. Do not stop the evaluation when a radiograph shows mild atelectatic change.

Early Postoperative Fever

Common Noninfectious Explanations

  • Inflammatory response to surgery
  • Hematoma
  • Transfusion reaction
  • Medication reaction
  • Pancreatitis
  • Tissue injury

Serious Infections Can Occur Early

  • Preexisting infection
  • Perforation or anastomotic leak
  • Necrotizing soft-tissue infection
  • Aspiration pneumonia
  • Catheter-related infection
  • Clostridial or streptococcal surgical infection

Red Flags

  • Severe pain out of proportion
  • Rapidly spreading erythema
  • Bullae or crepitus
  • Persistent tachycardia
  • Hypotension
  • New organ dysfunction
  • Progressive abdominal distention
  • Enteric drain output
Do not attribute early postoperative shock or progressive organ dysfunction to a benign inflammatory fever.

Later Postoperative Fever

Increasingly Consider:

  • Surgical-site infection
  • Organ-space abscess
  • Anastomotic leak
  • Hospital-acquired pneumonia
  • Catheter-associated infection
  • C. difficile infection
  • DVT or pulmonary embolism
  • Drug fever

Failure to Progress

Persistent postoperative findings that should trigger deeper evaluation include:

  • Failure of bowel function to return
  • Persistent tachycardia
  • Increasing analgesic requirement
  • New oxygen requirement
  • Unable to advance diet
  • Wound dehiscence
  • Unexpected drainage

Postoperative Fever Workup

Stable Patient With an Isolated Early Fever

  • Repeat temperature.
  • Review symptoms and operative course.
  • Perform focused examination.
  • Review medications and transfusions.
  • Observe closely if no concerning findings exist.

Escalate Testing With:

  • Persistent or recurrent fever
  • Systemic inflammatory response
  • Organ dysfunction
  • Localized symptoms
  • Unexpected postoperative course
  • High-risk surgery

Potential Tests

  • Blood cultures
  • Chest imaging
  • Urinalysis and culture when clinically indicated
  • CT of the operative region
  • Deep wound or fluid culture
  • Venous ultrasound or CT pulmonary angiography when thrombosis is suspected

Persistent or Recurrent Fever

Definitions Are Clinical

Persistent fever may mean:

  • Daily or sustained fever despite treatment
  • Failure to defervesce within the expected period
  • Recurrent fever after temporary improvement
  • New fever after an afebrile interval

Major Categories

  • Incorrect initial diagnosis
  • Uncontrolled infectious source
  • Inactive or inadequately dosed antibiotics
  • New hospital-acquired infection
  • Drug fever
  • Thromboembolism
  • Inflammatory or malignant disease

Fever Pattern Is Rarely Diagnostic

Intermittent, remittent, sustained, or nocturnal patterns may provide clues but generally do not identify a specific cause without supporting clinical findings.

Fever Despite Antibiotics

Do Not Automatically Broaden Therapy

Ask whether the problem is:

  1. Wrong diagnosis
  2. Wrong source
  3. Wrong organism
  4. Wrong antibiotic
  5. Wrong dose or exposure
  6. Inadequate source control
  7. New infection
  8. Noninfectious fever

Antibiotic Problems

  • Intrinsic resistance
  • Acquired resistance
  • Inadequate tissue penetration
  • Renal underdosing
  • Obesity-related underexposure
  • Drug interaction
  • Malabsorption
  • Failure to administer scheduled doses

Expected Slow Defervescence

Some infections may improve clinically before temperature fully normalizes, including:

  • Pyelonephritis
  • Severe pneumonia
  • Cellulitis
  • Endocarditis
  • Deep abscess after drainage

Use the Whole Trajectory

  • Hemodynamics
  • Oxygenation
  • Pain
  • Mental status
  • WBC trend
  • Culture clearance
  • Organ function
Persistent fever alone does not prove antibiotic failure, but persistent bacteremia, organ dysfunction, or clinical deterioration requires urgent reassessment.

Approach to Fever After 72 Hours

Step 1: Reconstruct the Case

  • Original diagnosis
  • All microbiology
  • All imaging
  • Procedures and devices
  • Antibiotic start dates
  • New medications
  • Clinical response

Step 2: Re-Examine the Patient Completely

  • Lines and peripheral IVs
  • Oral cavity
  • Lungs and pleura
  • Abdomen
  • Wounds and drains
  • Back and joints
  • Skin and perineum
  • Neurologic findings

Step 3: Reconsider the Source

  • Undrained abscess
  • Endocarditis
  • Infected thrombus
  • Prosthetic infection
  • Empyema
  • Obstructed urinary or biliary system
  • Postoperative leak

Step 4: Review Treatment Adequacy

  • Susceptibility
  • Dose
  • Renal adjustment
  • Infusion method
  • Drug interaction
  • Adherence and administration record

Step 5: Search for a New Hospital-Acquired Process

  • Central-line infection
  • CAUTI
  • HAP or VAP
  • C. difficile
  • Pressure-wound infection
  • Fungal infection

Step 6: Reconsider Noninfectious Causes

  • Drug fever
  • DVT or PE
  • Transfusion reaction
  • Central fever
  • Pancreatitis
  • Autoimmune disease
  • Malignancy
After 72 Hours: Repeat the Reasoning, Not Just the Same Tests

Drug Fever

Commonly Implicated Medications

  • Beta-lactam antibiotics
  • Sulfonamides
  • Vancomycin
  • Anticonvulsants
  • Allopurinol
  • Heparin
  • Antiarrhythmics
  • Biologic agents
  • Selected antineoplastic drugs

Clinical Clues

  • Fever begins after a new medication.
  • Infection appears to be improving.
  • Cultures and imaging remain unrevealing.
  • Patient may appear better than expected for the temperature.
  • Rash or eosinophilia may occur.
  • Liver-test abnormalities may occur.

Features Are Not Required

Absence of rash, eosinophilia, or relative bradycardia does not exclude drug fever. These findings are neither sufficiently sensitive nor specific.

Diagnosis

Drug fever is a diagnosis of exclusion supported by:

  • A plausible medication timeline
  • No better infectious or inflammatory explanation
  • Defervescence after medication withdrawal

Management

  • Stop the most likely nonessential medication.
  • Avoid stopping essential treatment without an alternative plan.
  • Evaluate for DRESS, Stevens–Johnson syndrome, interstitial nephritis, hepatitis, and cytopenias.
  • Monitor for improvement over the following days.

Rechallenge

Intentional rechallenge is generally avoided when the suspected reaction could be severe.

Clostridioides difficile as a New Fever Source

When to Suspect

  • New diarrhea
  • Recent or ongoing antibiotics
  • Abdominal pain
  • Leukocytosis
  • Ileus or distention
  • Unexplained shock

Ileus

Fulminant C. difficile may cause ileus with little or no diarrhea. Worsening abdominal distention and shock should not be dismissed because stool output has stopped.

Testing

  • Test only appropriate unformed stool.
  • Review laxatives.
  • Do not order a test of cure.
  • Interpret NAAT with toxin results and the clinical syndrome.

Fungal Infection

Risk Factors

  • Prolonged hospitalization
  • Broad-spectrum antibiotics
  • Central venous catheter
  • Parenteral nutrition
  • Neutropenia
  • Transplantation
  • Recurrent bowel perforation
  • Necrotizing pancreatitis
  • Recent abdominal surgery

Potential Syndromes

  • Candidemia
  • Intra-abdominal candidiasis
  • Invasive aspergillosis
  • Endemic fungal infection
  • Device-associated fungal infection

Empiric Antifungals

Do not administer empiric antifungal therapy solely because fever persists. Consider it when:

  • The patient is severely ill.
  • Strong fungal risk factors exist.
  • No alternative explanation is identified.
  • Appropriate cultures and source evaluation are underway.

Occult Abscess and Inadequate Source Control

Potential Locations

  • Intra-abdominal or pelvic space
  • Liver or spleen
  • Kidney or perinephric space
  • Prostate
  • Psoas muscle
  • Paraspinal or epidural space
  • Pleural space
  • Deep surgical site

Clues

  • Persistent focal pain
  • Continued bacteremia
  • Persistent ileus
  • Unexplained anemia
  • Failure to improve despite active antibiotics
  • Previous surgery, trauma, or bacteremia

Management

Localize the Collection → Drain It → Culture It → Narrow Therapy

Endocarditis and Endovascular Infection

Consider With:

  • Persistent bacteremia
  • S. aureus bacteremia
  • Enterococcus faecalis bacteremia
  • Candidemia
  • Prosthetic valve
  • Pacemaker or ICD
  • New murmur
  • Embolic phenomena
  • Vertebral pain

Evaluation

  • Repeat blood cultures
  • Transthoracic echocardiography
  • Transesophageal echocardiography when indicated
  • Examination for embolic and metastatic disease
  • Imaging of symptomatic spine, joints, brain, or abdomen

Other Endovascular Sources

  • Suppurative thrombophlebitis
  • Infected vascular graft
  • Mycotic aneurysm
  • Septic pelvic thrombophlebitis

Major Noninfectious Causes

Category Examples
Medication related Drug fever, DRESS, serotonin syndrome, neuroleptic malignant syndrome
Thrombotic DVT, pulmonary embolism, septic or sterile thrombophlebitis
Tissue injury Surgery, trauma, hematoma, pancreatitis, ischemia, infarction
Neurologic Subarachnoid hemorrhage, intracerebral hemorrhage, severe brain injury

DVT and Pulmonary Embolism

Clinical Clues

  • New unilateral limb swelling
  • Line-associated arm swelling
  • Pleuritic chest pain
  • Unexplained tachycardia
  • New hypoxemia
  • Hemoptysis
  • Persistent low-grade fever without another source

Important Nuance

Venous thromboembolism can cause fever, but fever should not be attributed to PE or DVT without objective evaluation and consideration of infection.

Septic Thrombophlebitis

Consider when thrombosis is accompanied by:

  • Persistent bacteremia
  • Central or peripheral catheter
  • Local vein tenderness
  • S. aureus, Candida, or gram-negative bacteremia

Fever During or After Transfusion

Immediate Action

Stop the Transfusion and Evaluate the Reaction

Do Not Immediately Assume a Benign Febrile Reaction

Fever or rigors during transfusion may represent:

  • Febrile nonhemolytic transfusion reaction
  • Acute hemolytic reaction
  • Bacterial contamination of the blood product
  • Transfusion-related acute lung injury
  • Another concurrent infection

Initial Steps

  • Stop the transfusion.
  • Maintain IV access with appropriate fluid.
  • Recheck patient and product identification.
  • Notify the blood bank.
  • Assess for hypotension, pain, dyspnea, hemoglobinuria, and shock.
  • Send the recommended transfusion-reaction specimens.

Septic Transfusion Reaction

Suspect with:

  • High fever
  • Severe rigors
  • Hypotension
  • Nausea or vomiting
  • Rapid deterioration

Obtain patient blood cultures and culture the product according to transfusion-service protocol.

Central Fever

Associated Neurologic Conditions

  • Subarachnoid hemorrhage
  • Intracerebral hemorrhage
  • Severe traumatic brain injury
  • Large ischemic stroke
  • Hypoxic-ischemic injury
  • Intraventricular hemorrhage

Possible Clues

  • Begins early after neurologic injury
  • High and persistent temperature
  • No localizing infectious syndrome
  • Repeated negative cultures
  • Relative resistance to standard antipyretics
  • Associated autonomic dysfunction

Diagnosis of Exclusion

There is no single diagnostic test for central fever. Pneumonia, line infection, urinary infection, ventriculitis, C. difficile, thrombosis, and drug fever must be considered first.

Paroxysmal Sympathetic Hyperactivity

Fever may occur with episodes of:

  • Tachycardia
  • Hypertension
  • Tachypnea
  • Diaphoresis
  • Dystonia or posturing

Acalculous Cholecystitis

Risk Factors

  • Critical illness
  • Trauma
  • Burns
  • Prolonged fasting
  • Parenteral nutrition
  • Mechanical ventilation
  • Vasopressor use

Clinical Presentation

  • Unexplained fever or sepsis
  • Right-upper-quadrant tenderness
  • Cholestatic liver-test abnormalities
  • Abdominal distention
  • May lack classic symptoms in sedated patients

Diagnosis

  • Right-upper-quadrant ultrasound
  • HIDA scan when needed
  • CT in selected cases

Management

  • Broad appropriate antibiotics
  • Cholecystectomy when feasible
  • Percutaneous cholecystostomy in selected unstable patients

Pancreatitis, Hematoma, and Tissue Ischemia

Pancreatitis

Acute pancreatitis may cause:

  • Fever
  • Leukocytosis
  • Tachycardia
  • Organ dysfunction
Sterile pancreatic necrosis may produce substantial inflammation. Do not prescribe prophylactic antibiotics solely because necrosis is present.

Infected Necrosis

Consider when there is:

  • Clinical deterioration after initial improvement
  • Gas in a collection
  • Bacteremia
  • Persistent or recurrent organ failure

Tissue Ischemia or Infarction

  • Mesenteric ischemia
  • Myocardial infarction
  • Pulmonary infarction
  • Splenic infarction
  • Large hematoma
  • Limb ischemia

These may cause fever and inflammatory-marker elevation without primary infection.

Inflammatory and Autoimmune Causes

Potential Diagnoses

  • Adult-onset Still disease
  • Giant-cell arteritis
  • Other systemic vasculitis
  • Systemic lupus erythematosus
  • Inflammatory bowel disease
  • Sarcoidosis
  • Gout or pseudogout
  • Sweet syndrome
  • Behçet disease

Clinical Clues

  • Rash
  • Arthritis
  • Myalgia
  • Temporal headache
  • Jaw claudication
  • Oral or genital ulcers
  • Pulmonary-renal syndrome
  • Very high ferritin

Important Caution

Do not start high-dose immunosuppression for presumed inflammatory fever until major infections have been reasonably excluded.

Malignancy and Hyperinflammatory Syndromes

Malignancy-Associated Fever

Particularly consider:

  • Lymphoma
  • Leukemia
  • Renal-cell carcinoma
  • Hepatocellular carcinoma
  • Large necrotic tumors

Hemophagocytic Lymphohistiocytosis

Potential clues include:

  • Persistent fever
  • Cytopenias
  • Hepatosplenomegaly
  • Marked ferritin elevation
  • Hypertriglyceridemia
  • Low fibrinogen
  • Hepatitis

Tumor Lysis and Cytokine Release

Fever may accompany:

  • Cellular therapy
  • Bispecific antibodies
  • Rapid tumor breakdown
  • Immune-checkpoint therapy

These syndromes may coexist with infection and require protocol-specific evaluation.

Endocrine and Metabolic Emergencies

Thyroid Storm

  • High fever
  • Severe tachycardia or atrial fibrillation
  • Heart failure
  • Agitation or delirium
  • GI or hepatic dysfunction

Adrenal Crisis

  • Fever
  • Hypotension
  • Hyponatremia
  • Hyperkalemia
  • Hypoglycemia
  • Abdominal symptoms

Medication Withdrawal

Fever and autonomic instability may occur with withdrawal from:

  • Alcohol
  • Benzodiazepines
  • Baclofen
  • Dopaminergic medications

Special Populations

Standard fever thresholds and diagnostic strategies may be less reliable in:

  • Neutropenic patients
  • Transplant recipients
  • Older adults
  • Patients with acute brain injury
  • Patients receiving immunosuppressive therapy

The threshold for urgent evaluation is generally lower because clinical signs may be muted and disease progression rapid.

Neutropenic Fever

Common Definition

Single Oral Temperature ≥38.3°C or ≥38.0°C Sustained for at Least 1 Hour

with:

ANC <500 Cells/µL or Expected to Fall Below 500 Cells/µL

Immediate Management

  • Obtain blood cultures promptly.
  • Culture all relevant central-line lumens and obtain peripheral cultures.
  • Begin an antipseudomonal beta-lactam immediately.
  • Do not wait for localizing symptoms.

Potential Initial Agents

  • Cefepime
  • Piperacillin-tazobactam
  • Meropenem or imipenem in selected patients

Vancomycin Is Not Automatic

Add resistant gram-positive coverage with:

  • Hemodynamic instability
  • Suspected catheter infection
  • Skin or soft-tissue infection
  • Pneumonia
  • Known MRSA colonization with compatible illness
  • Positive gram-positive blood culture pending identification

Examination

Avoid rectal examination, rectal temperature measurement, suppositories, and enemas during severe neutropenia because of mucosal injury and infection risk.

Transplant and Profound Immunocompromise

Consider Opportunistic and Atypical Infection

  • CMV
  • Invasive mold infection
  • Pneumocystis
  • Nocardia
  • Mycobacterial infection
  • Disseminated viral infection
  • Endemic fungi

Important Nuances

  • Fever may be absent.
  • Imaging findings may be subtle.
  • Multiple infections may coexist.
  • Drug interactions are common.
  • Early tissue diagnosis may be necessary.

Noninfectious Mimics

  • Rejection
  • Graft-versus-host disease
  • Drug toxicity
  • Engraftment syndrome
  • Cytokine-release syndrome

Older Adults

Possible Presentations of Infection

  • Functional decline
  • Delirium
  • Falls
  • Anorexia
  • Hypothermia
  • New incontinence
  • Unexplained hyperglycemia

Avoid Anchoring on Bacteriuria

A positive urine culture is common in older hospitalized adults and should not be assumed to explain delirium or fever without compatible urinary or systemic findings.

Compare With Baseline

A rise of approximately 1.1°C above the patient’s usual baseline may be clinically meaningful even when the absolute temperature is not high.

Neurocritical Care

Why Fever Matters

Elevated temperature may increase cerebral metabolic demand and worsen secondary injury after:

  • Stroke
  • Intracranial hemorrhage
  • Traumatic brain injury
  • Cardiac arrest

Potential Causes

  • Pneumonia
  • Central-line infection
  • Urinary infection
  • Ventriculitis
  • Central fever
  • Paroxysmal sympathetic hyperactivity
  • Drug fever
  • DVT or PE

Temperature Management

More active temperature control may be appropriate in neurologically injured patients, even though routine fever suppression has not consistently improved outcomes in general sepsis populations.

Antipyretics and Cooling

Antipyretics

  • Acetaminophen may improve comfort.
  • NSAIDs may reduce fever but have renal, gastrointestinal, and bleeding risks.
  • Routine suppression solely to improve sepsis outcomes is not generally recommended.

Reasons to Treat Temperature

  • Patient discomfort
  • Severe tachycardia or cardiopulmonary stress
  • Neurologic injury
  • Very high temperature
  • Pregnancy-specific concerns

Cooling

Physical cooling is essential in hyperthermia and may be used selectively for severe fever when clinically indicated.

Do Not Mask the Clinical Course

Document:

  • Temperature before intervention
  • Medication administered
  • Response
  • Associated hemodynamic changes
The response to acetaminophen does not distinguish infectious from noninfectious fever.

How to Document a Hospital Fever

Useful Assessment

Document:

  • Highest confirmed temperature and measurement method
  • Associated hemodynamics and organ dysfunction
  • Localizing symptoms and examination findings
  • Device presence and necessity
  • Recent procedures, transfusions, and medications
  • Tests obtained and rationale
  • Whether antibiotics were started, withheld, narrowed, or stopped
  • Source-control plan

Example

New fever to 38.6°C orally on hospital day 5. Hemodynamically stable without new oxygen requirement. No urinary symptoms or diarrhea. PICC site is normal, but line remains a possible source. Obtaining two peripheral culture sets and simultaneous PICC cultures. Holding urine culture and empiric antibiotics pending focused evaluation because bacterial infection probability is presently low to intermediate and the patient can be closely monitored.

Daily Fever Checklist

  • Was the temperature confirmed with a reliable method?
  • Is the patient unstable or developing organ dysfunction?
  • Is this sepsis, hyperthermia, or isolated fever?
  • What changed immediately before the fever?
  • Are there localizing symptoms?
  • Were the skin, lines, wounds, sacrum, and perineum examined?
  • Which devices are present, and are they still needed?
  • Are blood cultures indicated?
  • Is urine testing actually indicated?
  • Is there a respiratory or diarrheal syndrome?
  • Does the patient need imaging?
  • Is there a source requiring drainage or removal?
  • Are current antibiotics active and correctly dosed?
  • Could this be drug fever?
  • Could this be DVT, PE, transfusion reaction, or central fever?
  • Can antibiotics be narrowed or stopped?
  • What is the plan if fever continues for another 24–48 hours?

How to Present on ID Rounds

  • Hospital day and postoperative day
  • Maximum temperature and measurement method
  • Hemodynamic and respiratory status
  • Device days
  • New medications
  • Localizing findings
  • Culture and imaging results
  • Current antibiotics and treatment day
  • Source-control status
  • Infectious and noninfectious differential

Common Clinical Scenarios

Case 1: Single Temperature of 38.1°C

A stable ward patient has one temporal-artery temperature of 38.1°C without symptoms or organ dysfunction.

Repeat the temperature using a more reliable method and reassess clinically before ordering automatic cultures and broad antibiotics.

Case 2: Fever and Septic Shock

A patient develops fever, hypotension, confusion, and rising lactate.

Obtain appropriate cultures immediately, begin resuscitation and empiric antibiotics, and search urgently for a source requiring control.

Case 3: Fever With a Central Line

An ICU patient with a central catheter has unexplained fever.

Obtain simultaneous peripheral and catheter cultures, including relevant lumens, rather than drawing all cultures through the line.

Case 4: Delirium and Positive Urinalysis

An older patient has delirium and pyuria but no urinary symptoms, fever, hypotension, or leukocytosis.

Do not automatically diagnose UTI. Review medications, metabolic abnormalities, dehydration, pain, retention, and other delirium causes.

Case 5: Early Postoperative Fever

A stable patient has a temperature of 38.2°C twelve hours after uncomplicated surgery and has no localizing findings.

A focused examination and observation may be appropriate. Routine pan-culturing and broad antibiotics are not automatically required.

Case 6: Early Postoperative Fever With Shock

A patient develops severe wound pain, tachycardia, hypotension, and fever eight hours after surgery.

Do not label this benign postoperative fever. Evaluate immediately for necrotizing infection, uncontrolled source, hemorrhage, and other surgical emergencies.

Case 7: Persistent Fever on Ceftriaxone

A patient treated for pyelonephritis remains febrile after 72 hours.

Review susceptibility, dosing, obstruction, renal or perinephric abscess, incorrect diagnosis, and drug fever before simply adding vancomycin.

Case 8: Fever After Piperacillin-Tazobactam

A clinically improving patient develops persistent fever and rash after nine days of therapy. Cultures remain negative.

Consider drug fever and hypersensitivity. Review eosinophils, liver and kidney function, and stop or replace the suspected drug when safe.

Case 9: Fever During Blood Transfusion

The patient develops fever and rigors twenty minutes into a platelet transfusion.

Stop the transfusion and notify the blood bank. Exclude hemolytic and septic transfusion reactions before labeling it febrile nonhemolytic.

Case 10: Fever After Subarachnoid Hemorrhage

A patient develops persistent high fever after aneurysmal subarachnoid hemorrhage. Cultures and imaging are unrevealing.

Central fever is possible, but it remains a diagnosis of exclusion. Reassess for pneumonia, ventriculitis, line infection, urinary infection, and thrombosis.

Case 11: Persistent Fever and Arm Swelling

A patient with a PICC has fever and unilateral arm swelling despite negative initial cultures.

Evaluate for catheter-associated thrombosis and suppurative thrombophlebitis.

Case 12: Ventilated Patient With Positive Sputum Culture

A ventilated patient has a positive respiratory culture but stable oxygenation and no new infiltrate.

This is more consistent with airway colonization than VAP. Do not treat the culture in isolation.

Case 13: Persistent Fever After Abdominal Surgery

A postoperative patient has fever, tachycardia, ileus, and increasing abdominal pain on day four.

Obtain urgent surgical reassessment and cross-sectional imaging for an anastomotic leak or organ-space infection.

Case 14: Fever With Pancreatic Necrosis

A patient has severe pancreatitis, fever, leukocytosis, and sterile-appearing necrosis early in the course.

Pancreatic inflammation can produce fever. Do not give prophylactic antibiotics unless infected necrosis or another infection becomes likely.

Case 15: Neutropenic Fever

A patient receiving leukemia chemotherapy has an ANC of 100 cells/µL and an oral temperature of 38.4°C.

Obtain cultures promptly and begin an antipseudomonal beta-lactam immediately. Do not wait for additional symptoms.

Common Pitfalls

  • Failing to confirm an inaccurate temperature reading
  • Assuming every fever is infection
  • Assuming absence of fever excludes infection
  • Automatically ordering blood, urine, sputum, and stool cultures for every temperature elevation
  • Drawing all blood cultures through one central line
  • Collecting inadequate blood-culture volume
  • Collecting urine from a catheter drainage bag
  • Treating pyuria or bacteriuria without a compatible syndrome
  • Treating airway colonization as pneumonia
  • Ordering C. difficile testing for formed stool
  • Using a positive chronic-drain culture to guide broad therapy
  • Starting broad antibiotics in a stable low-risk patient without focused assessment
  • Delaying antibiotics in septic shock while obtaining extensive testing
  • Using procalcitonin to rule out a high-probability infection
  • Broadening antibiotics without checking source control
  • Failing to remove an unnecessary catheter
  • Assuming early postoperative fever is always benign
  • Assuming atelectasis explains significant fever
  • Using the “five Ws” as a rigid postoperative rule
  • Diagnosing drug fever only when rash or eosinophilia is present
  • Failing to evaluate DRESS or organ injury in suspected drug fever
  • Attributing fever to DVT or PE without objective evidence
  • Calling fever during transfusion a benign reaction before excluding hemolysis or contamination
  • Diagnosing central fever before excluding infection
  • Giving prophylactic antibiotics for sterile pancreatic necrosis
  • Continuing unnecessary antibiotics because fever has not fully resolved
  • Failing to repeat cultures in S. aureus bacteremia or candidemia
  • Failing to examine the sacrum, perineum, IV sites, wounds, and back

Board and ID Consult Pearls

  • A single temperature of at least 38.3°C is a commonly used ICU fever definition.
  • Verify unexpected temperatures with an accurate method.
  • Central temperature measurements are preferred when accurate measurement is clinically critical.
  • Fever is not synonymous with infection.
  • Hypothermia may represent severe infection.
  • Fever alone does not require blood cultures.
  • Obtain at least two blood-culture sets from separate sites when cultures are indicated.
  • Adequate blood volume is essential for blood-culture sensitivity.
  • When a central line is a possible source, obtain simultaneous peripheral and catheter cultures.
  • A catheter culture becoming positive at least two hours earlier supports catheter-related infection.
  • Blood-culture contamination leads to unnecessary antibiotics and procedures.
  • Repeat blood cultures for S. aureus bacteremia and candidemia.
  • Routine repeat cultures are unnecessary for many improving uncomplicated gram-negative bacteremias.
  • Pyuria does not diagnose UTI in a catheterized patient.
  • Never collect a urine culture from the drainage bag.
  • A positive respiratory culture does not diagnose HAP or VAP.
  • Test for respiratory viruses when respiratory symptoms or pneumonia are present.
  • Procalcitonin cannot safely exclude a high-probability infection.
  • Septic shock requires immediate cultures, antibiotics, resuscitation, and source assessment.
  • Stable patients with low infection probability may undergo time-limited evaluation without immediate antibiotics.
  • Source control is often more important than broader antibiotic coverage.
  • Early postoperative fever is often inflammatory but can represent serious infection.
  • Atelectasis is not a reliable explanation for significant postoperative fever.
  • The “five Ws” are not a dependable postoperative diagnostic rule.
  • Persistent fever on antibiotics should trigger diagnostic reassessment rather than automatic broadening.
  • After 72 hours, reconsider diagnosis, source control, drug exposure, thrombosis, and new hospital infection.
  • Drug fever may occur without rash, eosinophilia, or relative bradycardia.
  • Drug fever is supported by defervescence after stopping the responsible medication.
  • Fever during transfusion requires immediate interruption and blood-bank evaluation.
  • Central fever is a diagnosis of exclusion.
  • Acalculous cholecystitis may present as unexplained fever in a critically ill patient.
  • Sterile pancreatic necrosis does not require prophylactic antibiotics.
  • Neutropenic fever requires immediate antipseudomonal therapy.
  • Vancomycin is not automatically required for every neutropenic fever.
  • Antipyretic response does not distinguish infectious from noninfectious fever.
  • Daily removal of unnecessary devices prevents future hospital fever syndromes.

Bottom Line

New fever in a hospitalized patient requires confirmation of the temperature, immediate assessment of physiologic stability, review of the hospital timeline, focused examination, and targeted diagnostic testing. Fever is neither necessary nor sufficient for diagnosing infection.

Patients with sepsis, shock, neutropenia, severe immune suppression, meningitis, necrotizing infection, or another high-risk syndrome need prompt cultures and empiric therapy. Stable patients with a low likelihood of bacterial infection may be observed during a time-limited evaluation without automatic broad-spectrum antibiotics.

Blood cultures should be obtained in properly filled sets from separate sites when bacteremia is plausible. Urine, respiratory, wound, drain, and stool cultures should be ordered only when the corresponding clinical syndrome exists. Avoid treating colonization, contamination, pyuria, asymptomatic bacteriuria, chronic-drain flora, or airway colonization.

Persistent fever despite antibiotics should trigger reassessment of the diagnosis, source control, antimicrobial activity, dosing, new hospital-acquired infection, drug fever, thrombosis, transfusion reaction, inflammatory disease, and malignancy. After approximately 72 hours, repeat the reasoning rather than automatically repeating the same cultures and broadening therapy.

Postoperative fever should not be managed by a rigid day-based mnemonic. Isolated early fever may be inflammatory, but shock, severe pain, persistent tachycardia, ileus, wound changes, or organ dysfunction require urgent investigation for surgical complications. Central fever, drug fever, thrombosis, and transfusion reactions remain diagnoses requiring careful exclusion of dangerous infection.

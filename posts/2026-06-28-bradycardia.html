Bradycardia and Conduction System Disease: The Complete Resident Guide

Added June 25, 2026 • Cardiology • ~20 min read

Bradycardia is not managed by heart rate alone. The key question is whether the slow rhythm is physiologic or pathologic, and whether the patient is symptomatic or unstable. This guide reviews ACLS bradycardia management, atropine, chronotropic infusions, transcutaneous pacing, transvenous pacing, sinus bradycardia, AV blocks, post-MI conduction disease, permanent pacemaker indications, and high-yield bedside pearls for residents.

What Is Bradycardia?

Bradycardia is usually defined as a heart rate less than 60 beats per minute. However, the clinical importance of bradycardia depends less on the number itself and more on the patient’s symptoms, perfusion, and underlying rhythm.

Type Examples Clinical Meaning
Physiologic bradycardia Athletes, sleep, high vagal tone, vasovagal response Often benign if asymptomatic with appropriate chronotropic response
Pathologic bradycardia Sinus node dysfunction, AV block, ischemia, medication effect, metabolic disease May require urgent treatment or permanent pacing
Rate is slow, but symptoms drive management. A heart rate of 38 in an athlete may be normal, while a heart rate of 45 with hypotension and altered mental status is an emergency.

ACLS Bradycardia Algorithm

The first decision in bradycardia is whether the patient is stable or unstable.

Unstable Bradycardia

Unstable bradycardia requires immediate treatment regardless of the exact mechanism.

  • Hypotension
  • Altered mental status
  • Signs of shock
  • Ischemic chest pain
  • Acute heart failure
  • Syncope or near-syncope from poor perfusion

Stable Bradycardia

Stable bradycardia can be monitored while identifying the cause.

  • Obtain 12-lead ECG
  • Review medications
  • Check electrolytes
  • Assess thyroid function when appropriate
  • Evaluate ischemia if clinically suspected
  • Monitor for progression

Acute Management of Symptomatic Bradycardia

Step 1: ABCs and Initial Assessment

  • Assess airway, breathing, and circulation.
  • Give oxygen only if hypoxemic or in respiratory distress.
  • Place the patient on monitor and obtain vital signs.
  • Establish IV access.
  • Obtain a 12-lead ECG if it does not delay urgent treatment.
  • Identify the rhythm: sinus bradycardia, AV block, junctional escape, or ventricular escape.
Do not delay treatment of unstable bradycardia while waiting for a perfect ECG interpretation.

Step 2: Atropine

Atropine is the first-line medication for many cases of symptomatic bradycardia.

Step 3: Chronotropic Infusion or Pacing

If atropine fails, move quickly to transcutaneous pacing or chronotropic infusion while preparing for transvenous pacing when indicated.

Step 4: Transcutaneous Pacing

Transcutaneous pacing is a bridge for unstable bradycardia, especially high-grade AV block.

Step 5: Transvenous Pacing

Transvenous pacing is used when transcutaneous pacing fails, is not tolerated, or the bradycardia is expected to persist.

Atropine

Dose Route Frequency Maximum
0.5 mg IV push Every 3–5 minutes 3 mg total

Mechanism

Atropine is an anticholinergic medication. It blocks vagal tone, which increases SA node firing and improves AV nodal conduction.

When Atropine Works Best

  • Sinus bradycardia
  • Vagal-mediated bradycardia
  • Inferior MI with AV nodal involvement
  • Mobitz I / Wenckebach

When Atropine Often Fails

  • Mobitz II AV block
  • Infranodal block
  • Complete heart block with wide ventricular escape
  • Severe degenerative conduction disease
Atropine works “upstairs” at the SA node and AV node. It does not reliably work for infranodal disease such as Mobitz II or wide-complex complete heart block.
If atropine fails after 3 mg total, do not keep giving atropine. Move to pacing or chronotropic infusion.

Chronotropic Infusions

Chronotropic infusions are useful when pacing is delayed, unavailable, or being prepared.

Agent Dose Use Cautions
Dopamine 2–10 mcg/kg/min IV infusion Atropine-resistant symptomatic bradycardia; bridge to pacing Tachyarrhythmias, extravasation risk, increased myocardial oxygen demand
Epinephrine 2–10 mcg/min IV infusion Severe bradycardia, peri-arrest bradycardia, bridge to pacing Arrhythmias, ischemia, increased myocardial oxygen demand
Dopamine or epinephrine can buy time, but they do not replace pacing when the patient has unstable high-grade conduction disease.

Transcutaneous Pacing

Transcutaneous pacing uses external pads to deliver electrical impulses through the chest wall to stimulate ventricular contraction.

Indications

  • Symptomatic bradycardia unresponsive to atropine
  • Mobitz II with symptoms or instability
  • Complete heart block with symptoms
  • High-grade AV block
  • Bridge to transvenous pacing
  • Rarely attempted in asystole, though often ineffective

Technique

Step Action
Pad position Anterior-posterior placement preferred
Rate Set 60–80 bpm
Current Start around 40–60 mA and increase until capture
Capture Confirm electrical and mechanical capture
Sedation Provide analgesia/sedation when possible

Capture

  • Electrical capture: pacing spike followed by wide QRS complex
  • Mechanical capture: palpable pulse or arterial waveform corresponding to paced beats
Electrical capture alone is not enough. Always confirm mechanical capture with pulse, blood pressure, arterial line, or ultrasound.

Sedation and Analgesia

  • Midazolam 2–4 mg IV may be used for sedation.
  • Fentanyl 25–50 mcg IV may be used for pain control.
  • Unstable patients may require pacing before sedation is fully achieved.

Limitations

  • Painful
  • Temporary bridge only
  • Capture may fail in obesity, emphysema, pericardial effusion, or severe metabolic derangement
  • Motion artifact can make rhythm interpretation difficult

Transvenous Pacing

Transvenous pacing provides temporary internal pacing through a catheter positioned in the right ventricle.

Indications

  • Failed transcutaneous pacing
  • Persistent symptomatic bradycardia
  • High-grade AV block expected to persist
  • Complete heart block with unstable escape rhythm
  • Post-MI complete heart block, especially anterior MI
  • Prophylaxis before procedures in selected high-risk conduction disease

Technique Overview

  • Obtain central venous access, usually internal jugular or subclavian.
  • Float pacing catheter into the right ventricle.
  • Confirm capture electrically and mechanically.
  • Test pacing threshold.
  • Set output 2–3 times the capture threshold.
  • Use VVI mode in many temporary pacing situations.

Thresholds and Settings

Parameter Typical Goal
Threshold <1 mA ideal, <2 mA acceptable
Rate 60–70 bpm
Output 2–3 times threshold
Mode Usually VVI

Complications

  • Pneumothorax
  • Bleeding
  • Infection
  • Cardiac perforation
  • Lead dislodgement
  • Failure to capture
  • Arrhythmia during placement
Transvenous pacing is not the final answer for chronic conduction disease. It is a bridge to recovery or permanent pacemaker placement.

Sinus Bradycardia

Sinus bradycardia means the SA node is firing slower than 60 bpm, with normal P waves before every QRS complex.

Causes

Category Examples
Physiologic Athletes, sleep, high vagal tone
Ischemic Inferior MI affecting SA node blood supply
Medication-related Beta blockers, calcium channel blockers, digoxin, amiodarone
Metabolic Hypothyroidism, hypothermia, hyperkalemia
Neurologic Increased intracranial pressure with Cushing reflex

Management

Clinical Status Management
Asymptomatic Observe and treat underlying cause
Symptomatic Atropine, then pacing or chronotropic infusion if refractory
Medication-induced Hold offending agent and use antidote/supportive care when appropriate

Medication Toxicity Pearls

Drug Mechanism Treatment Pearl
Beta blockers Block SA and AV node sympathetic stimulation Glucagon 5–10 mg IV may be used in overdose
Diltiazem / verapamil Block L-type calcium channels IV calcium, high-dose insulin therapy in severe overdose
Digoxin Increases vagal tone and inhibits Na/K ATPase Digoxin immune Fab for severe toxicity
Amiodarone Multiple nodal and conduction effects Stop drug and provide supportive care

AV Blocks Overview

AV blocks occur when conduction from the atria to the ventricles is delayed or interrupted. The most important distinction is whether the block is at the AV node or below the AV node in the His-Purkinje system.

Block Location Danger Level Atropine Response
First-degree AV block Usually AV node Low if isolated Usually not needed
Mobitz I / Wenckebach Usually AV node Usually low Often responds
Mobitz II Below AV node High Usually poor response
Complete heart block AV node or infranodal Variable to high Depends on location, often unreliable
AV nodal blocks tend to be more stable and atropine-responsive. Infranodal blocks are dangerous and usually require pacing.

First-Degree AV Block

First-degree AV block is delayed AV conduction without dropped beats.

ECG Finding

  • PR interval greater than 200 ms
  • Every P wave is followed by a QRS complex

Clinical Significance

  • Often benign in isolation
  • Can be medication-related
  • May indicate underlying conduction system disease
  • Can progress if there is active ischemia, metabolic disease, or drug toxicity

Management

  • No treatment if asymptomatic
  • Review AV nodal blocking medications
  • Monitor if hospitalized for ischemia or electrolyte abnormalities

Second-Degree AV Block: Mobitz I / Wenckebach

Mobitz I is characterized by progressive AV nodal delay until a beat is dropped.

ECG Finding

  • Progressive PR prolongation
  • Eventually a non-conducted P wave
  • Cycle then repeats
  • Often grouped beating

Location

Mobitz I is usually at the AV node, also called supra-Hisian block.

Causes

  • Increased vagal tone
  • Inferior MI
  • Digoxin
  • Beta blockers
  • Non-dihydropyridine calcium channel blockers
  • Normal variant in athletes during sleep

Management

Clinical Status Management
Asymptomatic Observation
Symptomatic Atropine often effective
Inferior MI Usually transient; pace only if symptomatic or unstable
Wenckebach is like a train gradually slowing before stopping: the PR interval lengthens before the dropped beat.

Second-Degree AV Block: Mobitz II

Mobitz II is an infranodal conduction block with sudden dropped QRS complexes without progressive PR prolongation.

ECG Finding

  • Constant PR interval
  • Sudden non-conducted P waves
  • No warning PR prolongation
  • QRS may be wide if associated with bundle branch disease

Location

Mobitz II is usually below the AV node in the His-Purkinje system.

Causes

  • Anterior MI
  • Degenerative conduction system disease
  • Lenègre disease
  • Lev disease
  • Hyperkalemia
  • Structural conduction system disease

Why It Is Dangerous

  • Can suddenly progress to complete heart block
  • May produce long pauses or asystole
  • Atropine often does not work
  • Usually requires permanent pacemaker
Mobitz II is dangerous even if the patient looks okay right now. Do not wait for symptoms before arranging urgent pacing evaluation.

Management

  • Place on monitor.
  • Apply pacing pads.
  • Prepare for transcutaneous pacing if unstable.
  • Arrange urgent transvenous pacing if needed.
  • Plan for permanent pacemaker unless reversible cause is clearly identified.

Third-Degree AV Block: Complete Heart Block

Complete heart block occurs when atrial impulses do not conduct to the ventricles. The atria and ventricles beat independently.

ECG Finding

  • AV dissociation
  • P waves and QRS complexes have no consistent relationship
  • Atrial rate is usually faster than ventricular rate
  • Escape rhythm determines stability

Escape Rhythm Prognosis

Escape Rhythm QRS Width Rate Clinical Meaning
Junctional escape Narrow QRS 40–60 bpm More stable, usually AV nodal level
Ventricular escape Wide QRS 20–40 bpm Unstable, infranodal disease, higher asystole risk

Causes

  • Inferior MI, often transient with junctional escape
  • Anterior MI, often unstable with wide escape
  • Degenerative conduction disease
  • Medications
  • Congenital complete heart block
  • Post-procedure injury

Management

  • Temporary pacing if symptomatic, hypotensive, ischemic, or in heart failure
  • Temporary pacing if wide-complex escape rhythm
  • Temporary pacing if post-MI, especially anterior MI
  • Permanent pacemaker for chronic complete heart block
In complete heart block, a narrow escape is more reassuring than a wide escape. Wide escape rhythm is slower, less reliable, and more dangerous.

Permanent Pacemaker Indications

Permanent pacemaker decisions are based on symptoms, type of conduction disease, and likelihood of progression.

Condition Pacemaker Recommendation
Symptomatic sinus bradycardia Permanent pacing generally indicated
Asymptomatic sinus bradycardia Permanent pacing not recommended
Mobitz II second-degree AV block Permanent pacing indicated regardless of symptoms unless reversible
Complete heart block Permanent pacing indicated regardless of symptoms unless reversible
Bifascicular block Pacing if intermittent complete block, syncope, or progression
Post-MI AV block Pacing if persistent complete block or infranodal block
Do not implant pacemakers for asymptomatic physiologic sinus bradycardia.

The Special Bradycardias

Athlete’s Heart

  • Physiologic high vagal tone
  • Resting heart rates of 30–40 bpm can be normal
  • No pacemaker if asymptomatic
  • Exercise testing can confirm appropriate chronotropic response

Carotid Sinus Hypersensitivity

  • Triggered by shaving, tight collar, or head turning
  • Can cause sinus pause or hypotension
  • Avoid triggers
  • Pacing considered if recurrent cardioinhibitory syncope

Sick Sinus Syndrome / Tachy-Brady Syndrome

Sick sinus syndrome involves abnormal SA node function. Tachy-brady syndrome alternates bradycardia with tachyarrhythmias, often atrial fibrillation.

In tachy-brady syndrome, patients may need a pacemaker before beta blockers, calcium channel blockers, digoxin, or antiarrhythmics can be safely used for atrial fibrillation.

Medication-Induced Bradycardia

Medication Mechanism Treatment
Beta blockers SA/AV node blockade Hold drug; glucagon in overdose; vasopressors or high-dose insulin in severe cases
Verapamil / diltiazem L-type calcium channel blockade Hold drug; calcium; high-dose insulin therapy in severe overdose
Digoxin Increased vagal tone and Na/K ATPase inhibition Hold drug; correct potassium; DigiFab if severe
Amiodarone Multiple nodal and conduction effects Hold drug and provide supportive care

Post-Myocardial Infarction Bradycardia

The location of MI strongly affects the expected bradycardia pattern and need for pacing.

Inferior MI

Feature Explanation
Artery Often RCA, which may supply SA and AV nodes
Typical rhythm Sinus bradycardia or Mobitz I
Course Often transient
Atropine response Often responsive
Pacing need Temporary only if symptomatic or unstable

Anterior MI

Feature Explanation
Artery Usually LAD with septal conduction system involvement
Typical rhythm Bundle branch block, Mobitz II, complete heart block
Course More dangerous and less likely to resolve
Atropine response Often poor
Pacing need Temporary and often permanent pacing
Inferior MI bradycardia is usually nodal, transient, and atropine-responsive. Anterior MI bradycardia often reflects septal necrosis and infranodal disease.

Acute vs Chronic Management

Acute Emergency / ICU Management

Scenario Immediate Action Next Step
Symptomatic bradycardia Atropine 0.5 mg IV TCP if no response, then transvenous pacing
Mobitz II Apply pacing pads and prepare TCP Transvenous pacing and permanent pacemaker evaluation
Complete heart block with wide QRS TCP or transvenous pacing Permanent pacemaker
Asystole CPR and epinephrine Identify reversible causes; TCP rarely effective

Chronic Ward / Outpatient Management

Diagnosis Management
Symptomatic sinus node dysfunction Permanent pacemaker
Complete heart block Permanent pacemaker
Mobitz II Permanent pacemaker
Asymptomatic bifascicular block Observation unless syncope or progression
Physiologic sinus bradycardia Observation and reassurance

Permanent Pacemaker Basics

Common Modes

Mode Meaning Typical Use
AAI Atrial pacing and sensing Rarely used; isolated sinus node disease with intact AV conduction
VVI Ventricular pacing and sensing Atrial fibrillation with slow ventricular response
DDD Dual-chamber pacing and sensing Most common for AV block; preserves AV synchrony

Complications

  • Pacemaker syndrome from loss of AV synchrony
  • Pacemaker-mediated tachycardia
  • Lead fracture
  • Lead dislodgement
  • Pocket infection
  • Endocarditis
  • Pacing-induced cardiomyopathy with high RV pacing burden
DDD pacing preserves AV synchrony and is often preferred for AV block when the patient has organized atrial activity.

Common Clinical Scenarios

Scenario 1: Syncope with Complete Heart Block

A patient presents with syncope and ECG shows complete heart block.

Admit to monitored setting, apply pacing pads, use temporary pacing if unstable, and arrange permanent pacemaker placement.

Scenario 2: Inferior MI with HR 40 and BP 90/60

Inferior MI often causes transient nodal bradycardia.

Give atropine 0.5 mg IV. If hypotension persists, use dopamine or epinephrine as a bridge and prepare transcutaneous pacing.

Scenario 3: Asymptomatic Athlete with HR 45

A healthy athlete has sinus bradycardia but no symptoms.

No treatment is needed. Consider exercise testing if chronotropic response is uncertain.

Scenario 4: Digoxin Toxicity with Bradycardia

  • Hold digoxin.
  • Check digoxin level and electrolytes.
  • Correct potassium carefully.
  • Use digoxin immune Fab if severe toxicity or instability.

Scenario 5: New Bifascicular Block After MI

New conduction disease after MI requires telemetry and readiness for pacing if progression occurs.

New bifascicular block after anterior MI can progress to complete heart block.

Clinical Pearls

  • Bradycardia management depends on symptoms and perfusion, not heart rate alone.
  • Unstable bradycardia requires immediate treatment.
  • Atropine works best for sinus bradycardia and AV nodal block.
  • Atropine usually does not work for Mobitz II or infranodal complete heart block.
  • Do not keep giving atropine after 3 mg total.
  • Mobitz I gradually prolongs before dropping a beat.
  • Mobitz II drops beats suddenly and is dangerous.
  • Mobitz II can progress abruptly to complete heart block.
  • Complete heart block with narrow escape is more stable than wide escape.
  • Wide ventricular escape rhythm is slow, unreliable, and dangerous.
  • Inferior MI bradycardia is usually transient and atropine-responsive.
  • Anterior MI bradycardia suggests septal conduction system injury and often needs pacing.
  • Transcutaneous pacing requires both electrical and mechanical capture.
  • Transvenous pacing is a bridge to recovery or permanent pacemaker placement.
  • Tachy-brady syndrome often requires pacing before rate-slowing medications can be safely used.
  • Asymptomatic physiologic bradycardia does not require pacemaker placement.
  • Drug-induced bradycardia requires medication review and specific antidotes when severe.

Bottom Line

Bradycardia is managed by symptoms, perfusion, and conduction pattern. Stable asymptomatic bradycardia can often be observed while reversible causes are evaluated. Unstable bradycardia requires immediate treatment with atropine, pacing, or chronotropic infusion. Atropine works best for sinus bradycardia and AV nodal block, but it is unreliable for infranodal disease such as Mobitz II and wide-complex complete heart block. Mobitz II and complete heart block generally require pacing and often permanent pacemaker placement. Always distinguish inferior MI nodal bradycardia, which is often transient, from anterior MI conduction disease, which is more dangerous and frequently requires pacing.

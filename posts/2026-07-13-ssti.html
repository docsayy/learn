Skin and Soft-Tissue Infections: The Complete Advanced Resident Guide

Added July 13, 2026 • Infectious Disease • ~38 min read

Skin and soft-tissue infections range from superficial impetigo and uncomplicated cellulitis to abscesses, diabetic-foot infection, pyomyositis, necrotizing fasciitis, clostridial myonecrosis, Fournier gangrene, and rapidly fatal toxin-mediated disease. This guide reviews diagnostic classification, common mimics, culture interpretation, antimicrobial selection, drainage and surgical source control, MRSA coverage, special exposures, recurrent infection, treatment duration, and high-yield board and infectious disease consultation pearls.

Clinical Overview

Skin and soft-tissue infection is a broad term that includes infection of the epidermis, dermis, subcutaneous tissue, fascia, and muscle.

The most important initial distinction is whether the infection is:

  • Superficial or deep
  • Purulent or nonpurulent
  • Localized or systemic
  • Necrotizing or non-necrotizing
  • Associated with an abscess or another drainable focus
Nonpurulent Cellulitis ≠ Purulent Abscess ≠ Necrotizing Infection

The Major Clinical Questions

  1. Is this truly an infection?
  2. Is purulence or a drainable collection present?
  3. Is the patient systemically ill?
  4. Is necrotizing infection possible?
  5. What organisms are likely based on the syndrome and exposure?
  6. Is surgical source control required?
  7. Does the patient need MRSA or broad gram-negative coverage?
  8. What is the shortest appropriate duration?
The most important treatment for an abscess is drainage. The most important treatment for a necrotizing infection is immediate surgery.

Why SSTIs Are Frequently Mismanaged

  • Cellulitis mimics are mistaken for infection.
  • Every cellulitis case is unnecessarily treated for MRSA.
  • Abscesses are treated with antibiotics without adequate drainage.
  • Superficial wound cultures drive unnecessarily broad therapy.
  • Necrotizing infection is delayed while waiting for imaging or laboratory scores.
  • Chronic wounds are treated because bacteria are present rather than because invasive infection exists.
  • Source control is delayed.

Anatomic Classification

Layer Common Infection Typical Clinical Features
Epidermis Impetigo Superficial crusted lesions
Hair follicle Folliculitis, furuncle, carbuncle Follicular pustule or deeper tender nodule
Superficial dermis and lymphatics Erysipelas Raised, bright-red, sharply demarcated plaque
Deep dermis and subcutaneous tissue Cellulitis Warm, tender, expanding erythema with indistinct margins
Localized dermis/subcutaneous tissue Abscess Fluctuant purulent collection
Deep fascia Necrotizing fasciitis Rapid progression, severe pain, systemic toxicity
Skeletal muscle Pyomyositis or myonecrosis Deep muscular pain, swelling, systemic illness

Why Anatomy Matters

  • Superficial infections may respond to topical or narrow oral therapy.
  • Subcutaneous infection requires systemic antibiotics.
  • Abscesses require drainage.
  • Fascial or muscle infection requires urgent surgical evaluation.
  • Antibiotic penetration and treatment duration differ by depth.

Clinical Classification

Nonpurulent Infection

  • Cellulitis
  • Erysipelas
  • No abscess or purulent drainage
  • Usually caused by beta-hemolytic streptococci

Purulent Infection

  • Abscess
  • Furuncle
  • Carbuncle
  • Purulent drainage
  • Frequently caused by Staphylococcus aureus

Necrotizing Infection

  • Necrotizing fasciitis
  • Fournier gangrene
  • Clostridial myonecrosis
  • Necrotizing polymicrobial infection
  • Requires immediate surgery

Severity Classification

Severity Features General Approach
Mild Localized disease without systemic signs Outpatient oral therapy or drainage
Moderate Systemic inflammatory findings without instability Systemic therapy and close reassessment
Severe Shock, organ dysfunction, rapid progression, failed outpatient therapy, immunocompromise, or concern for necrosis Hospitalization, IV therapy, surgery/source control

Risk Factors

Risk Factors for Cellulitis

  • Disruption of the skin barrier
  • Tinea pedis
  • Toe-web maceration
  • Chronic edema
  • Lymphedema
  • Venous insufficiency
  • Obesity
  • Prior cellulitis
  • Dermatitis
  • Trauma
  • Surgical wounds

Risk Factors for Purulent MRSA Infection

  • Previous MRSA infection or colonization
  • Close-contact living environments
  • Shared equipment
  • Contact sports
  • Injection drug use
  • Recent antibiotics
  • Recurrent abscesses
  • Household member with MRSA

Risk Factors for Necrotizing Infection

  • Diabetes mellitus
  • Peripheral vascular disease
  • Immunosuppression
  • Chronic kidney disease
  • Cirrhosis
  • Obesity
  • Recent surgery
  • Penetrating trauma
  • Injection drug use
  • Perineal infection
  • Varicella in children
  • Saltwater exposure in chronic liver disease
Necrotizing infection can occur in otherwise healthy patients. The absence of diabetes or immune compromise does not exclude it.

Common Cellulitis Mimics

A substantial proportion of patients admitted for cellulitis have a noninfectious inflammatory condition.

Mimic Clues Against Cellulitis
Venous stasis dermatitis Bilateral disease, chronic edema, scaling, hyperpigmentation, pruritus
Lipodermatosclerosis Chronic induration, inverted-champagne-bottle leg shape, venous disease
Contact dermatitis Pruritus, geometric distribution, new topical exposure
Deep venous thrombosis Swelling and pain without typical superficial warmth or skin infection
Gout or pseudogout Joint-centered erythema and severe pain
Septic arthritis Pain with passive joint movement, joint effusion
Hematoma Trauma, anticoagulation, ecchymosis
Superficial thrombophlebitis Tender palpable venous cord
Drug eruption Diffuse or symmetric rash, recent medication
Inflammatory breast cancer Persistent breast erythema, peau d’orange, no response to antibiotics
Calciphylaxis Severe pain, retiform purpura, ESRD, necrotic plaques
Pyoderma gangrenosum Painful ulcer with undermined violaceous border and pathergy
Bilateral lower-extremity “cellulitis” is uncommon. Consider venous stasis dermatitis, edema, lipodermatosclerosis, and contact dermatitis.

Features Supporting Cellulitis

  • Unilateral expanding erythema
  • Warmth
  • Tenderness
  • Edema
  • Regional lymphangitis
  • Portal of entry
  • Fever or systemic symptoms in more severe disease

Initial Clinical Approach

Step 1: Assess for Emergency Features

  • Hypotension
  • Rapid progression
  • Pain out of proportion
  • Bullae
  • Skin anesthesia
  • Crepitus
  • Dusky or necrotic skin
  • Severe systemic toxicity
  • Perineal involvement
  • Concern for compartment syndrome
Any concern for necrotizing infection requires immediate surgical consultation. Do not wait for CT, MRI, or a laboratory score before calling surgery.

Step 2: Determine Whether Purulence Is Present

  • Fluctuance
  • Drainage
  • Pustule
  • Ultrasound-confirmed collection

Step 3: Identify the Portal of Entry

  • Tinea pedis
  • Toe-web fissure
  • Ulcer
  • Traumatic wound
  • Injection site
  • Animal bite
  • Surgical incision
  • Pressure injury

Step 4: Review Exposure History

  • Freshwater or saltwater exposure
  • Animal or human bite
  • Travel
  • Contact sports
  • Injection drug use
  • Recent surgery
  • Healthcare exposure
  • Previous resistant organisms

Step 5: Assess Host Factors

  • Diabetes
  • Neutropenia
  • Transplantation
  • Chronic corticosteroids
  • Cirrhosis
  • Asplenia
  • Peripheral arterial disease
  • Chronic edema or lymphedema

Cultures and Diagnostic Testing

Cellulitis Cultures

Routine blood cultures, needle aspiration, and skin biopsies are generally low yield in uncomplicated cellulitis.

Consider Cultures When:

  • Severe systemic illness
  • Neutropenia
  • Severe cell-mediated immunodeficiency
  • Animal bite
  • Freshwater or saltwater exposure
  • Unusual exposure
  • Recurrent or treatment-refractory disease
  • Concern for deep or necrotizing infection

Abscess Cultures

Culture purulent material when:

  • Moderate or severe infection
  • Recurrent abscess
  • Immunocompromised host
  • Previous treatment failure
  • Healthcare-associated infection
  • Unusual exposure
  • Outbreak concern

A culture is helpful but should not delay incision and drainage.

Wound Cultures

Avoid superficial swabs of chronic wounds when deeper tissue or aspirate can be obtained.

Preferred Specimens

  • Aspirated pus
  • Deep tissue collected after cleaning and debridement
  • Surgical specimen
  • Bone specimen when osteomyelitis is suspected

Blood Cultures

Consider With:

  • Sepsis or shock
  • Necrotizing infection
  • Pyomyositis
  • Severe diabetic-foot infection
  • Immunocompromised host
  • Suspected endocarditis
  • Systemic animal-bite infection
  • Rapidly progressive water-associated infection

Inflammatory Markers

ESR and CRP may support the presence and trend of inflammation but cannot distinguish cellulitis from a mimic or exclude necrotizing infection.

Imaging

Bedside Ultrasound

Ultrasound is useful when examination cannot distinguish cellulitis from an abscess.

Potential Findings

  • “Cobblestoning” from subcutaneous edema
  • Hypoechoic or complex fluid collection
  • Foreign body
  • Guidance for drainage

Plain Radiograph

May identify:

  • Soft-tissue gas
  • Foreign body
  • Fracture
  • Advanced osteomyelitis
The absence of gas on radiograph does not exclude necrotizing infection.

CT

CT may identify:

  • Deep abscess
  • Fascial thickening
  • Soft-tissue gas
  • Pelvic or perineal extension
  • Foreign body
  • Bone involvement

MRI

MRI is highly sensitive for:

  • Fascial inflammation
  • Pyomyositis
  • Osteomyelitis
  • Deep-space infection
Imaging can support the diagnosis of necrotizing infection but should never delay operative exploration in a clinically concerning patient.

Cellulitis

Cellulitis is an acute spreading infection involving the deeper dermis and subcutaneous tissue.

Common Findings

  • Erythema
  • Warmth
  • Tenderness
  • Swelling
  • Indistinct borders
  • Possible lymphangitic streaking
  • Possible regional lymphadenopathy

Usual Organisms

Beta-Hemolytic Streptococci

Staphylococcus aureus becomes more likely when there is:

  • Penetrating trauma
  • Purulence
  • Open wound
  • Injection drug use
  • Previous MRSA

Mild Nonpurulent Cellulitis

Common Oral Options

  • Cephalexin
  • Dicloxacillin
  • Amoxicillin-clavulanate when broader coverage is needed
  • Clindamycin in selected beta-lactam allergy

Moderate Nonpurulent Cellulitis

Common IV Options

  • Cefazolin
  • Ceftriaxone in selected settings
  • Nafcillin or oxacillin
  • Ampicillin-sulbactam for selected polymicrobial risk

When to Add MRSA Coverage

  • Penetrating trauma
  • Previous MRSA infection or colonization
  • Injection drug use
  • Purulent drainage
  • Systemic inflammatory response
  • Failure of appropriate beta-lactam therapy

Supportive Management

  • Elevate the affected limb.
  • Treat edema.
  • Identify and treat tinea pedis.
  • Clean and protect open wounds.
  • Mark the border when progression monitoring is needed.

Expected Response

Erythema may appear unchanged or slightly worse during the first 24–48 hours because inflammation resolves more slowly than bacterial killing.

Do not broaden therapy solely because the erythema has not completely receded after one day if the patient is otherwise improving.

Duration

Uncomplicated Improving Cellulitis: Approximately 5 Days

Extend treatment when:

  • Clinical response is slow
  • Infection is extensive
  • Source control is incomplete
  • Immune compromise is substantial
  • Deep infection is present

Erysipelas

Erysipelas is a superficial infection involving the upper dermis and superficial lymphatics.

Classic Appearance

  • Bright-red plaque
  • Raised border
  • Sharply demarcated margin
  • Warmth and tenderness
  • Possible fever and chills

Common Organism

Group A Streptococcus

Common Sites

  • Lower extremity
  • Face

Treatment

  • Penicillin
  • Amoxicillin
  • Cephalexin
  • Cefazolin for hospitalized patients
  • Alternative based on true allergy history
Erysipelas is generally more sharply demarcated and raised than ordinary cellulitis.

Purulent Skin and Soft-Tissue Infection

Examples

  • Abscess
  • Furuncle
  • Carbuncle
  • Purulent cellulitis
  • Infected epidermoid cyst

Most Common Organism

Staphylococcus aureus, Including Community-Associated MRSA

Primary Management

Incision and Drainage

When Antibiotics Are More Likely Needed

  • Systemic inflammatory response
  • Hypotension
  • Rapid progression
  • Extensive surrounding cellulitis
  • Multiple lesions
  • Immunocompromised host
  • Extremes of age
  • Difficult-to-drain location
  • Failure of drainage alone
  • Recurrent infection

Abscess Management

Clinical Findings

  • Localized painful swelling
  • Fluctuance
  • Purulent drainage
  • Central pustule
  • Surrounding erythema

Incision and Drainage Steps

  1. Confirm the presence and extent of the collection.
  2. Provide adequate analgesia or anesthesia.
  3. Incise over the area of maximal fluctuance.
  4. Break up loculations.
  5. Drain purulent material.
  6. Irrigate when appropriate.
  7. Send culture when clinically indicated.
  8. Arrange wound care and reassessment.

Packing

Routine packing of a small uncomplicated abscess may increase pain without improving outcomes. Packing may still be considered for:

  • Large cavities
  • Ongoing bleeding
  • Complex anatomy
  • Concern for premature closure
  • Selected postoperative wounds

Locations Requiring Specialist Evaluation

  • Face or central facial triangle
  • Hand
  • Perirectal region
  • Perineum
  • Breast
  • Deep neck
  • Near major neurovascular structures

Antibiotic Options When Needed

  • TMP-SMX
  • Doxycycline
  • Clindamycin if susceptible
  • Linezolid in selected severe or resistant infection
  • IV vancomycin for severe hospitalized infection
Antibiotics without drainage frequently fail when a true abscess is present.

MRSA Coverage

Oral MRSA-Active Agents

Agent Advantages Important Limitations
TMP-SMX Effective for many purulent MRSA infections Hyperkalemia, renal effects, cytopenias; streptococcal coverage may be unreliable
Doxycycline Effective oral MRSA activity Streptococcal coverage may be unreliable; photosensitivity and esophagitis
Clindamycin Covers MRSA and streptococci when susceptible C. difficile risk and inducible resistance
Linezolid Excellent oral bioavailability and broad gram-positive activity Cytopenias, neuropathy, serotonin toxicity, cost

Covering Both MRSA and Streptococci

Potential approaches include:

  • Clindamycin alone when susceptible
  • TMP-SMX plus a beta-lactam
  • Doxycycline plus a beta-lactam
  • Linezolid alone in selected cases

IV MRSA Options

  • Vancomycin
  • Linezolid
  • Daptomycin for nonpulmonary invasive disease
  • Ceftaroline
  • Other susceptibility-guided agents

Inducible Clindamycin Resistance

A D-test should be reviewed when an erythromycin-resistant, clindamycin-susceptible S. aureus isolate is being considered for clindamycin therapy.

MRSA coverage is usually appropriate for purulent infection. It is not automatically required for uncomplicated nonpurulent cellulitis.

Impetigo and Ecthyma

Impetigo

Impetigo is a superficial epidermal infection commonly caused by S. aureus or group A Streptococcus.

Clinical Features

  • Honey-colored crusts
  • Small vesicles or pustules
  • Commonly affects exposed skin
  • Highly contagious

Bullous Impetigo

Bullous impetigo is caused by toxin-producing S. aureus.

Features

  • Flaccid bullae
  • Superficial erosions
  • Minimal surrounding erythema

Localized Treatment

  • Topical mupirocin
  • Topical retapamulin where available
  • Usually approximately 5 days

Extensive Disease or Outbreak

  • Oral cephalexin or dicloxacillin for MSSA
  • MRSA-active agent when MRSA is suspected or confirmed
  • Usually approximately 7 days

Ecthyma

Ecthyma extends deeper into the dermis and forms ulcerative crusted lesions.

Ecthyma generally requires oral antimicrobial therapy rather than topical therapy alone.

Folliculitis, Furuncles, and Carbuncles

Folliculitis

Folliculitis is a superficial infection or inflammation of hair follicles.

Causes

  • S. aureus
  • Occlusion or friction
  • Hot-tub-associated Pseudomonas
  • Malassezia in selected patients

Hot-Tub Folliculitis

  • Occurs after exposure to inadequately chlorinated water
  • Frequently affects swimsuit-covered areas
  • Usually self-limited
  • Antibiotics are rarely required unless severe or persistent

Furuncle

A furuncle is a deeper infection of a hair follicle producing a painful nodule or small abscess.

Carbuncle

A carbuncle is a coalescence of infected follicles with multiple draining points and deeper inflammation.

Management

  • Warm compresses for small lesions
  • Incision and drainage for larger lesions
  • Antibiotics when systemic illness or surrounding cellulitis is present

Consider Diabetes

Recurrent or extensive carbuncles, particularly on the posterior neck, should prompt assessment for diabetes or immune compromise.

Necrotizing Soft-Tissue Infection

Necrotizing soft-tissue infection is a rapidly progressive infection causing necrosis of fascia, subcutaneous tissue, or muscle.

Clinical Suspicion → Immediate Surgery

Red Flags

  • Pain out of proportion to visible findings
  • Rapid progression
  • Severe tenderness beyond the erythematous border
  • Wooden or firm subcutaneous tissue
  • Bullae
  • Ecchymosis
  • Dusky discoloration
  • Skin anesthesia
  • Crepitus
  • Systemic toxicity
  • Hypotension
  • Altered mental status
  • Failure of apparently appropriate cellulitis therapy

Early Disease May Look Benign

Early necrotizing fasciitis may present with only:

  • Severe pain
  • Mild erythema
  • Swelling
  • Fever
  • Tachycardia
Do not wait for bullae, gas, crepitus, or skin necrosis. These are often late findings.

Microbiologic Types

Type Typical Organisms Associations
Polymicrobial Mixed gram-positive, gram-negative, and anaerobic organisms Diabetes, postoperative infection, perineal infection
Monomicrobial Group A Streptococcus, S. aureus May occur after minor trauma in healthy patients
Clostridial Clostridium perfringens and related species Trauma, surgery, muscle ischemia
Water-associated Vibrio vulnificus or Aeromonas Saltwater or freshwater exposure

LRINEC Score

The LRINEC score uses laboratory values such as CRP, WBC count, hemoglobin, sodium, creatinine, and glucose.

A low LRINEC score does not exclude necrotizing fasciitis. Do not use it to delay surgery.

Initial Management

  1. Call surgery immediately.
  2. Begin sepsis resuscitation.
  3. Obtain blood cultures and operative cultures.
  4. Start broad IV antibiotics.
  5. Perform urgent operative exploration and debridement.
  6. Return for repeated debridement as required.
  7. Provide intensive-care support.

Empiric Antimicrobial Structure

MRSA Coverage + Broad Gram-Negative/Anaerobic Coverage + Toxin Suppression

Potential Regimen

  • Vancomycin or linezolid
  • Plus piperacillin-tazobactam or a carbapenem
  • Plus clindamycin when streptococcal or clostridial toxin-mediated disease is suspected

Confirmed Group A Streptococcal Necrotizing Fasciitis

High-Dose Penicillin + Clindamycin

Clindamycin is added because it suppresses toxin and protein production and remains active during stationary bacterial growth.

IV Immunoglobulin

IVIG may be considered in selected severe streptococcal toxic shock or necrotizing infection, but evidence of benefit remains uncertain.

Duration

Continue antibiotics until:

  • No further debridement is required
  • Clinical improvement is clear
  • Systemic signs have resolved
  • A defined period has passed after the final adequate debridement

Fournier Gangrene

Fournier gangrene is a necrotizing infection involving the perineum, genital region, or perianal tissues.

Risk Factors

  • Diabetes
  • Obesity
  • Immunosuppression
  • Alcohol use disorder
  • Perirectal infection
  • Urologic infection or procedure
  • Local trauma
  • SGLT2 inhibitor use has been associated with rare reported cases

Clinical Features

  • Severe perineal pain
  • Swelling
  • Erythema
  • Skin discoloration
  • Crepitus
  • Foul drainage
  • Systemic toxicity

Management

  • Immediate urologic or general surgical consultation
  • Urgent extensive debridement
  • Broad polymicrobial antibiotics
  • MRSA coverage
  • Repeated operative evaluation
  • Critical-care support
A benign-appearing perineal examination does not exclude deep Fournier gangrene when pain and systemic illness are severe.

Clostridial Myonecrosis

Clostridial myonecrosis, or gas gangrene, is a rapidly progressive infection of skeletal muscle.

Common Organisms

  • Clostridium perfringens
  • Clostridium septicum
  • Other Clostridium species

Clinical Features

  • Severe rapidly progressive pain
  • Tense edema
  • Bronze or purple skin
  • Hemorrhagic bullae
  • Thin brown drainage
  • Crepitus
  • Hemolysis
  • Shock

Treatment

  • Immediate surgical exploration and debridement
  • High-dose penicillin
  • Clindamycin for toxin suppression
  • Broad empiric therapy until organism is defined
  • Critical-care support

Clostridium septicum

Spontaneous Clostridium septicum infection should prompt evaluation for colonic malignancy and hematologic disease.

Hyperbaric Oxygen

Hyperbaric oxygen should never delay surgery. Its role remains adjunctive and institution dependent.

Toxic Shock Syndromes

Streptococcal Toxic Shock Syndrome

Streptococcal toxic shock syndrome is associated with invasive group A streptococcal infection and toxin-mediated shock.

Features

  • Hypotension
  • Multiorgan failure
  • Severe pain
  • Necrotizing infection
  • Renal failure
  • Coagulopathy
  • ARDS

Treatment

  • Immediate source control
  • Penicillin plus clindamycin
  • Hemodynamic support
  • Consider IVIG in selected severe cases

Staphylococcal Toxic Shock Syndrome

Features

  • High fever
  • Hypotension
  • Diffuse erythroderma
  • Multiorgan involvement
  • Later desquamation
  • Tampon, wound, packing, or postoperative association

Treatment

  • Remove the source or foreign material.
  • Drain infected wounds.
  • Use an antistaphylococcal agent.
  • Add toxin-suppressing therapy.
  • Provide aggressive supportive care.
In toxin-mediated disease, source control and toxin suppression are as important as organism killing.

Surgical-Site Infection

Classification

  • Superficial incisional infection
  • Deep incisional infection
  • Organ-space infection

Clinical Findings

  • Purulent drainage
  • Incisional pain
  • Erythema
  • Wound separation
  • Fever
  • Deep collection
  • Organ dysfunction from organ-space infection

Primary Management

  • Open the infected incision when appropriate.
  • Drain purulent material.
  • Remove infected sutures or foreign material when needed.
  • Debride necrotic tissue.
  • Culture deep material.

When Antibiotics Are Needed

  • Systemic inflammatory response
  • Significant surrounding cellulitis
  • Deep incisional disease
  • Organ-space infection
  • Immunocompromised host
  • Incomplete drainage

Expected Organisms by Surgical Site

Surgery Type Likely Organisms
Clean trunk or extremity surgery Staphylococci and streptococci
Axillary surgery Gram-positive organisms and selected gram-negative organisms
Gastrointestinal surgery Enteric gram-negative organisms and anaerobes
Perineal or gynecologic surgery Gram-negative organisms, enterococci, and anaerobes

Animal and Human Bite Wounds

Initial Wound Management

  1. Wash and irrigate thoroughly.
  2. Remove foreign material.
  3. Debride devitalized tissue.
  4. Assess tendon, joint, nerve, and vascular injury.
  5. Assess tetanus status.
  6. Assess rabies risk.
  7. Decide whether prophylactic antibiotics are indicated.

High-Risk Wounds for Antibiotic Prophylaxis

  • Hand wounds
  • Face wounds
  • Deep puncture wounds
  • Crush injuries
  • Possible joint or bone penetration
  • Moderate or severe wounds
  • Immunocompromised patient
  • Asplenia
  • Advanced liver disease
  • Significant edema
  • Delayed presentation

Common Prophylactic Duration

High-Risk Uninfected Bite: Approximately 3–5 Days

Preferred Oral Agent

Amoxicillin-Clavulanate

Dog and Cat Bites

Important Organisms

  • Pasteurella multocida
  • Streptococci
  • Staphylococci
  • Anaerobes
  • Capnocytophaga canimorsus

Cat Bites

Cat teeth create deep puncture wounds that may inoculate tendon sheaths, joints, and bone.

A cat bite to the hand has a high risk of deep-space infection and should be evaluated carefully.

Capnocytophaga

Severe infection is more likely in patients with:

  • Asplenia
  • Alcohol use disorder
  • Advanced liver disease
  • Immune compromise

It may cause rapidly progressive sepsis, DIC, meningitis, and purpura.

Human Bites

Important Organisms

  • Streptococci
  • S. aureus
  • Eikenella corrodens
  • Oral anaerobes

Clenched-Fist Injury

A clenched-fist injury may appear as a small wound over a metacarpophalangeal joint but can involve:

  • Extensor tendon
  • Tendon sheath
  • Joint capsule
  • Bone
Clenched-fist injuries require urgent hand-surgery evaluation, irrigation, exploration, and appropriate antibiotics.

Eikenella Pearl

Eikenella corrodens is not reliably covered by:

  • Clindamycin
  • Metronidazole
  • First-generation cephalosporins
  • Macrolides

Wound Closure

  • Many bite wounds are left open.
  • Selected facial wounds may be closed after thorough irrigation.
  • Do not close an infected wound.
  • Hand and puncture wounds generally require caution.

Tetanus Prevention

  • Clean and debride the wound.
  • Review vaccine history.
  • Administer tetanus vaccine when indicated.
  • Administer tetanus immune globulin when indicated for high-risk wounds and inadequate immunization.
Antibiotics are not used specifically to prevent tetanus. Prevention depends on wound care, vaccination, and tetanus immune globulin when indicated.

Rabies Assessment

Rabies decisions depend on:

  • Animal species
  • Geographic epidemiology
  • Whether the exposure involved saliva and broken skin or mucosa
  • Animal availability for observation or testing
  • Behavior and health of the animal
  • Public-health guidance

For an unvaccinated patient requiring rabies postexposure prophylaxis, treatment includes:

  • Immediate wound washing
  • Human rabies immune globulin infiltrated around the wound when feasible
  • Rabies vaccine series

Water-Associated Skin and Soft-Tissue Infections

Saltwater or Brackish Water

Vibrio vulnificus

Risk Factors for Severe Disease

  • Cirrhosis
  • Chronic liver disease
  • Hemochromatosis
  • Immunosuppression
  • Diabetes
  • Open wound exposed to coastal water or raw seafood

Clinical Features

  • Rapidly progressive pain and swelling
  • Hemorrhagic bullae
  • Skin necrosis
  • Sepsis and shock
  • Necrotizing fasciitis

Management

  • Immediate surgical consultation
  • Aggressive debridement
  • Doxycycline plus a third-generation cephalosporin as a classic regimen
  • Alternative susceptibility-guided therapy
  • Intensive-care support
Rapidly progressive bullous cellulitis after saltwater exposure in a patient with liver disease is Vibrio vulnificus until proven otherwise.

Freshwater Exposure

Aeromonas Species

Clinical Features

  • Cellulitis after freshwater trauma
  • Rapid progression
  • Myonecrosis or necrotizing infection
  • Gram-negative bacteremia in severe cases

Treatment

  • Fluoroquinolone
  • Cefepime or another active gram-negative agent
  • Urgent surgery for necrotizing infection
  • Susceptibility-guided therapy

Aquarium Exposure

Mycobacterium marinum

Clinical Features

  • Chronic nodular skin lesions
  • Lesions along lymphatic channels
  • Hand or upper-extremity involvement
  • Tenosynovitis in deeper disease
  • Limited response to routine cellulitis antibiotics

Diagnosis

  • Skin biopsy
  • Acid-fast culture
  • Notify the microbiology laboratory because special lower-temperature incubation may be required

Treatment

  • Macrolide-based therapy
  • Doxycycline
  • Rifampin plus ethambutol for selected deeper disease
  • Prolonged treatment based on depth and response

Diabetes-Related Foot Infection

A diabetic-foot infection is diagnosed clinically by evidence of inflammation or purulence in a foot wound.

Do not prescribe antibiotics for an uninfected diabetic-foot ulcer.

Clinical Signs of Infection

  • Purulent drainage
  • Erythema
  • Warmth
  • Swelling
  • Tenderness
  • Induration

Severity

Severity General Features
Mild Limited superficial infection without systemic signs
Moderate Deeper or more extensive infection without systemic instability
Severe Systemic inflammatory response, organ dysfunction, or metabolic instability

Microbiology

  • Mild acute infections are commonly caused by streptococci and S. aureus.
  • Chronic, previously treated, ischemic, or severe infections may be polymicrobial.
  • Gram-negative and anaerobic organisms become more likely with chronic or necrotic wounds.

Culture Technique

Clean + Debride + Deep Tissue Specimen

Avoid relying on superficial wound swabs.

Assess for Osteomyelitis

  • Probe-to-bone test
  • Plain radiograph
  • ESR and CRP
  • MRI when diagnosis remains uncertain
  • Bone culture when needed for definitive microbiology

Pseudomonas Coverage

Do not routinely cover Pseudomonas aeruginosa in a diabetic-foot infection occurring in a temperate climate unless:

  • It was recently isolated from the affected site.
  • There are relevant exposure or geographic factors.
  • The infection is severe with a clinically meaningful risk.

Treatment Duration

Soft-Tissue Diabetic-Foot Infection: Usually 1–2 Weeks

Consider longer treatment when:

  • Infection is extensive
  • Resolution is slow
  • Severe peripheral arterial disease is present
  • Source control is incomplete
  • Osteomyelitis is present

Urgent Surgical Evaluation

  • Necrotizing infection
  • Deep abscess
  • Compartment syndrome
  • Extensive gangrene
  • Severe ischemia
  • Infected necrotic tissue
  • Failure of medical treatment

Management Beyond Antibiotics

  • Debridement
  • Pressure off-loading
  • Glycemic control
  • Vascular assessment
  • Wound care
  • Footwear modification
  • Smoking cessation
A diabetic-foot infection cannot be managed adequately by choosing an antibiotic alone. Perfusion, pressure, necrotic tissue, osteomyelitis, and wound care must all be addressed.

Pressure Injuries and Chronic Wounds

Chronic pressure injuries are commonly colonized with bacteria.

Colonization Does Not Equal Infection

Do not prescribe systemic antibiotics solely for:

  • Positive superficial culture
  • Chronic drainage
  • Odor
  • Visible colonization
  • Stable eschar without surrounding infection

Features Supporting Invasive Infection

  • Expanding cellulitis
  • Increasing pain
  • Purulent drainage
  • Systemic illness
  • Deep abscess
  • Necrosis
  • Exposed or infected bone
  • Rapid deterioration

Management

  • Pressure off-loading
  • Debridement when appropriate
  • Moisture control
  • Nutritional assessment
  • Deep tissue culture when infection is suspected
  • Evaluation for osteomyelitis
  • Antibiotics only for clinically invasive infection
A superficial wound swab often reflects colonizing flora and may lead to unnecessary broad-spectrum treatment.

Pyomyositis

Pyomyositis is a bacterial infection of skeletal muscle, most commonly caused by S. aureus.

Risk Factors

  • Diabetes
  • HIV
  • Immunosuppression
  • Trauma
  • Injection drug use
  • Bacteremia
  • Tropical exposure

Clinical Stages

Stage Features
Invasive stage Muscle pain, low-grade fever, limited swelling
Suppurative stage Abscess formation, fever, marked pain and swelling
Late stage Sepsis, metastatic infection, shock

Diagnosis

  • MRI is the preferred imaging modality.
  • Blood cultures may be positive.
  • Culture aspirated or operative material.
  • Assess for bacteremia and endocarditis.

Treatment

  • Initial S. aureus coverage
  • MRSA coverage based on epidemiology and severity
  • Drainage of purulent collections
  • Narrow therapy after cultures
  • Usually several weeks of treatment depending on response and bacteremia

Injection-Associated Skin and Soft-Tissue Infection

Potential Infections

  • Abscess
  • Cellulitis
  • Necrotizing fasciitis
  • Pyomyositis
  • Septic thrombophlebitis
  • Endocarditis
  • Osteomyelitis
  • Botulism from black-tar heroin
  • Tetanus

Evaluation

  • Examine all injection sites.
  • Assess for deep venous thrombosis or septic thrombosis.
  • Obtain blood cultures when systemically ill.
  • Listen for a murmur.
  • Ask about back and joint pain.
  • Screen for HIV and viral hepatitis.

Microbiology

  • MRSA
  • MSSA
  • Streptococci
  • Oral flora when needles are licked
  • Gram-negative organisms
  • Anaerobes
  • Clostridial species

Management

  • Drain abscesses.
  • Use syndrome-appropriate antimicrobial therapy.
  • Evaluate for endovascular infection.
  • Provide tetanus vaccination when indicated.
  • Offer medications for opioid use disorder.
  • Provide naloxone and harm-reduction counseling.
Treating the infection without addressing substance use disorder leaves a major cause of recurrent infection untreated.

SSTI in the Immunocompromised Host

Potential Pathogens

  • Routine streptococci and staphylococci
  • Gram-negative bacilli
  • Pseudomonas aeruginosa
  • Fungi
  • Nontuberculous mycobacteria
  • Nocardia
  • Viruses
  • Parasites

Why Diagnosis Is Difficult

  • Inflammation may be minimal.
  • Lesions may be atypical.
  • Multiple infections may coexist.
  • Noninfectious drug eruptions and malignancy may mimic infection.

Diagnostic Approach

  • Low threshold for biopsy
  • Deep tissue culture
  • Bacterial, fungal, and mycobacterial cultures
  • Histopathology
  • Blood cultures when febrile
  • Early dermatology and infectious disease input

Ecthyma Gangrenosum

Ecthyma gangrenosum classically occurs with Pseudomonas bacteremia in neutropenic patients.

Features

  • Painless erythematous macule
  • Progression to hemorrhagic bulla
  • Necrotic black eschar
  • Perineal or gluteal distribution is common
A rapidly evolving necrotic lesion in a neutropenic patient requires immediate antipseudomonal therapy and diagnostic sampling.

Recurrent Cellulitis

Common Predisposing Factors

  • Chronic edema
  • Lymphedema
  • Venous insufficiency
  • Obesity
  • Tinea pedis
  • Toe-web fissures
  • Chronic wounds
  • Previous surgery or lymph-node dissection

Prevention Strategy

  • Compression therapy when appropriate
  • Edema control
  • Treat tinea pedis
  • Moisturize cracked skin
  • Weight management
  • Foot care
  • Manage venous insufficiency

Antibiotic Prophylaxis

Prophylactic penicillin or an alternative may be considered in selected patients with frequent recurrent cellulitis despite control of predisposing factors.

Potential Candidates

  • Several episodes per year
  • Persistent lymphedema
  • Documented recurrent nonpurulent streptococcal cellulitis
  • Failure of edema and skin-barrier management
Prophylaxis targets recurrent nonpurulent streptococcal cellulitis. It is not a standard strategy for recurrent MRSA abscesses.

Recurrent Abscess and MRSA Decolonization

Look for a Local Cause

  • Hidradenitis suppurativa
  • Pilonidal disease
  • Retained foreign body
  • Infected cyst
  • Household transmission

Hidradenitis Suppurativa

Hidradenitis is a chronic inflammatory follicular disease rather than simply recurrent bacterial abscess formation.

Clues

  • Axillary, inguinal, inframammary, or perineal lesions
  • Sinus tracts
  • Scarring
  • Double comedones
  • Recurrent painful nodules

Treatment may require dermatology, weight management, smoking cessation, anti-inflammatory therapy, biologic treatment, or surgery.

Management of Recurrent Abscess

  • Drain and culture early.
  • Treat according to susceptibility.
  • Review household infections.
  • Improve hygiene and wound coverage.
  • Avoid sharing towels, razors, or athletic equipment.

Decolonization

A selected decolonization regimen may include:

  • Intranasal mupirocin twice daily for 5 days
  • Daily chlorhexidine washes for 5 days
  • Cleaning towels, bedding, clothing, and personal items
Evidence for decolonization is variable. It is most useful when recurrent confirmed S. aureus infection continues despite hygiene and wound-care measures.

Practical Antibiotic Selection

Mild Nonpurulent Cellulitis

Agent Coverage Important Note
Cephalexin Streptococci and MSSA Common first-line oral option
Dicloxacillin Streptococci and MSSA Requires frequent dosing
Amoxicillin Streptococci Useful when streptococcal disease is strongly suspected
Clindamycin Streptococci and some MRSA Check susceptibility; high C. difficile risk

Moderate Nonpurulent Cellulitis

  • Cefazolin
  • Nafcillin
  • Oxacillin
  • Ceftriaxone in selected settings

Severe Cellulitis With MRSA Risk

  • Vancomycin
  • Linezolid
  • Daptomycin
  • Ceftaroline

Severe Polymicrobial Infection

  • Vancomycin plus piperacillin-tazobactam
  • Vancomycin plus cefepime plus metronidazole
  • Vancomycin plus meropenem
  • Linezolid-based alternative in selected patients

Purulent Outpatient Infection

  • TMP-SMX
  • Doxycycline
  • Clindamycin

Important Drug Toxicities

Drug Major Concerns
Vancomycin Nephrotoxicity and infusion reaction
TMP-SMX Hyperkalemia, AKI, cytopenias, rash
Clindamycin C. difficile infection
Linezolid Thrombocytopenia, neuropathy, serotonin toxicity
Daptomycin Myopathy and elevated CK
Doxycycline Photosensitivity and esophagitis
Piperacillin-tazobactam Renal dosing, sodium load, broad microbiome effect

Antibiotic Stewardship

  • Do not cover MRSA automatically in uncomplicated nonpurulent cellulitis.
  • Do not cover gram-negative organisms routinely in ordinary cellulitis.
  • Do not continue broad IV treatment when cultures and clinical response support narrowing.
  • Do not treat colonized chronic wounds.
  • Use the shortest effective duration.

Treatment Duration

Syndrome Typical Duration Framework Important Qualification
Uncomplicated cellulitis Approximately 5 days Extend if not adequately improved
Abscess after drainage Approximately 5–10 days when antibiotics are indicated Culture and clinical-response guided
Impetigo 5 days topical or approximately 7 days oral Depends on extent
Diabetic-foot soft-tissue infection Usually 1–2 weeks Longer for extensive slow-resolving disease or severe PAD
Pyomyositis Usually several weeks Depends on drainage, bacteremia, and response
Necrotizing infection Individualized after adequate debridement Continue until source control and systemic improvement
Infected bite wound Commonly approximately 5–10 days Longer for joint, bone, tendon, or deep-space infection
M. marinum Prolonged Often continued beyond lesion resolution

Reasons for Longer Therapy

  • Osteomyelitis
  • Septic arthritis
  • Endocarditis
  • Infected prosthetic material
  • Undrained abscess
  • Severe ischemia
  • Immune compromise
  • Necrotizing infection
Persistent erythema alone is not an indication for indefinite antibiotics. Assess pain, fever, progression, systemic findings, and source control.

Admission and Discharge Decisions

Outpatient Management Is Reasonable When:

  • Hemodynamically stable
  • No concern for necrotizing disease
  • No severe immune compromise
  • Able to take oral medication
  • Reliable wound care and follow-up
  • No urgent surgical issue

Hospitalization Should Be Considered When:

  • Systemic toxicity
  • Hypotension
  • Rapid progression
  • Concern for deep or necrotizing infection
  • Severe immunocompromise
  • Failed outpatient therapy
  • Unable to take oral medication
  • Uncontrolled pain
  • Need for operative drainage
  • Poor adherence or unsafe social situation

Discharge Readiness

  • Clinical improvement
  • Stable vital signs
  • Source control completed
  • Oral regimen available
  • Wound-care plan established
  • Follow-up arranged
  • Return precautions provided

Return Precautions

  • Rapid spread
  • Increasing pain
  • New bullae or discoloration
  • Fever or chills
  • New drainage
  • Numbness
  • Weakness
  • Failure to improve

Daily Inpatient SSTI Checklist

  • Is this truly an infection or a mimic?
  • Is the process purulent or nonpurulent?
  • Has an abscess been adequately drained?
  • Is necrotizing infection excluded clinically?
  • Are surgical consultants involved when needed?
  • What is the portal of entry?
  • What exposure changes the microbiology?
  • Are cultures meaningful and from a deep specimen?
  • Does MRSA coverage remain necessary?
  • Does broad gram-negative or anaerobic coverage remain necessary?
  • Is there bacteremia or endocarditis?
  • Is bone, joint, fascia, or muscle involved?
  • Can IV therapy change to oral?
  • What is the planned duration?
  • Are edema, tinea pedis, wound care, off-loading, and glucose being addressed?

How to Present SSTI on ID Rounds

  • Exact anatomic location
  • Purulent versus nonpurulent
  • Depth of infection
  • Rate of progression
  • Systemic findings
  • Portal of entry
  • Exposure history
  • Drainage or debridement status
  • Culture results
  • Current antibiotic and rationale
  • MRSA or resistant-organism risk
  • Planned duration and wound care
Avoid saying only, “The patient has cellulitis and is on vancomycin and Zosyn.” State why this is infection, whether purulence is present, what source control occurred, and which organisms require coverage.

Common Clinical Scenarios

Case 1: Uncomplicated Nonpurulent Cellulitis

A patient has unilateral lower-leg warmth, erythema, tenderness, no abscess, no MRSA history, and no systemic instability.

Treat primarily for streptococci with a beta-lactam. Routine MRSA and gram-negative coverage are unnecessary.

Case 2: Fluctuant Abscess

A stable patient has a 3-cm fluctuant abscess without extensive cellulitis.

Incision and drainage is the primary treatment. Antibiotic need depends on systemic findings and host risk.

Case 3: Pain Out of Proportion

A diabetic patient has severe leg pain, mild erythema, tachycardia, and rapidly increasing tenderness.

Suspect necrotizing infection. Call surgery immediately and begin broad antibiotics. Do not wait for a high LRINEC score.

Case 4: Bilateral Red Legs

A patient has chronic bilateral leg edema, hyperpigmentation, scaling, and pruritus without fever.

This is more consistent with venous stasis dermatitis than bilateral cellulitis.

Case 5: Saltwater Wound and Bullae

A patient with cirrhosis develops severe pain, hemorrhagic bullae, and shock after a wound was exposed to coastal water.

Suspect Vibrio vulnificus. Begin immediate active therapy and arrange emergency surgical debridement.

Case 6: Cat Bite to the Hand

A patient presents with hand swelling and pain after a cat bite.

Assess for tendon-sheath, joint, and deep-space infection. Use Pasteurella-active therapy such as amoxicillin-clavulanate and involve hand surgery when appropriate.

Case 7: Human Bite Over the MCP Joint

A patient punched another person and has a small wound over the third MCP joint.

Treat as a clenched-fist injury with possible joint and tendon involvement. Obtain urgent surgical evaluation and cover Eikenella and oral anaerobes.

Case 8: Chronic Diabetic Ulcer Without Inflammation

A diabetic-foot ulcer has no erythema, warmth, tenderness, swelling, or purulence, but a superficial swab grows several organisms.

The ulcer is colonized, not clinically infected. Do not prescribe antibiotics solely because of the swab.

Case 9: Recurrent Axillary Abscesses

A patient has recurrent axillary nodules, draining sinus tracts, and scarring.

Consider hidradenitis suppurativa rather than repeated isolated bacterial abscesses.

Case 10: Fever and Muscle Pain

An immunocompromised patient has focal thigh pain, fever, and no obvious skin lesion.

Consider pyomyositis. Obtain MRI, blood cultures, and evaluate for drainage.

Case 11: Aquarium-Associated Nodules

A fish-tank owner has chronic nodules ascending along the hand and forearm despite cephalexin.

Consider Mycobacterium marinum. Obtain biopsy and notify the microbiology laboratory about the suspected organism.

Case 12: Clostridium septicum Myonecrosis

A patient develops spontaneous atraumatic myonecrosis with C. septicum.

Arrange emergency surgery and evaluate for colonic malignancy or hematologic disease.

Common Pitfalls

  • Diagnosing bilateral leg erythema as cellulitis
  • Treating venous stasis dermatitis with antibiotics
  • Adding MRSA coverage to every nonpurulent cellulitis case
  • Using vancomycin plus piperacillin-tazobactam for uncomplicated cellulitis
  • Treating an abscess without drainage
  • Delaying incision and drainage while waiting for a culture
  • Using superficial chronic-wound cultures to guide broad antibiotics
  • Treating an uninfected diabetic-foot ulcer
  • Relying on LRINEC to exclude necrotizing fasciitis
  • Waiting for CT before calling surgery in suspected necrotizing infection
  • Waiting for crepitus or gas before considering necrosis
  • Failing to ask about water exposure
  • Missing Vibrio in a cirrhotic patient with bullous cellulitis
  • Missing deep injury after a human clenched-fist wound
  • Using clindamycin alone for a human bite without susceptibility data
  • Failing to assess tetanus and rabies after an animal bite
  • Using antibiotics specifically to prevent tetanus
  • Failing to address edema and tinea pedis in recurrent cellulitis
  • Calling hidradenitis repeated MRSA infection
  • Failing to consider endocarditis in injection-associated infection
  • Continuing IV antibiotics when drainage is complete and oral therapy is adequate
  • Extending therapy solely because residual erythema remains

Board and ID Consult Pearls

  • Nonpurulent cellulitis is usually caused by beta-hemolytic streptococci.
  • Purulent abscesses are frequently caused by S. aureus, including MRSA.
  • Incision and drainage is the primary treatment for an abscess.
  • Routine cultures are not required for uncomplicated cellulitis.
  • Uncomplicated improving cellulitis commonly requires approximately five days of treatment.
  • MRSA coverage is not automatically required for nonpurulent cellulitis.
  • Cephalexin and cefazolin are common treatments for uncomplicated nonpurulent cellulitis.
  • TMP-SMX and doxycycline have unreliable streptococcal coverage.
  • Clindamycin may cover both MRSA and streptococci when susceptible.
  • A D-test evaluates inducible clindamycin resistance.
  • Pain out of proportion is an early warning sign of necrotizing infection.
  • A low LRINEC score does not exclude necrotizing fasciitis.
  • Imaging should not delay surgical exploration.
  • Confirmed group A streptococcal necrotizing fasciitis is treated with penicillin plus clindamycin.
  • Necrotizing infection requires repeated surgical debridement.
  • Fournier gangrene is a perineal necrotizing infection requiring emergency surgery.
  • Spontaneous C. septicum infection is associated with colorectal and hematologic malignancy.
  • Empyema, abscess, infected hardware, and necrotic tissue require source control.
  • Amoxicillin-clavulanate is the standard oral choice for many bite wounds.
  • Human bites require coverage for Eikenella and oral anaerobes.
  • Cat bites can cause deep hand infections despite a small skin wound.
  • Asplenic patients are at risk for severe Capnocytophaga infection after dog exposure.
  • Saltwater wound plus hemorrhagic bullae suggests Vibrio vulnificus.
  • Freshwater wound infection suggests Aeromonas.
  • Aquarium-associated nodular lymphangitis suggests Mycobacterium marinum.
  • Do not treat an uninfected diabetic-foot ulcer with antibiotics.
  • Deep tissue culture is preferred over superficial swab in diabetic-foot infection.
  • Soft-tissue diabetic-foot infection is usually treated for one to two weeks.
  • Pseudomonas coverage is not routinely required in diabetic-foot infection in temperate climates.
  • Pyomyositis is commonly caused by S. aureus and is best imaged with MRI.
  • Bilateral lower-leg erythema usually suggests a cellulitis mimic.
  • Edema control and tinea-pedis treatment reduce recurrent cellulitis.
  • Hidradenitis suppurativa is a chronic inflammatory disease, not simply recurrent abscess formation.

Bottom Line

Skin and soft-tissue infections should be classified by depth, purulence, severity, and need for source control. Nonpurulent cellulitis is usually streptococcal and generally does not require routine MRSA or broad gram-negative coverage. Purulent abscesses require incision and drainage, with antibiotics added according to systemic illness, surrounding cellulitis, immune status, and recurrence risk.

Pain out of proportion, rapid progression, bullae, skin anesthesia, discoloration, shock, or severe systemic toxicity should trigger immediate concern for necrotizing infection. Surgical consultation and debridement must not be delayed for imaging or laboratory scoring. Confirmed group A streptococcal necrotizing fasciitis requires penicillin plus clindamycin.

Exposure history is essential. Human bites require Eikenella and anaerobic coverage, animal bites require Pasteurella-active therapy, saltwater wounds may involve Vibrio, and freshwater injuries may involve Aeromonas. Evaluate tetanus and rabies risk after bites and contaminated wounds.

Chronic diabetic ulcers and pressure injuries are frequently colonized. Treat only clinically infected wounds, obtain deep cultures after debridement, address perfusion and pressure, and evaluate for osteomyelitis when appropriate.

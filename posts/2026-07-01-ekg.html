ECG Stepwise Guide

Added July 1, 2026 • Cardiology • ECG

A structured cookbook approach to ECG interpretation: rhythm → rate → P waves → PR interval → axis → QRS duration → precordial transition → voltage and hypertrophy → infarction patterns → ST-T changes → QT interval and electrolytes. Use the same sequence every time to avoid missing rhythm abnormalities, conduction disease, dangerous intervals, or ischemic patterns.

Overview

Use the same sequence every time you look at a tracing. That keeps you from jumping straight to “STEMI or not?” and missing rhythm, blocks, axis deviation, hypertrophy, QT prolongation, or electrolyte patterns.

  • Steps 1–2: rhythm and rate
  • Steps 3–5: P waves, PR interval, and axis
  • Steps 6–8: QRS width, rotation, voltage, and hypertrophy
  • Steps 9–10: Q waves, infarct localization, ST segment, and T waves
  • Step 11: QT interval, U waves, and electrolyte patterns
Green = normal Red = do not miss Blue = pattern recognition

1 Rhythm and Sinus Check

Start by deciding whether the rhythm is sinus.

  • Is there a P wave before each QRS?
  • Are P waves upright in leads I and II?
  • Are PR and RR intervals reasonably regular?

If yes, this is likely sinus rhythm. If not, consider atrial fibrillation, atrial flutter, junctional rhythm, ventricular rhythm, or paced rhythm.

Sinus: P → QRS → T Irregularly irregular: think AF

2 Heart Rate

For a regular rhythm, use the big-box method.

  • Heart rate ≈ 300 divided by the number of large boxes between QRS peaks.
  • 1 large box ≈ 300 bpm
  • 2 large boxes ≈ 150 bpm
  • 3 large boxes ≈ 100 bpm
  • 4 large boxes ≈ 75 bpm
  • 5 large boxes ≈ 60 bpm
  • 6 large boxes ≈ 50 bpm

For irregular rhythms, count QRS complexes in 10 seconds and multiply by 6.

Steps 3–5: P Waves, PR Interval, and Axis

3 P Waves and Atrial Size

Look carefully at leads II, III, aVF, and V1.

  • Right atrial enlargement: tall, peaked P wave greater than 2.5 mm in II, III, or aVF. This is sometimes called P pulmonale.
  • Left atrial enlargement: broad, notched P wave in lead II, sometimes called P mitrale.
  • In V1, left atrial enlargement may show a biphasic P wave with a deep or wide terminal negative component.

4 PR Interval

Measure from the beginning of the P wave to the beginning of the QRS complex.

  • Normal PR interval is approximately 0.12–0.20 seconds, or 3–5 small boxes.
  • PR greater than 0.20 seconds with every P followed by QRS suggests first-degree AV block.
  • Very short PR less than 0.12 seconds suggests pre-excitation.
  • Short PR plus delta wave suggests Wolff-Parkinson-White pattern.

5 QRS Axis

Use a quick frontal-plane axis method.

  • Lead I positive and lead II positive → normal axis.
  • Lead I positive and lead II negative → left axis deviation.
  • Lead I negative and aVF positive → right axis deviation.
  • Lead I negative and aVF negative → northwest axis.

Always interpret axis together with the rest of the tracing. For example, RBBB plus left axis deviation suggests bifascicular block.

Axis Categories

Normal Axis

Approximately −30° to +90°.

Left Axis Deviation

Seen with LVH, left anterior fascicular block, and inferior MI.

Right Axis Deviation

Seen with RVH, pulmonary hypertension, and left posterior fascicular block.

Northwest Axis

Think ventricular rhythm, severe conduction disease, or toxicity.

Steps 6–8: QRS Duration, Rotation, Voltage, and Hypertrophy

6 QRS Duration and Bundle Branch Blocks

  • Narrow QRS: less than 0.10 seconds.
  • Borderline QRS: 0.10–0.12 seconds, possible incomplete bundle branch block.
  • Wide QRS: at least 0.12 seconds.
  • RBBB: rSR′ pattern in V1 and wide terminal S wave in I and V6.
  • LBBB: broad or notched R wave in I, V5, or V6, with deep S wave in V1.
A wide QRS changes how you interpret ST-T abnormalities because bundle branch blocks create secondary repolarization changes.

7 Precordial Transition and Rotation

  • Normal R/S transition occurs around V3–V4.
  • Late transition at V5 or later suggests clockwise rotation or poor R-wave progression.
  • Early transition at V2 or earlier suggests counterclockwise rotation.

8 Voltage and Ventricular Hypertrophy

  • LVH: high voltage criteria with or without strain pattern.
  • RVH: tall R wave in V1, R/S ratio greater than 1 in V1, deep S waves in V5–V6, and right axis deviation.
  • Low voltage: QRS amplitude less than 0.5 mV in limb leads. Consider pericardial effusion, COPD, obesity, or infiltrative disease.

When Voltage Matters

  • LVH with strain suggests chronic pressure load such as hypertension or aortic stenosis.
  • RVH with right axis deviation suggests pulmonary hypertension, chronic lung disease, or right-sided pressure overload.
  • Low voltage plus tachycardia should raise concern for pericardial effusion or tamponade, especially if the clinical picture fits.

Steps 9–10: Q Waves, Infarction, ST Segment, and T Waves

9 Infarction Signs and Localization

  • Look for pathologic Q waves, poor R-wave progression, or QS complexes.
  • Inferior territory: II, III, aVF.
  • Anterior territory: V1–V4.
  • Lateral territory: I, aVL, V5–V6.
  • Posterior MI: ST depression in V1–V3 with tall R waves, representing a mirror image pattern.

10 ST Segment and T Waves

  • Localized convex ST elevation in a coronary territory suggests STEMI.
  • ST depression and T-wave inversion suggest subendocardial ischemia, unstable angina, or NSTEMI depending on clinical context and biomarkers.
  • Diffuse concave ST elevation with PR depression suggests pericarditis.
  • LVH and bundle branch blocks can create secondary repolarization changes that make ischemia interpretation harder.

Step 11: QT Interval, U Waves, and Electrolytes

11 QT Interval and U Waves

  • Visual trick: if the T wave ends before half of the RR interval, QT is probably acceptable.
  • If the T wave extends beyond half the RR interval, verify the QTc and review medications and electrolytes.
  • Hyperkalemia: tall peaked T waves, loss of P waves, QRS widening, and possible sine-wave pattern.
  • Hypokalemia: flattened T waves, ST depression, and prominent U waves.
  • Hypocalcemia prolongs QT.
  • Hypercalcemia shortens QT.

Putting It Together

At the end of your ECG review, you should be able to produce two clean lines.

Rhythm line: “Sinus rhythm at 82 bpm with normal axis and normal intervals.”

Summary line: “LVH with lateral strain; no definite acute ischemic changes.”

Clinical Pearls

  • Use the same ECG sequence every time.
  • Start with rhythm before looking for ischemia.
  • Irregularly irregular rhythm should immediately raise concern for atrial fibrillation.
  • Short PR plus delta wave suggests WPW pattern.
  • RBBB plus left axis deviation suggests bifascicular block.
  • LBBB and LVH can make ST-T interpretation difficult.
  • Posterior MI can present as ST depression in V1–V3 with tall R waves.
  • Hyperkalemia progresses from peaked T waves to loss of P waves to QRS widening.
  • Hypokalemia can cause prominent U waves.
  • Always interpret the ECG in clinical context.

Bottom Line

A safe ECG interpretation follows a consistent order: rhythm, rate, P waves, PR interval, axis, QRS width, precordial transition, voltage, infarction patterns, ST-T changes, QT interval, and electrolyte clues. This prevents premature closure and helps you identify rhythm disorders, conduction disease, hypertrophy, ischemia, infarction, and dangerous electrolyte abnormalities.

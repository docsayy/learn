Practice-Changing Updates in Internal Medicine (2025–2026)

Added June 17, 2026 • General Internal Medicine • ~18 min read

A practical resident-level review of recent guideline updates and high-impact studies affecting hospital medicine, including acute MI transfusion thresholds, complicated UTI duration, community-acquired pneumonia guidance, AI in clinical reasoning, hypertonic saline in heart failure, ultrasound-guided lumbar puncture, automated ventilator weaning, Alzheimer disease research, SSRI-associated hyponatremia, and neuroimmunology.

Overview

This article reviews several recent updates that may change how residents and hospitalists think about common inpatient problems. The major themes include more liberal transfusion thresholds in acute myocardial infarction, shorter antibiotic courses for complicated urinary tract infection, newer guidance for community-acquired pneumonia, increasing clinical use of AI, and several emerging studies in procedures, critical care, heart failure, neurology, and hyponatremia.

Resident framing: These updates are best thought of as practice-changing or practice-informing pearls. Some are already reflected in guidelines, while others are promising but still require more validation before becoming routine bedside care.

Acute MI and Blood Transfusion Updates

For many hospitalized patients, a restrictive red blood cell transfusion strategy using a hemoglobin threshold near 7 g/dL has been standard. Acute myocardial infarction is increasingly becoming an exception because myocardial oxygen delivery may be especially important during active ischemia.

Recent guidance discussed in the transcript highlights a more liberal transfusion approach in hospitalized patients with acute MI, targeting a hemoglobin level greater than 10 g/dL. This recommendation was influenced by newer evidence, including the MINT trial, which suggested a trend toward improved outcomes with a more liberal strategy.

Practice-changing update: In hospitalized patients with acute MI, recent guideline discussions support transfusion to achieve a hemoglobin level above 10 g/dL rather than waiting until hemoglobin falls below 7 g/dL.
Clinical Scenario Traditional Approach Updated Direction
General stable hospitalized patient Restrictive strategy, often Hgb <7 g/dL Still commonly used
Hospitalized acute MI Historically often restrictive Consider transfusion goal Hgb >10 g/dL
Concern with liberal transfusion Less blood exposure Higher risk of transfusion reactions, volume overload, and related adverse events

How to Remember It

For exam and bedside purposes, remember that acute MI is a special situation where oxygen delivery matters. The transcript emphasizes making an Anki-style card: recent AABB and ACC/AHA guidance now supports transfusion to maintain hemoglobin above 10 g/dL in acute MI.

Updated Complicated UTI Guidance

The transcript highlights updated IDSA guidance for complicated urinary tract infections. A major conceptual shift is moving away from automatically labeling all UTIs in men as complicated. Instead, the distinction is based more on whether infection is confined to the bladder or extends beyond it.

New terminology: Uncomplicated UTI is confined to the bladder in an afebrile patient. Complicated UTI includes infection beyond the bladder, including pyelonephritis, febrile UTI, bacteremic UTI, and catheter-associated UTI.
UTI Category Practical Definition Examples
Uncomplicated UTI Confined to bladder, afebrile Simple cystitis
Complicated UTI Beyond the bladder or systemic features Pyelonephritis, febrile UTI, bacteremic UTI
Catheter-associated UTI UTI associated with urinary catheter Foley-associated infection with symptoms

Antibiotic Duration

A key update is shorter therapy for many complicated UTIs. The transcript notes 5–7 days for fluoroquinolones and 7 days for non-fluoroquinolone antibiotics. It also emphasizes that associated gram-negative bacteremia does not automatically require extending therapy to 14 days.

Scenario Suggested Duration Comment
Complicated UTI treated with fluoroquinolone 5–7 days When clinically appropriate and susceptible
Complicated UTI treated with non-fluoroquinolone 7 days Updated simplified duration
Gram-negative bacteremia from urinary source Often 7 days Do not automatically extend solely because bacteremia is present
Suspected bacterial prostatitis Often longer, such as 10–14 days or more depending on syndrome Requires clinical judgment
Renal transplant recipient Often 14–21 days Important caveat; many short-course studies excluded immunocompromised patients
Caveat: Short-course evidence may not apply to every patient. Use caution in severe sepsis, immunocompromised states, urinary abscess, obstruction, prostatitis, chronic kidney disease, indwelling catheters, and recent urologic procedures.

Updated Community-Acquired Pneumonia Guidance

The transcript reviews updated community-acquired pneumonia guidance, including point-of-care ultrasound, rapid molecular testing, shorter antibiotic courses, respiratory viral panels, and corticosteroids in severe CAP.

Lung Ultrasound

Lung ultrasound is described as an acceptable diagnostic alternative to chest X-ray for diagnosing CAP when clinicians are trained in its use.

Positive Respiratory Viral Panel

A major practice-changing point is that a positive respiratory viral panel does not automatically mean antibiotics should be stopped. The transcript emphasizes that hospitalized patients admitted for pneumonia should generally continue antibiotics even if viral testing is positive, because they are sick enough to require inpatient care.

CAP pearl: If a patient is hospitalized for pneumonia, antibiotics are generally suggested even when the respiratory viral panel is positive.
Patient Group Positive Viral Panel: Antibiotic Approach
Outpatient without comorbidities Antibiotics usually not needed
Outpatient with comorbidities Antibiotics suggested
Hospitalized patient Antibiotics suggested regardless of CAP severity

Duration of Therapy

The update discussed in the transcript suggests that antibiotic durations may be shorter than the traditional 5–7 day course in some patients, with 3–4 days discussed for selected improving cases. Always individualize based on severity, stability, organism, source control, and local guidance.

Steroids in Severe CAP

The transcript highlights growing evidence for systemic corticosteroids in severe community-acquired pneumonia, especially after data from the CAPE COD trial. Steroids are suggested for severe CAP unless concurrent influenza is present.

Severe CAP: Systemic corticosteroids may be considered in severe community-acquired pneumonia, but avoid routine use when influenza is present unless another indication exists.

AI and Clinical Reasoning

The transcript reviews studies evaluating large language models in clinical reasoning, including Google’s AMIE system. In one study using New England Journal of Medicine clinicopathologic conference cases, the AI system outperformed unassisted physicians in placing the correct diagnosis in the differential and ranking it first.

Outcome AI System Unassisted Physicians
Correct diagnosis included in differential 59% 34%
Correct diagnosis ranked first 29% 16%

The most clinically useful framing is not that AI immediately replaces physicians, but that AI-assisted clinicians may perform better than clinicians working alone. The transcript also notes that randomized trials measuring real patient outcomes will be the major next step.

Practical takeaway: AI is likely to become an adjunctive clinical reasoning tool. The key question is whether AI-enabled workflows improve real patient outcomes, not just test-case performance.

Hypertonic Saline and Acute Decompensated Heart Failure

The transcript discusses emerging evidence that sodium supplementation with loop diuretics may benefit selected patients with acute decompensated heart failure. This challenges the long-standing instinct to avoid sodium aggressively in all heart failure contexts.

A meta-analysis described in the transcript included thousands of patients and found that sodium supplementation plus loop diuretics was associated with more weight loss, shorter hospital length of stay, and improvement in laboratory parameters such as creatinine and NT-proBNP. Readmissions and 30-day mortality were similar, and worsening pulmonary edema was not increased in the reviewed data.

Potential future shift: Hypertonic saline plus loop diuretics may eventually become part of selected acute heart failure protocols, but it should not be applied broadly without institutional guidance and careful patient selection.
Outcome Finding Discussed
Weight loss Greater with sodium supplementation plus loop diuretic
Hospital length of stay Shorter in reviewed studies
Creatinine and NT-proBNP Improved laboratory parameters
30-day mortality/readmission Similar between groups
Pulmonary edema No increase reported in the reviewed analysis

Ultrasound-Guided Lumbar Puncture

The transcript highlights ultrasound-assisted lumbar puncture as a promising procedural improvement. Ultrasound can help identify landmarks and improve success, especially in patients with difficult anatomy.

Technique Success Rate Discussed
Traditional landmark-guided lumbar puncture 73%
Ultrasound-assisted paramedian technique 85%
Procedure pearl: Ultrasound does not replace procedural skill, but it can improve landmark identification and may increase first-pass or overall success in difficult LPs.

Automated Ventilator Weaning

Automated or closed-loop ventilator weaning systems adjust support in real time based on patient effort and respiratory parameters. The transcript reviews a large meta-analysis comparing automated weaning with usual care in ICU patients.

Outcome Improvement Discussed
Mechanical ventilation duration About 1.7 fewer days
ICU length of stay About 1.6 fewer days
Hospital length of stay About 2 fewer days
Reintubation Reduced
Tracheostomy Reduced
Mortality Similar between groups

This may become increasingly important as ICUs adopt more protocolized, algorithm-driven respiratory care. The likely benefit is not replacing clinicians, but providing consistent minute-to-minute ventilator adjustments.

Lithium and Alzheimer Disease

The transcript reviews emerging research suggesting that lithium depletion may play a role in Alzheimer disease pathogenesis. In the discussed studies, lower lithium concentrations were observed in affected brain regions, and animal models suggested that lithium restriction worsened Alzheimer-like pathology.

One particularly interesting point was that amyloid beta appeared to bind and sequester many lithium salts, potentially worsening lithium depletion. A specific lithium salt, lithium orotate, showed lower amyloid beta binding in the discussed animal model and appeared to prevent Alzheimer-like pathologic changes and memory loss in mice.

Important limitation: This is not a clinical recommendation to use lithium for Alzheimer disease. The transcript describes early mechanistic and animal-model research that may guide future human studies.

Potential Mechanistic Themes

  • Lithium depletion may contribute to Alzheimer-like pathology.
  • Amyloid beta may sequester lithium in the brain.
  • Lithium repletion in animal models may reduce amyloid, tau, inflammation, and cognitive decline.
  • Human clinical relevance remains uncertain.

Antidepressants and Severe Hyponatremia

SSRIs and other antidepressants are known to increase the risk of SIADH and hyponatremia. The transcript emphasizes a useful timing pearl: the risk appears highest soon after medication initiation.

Board pearl: SSRI-associated SIADH and hyponatremia are most likely during the first 3 months after starting therapy. Risk appears to diminish over time and is much lower after the first year.

This matters clinically because a new SSRI in an older patient with new hyponatremia should raise suspicion for drug-induced SIADH, while a patient stable on the same SSRI for years may require a broader workup before attributing hyponatremia to that medication alone.

Brain–Immune System Interactions

The transcript closes with a fascinating neuroimmunology study using virtual reality. Participants exposed to avatars that appeared sick developed measurable brain activation and downstream neuroimmune signaling before actual exposure to a pathogen.

The broader implication is that perceived infection risk may prime the immune system through brain-mediated pathways, including HPA-axis activation, catecholamines, eicosanoids, and innate immune cell activation.

Conceptual takeaway: The immune response may begin before microbial invasion. The brain can detect social or environmental cues of infection and prepare the body for a potential threat.

High-Yield Takeaways

  • Acute MI is increasingly treated with a more liberal transfusion target, often aiming for hemoglobin >10 g/dL.
  • Uncomplicated UTI is confined to the bladder; complicated UTI extends beyond the bladder or includes systemic features.
  • Many complicated UTIs can be treated with shorter antibiotic courses: 5–7 days for fluoroquinolones or 7 days for other agents.
  • Gram-negative bacteremia from a urinary source does not automatically require 14 days of antibiotics.
  • Hospitalized CAP patients should generally continue antibiotics even if a respiratory viral panel is positive.
  • Steroids may benefit severe CAP, but avoid routine use when influenza is present unless another indication exists.
  • AI is rapidly improving in clinical reasoning and may become a powerful physician-assistive tool.
  • Hypertonic saline plus loop diuretics is an emerging area in acute decompensated heart failure.
  • Ultrasound can improve lumbar puncture success.
  • Automated ventilator weaning may reduce ventilator days and ICU length of stay.
  • SSRI-associated hyponatremia is most likely in the first 3 months after initiation.
  • Perceived infection risk may activate immune pathways even before actual pathogen exposure.
Clinical caution: Literature updates are not a substitute for local protocols, specialist input, or patient-specific decision-making. Use these points as a framework for learning and discussion.

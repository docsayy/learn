Iron Deficiency: Practical Approach to Diagnosis and Replacement

Added June 20, 2026 • Hematology / Hospital Medicine • ~12 min read

A practical inpatient and outpatient framework for diagnosing iron deficiency, distinguishing it from anemia of chronic disease, choosing oral versus IV replacement, avoiding common dosing mistakes, and monitoring response to therapy.

Overview

Iron deficiency is common and often under-treated. The key steps are confirming the diagnosis, deciding whether the patient needs oral or IV iron, replacing an adequate amount, and making sure the underlying cause is evaluated.

Core approach: Order iron, ferritin, TIBC, and transferrin saturation when evaluating suspected iron deficiency.

Diagnosing Iron Deficiency

Classic iron deficiency has a predictable lab pattern.

Test Iron Deficiency Pattern
Serum iron Low
TIBC High
Ferritin Low, often <30
Transferrin saturation Low
Iron Deficiency = Low Iron + High TIBC + Low Ferritin + Low Transferrin Saturation
Transferrin saturation <10% is strongly consistent with iron deficiency. Less than 20% is highly suggestive.

Anemia of Chronic Disease

Anemia of chronic disease, also called anemia of inflammation, can look similar because serum iron may also be low. The key difference is ferritin and TIBC.

Test Iron Deficiency Anemia of Chronic Disease
Iron Low Low or normal
TIBC High Low
Ferritin Low Normal or high
Transferrin saturation Low Low or normal
Clinical meaning: High ferritin suggests iron stores may be present, but inflammation prevents effective iron utilization.

Who Needs Iron?

Patients with iron deficiency anemia should generally receive iron replacement.

Typical Treatment Criteria

  • Ferritin <30
  • Transferrin saturation <10–20%
  • Anemia present

Patients with iron deficiency without anemia may still benefit from replacement, especially if symptomatic or in specific populations such as heart failure or chronic kidney disease.

Heart Failure and CKD

Heart failure and CKD use more permissive criteria because ferritin can be falsely normal or elevated in inflammatory states.

HFrEF Criteria

Finding Treat?
Ferritin <100 Yes
Ferritin <300 with transferrin saturation <20% Yes

CKD Criteria

Finding Treat?
Ferritin <100 and TSAT <20% Yes
Hemoglobin <10 with ferritin <500 and TSAT <30% Often favored
FAIR-HF pearl: IV iron improved symptoms, quality of life, and six-minute walk distance in selected patients with symptomatic HFrEF and iron deficiency.

How Much Iron?

The Ganzoni equation can estimate total iron deficit.

Iron Deficit = Weight × (Target Hgb − Actual Hgb) × 2.4 + Iron Stores

Typical target hemoglobin values:

  • Women: 12 g/dL
  • Men: 13 g/dL
  • Adult iron stores: often estimated as 500 mg
Many patients do not need more than 1000 mg of IV iron replacement during an initial repletion course.

Iron in Blood Transfusion

1 unit PRBC ≈ 250 mg elemental iron

This matters when estimating how much additional iron is needed after multiple transfusions.

IV vs Oral Iron

Choose IV Iron When Choose Oral Iron When
Heart failure patients meeting criteria Stable outpatient
Highly symptomatic anemia Mild deficiency
Ongoing blood loss Able to tolerate GI side effects
Cannot tolerate oral iron No malabsorption concern
Malabsorption or gastric surgery Slower replacement acceptable
Need rapid repletion Patient preference

IV Iron Options

Formulation Typical Dosing Comments
Iron dextran Can give 1000 mg once after test dose Older formulations had higher anaphylaxis risk
Iron sucrose 200 mg IV daily × 5 doses Common inpatient regimen
Ferric gluconate 150–250 mg IV for multiple doses Another IV option
Ferric carboxymaltose 750 mg IV, often 1–2 doses separated by 7 days Used in many heart failure trials
Iron dextran: Give a test dose first when required by institutional protocol because of historical anaphylaxis risk.

Oral Iron Options

Medication Common Dose Elemental Iron
Ferrous sulfate 325 mg every other day About 65 mg
Ferrous gluconate 325 mg every other day About 36 mg

Common Side Effects

  • Constipation
  • Nausea
  • Abdominal discomfort
  • Black stool
Iron can turn stool black, but this is different from melena. Iron-associated stool is often hard and constipated, while melena is typically black, tarry, sticky, and may be associated with diarrhea or GI bleeding symptoms.

Common Oral Iron Mistakes

Mistake 1: Giving Iron Too Often

Every-other-day dosing is preferred for many patients because it improves tolerability and may improve absorption compared with daily or multiple-times-daily dosing.

Avoid prescribing oral iron three times daily. It causes more constipation and may not improve absorption.

Mistake 2: Enteric-Coated Iron

Iron is absorbed primarily in the duodenum. Enteric-coated formulations may release iron too far down the intestine, reducing absorption.

Iron absorption: Duodenum
Folate absorption: Jejunum
B12 absorption: Ileum

Mistake 3: Overemphasizing Vitamin C

Vitamin C is commonly recommended to improve absorption, but the evidence is not as strong as many clinicians assume.

Mistake 4: Taking With Food When Not Needed

Taking iron without food improves absorption, but taking it with food is reasonable if GI side effects prevent adherence.

Follow-Up

Clinical Response

Response Expected Timing
Pica / pagophagia improvement Can improve rapidly after IV iron
Improved well-being Several days
Restless legs improvement About 72 hours
Hemoglobin rise Usually within 1–2 weeks

Repeat Iron Studies

Therapy When to Recheck
Oral iron About 6–8 weeks
IV iron About 2 weeks
Continue replacement until ferritin and transferrin saturation normalize.

Evaluate the Cause

Iron deficiency anemia without a clear source requires evaluation for blood loss, especially gastrointestinal blood loss.

Adults with unexplained iron deficiency anemia often require colonoscopy or GI evaluation, especially when screening-age or without an obvious explanation.

Clinical Pearls

  1. Order iron, ferritin, TIBC, and transferrin saturation for suspected iron deficiency.
  2. Iron deficiency usually shows low iron, high TIBC, low ferritin, and low transferrin saturation.
  3. Anemia of chronic disease usually shows low iron, low TIBC, and high ferritin.
  4. Transferrin saturation <10% is strongly consistent with iron deficiency.
  5. Ferritin can be misleadingly normal or high in inflammation.
  6. HFrEF and CKD use more lenient iron deficiency criteria.
  7. IV iron is preferred in many symptomatic HFrEF patients meeting criteria.
  8. Iron sucrose 200 mg IV daily for 5 days gives 1000 mg total.
  9. Iron dextran can be given as a single large dose but may require a test dose.
  10. One unit of PRBC contains about 250 mg iron.
  11. Oral iron should usually be dosed every other day.
  12. Avoid routine TID oral iron dosing.
  13. Ferrous sulfate has more elemental iron than ferrous gluconate.
  14. Ferrous gluconate may be better tolerated in patients with GI side effects.
  15. Enteric-coated iron may reduce absorption.
  16. Iron is absorbed mainly in the duodenum.
  17. Oral iron can cause constipation and black stools.
  18. Unexplained iron deficiency anemia requires evaluation for occult blood loss.

Bottom Line

Iron deficiency is diagnosed with low iron, high TIBC, low ferritin, and low transferrin saturation. Distinguish it from anemia of chronic disease, where ferritin is often high and TIBC is low. Treat iron deficiency anemia, consider more permissive criteria in HFrEF and CKD, and choose IV iron when rapid repletion, malabsorption, intolerance, ongoing blood loss, or heart failure indications are present. For oral therapy, every-other-day dosing is preferred, enteric-coated formulations should generally be avoided, and unexplained iron deficiency anemia requires evaluation for occult blood loss.

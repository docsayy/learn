Acute Respiratory Distress Syndrome: Diagnosis and Lung-Protective Management

Added July 15, 2026 • Pulmonary and Critical Care • ~30 min read

A comprehensive resident guide to the global ARDS definition, differential diagnosis, lung-protective ventilation, PEEP, prone positioning, neuromuscular blockade, conservative fluid management, rescue therapies, ECMO referral, and prevention of ventilator-induced lung injury.

Clinical Overview

ARDS is acute diffuse inflammatory lung injury causing noncardiogenic pulmonary edema, loss of aerated lung, severe shunt, and heterogeneous mechanical stress. Management is primarily supportive while the precipitating cause is treated.

Treat the Cause + Protect the Lung + Protect the Patient

Global ARDS Definition

  • Acute onset within about one week of a known clinical insult or new/worsening respiratory symptoms.
  • Bilateral opacities not fully explained by effusions, collapse, nodules, or masses.
  • Pulmonary edema not primarily explained by cardiogenic or fluid-overload causes.
  • Hypoxemia assessed using PaO₂/FiO₂ or SpO₂/FiO₂ under specified oxygen-support conditions.
  • The global definition includes patients on HFNO at flow at least 30 L/min and allows ultrasound as an imaging modality in appropriate settings.

Common Causes

  • Pneumonia
  • Sepsis without pneumonia
  • Aspiration
  • Pancreatitis
  • Trauma or burns
  • Massive transfusion or TRALI
  • Near drowning
  • Inhalational injury

Important Mimics

  • Cardiogenic pulmonary edema
  • Diffuse alveolar hemorrhage
  • Acute eosinophilic pneumonia
  • Organizing pneumonia
  • Drug-induced pneumonitis
  • Acute exacerbation of ILD
  • Pulmonary embolism with infarction
  • Pulmonary veno-occlusive disease

Use history, echocardiography, ultrasound, biomarkers, bronchoscopy, and response to therapy selectively; no single test distinguishes ARDS from all mimics.

Lung-Protective Ventilation

  • Use predicted body weight, not actual weight.
  • Target tidal volume about 6 mL/kg predicted body weight; reduce toward 4 mL/kg if needed.
  • Keep plateau pressure generally at or below 30 cm H₂O.
  • Minimize driving pressure when possible.
  • Accept permissive hypercapnia when safe.
Driving Pressure = Plateau Pressure − Total PEEP

PEEP and Recruitment

PEEP can prevent cyclic collapse but can also overdistend aerated lung, reduce venous return, and worsen RV function. Use a structured PEEP-FiO₂ strategy, oxygenation response, compliance, hemodynamics, and recruitability rather than escalating blindly.

A rising plateau pressure, falling compliance, or new shock after PEEP escalation suggests harm or overdistention.

Prone Positioning

  • Strongly consider prolonged prone sessions in moderate-to-severe ARDS, particularly P/F below about 150 despite optimized ventilation.
  • Use sessions of at least 12–16 hours.
  • Continue lung-protective settings while prone.
  • Use a trained team and protect eyes, pressure points, lines, and the airway.

Neuromuscular Blockade

Short-course neuromuscular blockade may be useful for severe dyssynchrony, proning, very high respiratory drive, or inability to maintain lung-protective ventilation. It should be paired with deep analgesia and sedation and discontinued as soon as the indication resolves.

Fluid and Hemodynamic Strategy

  • Resuscitate shock promptly.
  • After shock resolves, use a conservative fluid strategy.
  • Actively evaluate cumulative balance, venous congestion, and diuretic responsiveness.
  • Avoid reflex fluid boluses for oliguria without evidence of fluid responsiveness.

Corticosteroids

Current critical-care guidelines support corticosteroids in selected ARDS patients. Choice, dose, and timing depend on etiology, severity, contraindications, and local protocol. Avoid allowing a steroid decision to delay source control or antimicrobial therapy.

Rescue Therapies and ECMO

  • Ensure proning, lung-protective ventilation, and adequate PEEP have been implemented.
  • Use inhaled nitric oxide or epoprostenol as a temporary bridge in selected severe hypoxemia or RV failure—not as definitive therapy.
  • Consider ECMO referral early for refractory hypoxemia or uncompensated hypercapnia despite optimized conventional management.

Common Pitfalls

  • Using actual body weight for tidal volume
  • Treating saturation with injurious tidal volume
  • Delaying proning
  • Giving repeated fluids after shock resolves
  • Ignoring RV failure
  • Calling every bilateral infiltrate ARDS
  • Late ECMO referral after prolonged injurious ventilation

Bottom Line

ARDS care is disciplined supportive care: low tidal volume based on predicted body weight, limited plateau and driving pressures, appropriate PEEP, early proning for severe disease, conservative fluid management after resuscitation, and early referral when conventional support is failing.

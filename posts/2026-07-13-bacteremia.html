Bloodstream Infections and Staphylococcus aureus Bacteremia: The Complete Advanced Resident Guide

Added July 13, 2026 • Infectious Disease • ~32 min read

Bloodstream infection is a high-risk clinical syndrome that requires rapid recognition, accurate interpretation of blood cultures, effective antimicrobial therapy, source control, and evaluation for endovascular or metastatic infection. This guide focuses on the approach expected of a senior medicine resident, with special emphasis on Staphylococcus aureus bacteremia, catheter-related infection, gram-negative bacteremia, persistent bacteremia, echocardiography, treatment duration, and oral step-down.

Clinical Overview

A bloodstream infection occurs when a pathogenic microorganism is present in the blood and is associated with a compatible clinical syndrome. Bacteremia may be transient and rapidly cleared, or it may persist because of an endovascular infection, retained device, undrained focus, infected thrombus, or metastatic seeding.

A positive blood culture is not the final diagnosis. The medicine team must determine:

  • Whether the isolate is a true pathogen or contaminant
  • The likely source of bloodstream invasion
  • Whether bacteremia has cleared
  • Whether endocarditis or metastatic infection is present
  • Whether an intravascular device must be removed
  • The narrowest effective antimicrobial regimen
  • The correct treatment start date and duration
Organism + Source + Clearance + Source Control + Metastatic Evaluation = Complete Bacteremia Assessment
The central question is not merely, “What antibiotic covers this organism?” It is, “Why is this organism in the bloodstream, and what must be done to prevent persistence, relapse, embolization, or metastatic infection?”

Why Bloodstream Infections Matter

  • Can rapidly cause sepsis and multiorgan dysfunction
  • May indicate infective endocarditis
  • Can seed bone, joints, spine, brain, spleen, kidneys, and prosthetic material
  • Often require removal of infected devices
  • May relapse if treatment duration is inadequate
  • Require organism-specific diagnostic evaluation
  • Have substantial mortality, particularly with Staphylococcus aureus, Candida, resistant gram-negative organisms, and persistent bacteremia

Key Definitions

Term Definition Clinical Importance
Bacteremia Viable bacteria present in the bloodstream May be transient, intermittent, or continuous
Bloodstream infection Bacteremia or fungemia associated with true infection Requires treatment and source evaluation
Primary bloodstream infection No clear nonvascular source identified Often related to an intravascular catheter
Secondary bloodstream infection Bloodstream spread from another infected site Examples include pyelonephritis, pneumonia, cholangitis, or abscess
Transient bacteremia Brief bloodstream presence after mucosal manipulation or localized infection May clear without persistent endovascular focus
Persistent bacteremia Blood cultures remain positive despite active therapy Suggests endocarditis, retained device, deep focus, thrombophlebitis, resistance, or failed source control
Relapsed bacteremia Same organism returns after apparent clearance Suggests inadequately treated or uncontrolled focus
CLABSI Surveillance definition for central line-associated bloodstream infection Used for infection-prevention reporting
CRBSI Clinical catheter-related bloodstream infection supported by microbiologic evidence Used for patient-level diagnosis and management
CLABSI is primarily a surveillance definition. CRBSI is a clinical diagnosis linking the catheter to the bloodstream infection.

Obtaining Blood Cultures Correctly

Blood culture sensitivity depends heavily on the volume of blood collected and the number of properly obtained sets.

What Is a Blood Culture Set?

One blood culture set generally consists of:

  • One aerobic bottle
  • One anaerobic bottle
  • Blood obtained from a single venipuncture site

General Collection Principles

  • Obtain at least two appropriately filled sets when bacteremia is suspected.
  • Draw from separate peripheral venipuncture sites when possible.
  • Obtain cultures before antibiotics if doing so does not delay urgent treatment.
  • Use meticulous skin antisepsis.
  • Do not routinely draw all cultures from an existing central line.
  • If catheter infection is suspected, obtain paired peripheral and catheter cultures.
  • Document the collection site and time for each set.
  • Adequate blood volume is more important than prolonged spacing between sets.

When Blood Cultures Are High Yield

  • Sepsis or septic shock
  • Suspected infective endocarditis
  • Suspected catheter-related infection
  • Severe community-acquired or healthcare-associated pneumonia
  • Vertebral osteomyelitis or spinal epidural abscess
  • Septic arthritis
  • Cholangitis
  • Severe pyelonephritis
  • Febrile neutropenia
  • Severe immunocompromise
  • Possible disseminated fungal infection
  • Persistent unexplained fever with systemic illness

When Blood Cultures Are Often Low Yield

  • Uncomplicated cellulitis without systemic toxicity
  • Simple cystitis
  • Mild outpatient community-acquired pneumonia
  • Viral upper respiratory infection
  • Routine fever without clinical concern for bloodstream infection
Do not delay antibiotics in septic shock, bacterial meningitis, or another immediately life-threatening infection while waiting for difficult culture collection.

Cultures Through a Central Line

When a catheter-related bloodstream infection is suspected:

  • Obtain at least one peripheral culture set.
  • Obtain a culture from each relevant catheter lumen when feasible.
  • Label the source of every specimen.
  • Compare time to positivity between catheter and peripheral cultures.

Differential Time to Positivity

A catheter-drawn culture becoming positive at least approximately two hours before a simultaneously collected peripheral culture supports catheter-related infection.

Differential time to positivity is useful only when cultures are drawn at approximately the same time, properly labeled, and contain comparable blood volumes.

Interpreting Positive Blood Cultures

Interpretation requires integration of the organism, number of positive bottles and sets, time to positivity, collection site, host factors, devices, symptoms, and repeat culture results.

Questions to Ask for Every Positive Culture

  1. What organism was identified?
  2. How many sets and bottles are positive?
  3. Were cultures drawn peripherally or through a line?
  4. How quickly did the cultures become positive?
  5. Was the patient already receiving antibiotics?
  6. Is there a compatible clinical syndrome?
  7. Does the patient have prosthetic material or intravascular devices?
  8. Has the organism been isolated previously?
  9. Are repeat cultures positive?
  10. Is there a plausible source?

Factors Supporting True Bloodstream Infection

  • Same organism isolated from multiple separate culture sets
  • Rapid time to positivity
  • Compatible sepsis syndrome
  • Organism known to be a true blood pathogen
  • Positive culture from another infected sterile site
  • Persistent positivity
  • Presence of prosthetic material or vascular device
  • Clinical improvement after targeted treatment and source control

Factors Supporting Contamination

  • Only one bottle positive from several sets
  • Organism commonly associated with skin contamination
  • Delayed time to positivity
  • No compatible symptoms or source
  • Repeat cultures remain negative without active treatment
  • No prosthetic material or intravascular device
No single feature proves contamination. Coagulase-negative staphylococci can be contaminants in one patient and life-threatening pathogens in another patient with a prosthetic valve or central line.

Contaminant vs True Pathogen

Organisms Usually Treated as True Pathogens

Organism Clinical Implication
Staphylococcus aureus True bacteremia until proven otherwise; evaluate for endocarditis and metastatic infection
Candida species True invasive infection; never dismiss as contamination
Enterobacterales Usually true infection; search urinary, biliary, abdominal, or other source
Pseudomonas aeruginosa Usually true infection; evaluate devices, urinary tract, lung, skin, and neutropenia
Streptococcus pneumoniae Usually true pathogen; commonly pneumonia or meningitis
Beta-hemolytic streptococci True pathogen; evaluate skin, soft tissue, bone, joint, and invasive disease
Enterococcus species Usually clinically meaningful; consider urinary, abdominal, biliary, device, or endocardial source
Anaerobic gram-negative rods Usually true infection; consider abdominal, pelvic, oral, or necrotic source

Organisms Frequently Associated with Contamination

  • Coagulase-negative staphylococci
  • Corynebacterium species
  • Cutibacterium acnes
  • Bacillus species other than B. anthracis
  • Micrococcus species

When a Common Contaminant May Be a True Pathogen

  • Multiple positive culture sets
  • Prosthetic valve
  • Cardiac implantable electronic device
  • Vascular graft
  • Prosthetic joint
  • Central venous catheter
  • Immunocompromised host
  • Persistent bacteremia
  • Compatible focal infection
Do not dismiss Cutibacterium acnes or coagulase-negative staphylococci in patients with prosthetic cardiac, orthopedic, or vascular material without careful evaluation.

Initial Management of Suspected Bloodstream Infection

Step 1: Assess Stability

Status Immediate Action
Septic shock Obtain cultures rapidly, begin effective empiric antibiotics, resuscitate, and pursue urgent source control
Sepsis without shock Obtain cultures, begin syndrome-appropriate therapy, and evaluate source promptly
Stable with uncertain diagnosis Obtain high-quality cultures and focused testing before unnecessary broad therapy

Step 2: Start Syndrome-Appropriate Empiric Therapy

Empiric therapy should reflect:

  • Likely source
  • Severity of illness
  • Previous microbiology
  • Recent antibiotic exposure
  • Local resistance patterns
  • Healthcare exposure
  • Immunocompromised state
  • Drug allergy and organ function

Step 3: Review Devices and Prosthetic Material

  • Central venous catheter
  • Hemodialysis catheter
  • Pacemaker or ICD
  • Prosthetic valve
  • Vascular graft
  • Prosthetic joint
  • Urinary catheter
  • Ventricular shunt

Step 4: Search for the Source

  • Detailed skin and soft-tissue examination
  • Line sites
  • Cardiac examination
  • Spine examination
  • Joint examination
  • Neurologic examination
  • Abdominal and genitourinary examination
  • Review of recent procedures and injections

Step 5: Reassess at 24–72 Hours

  • Review organism identification and susceptibilities.
  • Narrow therapy.
  • Repeat blood cultures when indicated.
  • Confirm source control.
  • Determine need for echocardiography.
  • Search for metastatic infection.
  • Define treatment duration.

Finding the Source

Common Sources of Bacteremia

Source Common Organisms
Urinary tract Enterobacterales, Enterococcus, Pseudomonas
Biliary tract Enterobacterales, Enterococcus, anaerobes
Intra-abdominal infection Enterobacterales, streptococci, enterococci, anaerobes
Skin and soft tissue S. aureus, beta-hemolytic streptococci, polymicrobial organisms in selected wounds
Pneumonia Pneumococcus, S. aureus, gram-negative organisms
Central venous catheter Coagulase-negative staphylococci, S. aureus, gram-negative rods, Candida
Endocarditis S. aureus, viridans streptococci, Enterococcus, HACEK, coagulase-negative staphylococci
Bone or joint S. aureus, streptococci, gram-negative organisms
Oral or dental source Viridans streptococci, oral anaerobes

Clues to an Endovascular Source

  • Persistent bacteremia
  • No clear removable source
  • New murmur
  • Embolic phenomena
  • Prosthetic valve
  • Cardiac device
  • Hemodialysis dependence
  • Injection drug use
  • Vascular graft
  • Relapsing bacteremia

Clues to Metastatic Infection

  • Back or neck pain
  • New focal joint pain
  • Persistent headache
  • Neurologic deficits
  • Abdominal or flank pain
  • Persistent fevers
  • Localizing tenderness
  • New pulmonary nodules suggesting septic emboli
In bacteremia, new focal pain is metastatic infection until adequately evaluated.

When to Repeat Blood Cultures

Repeat Cultures Are Required or Strongly Indicated

  • Staphylococcus aureus bacteremia
  • Candidemia
  • Suspected or confirmed endocarditis
  • Persistent fever or hemodynamic instability
  • Retained infected intravascular catheter
  • Cardiac device infection
  • Concern for endovascular infection
  • Inadequate source control
  • Initial culture obtained during ineffective therapy
  • Concern for relapse

Repeat Cultures Are Often Unnecessary

  • Clinically improving uncomplicated gram-negative bacteremia
  • Clear source control with rapid response
  • No endovascular concern
  • No retained infected device
  • No persistent fever or instability

Timing

For S. aureus bacteremia, repeat cultures are commonly obtained every 24–48 hours until clearance is documented.

Do not assume bacteremia has cleared because the patient feels better. Clearance is a microbiologic endpoint when repeat cultures are indicated.

Persistent Bacteremia

Persistent bacteremia should trigger immediate reassessment. It is not simply a reason to continue the same antibiotics for a longer period.

Major Causes

  • Infective endocarditis
  • Retained infected vascular catheter
  • Cardiac device infection
  • Infected vascular graft
  • Undrained abscess
  • Septic thrombophlebitis
  • Vertebral osteomyelitis
  • Epidural abscess
  • Septic arthritis
  • Prosthetic joint infection
  • Obstructed urinary or biliary system
  • Inadequate antimicrobial activity or dosing

Persistent Bacteremia Checklist

  1. Confirm the organism and susceptibility.
  2. Confirm that therapy is active and correctly dosed.
  3. Repeat a complete physical examination.
  4. Remove or exchange suspected infected vascular access.
  5. Obtain echocardiography.
  6. Evaluate new focal symptoms with targeted imaging.
  7. Look for septic thrombophlebitis.
  8. Assess all prosthetic material.
  9. Obtain source control.
  10. Discuss salvage therapy with infectious disease specialists.
Persistent positivity beyond approximately 48 hours in S. aureus bacteremia increases concern for deep-seated or metastatic infection.

Staphylococcus aureus Bacteremia

Staphylococcus aureus bacteremia is one of the most consequential bloodstream infections in internal medicine. It has a strong tendency to adhere to endovascular and prosthetic surfaces, form abscesses, and seed distant sites.

Every S. aureus Blood Culture Is Clinically Significant Until Proven Otherwise

Common Sources

  • Skin and soft-tissue infection
  • Infected vascular catheter
  • Hemodialysis access
  • Endocarditis
  • Bone or joint infection
  • Postoperative wound
  • Pneumonia
  • Cardiac device infection
  • Injection-related infection
  • Unknown source

Mandatory SAB Management Bundle

  • Begin active therapy promptly.
  • Repeat blood cultures until clearance.
  • Remove infected intravascular catheters when feasible.
  • Perform echocardiographic evaluation.
  • Search for metastatic foci.
  • Differentiate uncomplicated from complicated bacteremia.
  • Use a beta-lactam for MSSA unless contraindicated.
  • Define treatment duration from an appropriate anchor point.
  • Obtain infectious disease consultation.
Formal infectious disease consultation in SAB is associated with improved adherence to evidence-based management and better outcomes.

History Questions in SAB

  • Back or neck pain
  • Joint pain or swelling
  • Headache or focal neurologic symptoms
  • Visual symptoms
  • Chest pain or dyspnea
  • Abdominal or flank pain
  • Recent surgery or injection
  • Prosthetic joints or hardware
  • Cardiac valve or implanted cardiac device
  • Injection drug use
  • Hemodialysis access

Focused Examination

  • All vascular access sites
  • Skin, wounds, and pressure injuries
  • Cardiac examination
  • Spine percussion and palpation
  • All painful joints
  • Neurologic examination
  • Signs of embolic disease
  • Cardiac-device pocket

Uncomplicated vs Complicated S. aureus Bacteremia

A short course is appropriate only when all criteria for uncomplicated SAB are satisfied.

Typical Criteria for Uncomplicated SAB

  • Endocarditis has been excluded.
  • No implanted prosthetic material is infected.
  • Follow-up blood cultures become negative promptly.
  • Fever resolves within approximately 72 hours of effective treatment.
  • No evidence of metastatic infection.
  • The source has been adequately controlled.

Features of Complicated SAB

  • Endocarditis
  • Persistent positive blood cultures
  • Persistent fever
  • Unknown or uncontrolled source
  • Vertebral osteomyelitis
  • Epidural abscess
  • Septic arthritis
  • Deep abscess
  • Septic thrombophlebitis
  • Prosthetic valve or infected device
  • Metastatic infection
  • Relapsed bacteremia
Feature Uncomplicated SAB Complicated SAB
Blood-culture clearance Prompt Delayed or persistent
Endocarditis Excluded Present or not adequately excluded
Metastatic infection Absent Present or suspected
Source control Complete Incomplete or uncertain
Typical duration At least 14 days Usually 4–6 weeks or syndrome-specific
If one uncomplicated criterion is not met, manage the patient as complicated SAB until the clinical evaluation proves otherwise.

Echocardiography: TTE vs TEE

Echocardiography is used to evaluate for infective endocarditis, valve dysfunction, abscess, and device infection.

Transthoracic Echocardiography

Advantages

  • Noninvasive
  • Rapidly available
  • Assesses valve function and ventricular function
  • Useful initial study in many patients

Limitations

  • Lower sensitivity for small vegetations
  • Limited evaluation of prosthetic valves
  • Limited evaluation of cardiac devices
  • May miss periannular abscess

Transesophageal Echocardiography

TEE Is Strongly Favored When:

  • Prosthetic valve is present
  • Pacemaker or ICD is present
  • Persistent bacteremia
  • Abnormal or nondiagnostic TTE
  • High clinical suspicion for endocarditis
  • Embolic findings
  • No clear source
  • Hemodialysis dependence
  • Injection drug use
  • Preexisting valve disease
  • Relapsed bacteremia

When TEE May Potentially Be Deferred

TEE may be deferred in carefully selected low-risk patients when:

  • TTE is high quality and negative.
  • Bacteremia clears promptly.
  • A removable low-risk source is identified and controlled.
  • No prosthetic valve or cardiac device is present.
  • No clinical evidence of metastatic infection exists.
  • There is no other indication for a prolonged treatment course.
The decision to omit TEE should be deliberate and documented. A negative TTE alone does not reliably exclude endocarditis in a high-risk patient.

Treatment of MSSA Bacteremia

For methicillin-susceptible Staphylococcus aureus, an antistaphylococcal beta-lactam is preferred over vancomycin for definitive therapy.

Preferred Agents

Agent Advantages Important Considerations
Cefazolin Effective, generally well tolerated, less nephrotoxic, convenient dosing Renal adjustment required; commonly preferred in practice
Nafcillin Traditional definitive MSSA therapy Frequent dosing, hepatotoxicity, nephritis, sodium load, phlebitis
Oxacillin Traditional definitive MSSA therapy Similar toxicity and administration issues to nafcillin

Why Not Continue Vancomycin?

  • Slower bactericidal activity against MSSA
  • Higher risk of treatment failure in serious MSSA infection
  • Nephrotoxicity
  • Inferior definitive treatment when a beta-lactam can be used
Vancomycin may be appropriate empirically, but it should usually be changed to cefazolin, nafcillin, or oxacillin when MSSA is confirmed.

Cefazolin Inoculum Effect

Some MSSA isolates show reduced cefazolin activity at high bacterial burden in laboratory testing. The clinical importance remains debated. Cefazolin is widely used successfully for MSSA bacteremia, including many deep infections, but agent selection should consider the infection site, bacterial burden, and specialist input.

Severe Penicillin Allergy

  • Clarify the exact reaction.
  • Many patients with a penicillin-allergy label can receive cefazolin.
  • Consider allergy assessment when a beta-lactam is preferred.
  • If a true severe immediate or delayed beta-lactam reaction exists, use an active alternative with ID guidance.

Treatment of MRSA Bacteremia

First-Line Agents

Agent Typical Role Major Monitoring
Vancomycin Common initial and definitive therapy AUC-guided exposure, renal function, infusion reaction
Daptomycin Alternative for bacteremia and right-sided endocarditis CK, muscle symptoms, eosinophilic pneumonia
Ceftobiprole Potential option where approved and available Use according to indication, susceptibility, and specialist guidance

Vancomycin

  • Use AUC-guided dosing for serious MRSA infections when feasible.
  • Monitor renal function closely.
  • Confirm adequate exposure without excessive nephrotoxicity.
  • Reassess if bacteremia persists despite apparently adequate therapy.

Daptomycin

  • Weight-based IV therapy
  • Higher doses are frequently used for complicated bacteremia or endovascular infection
  • Monitor baseline and serial CK
  • Review concomitant statin use
  • Do not use for primary pneumonia because pulmonary surfactant inactivates the drug
Daptomycin is appropriate for bloodstream and endovascular MRSA infection but not for MRSA pneumonia.

Linezolid

Linezolid has excellent oral bioavailability and lung penetration but is generally not preferred as initial therapy for MRSA bacteremia because it is bacteriostatic and has limited evidence for endovascular infection.

Vancomycin MIC

Do not change therapy solely because the reported vancomycin MIC is at the upper end of the susceptible range. The clinical response, culture clearance, source control, drug exposure, and toxicity matter more than an isolated MIC value.

Persistent MRSA Bacteremia and Salvage Therapy

Persistent MRSA bacteremia requires a diagnostic and source-control response before simply adding additional antibiotics.

First Reassess

  • Is vancomycin exposure adequate?
  • Is daptomycin dosed appropriately?
  • Has the source been controlled?
  • Has every intravascular device been evaluated?
  • Has endocarditis been adequately assessed?
  • Is there vertebral osteomyelitis, epidural abscess, septic arthritis, or septic thrombosis?
  • Are repeat cultures drawn correctly?

Potential Salvage Strategies

  • Switch vancomycin to high-dose daptomycin
  • Daptomycin plus ceftaroline in selected persistent cases
  • Alternative combination or newer-agent strategies guided by susceptibility and expert consultation
Routine combination therapy is not required for all MRSA bacteremia. Combination regimens are generally reserved for selected persistent, refractory, or complicated infections.

Do Not Use Aminoglycosides Routinely

Routine addition of gentamicin for native-valve staphylococcal bacteremia or endocarditis increases nephrotoxicity and generally does not provide sufficient benefit to justify use.

Gram-Negative Bacteremia

Common Organisms

  • Escherichia coli
  • Klebsiella species
  • Proteus species
  • Enterobacter species
  • Serratia species
  • Pseudomonas aeruginosa
  • Acinetobacter species

Common Sources

  • Urinary tract
  • Biliary tract
  • Intra-abdominal infection
  • Central venous catheter
  • Pneumonia
  • Skin or pressure injury in selected patients

Management Principles

  • Begin empiric therapy based on severity and resistance risk.
  • Review previous cultures for ESBL, AmpC, CRE, or resistant Pseudomonas.
  • Obtain source control.
  • Narrow once susceptibilities return.
  • Assess for obstruction in urinary or biliary infection.
  • Use oral step-down in selected stable patients.
  • Shorter courses are appropriate in many uncomplicated cases.

Uncomplicated Gram-Negative Bacteremia

A patient may be considered for a shorter course when:

  • Hemodynamically stable
  • Clinical improvement achieved
  • Source controlled
  • No endocarditis or vascular infection
  • No retained infected prosthetic material
  • No uncontrolled abscess
  • Active antimicrobial therapy is available
Seven days of effective treatment is sufficient for many selected uncomplicated gram-negative bloodstream infections.

Repeat Blood Cultures

Routine repeat cultures are not required for every improving uncomplicated gram-negative bacteremia. Repeat cultures when:

  • Persistent fever or instability
  • Endovascular concern
  • Retained infected device
  • Inadequate source control
  • Multidrug-resistant organism with uncertain response
  • Relapse is suspected

ESBL-Producing Organisms

  • Carbapenems are preferred for many invasive ESBL infections, particularly outside the urinary tract.
  • Do not assume piperacillin-tazobactam is equivalent for serious invasive ESBL bacteremia.
  • Oral TMP-SMX or a fluoroquinolone may be used for step-down in carefully selected susceptible isolates.
  • Nitrofurantoin and fosfomycin do not achieve adequate blood concentrations for bacteremia.

AmpC Risk

For organisms such as Enterobacter cloacae complex, Klebsiella aerogenes, and Citrobacter freundii, third-generation cephalosporin therapy may select resistance during invasive infection. Cefepime or another mechanism-appropriate agent may be preferred when susceptible.

Pseudomonas Bacteremia

  • Use an active antipseudomonal beta-lactam.
  • Empiric combination therapy may be reasonable in septic shock or high resistance risk.
  • Once susceptibility confirms a reliable active beta-lactam, routine continued dual therapy is usually unnecessary.
  • Evaluate lines, urinary tract, pulmonary source, neutropenia, and skin lesions.

Streptococcal Bacteremia

Viridans Group Streptococci

Consider:

  • Dental or oral source
  • Infective endocarditis
  • Mucositis and neutropenia
  • Gastrointestinal source in selected species

Streptococcus gallolyticus

S. gallolyticus bacteremia or endocarditis should prompt evaluation for infective endocarditis and colonic neoplasia.

Beta-Hemolytic Streptococci

  • Group A Streptococcus: skin, soft tissue, necrotizing infection, toxic shock
  • Group B Streptococcus: older adults, diabetes, pregnancy, urinary and skin sources
  • Groups C and G: skin, pharyngeal, vascular, or endocardial infection

Streptococcus anginosus Group

The S. anginosus group is associated with abscess formation. When isolated from blood, search for:

  • Brain abscess
  • Liver abscess
  • Dental or deep neck infection
  • Thoracic infection
  • Intra-abdominal abscess

Echocardiography

The need for echocardiography depends on the streptococcal species, number of positive cultures, cardiac risk factors, persistence, and clinical findings.

Enterococcal Bacteremia

Common Sources

  • Urinary tract
  • Biliary tract
  • Intra-abdominal infection
  • Central venous catheter
  • Endocarditis
  • Wounds and healthcare-associated infection

Enterococcus faecalis

  • Often susceptible to ampicillin
  • Important cause of endocarditis
  • May require combination therapy for endocarditis

Enterococcus faecium

  • More frequently ampicillin resistant
  • May be vancomycin resistant
  • Often healthcare associated

When to Suspect Endocarditis

  • Persistent bacteremia
  • Community-acquired bacteremia
  • Unknown source
  • Multiple positive cultures
  • Prosthetic valve
  • Cardiac murmur
  • Embolic findings
  • Relapse

VRE Treatment

Agent Major Consideration
Linezolid Excellent oral bioavailability; monitor cytopenias and drug interactions
Daptomycin Bactericidal; often requires high-dose strategy for bloodstream infection
Species identification and susceptibility testing are essential because treatment options differ substantially between E. faecalis and E. faecium.

Anaerobic Bacteremia

Common Sources

  • Intra-abdominal infection
  • Pelvic infection
  • Colorectal malignancy
  • Oral or dental infection
  • Necrotic tissue
  • Diabetic foot infection
  • Aspiration-associated abscess or empyema

Important Organisms

  • Bacteroides fragilis group
  • Clostridium species
  • Fusobacterium species
  • Prevotella species
  • Anaerobic gram-positive cocci

Clinical Pearls

  • Bacteroides fragilis bacteremia usually represents true invasive disease.
  • Clostridium septicum bacteremia is associated with colonic malignancy and hematologic disease.
  • Fusobacterium necrophorum bacteremia may indicate Lemierre syndrome.
  • Source control is essential in anaerobic bloodstream infection.
Anaerobic bacteremia should trigger an active search for tissue necrosis, abscess, bowel pathology, or malignancy rather than treatment with antibiotics alone.

Candidemia

Candida in Blood Is Never a Contaminant

Risk Factors

  • Central venous catheter
  • Total parenteral nutrition
  • Recent abdominal surgery
  • Broad-spectrum antibiotics
  • Neutropenia
  • ICU admission
  • Hemodialysis
  • Pancreatitis
  • Gastrointestinal perforation
  • Injection drug use

Initial Management

  • Start an echinocandin in most unstable or recently azole-exposed adults.
  • Repeat blood cultures daily or every other day until clearance.
  • Remove the central venous catheter when feasible and clinically appropriate.
  • Evaluate for deep-seated infection.
  • Perform a careful eye-symptom assessment and arrange ophthalmologic evaluation when clinically indicated.
  • Step down to fluconazole when stable, susceptible, and cultures have cleared.

Typical Initial Echinocandins

  • Micafungin
  • Caspofungin
  • Anidulafungin

Duration

For uncomplicated candidemia without metastatic complications, treatment generally continues for at least 14 days after:

  • The first negative blood culture
  • Resolution of attributable symptoms

Look for Complications

  • Endocarditis
  • Endophthalmitis
  • Hepatosplenic candidiasis
  • Osteomyelitis
  • Septic thrombophlebitis
  • Infected prosthetic material

Catheter-Related Bloodstream Infection

When to Suspect CRBSI

  • Fever without another clear source
  • Rigors during catheter infusion
  • Exit-site erythema or drainage
  • Catheter malfunction
  • Positive catheter cultures before peripheral cultures
  • Persistent bacteremia with an indwelling line
  • Matching organism from catheter tip and blood

Common Organisms

  • Coagulase-negative staphylococci
  • Staphylococcus aureus
  • Enterococcus
  • Enterobacterales
  • Pseudomonas
  • Candida

Remove the Catheter When:

  • Staphylococcus aureus bacteremia
  • Candidemia
  • Pseudomonas infection
  • Severe sepsis or shock
  • Endocarditis
  • Suppurative thrombophlebitis
  • Persistent bacteremia despite active therapy
  • Tunnel infection or port abscess
  • Organism difficult to eradicate

When Catheter Salvage May Be Considered

  • Long-term access is essential
  • No tunnel or pocket infection
  • No endocarditis
  • No metastatic infection
  • Patient is stable
  • Organism has a reasonable probability of eradication
  • Close follow-up cultures can be obtained

Antibiotic Lock Therapy

Antibiotic lock therapy may be used with systemic therapy in selected long-term catheter infections when salvage is attempted. It is not a substitute for systemic treatment in bloodstream infection.

Catheter salvage is generally inappropriate for S. aureus, Candida, severe sepsis, tunnel infection, endocarditis, or persistent bacteremia.

Prosthetic Material and Cardiac Devices

Cardiac Implantable Electronic Devices

Patients with a pacemaker or ICD and S. aureus bacteremia have substantial risk of device infection, even when the device pocket appears normal.

Evaluate for:

  • Pocket erythema, drainage, erosion, or pain
  • Lead vegetation
  • Valvular endocarditis
  • Persistent or relapsing bacteremia

Management Principles

  • Obtain TEE.
  • Consult electrophysiology and infectious disease.
  • Complete device extraction is generally required for definite device infection.
  • Antibiotics without device removal have a high relapse risk when hardware is infected.

Prosthetic Joints and Orthopedic Hardware

Bacteremia may seed orthopedic hardware. Evaluate new joint pain, swelling, reduced range of motion, or unexplained relapse.

Vascular Grafts

Vascular graft infection should be considered with:

  • Persistent bacteremia
  • Relapse with the same organism
  • Local pain
  • Perigraft fluid or inflammation
  • Embolic or bleeding complications
Antibiotics alone frequently fail when infected prosthetic material remains in place.

Source Control

Effective antimicrobial therapy cannot compensate for an uncontrolled infected focus.

Examples of Source Control

  • Remove infected central venous catheter
  • Drain an abscess
  • Debride infected or necrotic tissue
  • Drain an infected joint
  • Relieve infected urinary obstruction
  • Perform biliary drainage for cholangitis
  • Remove infected cardiac device
  • Revise or remove infected prosthetic material
  • Treat septic thrombophlebitis

Signs of Failed Source Control

  • Persistent blood-culture positivity
  • Relapsed bacteremia
  • Ongoing fever
  • Persistent hemodynamic instability
  • Progressive local pain
  • Enlarging collection on imaging
  • New metastatic lesions
Persistent Bacteremia = Search for an Uncontrolled Source

Treatment Duration

Treatment duration depends on the organism, source, clearance, source control, host, and presence of endovascular or metastatic infection.

Clinical Syndrome Common Duration Framework Important Qualification
Uncomplicated SAB At least 14 days All uncomplicated criteria must be met
Complicated SAB Usually 4–6 weeks May be longer depending on focus
Staphylococcal endocarditis Usually approximately 6 weeks Valve and surgical factors matter
Uncomplicated gram-negative bacteremia Often approximately 7 days Stable patient with source control
Enterococcal bacteremia without endocarditis Source- and response-dependent Exclude endocarditis when indicated
Candidemia At least 14 days after first negative culture and symptom resolution Longer if metastatic disease
Catheter-related bacteremia Organism- and complication-specific Depends on catheter removal and metastatic infection
Septic thrombophlebitis Prolonged therapy Requires source control and individualized duration

When Does the Clock Start?

The treatment start date may be anchored to:

  • The first day of effective therapy
  • The first negative blood culture
  • The date of infected-device removal
  • The date of definitive drainage or surgery
For SAB and candidemia, duration is commonly counted from documented bloodstream clearance when the source has been controlled.

Reasons to Extend Therapy

  • Endocarditis
  • Osteomyelitis
  • Epidural abscess
  • Septic arthritis
  • Retained infected prosthetic material
  • Persistent bacteremia
  • Incomplete source control
  • Metastatic abscess
  • Immunocompromised host with complicated disease

IV-to-Oral Step-Down

General Eligibility

  • Hemodynamically stable
  • Clinical improvement
  • Bloodstream infection has cleared when repeat cultures are required
  • Source controlled
  • Active oral agent with adequate bioavailability
  • Functional gastrointestinal absorption
  • No uncontrolled endocarditis, CNS infection, or retained infected device
  • Reliable adherence and follow-up

Gram-Negative Bacteremia

Oral step-down is appropriate in many selected uncomplicated gram-negative infections.

Potential Oral Agents When Susceptible

  • Fluoroquinolones
  • TMP-SMX
  • Selected appropriately dosed oral beta-lactams in carefully chosen infections

Agents Inadequate for Bloodstream Infection

  • Nitrofurantoin
  • Oral fosfomycin for systemic infection
  • Agents without adequate serum or tissue exposure

Staphylococcus aureus Bacteremia

Standard treatment for SAB has traditionally used prolonged IV therapy. Oral step-down may be considered only in carefully selected low-risk patients after:

  • Documented clearance
  • Adequate source control
  • Exclusion of endocarditis
  • Exclusion of metastatic infection
  • Initial effective IV therapy
  • Specialist review
  • Reliable follow-up
Do not routinely discharge a patient with incompletely evaluated SAB on oral antibiotics simply because the patient is clinically improved.

Long-Acting Lipoglycopeptides

Long-acting IV agents such as dalbavancin may be used for completion therapy in selected complicated gram-positive infections after initial stabilization and clearance. They are not substitutes for diagnostic evaluation, source control, or appropriate initial management.

Special Situations

Hemodialysis Patients

  • High risk for S. aureus bacteremia and endocarditis
  • Evaluate dialysis catheter or graft
  • Low threshold for TEE
  • Coordinate antibiotic dosing with dialysis schedule
  • Remove infected catheter when required

Injection Drug Use

  • Evaluate for right-sided endocarditis
  • Look for septic pulmonary emboli
  • Screen for HIV and viral hepatitis
  • Assess for spinal, joint, and soft-tissue infection
  • Offer addiction treatment
  • Do not automatically exclude patients from outpatient antimicrobial strategies solely because of substance use

Neutropenia

  • Begin rapid antipseudomonal therapy.
  • Bloodstream isolates may arise from gut translocation or central lines.
  • Signs of local inflammation may be minimal.
  • Persistent fever requires reassessment for fungal disease and resistant organisms.

Pregnancy

  • Choose pregnancy-compatible therapy.
  • Do not undertreat serious bacteremia.
  • Consider maternal and fetal consequences of uncontrolled infection.
  • Coordinate with obstetrics and infectious disease.

Asplenia

Asplenic patients are at risk for overwhelming infection from encapsulated organisms, especially:

  • Streptococcus pneumoniae
  • Haemophilus influenzae
  • Neisseria meningitidis
Fever and sepsis in an asplenic patient require immediate evaluation and empiric therapy.

Daily Bacteremia Checklist

  • What organism is growing?
  • Is it a pathogen or contaminant?
  • How many bottles and sets are positive?
  • What is the likely source?
  • Has source control occurred?
  • Are repeat blood cultures indicated?
  • Has bloodstream clearance been documented?
  • Is echocardiography needed?
  • Does the patient have prosthetic material?
  • Are there symptoms of metastatic infection?
  • Is the current antibiotic the narrowest effective choice?
  • Is the dose correct for renal function, weight, and infection site?
  • Can an infected catheter or device be removed?
  • What is day 1 of treatment?
  • What is the planned duration?
  • Can the patient eventually transition to oral therapy?
  • What outpatient monitoring is required?

How to Present Bacteremia on ID Rounds

A strong presentation includes:

  • Organism and susceptibility
  • Dates and sites of positive cultures
  • Date of first negative culture
  • Presumed source
  • Source-control status
  • TTE or TEE findings
  • Evaluation for metastatic infection
  • Current antibiotic, dose, and start date
  • Planned treatment duration
  • Potential discharge regimen
Avoid saying only, “Blood cultures are positive for staph.” State the species, susceptibility, number of sets, persistence, source, clearance date, and diagnostic plan.

Common Clinical Scenarios

Case 1: One Blood Culture with Coagulase-Negative Staphylococcus

One of four bottles grows coagulase-negative staphylococcus after prolonged incubation. The patient is stable, repeat cultures are negative, and no prosthetic material is present.

Likely contamination. Avoid prolonged vancomycin unless additional clinical evidence supports true infection.

Case 2: MSSA Bacteremia on Vancomycin

A patient started on vancomycin has blood cultures identifying MSSA. The source is an infected peripheral IV site.

Change definitive treatment to cefazolin, nafcillin, or oxacillin unless a true contraindication exists. Repeat cultures, remove the infected catheter, and evaluate for endocarditis and metastatic disease.

Case 3: Persistent MRSA Bacteremia

Blood cultures remain positive after three days of vancomycin with therapeutic exposure.

Do not simply continue vancomycin unchanged. Reevaluate source control, remove devices, obtain TEE, search for metastatic infection, and discuss alternative or salvage therapy.

Case 4: Gram-Negative Bacteremia from Pyelonephritis

An improving patient has susceptible E. coli bacteremia from pyelonephritis. Imaging shows no obstruction, and the patient tolerates oral medication.

Narrow therapy, consider active oral step-down, and use a short evidence-based course if the infection is uncomplicated.

Case 5: Candida in One Blood Culture Bottle

One blood culture bottle grows Candida glabrata.

This is candidemia, not contamination. Start active antifungal therapy, repeat cultures until clearance, evaluate the catheter, and assess for complications.

Case 6: S. aureus Bacteremia with New Back Pain

A patient with SAB develops focal thoracic back pain.

Evaluate urgently for vertebral osteomyelitis and epidural abscess, usually with MRI.

Case 7: Central Line Culture Positive Before Peripheral Culture

A catheter-drawn culture becomes positive more than two hours before the paired peripheral culture.

This supports catheter-related bloodstream infection when collection conditions are appropriate.

Case 8: S. gallolyticus Bacteremia

Blood cultures identify Streptococcus gallolyticus.

Evaluate for infective endocarditis and arrange colonic evaluation for neoplasia.

Case 9: Clostridium septicum Bacteremia

Blood cultures grow Clostridium septicum without an obvious wound.

Search for colonic malignancy, bowel pathology, and hematologic disease.

Case 10: Pacemaker Patient with SAB

A patient with an ICD has SAB but no pocket erythema.

A normal pocket examination does not exclude device infection. Obtain TEE and involve electrophysiology and infectious disease.

Common Pitfalls

  • Calling Staphylococcus aureus a contaminant
  • Calling Candida in blood a contaminant
  • Continuing vancomycin for definitive MSSA bacteremia
  • Failing to repeat cultures in SAB
  • Assuming clinical improvement proves bacteremia clearance
  • Failing to remove an infected catheter
  • Using TTE alone to exclude endocarditis in a high-risk patient
  • Not asking about back, joint, or neurologic symptoms
  • Missing an infected cardiac device because the pocket appears normal
  • Extending antibiotics instead of obtaining source control
  • Repeating blood cultures unnecessarily in improving uncomplicated gram-negative bacteremia
  • Using nitrofurantoin for bacteremic UTI
  • Using daptomycin for pneumonia
  • Failing to distinguish CLABSI surveillance criteria from clinical CRBSI
  • Starting the treatment clock before clearance or source control
  • Using a short SAB course without meeting every uncomplicated criterion
  • Ignoring persistent low-grade bacteremia
  • Discharging a patient with SAB before completing the diagnostic evaluation

Board and ID Consult Pearls

  • Staphylococcus aureus in blood is a true pathogen until proven otherwise.
  • Candida in blood is never a contaminant.
  • Repeat SAB cultures every 24–48 hours until clearance.
  • Persistent SAB raises concern for endocarditis and metastatic infection.
  • New focal pain during bacteremia requires targeted evaluation.
  • Cefazolin, nafcillin, or oxacillin is preferred for MSSA bacteremia.
  • Vancomycin or daptomycin is commonly used for MRSA bacteremia.
  • Daptomycin cannot treat pneumonia.
  • A negative TTE does not exclude endocarditis in a high-risk patient.
  • TEE is favored with prosthetic valves, cardiac devices, persistent bacteremia, or embolic findings.
  • Uncomplicated SAB requires all uncomplicated criteria to be met.
  • Uncomplicated SAB requires at least 14 days of treatment.
  • Complicated SAB generally requires 4–6 weeks or syndrome-specific therapy.
  • Many uncomplicated gram-negative bloodstream infections can be treated for approximately seven days.
  • Routine repeat cultures are often unnecessary in improving uncomplicated gram-negative bacteremia.
  • Nitrofurantoin and oral fosfomycin are not treatments for bloodstream infection.
  • S. gallolyticus bacteremia requires endocarditis and colon-neoplasia evaluation.
  • Clostridium septicum bacteremia is associated with colonic and hematologic malignancy.
  • S. anginosus group bacteremia should trigger a search for abscess.
  • Enterococcal bacteremia may represent endocarditis, particularly when persistent or without a clear source.
  • A catheter culture turning positive at least two hours before a paired peripheral culture supports CRBSI.
  • Source control is central to bacteremia management.
  • The first negative culture date should be clearly documented.
  • Every discharge plan should state the drug, route, dose, duration, monitoring, and follow-up.

Bottom Line

A positive blood culture begins the diagnostic process rather than ending it. Determine whether the isolate is a true pathogen, identify the source, obtain source control, document bloodstream clearance when indicated, evaluate for endocarditis and metastatic infection, and select the narrowest effective antimicrobial.

Staphylococcus aureus bacteremia requires a structured management bundle: repeat cultures until clearance, source control, echocardiography, assessment for metastatic infection, beta-lactam therapy for MSSA, appropriate MRSA therapy when resistant, and treatment duration based on whether disease is uncomplicated or complicated.

For gram-negative bacteremia, many stable patients with a controlled source can receive shorter treatment and oral step-down. Persistent bacteremia of any organism should trigger reassessment for endovascular disease, infected hardware, septic thrombosis, an undrained focus, or inadequate antimicrobial therapy.

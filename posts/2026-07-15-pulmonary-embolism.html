Pulmonary Embolism: Diagnosis, Risk Stratification, and Acute Management

Added July 15, 2026 • Pulmonary and Critical Care • ~30 min read

A complete approach to pretest probability, D-dimer, CT angiography, V/Q scanning, RV assessment, anticoagulation, high-risk and intermediate-risk PE, thrombolysis, catheter-based therapy, thrombectomy, duration, and chronic thromboembolic disease.

Clinical Overview

PE ranges from incidental small clot to obstructive shock. Management depends on hemodynamics, RV injury, bleeding risk, clot burden, comorbidity, and whether deterioration is occurring—not CT clot size alone.

Pretest Probability

  • Use clinical gestalt or a validated score such as Wells or Geneva.
  • In very low-risk patients, PERC can reduce unnecessary testing when applied correctly.
  • Use age-adjusted or probability-adjusted D-dimer strategies in appropriate patients.
  • Do not use D-dimer to exclude PE in a high-probability patient.

Imaging

  • CT pulmonary angiography is first-line for many stable patients.
  • V/Q scanning is useful with contrast contraindication, pregnancy, or selected normal chest imaging.
  • Compression ultrasound can establish VTE when chest imaging is unsafe or delayed.
  • Bedside echo supports high-risk PE but does not exclude PE when normal.

Anticoagulation

  • Start promptly when probability is high and bleeding risk acceptable.
  • UFH is useful when immediate procedures, thrombolysis, or rapid reversal may be needed.
  • LMWH is predictable in many stable patients.
  • DOACs are preferred for many outpatient and long-term patients.
  • Pregnancy generally uses LMWH.

Reperfusion Therapy

  • Systemic thrombolysis is standard for many high-risk PE patients without prohibitive bleeding risk.
  • Surgical embolectomy or catheter-based treatment is considered when lysis is contraindicated, fails, or local expertise favors it.
  • Routine lysis is not recommended for stable intermediate-risk PE because major bleeding risk can outweigh benefit.

Supporting the Failing RV

  • Avoid excessive fluid loading.
  • Use norepinephrine to support systemic and RV coronary perfusion.
  • Correct hypoxemia and severe acidosis.
  • Use cautious ventilation with low mean airway pressure.
  • Consider inhaled pulmonary vasodilator as a bridge.
  • Escalate early to reperfusion or mechanical support.

Duration of Anticoagulation

  • At least three months for treated acute PE.
  • Stop after three months when a major transient provoking factor has resolved and recurrence risk is low.
  • Consider extended treatment for unprovoked PE, persistent risk, recurrent VTE, or selected cancer-associated thrombosis.
  • Reassess bleeding risk and patient preference periodically.

Persistent Dyspnea and CTEPH

Evaluate persistent exercise limitation after PE for recurrent clot, heart or lung disease, deconditioning, chronic thromboembolic pulmonary disease, or CTEPH. V/Q scanning is a sensitive screening test for chronic thromboembolic obstruction.

Common Pitfalls

  • Ordering D-dimer after deciding probability is high
  • Calling RV dilation diagnostic of PE
  • Giving large fluid boluses in RV shock
  • Thrombolysing stable patients based on clot size alone
  • Failing to arrange anticoagulation follow-up
  • Ignoring persistent dyspnea

Bottom Line

Diagnose PE using probability-based testing, then risk-stratify by hemodynamics and RV injury. Anticoagulate promptly; reserve reperfusion for shock or deterioration, and support the RV without overloading it.

Noninvasive Ventilation: CPAP, BiPAP, and Bedside Failure Recognition

Added July 15, 2026 • Pulmonary and Critical Care • ~30 min read

A practical guide to selecting CPAP or bilevel noninvasive ventilation, choosing initial settings, treating COPD exacerbation and cardiogenic pulmonary edema, avoiding inappropriate NIV, troubleshooting mask and synchrony problems, and recognizing failure before emergent intubation.

Clinical Overview

Noninvasive ventilation can reduce intubation and mortality in carefully selected patients, but it is a monitored therapeutic trial—not a destination. Benefit is strongest in acute hypercapnic COPD exacerbation and cardiogenic pulmonary edema.

Right Patient + Right Interface + Right Settings + Early Reassessment

CPAP vs Bilevel NIV

ModePrimary EffectTypical Use
CPAPContinuous distending pressure; improves recruitment and reduces preload/afterloadCardiogenic pulmonary edema, selected hypoxemia
Bilevel NIVInspiratory pressure support plus expiratory pressureAcute hypercapnic respiratory failure, COPD
NIV with backup rateAdds timed breaths when spontaneous rate fallsSelected hypoventilation; not a substitute for intubation in an unstable patient

Pressure support equals IPAP minus EPAP. Increasing pressure support generally increases tidal volume; increasing EPAP improves recruitment but may worsen hypotension or hyperinflation.

Best-Supported Indications

  • COPD exacerbation with acute or acute-on-chronic respiratory acidosis
  • Acute cardiogenic pulmonary edema
  • Selected immunocompromised patients with early respiratory failure
  • Post-extubation support in high-risk patients as part of a planned strategy
  • Selected postoperative respiratory failure

Contraindications and High-Risk Features

  • Cardiac or respiratory arrest
  • Inability to protect the airway
  • Severe encephalopathy or uncontrolled agitation
  • Copious secretions or active vomiting
  • Facial trauma or inability to fit an interface
  • Severe shock or unstable arrhythmia
  • Immediate need for intubation
A pH that is very low is not an automatic prohibition in COPD, but it predicts higher failure risk and requires an ICU-level trial with immediate intubation capability.

Starting Settings

  • COPD: consider IPAP 10–12 cm H₂O and EPAP 4–5, then titrate pressure support to reduce respiratory effort and improve ventilation.
  • Cardiogenic edema: CPAP 8–12 or bilevel support may be used depending on hypercapnia and work of breathing.
  • Use the lowest effective FiO₂ to reach the target.
  • Increase pressures gradually while monitoring leaks, tidal volume, comfort, and hemodynamics.

The First 1–2 Hours

  • Respiratory rate should fall.
  • Accessory-muscle use and dyspnea should improve.
  • pH and PaCO₂ should trend in the correct direction in hypercapnic failure.
  • Mental status should remain stable or improve.
  • Oxygen requirement should stabilize or decrease.
The most important NIV response is the trajectory. A patient who looks more exhausted despite a better saturation is failing.

Troubleshooting

  • Leak: reposition mask, change interface, adjust straps without causing skin injury.
  • Persistent effort: increase inspiratory support after excluding auto-PEEP and obstruction.
  • Poor triggering: reduce leaks, adjust trigger sensitivity, address intrinsic PEEP.
  • Dryness or thick secretions: heated humidification and secretion management.
  • Anxiety: coaching and interface adjustment are preferred; avoid deep sedation that hides failure.

NIV Failure and Intubation

  • Worsening acidosis or rising PaCO₂
  • Persistent tachypnea or fatigue
  • Refractory hypoxemia
  • Hemodynamic instability
  • Declining mental status
  • Inability to tolerate interface
  • Secretion burden or aspiration risk
Do not use NIV to postpone an inevitable intubation. Call for airway help while the patient is still cooperative and perfusing.

Disease-Specific Nuance

  • Asthma: evidence is less robust than COPD; use only in selected cooperative patients with close monitoring.
  • De novo severe hypoxemia or ARDS: failure risk is higher; HFNC may be preferable in many patients.
  • Neuromuscular disease: NIV can support ventilatory pump failure if bulbar function and secretions remain manageable.
  • Obesity hypoventilation: bilevel support is often effective, but investigate concurrent heart failure, infection, and pulmonary embolism.

NIV Checklist

  • Confirm a reversible indication.
  • Choose mode and interface.
  • Set an oxygen target.
  • Obtain baseline gas when indicated.
  • Define reassessment time and failure criteria.
  • Keep airway equipment and an intubation plan available.

Common Pitfalls

  • Using CPAP when ventilatory support is needed
  • Using bilevel NIV in an unprotected airway
  • Tightening the mask until pressure injury occurs
  • Ignoring intrinsic PEEP
  • Sedating a failing patient
  • Continuing NIV despite worsening shock or acidosis

Bottom Line

NIV is most effective when the diagnosis is correct, the patient can cooperate and protect the airway, and objective improvement occurs early. COPD with respiratory acidosis and cardiogenic pulmonary edema are the classic high-benefit syndromes.

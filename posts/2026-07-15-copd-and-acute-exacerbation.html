COPD and Acute Exacerbation

Added July 15, 2026 • Pulmonary and Critical Care • ~30 min read

A practical guide to COPD diagnosis, spirometric confirmation, symptom and exacerbation assessment, inhaler selection, oxygen and NIV in acute exacerbation, corticosteroids, antibiotics, chronic hypercapnia, pulmonary rehabilitation, and prevention.

Clinical Overview

COPD is persistent respiratory symptoms and airflow obstruction caused by airway and alveolar abnormalities, usually after significant exposure. Diagnosis requires post-bronchodilator spirometry; symptoms alone are insufficient.

Diagnosis and Assessment

  • Post-bronchodilator FEV₁/FVC below 0.70 confirms persistent obstruction in the appropriate context.
  • Assess dyspnea, health status, exacerbation history, eosinophils, smoking, oxygenation, and comorbidities.
  • Consider alpha-1 antitrypsin testing according to guideline recommendations.

Stable Pharmacologic Treatment

  • Long-acting bronchodilators are the foundation.
  • Dual LABA/LAMA is appropriate for many symptomatic patients.
  • Add ICS when exacerbation risk and eosinophil pattern suggest benefit, especially with asthma features.
  • Avoid chronic oral corticosteroids.
  • Reassess technique, adherence, and inhaler device suitability.

Defining an Exacerbation

An exacerbation is acute worsening of dyspnea, cough, or sputum over days, often triggered by infection or pollution. Always consider pneumonia, heart failure, PE, pneumothorax, arrhythmia, and medication nonadherence.

Acute Treatment

  • Short-acting beta agonist with or without ipratropium
  • Systemic corticosteroid, commonly prednisone-equivalent 40 mg daily for five days
  • Controlled oxygen target commonly 88–92% until blood gas clarifies risk
  • Antibiotics for increased sputum purulence with increased volume or dyspnea, or when ventilatory support is required
  • VTE prophylaxis and treatment of triggers

NIV in Acute Hypercapnic Failure

  • Bilevel NIV reduces intubation and mortality when acute respiratory acidosis is present.
  • Obtain a gas and reassess pH, PaCO₂, respiratory rate, and mentation within 1–2 hours.
  • Intubate for failure, arrest, inability to protect the airway, or hemodynamic instability.

Oxygen-Induced Hypercapnia

Excess oxygen can worsen hypercapnia through V/Q effects, the Haldane effect, and reduced ventilatory drive in some patients. Do not withhold oxygen from severe hypoxemia; titrate to target and monitor gases.

Discharge Bundle

  • Optimize long-acting inhalers.
  • Smoking-cessation treatment.
  • Pulmonary rehabilitation after hospitalization.
  • Vaccination.
  • Written action plan and inhaler teaching.
  • Assess need for long-term oxygen only after stable measurements.
  • Arrange follow-up and evaluate frequent exacerbations.

Chronic Hypercapnia and Oxygen

  • Long-term oxygen improves survival in selected severe chronic resting hypoxemia.
  • It should not be prescribed solely for exertional desaturation without individualized assessment.
  • Long-term NIV may benefit selected stable chronic hypercapnic patients after recovery from an exacerbation.

Common Pitfalls

  • Diagnosing COPD without spirometry
  • Giving uncontrolled oxygen without gas monitoring
  • Missing PE or heart failure
  • Prolonged steroid courses
  • Antibiotics for every exacerbation
  • Failure to arrange pulmonary rehabilitation

Bottom Line

Confirm COPD with spirometry, treat stable disease with long-acting bronchodilation, and use controlled oxygen, short systemic steroids, selective antibiotics, and early NIV for acute hypercapnic respiratory acidosis.

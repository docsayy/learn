Headache in the Hospital: A Practical Inpatient Approach

Added June 19, 2026 • Hospital Medicine / Neurology • ~12 min read

Headaches are extremely common among hospitalized patients. For many interns, the challenge is not recognizing a headache but deciding what medication to order and when to worry about dangerous secondary causes. This guide provides a practical inpatient approach to evaluating headaches, identifying red flags, and building an effective migraine treatment regimen.

Overview

Most inpatient headaches are benign primary headaches such as migraines or tension headaches. However, every clinician must first determine whether a dangerous secondary cause is present before focusing on symptom control.

Never treat a headache reflexively without first considering whether neuroimaging or further evaluation is needed.

Rule Out Dangerous Causes First

If there is anything concerning about the history or examination, obtain neuroimaging and discuss the case with senior residents or attending physicians.

If a focal neurologic deficit or concerning history is present, get the CT scan first and ask questions later.

Concerning Features

  • Focal neurologic deficits
  • Acute change in mental status
  • New seizure
  • Sudden thunderclap headache
  • Immunocompromised state
  • Cancer history
  • Age >50 with new headache
  • Signs of increased intracranial pressure
  • Fever or meningismus

Primary vs Secondary Headaches

After excluding dangerous causes, determine whether the patient likely has a primary headache disorder.

Primary Headache Secondary Headache
Migraine Intracranial hemorrhage
Tension headache Meningitis
Cluster headache Mass lesion
Chronic migraine Stroke
Medication overuse headache Temporal arteritis

Environmental Measures

Simple supportive interventions can significantly improve symptoms.

  • Turn off lights
  • Reduce noise
  • Close the door
  • Encourage rest
Many migraine patients improve substantially simply by minimizing sensory stimulation.

Acetaminophen and NSAIDs

Acetaminophen

Dose Notes
650–1000 mg PO every 4–6 hours Max 4 g/day
Max 2 g/day Cirrhosis or significant liver disease

Ibuprofen

Dose Comments
400–600 mg PO every 6–8 hours 400 mg often provides similar analgesia with fewer side effects
Avoid NSAIDs in patients with AKI, advanced CKD, significant GI bleeding risk, or active peptic ulcer disease.

Ketorolac (Toradol)

  • 30 mg IV or IM
  • Useful when nausea prevents oral intake
  • Higher GI bleeding risk than oral NSAIDs

Metoclopramide and Prochlorperazine

These medications have some of the strongest evidence for acute migraine treatment.

Medication Typical Dose
Metoclopramide 5–10 mg IV every 8 hours PRN
Prochlorperazine 5–10 mg IV every 8 hours PRN
Metoclopramide and prochlorperazine are among the highest-yield medications for acute inpatient migraine treatment.

Potential Side Effects

  • Akathisia
  • Dizziness
  • Orthostatic hypotension
  • Extrapyramidal symptoms
  • Rare neuroleptic malignant syndrome

Supportive Therapies

IV Fluids

Evidence for IV fluids as a direct headache treatment is weak, but they may help patients who are dehydrated, vomiting, or orthostatic.

Dexamethasone

  • 10 mg IV once
  • May reduce recurrence of migraine
  • Does not typically relieve headache immediately

Diphenhydramine

  • 25 mg PO
  • Limited evidence for headache treatment
  • May help treat akathisia if it develops

IV Magnesium

Magnesium is commonly used in refractory migraine treatment and is generally well tolerated.

Medication Dose
Magnesium Sulfate 2 g IV

Many clinicians will repeat anti-dopaminergic therapy and add magnesium when initial treatment fails.

Triptans

Sumatriptan and other triptans have good evidence but are often difficult to use in hospitalized patients due to extensive contraindications.

Medication Dose
Sumatriptan 6 mg subcutaneous

Contraindications

  • Coronary artery disease
  • Coronary vasospasm
  • Prior stroke or TIA
  • Peripheral vascular disease
  • Hemiplegic migraine
  • Basilar migraine
  • WPW syndrome
  • Significant arrhythmias
Triptans work best early in migraine attacks and should generally not be used in patients with ischemic vascular disease.

The Classic Migraine Cocktail

A common emergency department and inpatient regimen includes:

Medication Dose
Acetaminophen 1000 mg PO
Ketorolac 30 mg IV
Magnesium Sulfate 2 g IV
Metoclopramide or Prochlorperazine 10 mg IV
Diphenhydramine 25 mg PO
Normal Saline 1 L IV bolus

Evidence Summary

Therapy Evidence
Metoclopramide Strong evidence
Prochlorperazine Strong evidence
Sumatriptan Strong evidence but many contraindications
Acetaminophen Moderate evidence
NSAIDs Moderate evidence
Dexamethasone Recurrence prevention
IV Fluids Limited benefit except dehydration
Diphenhydramine Poor evidence for headache relief
Opioids should generally be avoided for migraine treatment. They are less effective, increase recurrence risk, and contribute to medication overuse headaches.

Clinical Pearls

  • Rule out dangerous secondary causes first.
  • Know the 2SNOOP4 headache red flags.
  • Treat migraines early whenever possible.
  • Metoclopramide and prochlorperazine have some of the strongest evidence.
  • IV magnesium is a reasonable adjunctive therapy.
  • Triptans are effective but have many contraindications.
  • Avoid opioids for routine headache treatment.
  • Fioricet carries a high risk of rebound headaches and medication overuse headaches.
  • Persistent or refractory headaches should prompt neurology consultation.
  • Newer CGRP antagonists such as rimegepant and ubrogepant are promising options but are frequently non-formulary inpatient.

Bottom Line

For most hospitalized patients with a benign primary headache, start with environmental modifications, acetaminophen or NSAIDs when appropriate, and anti-dopaminergic therapy such as metoclopramide or prochlorperazine. Add IV magnesium and supportive measures when needed. Avoid opioids, know the red flags for secondary headache, and obtain imaging whenever the history or examination raises concern.

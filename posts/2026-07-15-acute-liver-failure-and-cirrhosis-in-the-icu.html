Acute Liver Failure and Cirrhosis in the ICU

Added July 15, 2026 • Pulmonary and Critical Care • ~30 min read

A bedside guide to acute liver failure, encephalopathy, cerebral edema, acetaminophen toxicity, transplant referral, acute-on-chronic liver failure, variceal bleeding, spontaneous bacterial peritonitis, hepatorenal syndrome, ventilation, coagulopathy, and prognostic communication.

Clinical Overview

Acute liver failure is acute hepatic injury with coagulopathy and encephalopathy in a patient without established cirrhosis. Critically ill cirrhosis and acute-on-chronic liver failure have different physiology but share high risks of infection, vasodilation, kidney injury, bleeding, and multiorgan failure.

Acute Liver Failure

  • Urgently identify acetaminophen, viral, ischemic, autoimmune, drug-induced, pregnancy-related, and metabolic causes.
  • Contact a liver-transplant center early.
  • Administer N-acetylcysteine promptly for acetaminophen toxicity and consider it in selected non-acetaminophen ALF.
  • Monitor glucose, ammonia, lactate, INR, renal function, and neurologic status.

Encephalopathy and Cerebral Edema

  • Elevate head and avoid fever, hypoxemia, hyponatremia, and severe hypercapnia.
  • Intubate for advanced encephalopathy and airway risk.
  • Use hypertonic saline or mannitol for intracranial hypertension according to specialist guidance.
  • Avoid routine sedative accumulation that obscures neurologic examination.

Critical Illness in Cirrhosis

  • Search aggressively for infection, GI bleeding, alcohol-associated hepatitis, drug injury, thrombosis, and medication triggers.
  • Perform prompt diagnostic paracentesis in hospitalized patients with ascites and decompensation.
  • Recognize that fever and leukocytosis may be absent.
  • Use early transplant and hepatology consultation for ACLF.

Spontaneous Bacterial Peritonitis

  • Ascitic PMN at least 250 cells/mm³ supports diagnosis.
  • Give appropriate antibiotics immediately.
  • Use albumin in higher-risk SBP to reduce kidney injury.
  • Repeat paracentesis when response is uncertain or secondary peritonitis is possible.

Hepatorenal Syndrome–AKI

  • Exclude shock, nephrotoxins, obstruction, and structural kidney disease.
  • Stop unnecessary diuretics and give an albumin challenge when appropriate.
  • Use vasoconstrictor therapy such as terlipressin or norepinephrine with albumin in suitable patients.
  • Monitor respiratory status because albumin and vasoconstrictor therapy can worsen volume-related complications.

Variceal Hemorrhage

  • Resuscitate cautiously and use a restrictive transfusion strategy in many patients.
  • Start vasoactive therapy and antibiotic prophylaxis early.
  • Perform urgent endoscopy.
  • Use balloon tamponade or esophageal stent only as a bridge.
  • Consider early TIPS in selected high-risk patients.

Coagulation and Procedures

INR does not measure total bleeding balance in cirrhosis. Avoid routine plasma correction for a number alone. Use clinical bleeding, platelet/fibrinogen status, procedure risk, and viscoelastic testing where available.

ICU Support

  • Avoid nephrotoxins and excessive chloride or fluid.
  • Provide adequate nutrition and avoid prolonged fasting.
  • Treat encephalopathy triggers; lactulose and rifaximin are commonly used in cirrhosis.
  • Use CRRT for appropriate indications and cerebral-edema considerations.
  • Discuss transplant candidacy and goals early.

Common Pitfalls

  • Late transplant-center referral
  • Correcting INR routinely with plasma
  • Missing SBP because abdominal pain is absent
  • Large unselected fluid boluses
  • Delaying antibiotics in cirrhosis
  • Using lactulose as the only response to worsening encephalopathy without searching for triggers

Bottom Line

Acute liver failure requires immediate cause-specific therapy and early transplant-center involvement. In cirrhosis, search for precipitants, diagnose infection early, protect kidney and brain function, and avoid treating INR rather than the patient.

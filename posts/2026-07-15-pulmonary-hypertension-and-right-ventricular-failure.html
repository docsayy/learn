Pulmonary Hypertension and Right-Ventricular Failure

Added July 15, 2026 • Pulmonary and Critical Care • ~30 min read

A structured guide to pulmonary hypertension classification, echocardiographic suspicion, right-heart catheterization, acute RV failure, preload and afterload management, vasopressors, inotropes, pulmonary vasodilators, and avoiding harmful empiric PAH therapy.

Clinical Overview

Pulmonary hypertension is a hemodynamic condition with multiple causes. The failing RV is exquisitely sensitive to changes in preload, pulmonary vascular resistance, systemic pressure, and intrathoracic pressure.

RV Perfusion Requires Adequate Systemic Pressure and Controlled Afterload

Hemodynamic Definition

  • Pulmonary hypertension: mean pulmonary artery pressure above 20 mm Hg by right-heart catheterization.
  • Precapillary PH: elevated mean pressure with pulmonary vascular resistance above 2 Wood units and pulmonary artery wedge pressure at or below 15 mm Hg.
  • Postcapillary PH: elevated wedge pressure, with or without a precapillary component.

Clinical Classification

GroupCause
1Pulmonary arterial hypertension
2Left-heart disease
3Lung disease or hypoxia
4Pulmonary artery obstruction, especially CTEPH
5Multifactorial or unclear mechanisms

Diagnostic Workup

  • ECG, chest imaging, PFTs, sleep and hypoxemia assessment
  • Echocardiography for probability and RV function
  • V/Q scan to screen for chronic thromboembolic disease
  • Serologic and liver/HIV testing when appropriate
  • Right-heart catheterization before definitive PAH treatment

Acute RV Failure

  • Identify PE, RV infarction, sepsis, arrhythmia, hypoxemia, acidosis, or excessive ventilator pressure.
  • Maintain systemic MAP with norepinephrine or vasopressin.
  • Use small, response-guided fluid only when underfilled.
  • Diurese or ultrafilter when congested.
  • Consider dobutamine or milrinone for low output with adequate pressure.

Ventilation and the RV

  • Avoid hypoxemia, severe hypercapnia, and acidemia.
  • Use the lowest effective PEEP and plateau pressure.
  • Prevent auto-PEEP.
  • Anticipate collapse during induction and positive-pressure ventilation.
  • Use prone positioning in ARDS when indicated; improved oxygenation can reduce RV afterload.

Pulmonary Vasodilators

  • Inhaled nitric oxide or epoprostenol can temporarily reduce RV afterload with less systemic hypotension.
  • They are bridge therapies in acute decompensation, not substitutes for treating PE, hypoxemia, or left-heart disease.
  • Chronic PAH therapy should be directed by a pulmonary-hypertension center.

What to Avoid

  • Large unselected fluid boluses
  • Isolated phenylephrine in a low-output RV
  • Abrupt interruption of prostacyclin infusion
  • Empiric sildenafil in undifferentiated PH
  • High intrathoracic pressure
  • Uncontrolled tachyarrhythmia
Abrupt interruption of continuous prostacyclin can cause catastrophic rebound pulmonary hypertension.

CTEPH

CTEPH is potentially curable. Refer confirmed disease to a multidisciplinary center for pulmonary endarterectomy assessment, balloon pulmonary angioplasty, medical therapy, or combined treatment.

Common Pitfalls

  • Diagnosing PAH by echo alone
  • Failing to obtain V/Q imaging
  • Treating group 2 or 3 PH with PAH drugs without expert review
  • Overloading the RV
  • Ignoring venous congestion
  • Intubating without vasopressor and rescue planning

Bottom Line

Classify pulmonary hypertension before treating it. In acute RV failure, maintain systemic pressure, avoid hypoxemia/acidosis/high airway pressure, optimize rather than maximize preload, and rapidly treat the cause.

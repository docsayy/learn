Blood Gas ICU Cases and Pimp Questions: Resident Drill Guide

Added July 5, 2026 • Critical Care • ~50 min read

This is a practical ICU drill companion for blood gas interpretation. It is built around real attending-style questions, rapid ABG/VBG interpretation, acid-base formulas, ventilator adjustments, BiPAP and HFNC decisions, and mixed disorder recognition.

Rapid Formula Sheet

Core Values

  • pH: 7.35–7.45
  • PaCO₂: 35–45 mm Hg
  • HCO₃⁻: 22–26 mEq/L
  • PaO₂: roughly 80–100 mm Hg on room air, age dependent
  • Lactate: usually <2 mmol/L

Core Formulas

Anion gap = Na − (Cl + HCO₃)
Corrected AG = AG + 2.5 × (4 − albumin)
Winter formula = 1.5 × HCO₃ + 8 ± 2
P/F ratio = PaO₂ / FiO₂
Disorder Expected Compensation
Metabolic acidosis Expected PaCO₂ = 1.5 × HCO₃ + 8 ± 2
Metabolic alkalosis Expected PaCO₂ ≈ 0.7 × (HCO₃ − 24) + 40 ± 5
Acute respiratory acidosis HCO₃ rises ~1 for every 10 PaCO₂ above 40
Chronic respiratory acidosis HCO₃ rises ~4 for every 10 PaCO₂ above 40
Acute respiratory alkalosis HCO₃ falls ~2 for every 10 PaCO₂ below 40
Chronic respiratory alkalosis HCO₃ falls ~4–5 for every 10 PaCO₂ below 40

Fast method: identify pH → identify primary process → check compensation → calculate anion gap → correct for albumin → delta ratio if gap is high → assess oxygenation → decide whether ventilator/NIV/O₂ support needs adjustment.

Basic ICU Cases

Case 1: Simple Acute Respiratory Acidosis

ABG: pH 7.22 / PaCO₂ 68 / PaO₂ 90 / HCO₃ 27

Scenario: Patient received opioids after surgery and is somnolent.

Interpretation: Primary respiratory acidosis. HCO₃ is only mildly elevated, appropriate for acute compensation.

Likely cause: Hypoventilation from opioid/sedative effect.

Management: Assess airway, reduce sedatives, consider naloxone if opioid toxicity, support ventilation, repeat gas. Intubate if unable to protect airway or worsening mental status.

Do not give bicarbonate for isolated respiratory acidosis. The problem is ventilation, not bicarbonate deficiency.

Case 2: Chronic Respiratory Acidosis

ABG: pH 7.36 / PaCO₂ 62 / PaO₂ 68 / HCO₃ 35

Scenario: Severe COPD, awake, comfortable, baseline home oxygen.

Interpretation: Chronic respiratory acidosis with renal compensation. pH is near normal because kidneys retained bicarbonate.

Management: Do not chase a normal PaCO₂ if this is baseline. Treat the patient and compare with prior gases if available.

Case 3: Acute on Chronic Respiratory Acidosis

ABG: pH 7.24 / PaCO₂ 78 / PaO₂ 58 / HCO₃ 34

Scenario: COPD exacerbation, wheezing, accessory muscle use.

Interpretation: Acute on chronic hypercapnic respiratory failure. The high HCO₃ shows chronic compensation, but the low pH shows acute worsening.

Management: BiPAP if awake and protecting airway, bronchodilators, steroids, controlled O₂, antibiotics if indicated, reassess within 1–2 hours.

If mental status worsens, pH falls, work of breathing increases, or BiPAP is not tolerated, intubate.

Case 4: Simple Metabolic Acidosis

ABG: pH 7.21 / PaCO₂ 25 / HCO₃ 10

BMP: Na 138, Cl 100, HCO₃ 10

Step 1: pH low = acidemia.

Step 2: HCO₃ low = metabolic acidosis.

Step 3: AG = 138 − (100 + 10) = 28 → high anion gap metabolic acidosis.

Step 4: Winter = 1.5 × 10 + 8 ±2 = 23 ±2. Actual PaCO₂ 25 is appropriate.

Interpretation: HAGMA with appropriate respiratory compensation.

Case 5: Metabolic Alkalosis from Vomiting

ABG: pH 7.55 / PaCO₂ 50 / HCO₃ 42

Scenario: Persistent vomiting, low K, low chloride.

Interpretation: Primary metabolic alkalosis with respiratory compensation.

Likely cause: Gastric acid loss, volume contraction, hypokalemia, chloride depletion.

Management: Normal saline, potassium chloride, magnesium if low, antiemetics, stop NG suction if possible.

Mixed Disorder Cases

Case 6: DKA with Respiratory Fatigue

ABG: pH 7.08 / PaCO₂ 30 / HCO₃ 8

BMP: Na 132, Cl 94, HCO₃ 8, glucose 620

AG: 132 − (94 + 8) = 30.

Winter: 1.5 × 8 + 8 ±2 = 20 ±2.

Actual PaCO₂: 30, which is higher than expected.

Interpretation: DKA causing HAGMA plus superimposed respiratory acidosis/inadequate compensation.

Clinical concern: Patient may be tiring. In severe metabolic acidosis, the patient must hyperventilate to compensate. If PaCO₂ rises, the pH can crash.

Do not sedate this patient casually. Intubation is high risk because losing their compensatory hyperventilation can cause severe acidemia.

Case 7: Salicylate Poisoning with Normal pH

ABG: pH 7.42 / PaCO₂ 20 / HCO₃ 13

BMP: Na 140, Cl 102, HCO₃ 13

AG: 140 − (102 + 13) = 25.

Interpretation: High anion gap metabolic acidosis plus primary respiratory alkalosis. pH looks “normal-ish” because two dangerous disorders offset each other.

Likely cause: Salicylate toxicity is classic.

Management: Salicylate level, poison control, sodium bicarbonate infusion, potassium repletion, dialysis if severe.

Normal pH does not mean normal physiology. Always inspect PaCO₂ and HCO₃.

Case 8: Sepsis with Lactic Acidosis and Respiratory Alkalosis

ABG: pH 7.36 / PaCO₂ 22 / HCO₃ 12

Lactate: 7 mmol/L

Interpretation: HAGMA from lactic acidosis plus respiratory alkalosis from sepsis/pain/hyperventilation. pH is near normal because disorders oppose each other.

Management: Treat sepsis: source control, antibiotics, fluids/pressors as clinically indicated, oxygen delivery, trend lactate in context.

Case 9: COPD + Diuresis

ABG: pH 7.46 / PaCO₂ 65 / HCO₃ 45

Scenario: COPD patient aggressively diuresed for HF.

Interpretation: Chronic respiratory acidosis plus metabolic alkalosis. HCO₃ is higher than expected for chronic CO₂ retention alone, and pH is alkalemic.

Likely cause: Loop diuretics, chloride depletion, hypokalemia, post-hypercapnic alkalosis.

Management: Check K, Cl, Mg, volume status. Replete chloride/potassium; consider acetazolamide in selected ventilated or volume-overloaded patients.

Case 10: HAGMA + NAGMA

ABG: pH 7.18 / PaCO₂ 25 / HCO₃ 9

BMP: Na 138, Cl 112, HCO₃ 9

AG: 138 − (112 + 9) = 17.

Delta ratio: (17 − 12) / (24 − 9) = 5 / 15 = 0.33.

Interpretation: Mixed HAGMA + NAGMA. The chloride is high and bicarbonate fell more than the anion gap rose.

Common causes: DKA after saline resuscitation, diarrhea plus lactic acidosis, renal failure plus RTA.

Case 11: HAGMA + Metabolic Alkalosis

ABG: pH 7.47 / PaCO₂ 45 / HCO₃ 32

BMP: Na 140, Cl 92, HCO₃ 32

AG: 140 − (92 + 32) = 16.

Delta ratio: (16 − 12) / (24 − 32) cannot be interpreted normally because HCO₃ is elevated, not low.

Interpretation: Metabolic alkalosis with an elevated anion gap process hiding underneath. Think vomiting/diuresis plus lactate, renal failure, or ketoacids.

Ventilator Cases

Case 12: Ventilated Patient with Respiratory Acidosis

Vent: Volume control, RR 16, TV 420 mL, PEEP 5, FiO₂ 40%

ABG: 7.20 / 62 / 88 / 24

Interpretation: Acute respiratory acidosis with acceptable oxygenation.

Ventilator move: Increase minute ventilation. Usually increase RR first if no severe air trapping. Example: RR 16 → 20.

Check: Plateau pressure, driving pressure, expiratory flow returning to baseline, auto-PEEP.

Case 13: Ventilated Patient with Respiratory Alkalosis

Vent: RR 26, TV 450, PEEP 5, FiO₂ 30%

ABG: 7.55 / 24 / 110 / 22

Interpretation: Respiratory alkalosis from excessive ventilation or patient overbreathing.

Ventilator move: Reduce minute ventilation if appropriate. Decrease set RR, assess pain/anxiety/sepsis, review sedation and synchrony.

Case 14: ARDS Hypoxemia

Vent: FiO₂ 80%, PEEP 8

ABG: 7.36 / 42 / 70 / 24

P/F ratio: 70 / 0.80 = 87.5.

Interpretation: Severe oxygenation defect if ARDS criteria met.

Ventilator move: Increase PEEP per ARDS table/strategy, maintain lung-protective tidal volume, consider proning if P/F <150 despite optimization, treat underlying cause.

Case 15: Too Much Oxygen

Vent: FiO₂ 100%, PEEP 5

ABG: 7.40 / 40 / 280 / 24

Interpretation: Hyperoxia.

Ventilator move: Decrease FiO₂. If oxygenation remains stable, continue titrating down to a safe SpO₂ goal.

Do not leave patients on FiO₂ 100% longer than necessary.

Case 16: Severe Asthma on Vent

Vent: RR 24, TV 500, PEEP 5

ABG: 7.16 / 75 / 95 / 26

Waveform: Expiratory flow does not return to baseline.

Interpretation: Respiratory acidosis from severe obstruction with air trapping.

Correct move: Do not reflexively increase RR. Reduce RR, increase expiratory time, consider lower TV, bronchodilators, deep sedation if needed, permissive hypercapnia.

In asthma, increasing RR can worsen dynamic hyperinflation, hypotension, barotrauma, and arrest.

Case 17: Post-Intubation DKA Crash

Before intubation: pH 7.05 / PaCO₂ 15 / HCO₃ 4

After intubation: Vent RR 16, TV 420

Problem: The patient was compensating with extreme minute ventilation. If the ventilator does not match that compensation, PaCO₂ rises and pH can collapse.

Management: Pre-intubation resuscitation if possible, avoid apnea, set high minute ventilation initially, monitor closely, repeat gas rapidly.

BiPAP / HFNC Cases

Case 18: COPD Exacerbation Needing BiPAP

ABG: 7.28 / 68 / 58 / 31

Patient: Awake, wheezing, using accessory muscles, protecting airway.

Best support: BiPAP.

Why: Main problem is ventilatory failure with hypercapnic acidemia. IPAP helps CO₂ clearance.

Follow-up: Repeat gas after trial. Improvement in pH, PaCO₂, RR, work of breathing, and mental status suggests success.

Case 19: COPD Exacerbation Failing BiPAP

Initial ABG: 7.28 / 68 / 58 / 31

After BiPAP: 7.18 / 82 / 55 / 30

Patient: Sleepy, poor mask tolerance.

Interpretation: Worsening hypercapnic respiratory failure despite NIV.

Management: Intubate if consistent with goals of care.

Case 20: Pneumonia Hypoxemia Needing HFNC

ABG: 7.46 / 32 / 58 / 22 on non-rebreather

Patient: Awake, tachypneic, not hypercapnic.

Best support: HFNC is reasonable if no immediate intubation criteria.

Why: Main problem is hypoxemic respiratory failure, not CO₂ retention.

Watch for failure: Increasing work of breathing, rising oxygen needs, worsening mental status, shock, inability to maintain saturation.

Case 21: Cardiogenic Pulmonary Edema

ABG: 7.30 / 50 / 60 / 24

Patient: Hypertensive, pulmonary edema, severe dyspnea.

Best support: CPAP or BiPAP depending on hypercapnia/work of breathing.

Why: Positive pressure reduces preload/afterload and improves oxygenation. BiPAP adds ventilatory support if CO₂ is elevated.

Management: Nitrates if hypertensive, diuresis when appropriate, treat trigger.

Toxicology Cases

Case 22: Methanol

ABG: 7.10 / 24 / 7

BMP: Na 140, Cl 100, HCO₃ 7

Osm gap: Elevated

Symptoms: Blurry vision.

Interpretation: Severe HAGMA with osmolar gap and visual symptoms.

Diagnosis: Methanol toxicity.

Management: Fomepizole, dialysis if severe, folinic/folic acid, poison control.

Case 23: Ethylene Glycol

ABG: 7.12 / 20 / 6

BMP: Na 138, Cl 98, HCO₃ 6

Findings: AKI, calcium oxalate crystals.

Interpretation: Severe HAGMA with toxic alcohol concern.

Diagnosis: Ethylene glycol toxicity.

Management: Fomepizole, dialysis if severe, thiamine/pyridoxine, poison control.

Case 24: Carbon Monoxide

ABG: PaO₂ 300 on high FiO₂, saturation by pulse ox 100%

Patient: Headache, confusion, house fire exposure.

Interpretation: PaO₂ can be normal or high because dissolved oxygen is present. Pulse ox can be falsely normal because it misreads carboxyhemoglobin.

Diagnosis: Carbon monoxide poisoning.

Management: Co-oximetry, 100% oxygen, consider hyperbaric oxygen depending on severity/pregnancy/neurologic or cardiac findings.

Renal / Electrolyte Cases

Case 25: Type 4 RTA

ABG: 7.29 / 32 / 15

BMP: Na 138, Cl 113, HCO₃ 15, K 5.8

AG: 138 − (113 + 15) = 10.

Interpretation: NAGMA with hyperkalemia.

Likely diagnosis: Type 4 RTA, often from diabetes, CKD, hypoaldosteronism, ACEi/ARB, spironolactone, heparin, or TMP-SMX.

Case 26: Diarrhea

ABG: 7.25 / 30 / 13

BMP: Na 140, Cl 116, HCO₃ 13, K 2.9

AG: 140 − (116 + 13) = 11.

Interpretation: NAGMA from bicarbonate loss.

Likely cause: Diarrhea, especially with hypokalemia.

Management: Fluids, potassium, treat cause of diarrhea.

Case 27: Uremic Acidosis

ABG: 7.22 / 28 / 11

BMP: Na 136, Cl 100, HCO₃ 11, BUN 110, Cr 8.5

AG: 136 − (100 + 11) = 25.

Interpretation: HAGMA from renal failure/uremia.

Management: Assess dialysis indications: acidosis, electrolyte abnormalities, intoxications, overload, uremic symptoms.

Pimp Questions: Basics

Q1. What is an ABG?

An arterial blood gas measures arterial pH, PaCO₂, PaO₂, and often calculated bicarbonate and oxygen saturation. It tells you ventilation, oxygenation, and acid-base status.

Q2. What is the difference between oxygenation and ventilation?

Oxygenation is getting oxygen into arterial blood, reflected by PaO₂ and saturation. Ventilation is clearing CO₂, reflected by PaCO₂.

Q3. What number tells you ventilation?

PaCO₂. High PaCO₂ means inadequate alveolar ventilation. Low PaCO₂ means hyperventilation.

Q4. What number tells you oxygenation?

PaO₂ on an ABG, interpreted with FiO₂. SpO₂ is useful but can be misleading in CO poisoning, methemoglobinemia, or poor waveform states.

Q5. Why can oxygen saturation be okay while PaCO₂ is terrible?

Oxygenation and ventilation are different. A patient can receive enough oxygen to maintain saturation but still hypoventilate and retain CO₂.

Q6. Is VBG good enough?

Often for pH and acid-base trend, especially DKA or stable metabolic disorders. Not for oxygenation, P/F ratio, shunt assessment, or precise PaCO₂ in unstable patients.

Q7. What is serum chemistry CO₂?

Mostly bicarbonate. BMP CO₂ is not PaCO₂. BMP CO₂ reflects the metabolic side; ABG PaCO₂ reflects ventilation.

Q8. Why does bicarbonate on ABG differ from BMP CO₂?

ABG bicarbonate is calculated from pH and PaCO₂. BMP CO₂ is measured total CO₂, mostly bicarbonate. Timing, sample handling, and rapid changes can cause differences.

Q9. What does base excess mean?

Base excess estimates the metabolic component of acid-base disturbance. Negative base excess suggests metabolic acidosis; positive suggests metabolic alkalosis.

Q10. What is the first step in ABG interpretation?

Look at pH. Decide acidemia, alkalemia, or normal pH with possible mixed disorder.

Pimp Questions: Compensation

Q11. Can compensation fully normalize pH?

Compensation can bring pH closer to normal but should not overshoot. If it overshoots, suspect a mixed disorder.

Q12. How fast does respiratory compensation occur?

Minutes. The lungs can change PaCO₂ quickly by changing alveolar ventilation.

Q13. How fast does renal compensation occur?

Hours to days. Kidney bicarbonate retention or excretion is slower than respiratory compensation.

Q14. What is Winter’s formula?

Expected PaCO₂ = 1.5 × HCO₃ + 8 ± 2. Use it in metabolic acidosis.

Q15. In metabolic acidosis, what if actual PaCO₂ is higher than Winter’s expected PaCO₂?

There is superimposed respiratory acidosis or inadequate respiratory compensation. In ICU this may mean fatigue, COPD, CNS depression, or impending respiratory failure.

Q16. In metabolic acidosis, what if actual PaCO₂ is lower than expected?

There is superimposed respiratory alkalosis, such as sepsis, pain, anxiety, pregnancy, liver disease, PE, or salicylates.

Q17. PaCO₂ is 70 and HCO₃ is 27. Acute or chronic respiratory acidosis?

Acute. PaCO₂ is 30 above 40, so acute HCO₃ rise should be about 3, from 24 to 27.

Q18. PaCO₂ is 70 and HCO₃ is 36. Acute or chronic?

Chronic. PaCO₂ is 30 above 40, so chronic HCO₃ rise should be about 12, from 24 to 36.

Q19. Why is acute respiratory acidosis more acidemic than chronic respiratory acidosis?

The kidneys have not had time to retain bicarbonate. Chronic CO₂ retention allows renal compensation.

Q20. Why is a “normal” PaCO₂ dangerous in severe metabolic acidosis?

Because PaCO₂ should be low as compensation. A normal PaCO₂ in severe metabolic acidosis may actually represent ventilatory failure.

Pimp Questions: Anion Gap and Delta Gap

Q21. Why calculate anion gap?

To determine whether metabolic acidosis is due to unmeasured acids. HAGMA changes the differential and can reveal toxins, lactate, ketoacids, or renal failure.

Q22. Why correct anion gap for albumin?

Albumin is the main unmeasured anion. Low albumin lowers the measured gap and can hide high anion gap acidosis.

Q23. What is the albumin correction?

Corrected AG = AG + 2.5 × (4 − albumin).

Q24. What is GOLD MARK?

Glycols, Oxoproline, L-lactate, D-lactate, Methanol, Aspirin, Renal failure, Ketoacidosis.

Q25. What is HARDUP?

Hyperalimentation, Acetazolamide/Addison, RTA, Diarrhea, Ureteral diversion, Pancreatic fistula.

Q26. What does delta ratio tell you?

Whether the fall in bicarbonate matches the rise in anion gap. It helps detect HAGMA plus NAGMA or HAGMA plus metabolic alkalosis.

Q27. Delta ratio less than 0.4?

Mostly NAGMA or HAGMA plus significant NAGMA.

Q28. Delta ratio 0.8 to 2?

Typical pure HAGMA pattern.

Q29. Delta ratio greater than 2?

HAGMA plus metabolic alkalosis, or high baseline bicarbonate from chronic respiratory acidosis.

Q30. Why can DKA become hyperchloremic NAGMA during treatment?

Ketoacids are cleared and bicarbonate regenerates slowly; large-volume normal saline adds chloride and lowers strong ion difference, causing hyperchloremic acidosis.

Pimp Questions: Oxygenation

Q31. What is the P/F ratio?

PaO₂ divided by FiO₂. Example: PaO₂ 80 on FiO₂ 0.40 gives P/F 200.

Q32. What P/F ratio suggests severe ARDS?

P/F ≤100, if ARDS criteria are met and PEEP/CPAP requirement is satisfied by the definition used.

Q33. What is the A-a gradient?

The difference between alveolar oxygen and arterial oxygen. It helps separate hypoventilation from gas exchange problems.

Q34. Normal A-a gradient with hypoxemia means what?

Hypoventilation or low inspired oxygen/high altitude. Gas exchange across the lung is relatively preserved.

Q35. High A-a gradient means what?

V/Q mismatch, shunt, or diffusion limitation.

Q36. Why does PEEP improve oxygenation?

PEEP recruits alveoli, prevents derecruitment, reduces shunt, and improves functional residual capacity.

Q37. Why does FiO₂ not fix shunt as well as V/Q mismatch?

In true shunt, blood passes through nonventilated lung units and never sees alveolar oxygen, so extra FiO₂ has limited effect.

Q38. Why can pulse ox be falsely normal in carbon monoxide poisoning?

Standard pulse oximetry cannot reliably distinguish oxyhemoglobin from carboxyhemoglobin. Use co-oximetry.

Pimp Questions: Ventilator / NIV

Q39. If PaCO₂ is high on the ventilator, what do you change?

Increase minute ventilation: RR × tidal volume. Usually increase RR first if safe, while monitoring auto-PEEP and pressures.

Q40. If PaO₂ is low on the ventilator, what do you change?

Increase FiO₂ and/or PEEP. FiO₂ works quickly; PEEP recruits alveoli and reduces shunt.

Q41. In ARDS, why not simply increase tidal volume to clear CO₂?

High tidal volumes can cause ventilator-induced lung injury. Use lung-protective ventilation and accept permissive hypercapnia if pH tolerates it.

Q42. What is permissive hypercapnia?

Accepting elevated PaCO₂ to avoid injurious ventilation, especially in ARDS or obstructive lung disease, as long as pH and hemodynamics are acceptable.

Q43. What does IPAP do on BiPAP?

IPAP increases inspiratory pressure support, tidal volume, and CO₂ clearance.

Q44. What does EPAP do on BiPAP?

EPAP is similar to PEEP. It improves oxygenation and helps keep airways/alveoli open.

Q45. When is BiPAP best?

COPD exacerbation with hypercapnic acidemia, obesity hypoventilation, cardiogenic pulmonary edema, and selected neuromuscular/chest wall disorders if airway protection is intact.

Q46. When is HFNC best?

Hypoxemic respiratory failure where oxygenation and high flow are needed, such as pneumonia or early ARDS, without primary severe ventilatory failure.

Q47. When should you intubate instead of trying BiPAP/HFNC?

Airway protection failure, severe shock, cardiac arrest, worsening mental status, inability to tolerate interface, severe aspiration risk, exhaustion, or worsening gas despite noninvasive support.

Q48. Why can intubating a severe metabolic acidosis patient be dangerous?

They may be maintaining pH by extreme hyperventilation. Sedation/apnea or inadequate ventilator minute ventilation can cause PaCO₂ to rise and pH to crash.

Q49. What is the ABG target after starting BiPAP for COPD?

Improving pH and decreasing PaCO₂, but not necessarily normal PaCO₂. Clinical work of breathing and mental status matter just as much.

Q50. Why should you avoid excessive oxygen in COPD?

Excess oxygen can worsen hypercapnia through V/Q changes, Haldane effect, and reduced hypoxic pulmonary vasoconstriction. Target controlled oxygen saturation per local protocol.

Common Pitfalls

  • Calling a normal pH “normal ABG” without checking PaCO₂ and HCO₃.
  • Using VBG oxygen values to make oxygenation decisions.
  • Forgetting Winter’s formula in metabolic acidosis.
  • Not correcting anion gap for albumin.
  • Missing salicylate toxicity because pH is normal.
  • Giving bicarbonate for isolated respiratory acidosis.
  • Increasing RR in severe asthma and worsening auto-PEEP.
  • Forgetting that chemistry CO₂ is mostly bicarbonate, not PaCO₂.
  • Assuming all high lactate is sepsis; seizures, beta-agonists, liver failure, ischemia, and shock states matter.
  • Leaving FiO₂ at 100% when PaO₂ is very high.
  • Waiting too long to intubate a failing BiPAP patient.
  • Intubating severe metabolic acidosis without matching pre-intubation minute ventilation.
  • Ignoring the patient because the ABG “looks better.”

The ICU skill is not just naming the disorder. The skill is identifying whether compensation is appropriate, whether oxygenation or ventilation is failing, and what bedside intervention follows from the physiology.

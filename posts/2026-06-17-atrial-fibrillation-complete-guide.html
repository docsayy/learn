Definition & ECG Findings

Atrial fibrillation (AF) is the most common sustained cardiac arrhythmia. It results from chaotic atrial electrical activity leading to loss of coordinated atrial contraction.

Instead of generating a single organized impulse from the sinoatrial node, multiple abnormal electrical foci fire simultaneously, causing the atria to fibrillate.

Classic ECG Findings

  • No distinct P waves
  • Fibrillatory baseline
  • Irregularly irregular rhythm
  • Variable R-R intervals
Board Pearl: An "irregularly irregular rhythm" is one of the most important physical exam and ECG descriptions for atrial fibrillation.

Always look for reversible triggers such as hypokalemia, hypomagnesemia, sepsis, hyperthyroidism, alcohol use, and pulmonary disease.

Why Atrial Fibrillation Matters

1. Stroke and Systemic Embolism

The atria lose their organized contraction and become quivering chambers. Blood flow becomes stagnant, particularly within the left atrial appendage.

This stasis promotes thrombus formation which may embolize into the systemic circulation.

Most Common Location of Thrombus Formation

Left Atrial Appendage (LAA)

Embolization may result in:

  • Ischemic stroke
  • Transient ischemic attack (TIA)
  • Mesenteric ischemia
  • Limb ischemia
  • Other systemic embolic events

2. Hemodynamic Compromise

Rapid ventricular rates reduce diastolic filling time and may significantly decrease cardiac output.

AF with rapid ventricular response (RVR) can lead to:

  • Hypotension
  • Heart failure exacerbation
  • Myocardial ischemia
  • Cardiogenic shock

Pathophysiology

Where Does AF Originate?

Contrary to a common misconception, atrial fibrillation is primarily a left atrial process.

The most common source of abnormal electrical activity arises from myocardial sleeves surrounding the pulmonary veins.

AF originates from ectopic electrical activity near the pulmonary veins of the left atrium.

AF versus Atrial Flutter

Atrial Fibrillation Atrial Flutter
Left atrial process Right atrial process
Pulmonary veins Cavotricuspid isthmus
Chaotic electrical activity Organized re-entry circuit
Irregular rhythm Sawtooth flutter waves

Ablation Therapy

Pulmonary vein isolation is a common ablation strategy for AF. The goal is to eliminate abnormal electrical activity originating around the pulmonary veins.

Maze Procedure

The Maze procedure is a surgical treatment in which multiple scar lines are created within the atria to interrupt re-entry pathways.

One rare complication of AF ablation is atrio-esophageal fistula due to the close anatomical relationship between the left atrium and esophagus.

AF Classification

Type Definition
Paroxysmal Terminates spontaneously within 7 days
Persistent Lasts more than 7 days
Long-Standing Persistent Persists longer than 1 year
Permanent Rhythm control abandoned
Always document the AF subtype in your notes because it influences management and long-term planning.

Valvular vs Non-Valvular AF

Valvular AF

Current definitions include only:

  • Mechanical heart valves
  • Rheumatic mitral stenosis

Anticoagulation Choices

Condition Preferred Anticoagulant
Mechanical Valve Warfarin
Rheumatic Mitral Stenosis Warfarin
Non-Valvular AF DOAC Preferred
This distinction is highly tested and commonly appears on rounds.

Rate Control

Target Heart Rate

Goal Heart Rate < 110 bpm

For symptomatic patients, a stricter goal below 80 bpm may be appropriate.

Management of AF with RVR

Metoprolol

  • 2.5–5 mg IV every 5 minutes
  • Usually first-line therapy
  • Particularly useful in hyperthyroidism

Diltiazem

  • More potent AV nodal blockade
  • Frequently used in emergency departments
  • Avoid in HFrEF due to negative inotropic effects

Digoxin

  • Useful when hypotension limits beta blocker use
  • Positive inotropic effects
  • Slow onset
  • Requires level monitoring

Esmolol

  • Very short half-life
  • Rapid titration
  • Ideal ICU medication
Always correct potassium and magnesium deficiencies when treating AF with RVR.

Rhythm Control

Rhythm control is used to restore and maintain sinus rhythm, particularly in symptomatic patients or those with early AF.

Common Antiarrhythmic Agents

Drug Class Key Use Major Risk
Amiodarone Class III Most effective, ICU use Multi-organ toxicity
Dronedarone Class III Safer alternative Hepatotoxicity
Flecainide Class IC Structurally normal hearts Proarrhythmia
Propafenone Class IC Pill-in-pocket Proarrhythmia
Dofetilide Class III Hospital initiation QT prolongation
Sotalol Class III AF suppression Torsades de pointes
Important: All antiarrhythmic drugs are potentially pro-arrhythmic.

Amiodarone

Amiodarone is the most effective antiarrhythmic for atrial fibrillation, but it has significant long-term toxicity.

Toxicities

  • Pulmonary fibrosis
  • Thyroid dysfunction (hypo or hyper)
  • Hepatotoxicity
  • Corneal deposits
  • Skin discoloration
  • Bradycardia and QT prolongation

Monitoring

  • Baseline PFTs
  • Thyroid function tests
  • Liver function tests
  • Chest X-ray annually
Amiodarone has an extremely long half-life (~1 month), so toxicity may persist for weeks after discontinuation.

Dronedarone

Dronedarone is structurally related to amiodarone but has fewer organ toxicities. However, it is less effective.

Rare but serious risk: fulminant hepatic failure.

Class IC Agents

Flecainide & Propafenone

  • Only for patients without structural heart disease
  • Avoid in CAD, prior MI, LV dysfunction
  • Used in “pill-in-the-pocket” strategy
These agents significantly increase mortality in patients with structural heart disease.

Dofetilide & Sotalol

  • Require QT interval monitoring
  • Initiation typically inpatient
  • Risk of torsades de pointes

Cardioversion Strategy

Unstable Patient

Immediate synchronized electrical cardioversion is required.

Stable Patient < 48 Hours

Cardioversion can be performed without prior anticoagulation in many cases.

Stable Patient > 48 Hours

  • Option 1: TEE-guided cardioversion
  • Option 2: 3 weeks anticoagulation → cardioversion → 4 weeks anticoagulation
The 48-hour rule is based on risk of atrial thrombus formation.

Delayed Cardioversion Strategy

Many patients spontaneously convert or can be safely managed with delayed cardioversion after anticoagulation.

Pre-Excited Atrial Fibrillation (WPW)

Pre-excited AF occurs in patients with accessory pathways (Wolff-Parkinson-White syndrome).

ECG Features

  • Irregularly irregular rhythm
  • Wide QRS complexes
  • Very rapid ventricular response

Danger

AV nodal blockers can force conduction down the accessory pathway, leading to ventricular fibrillation.

Management

  • Procainamide
  • Electrical cardioversion if unstable
Never use beta blockers, calcium channel blockers, digoxin, or amiodarone in pre-excited AF.

Landmark Clinical Trials

AFFIRM Trial

  • Rate control vs rhythm control
  • No mortality difference
  • Rate control preferred due to fewer side effects

RACE II Trial

  • Lenient rate control (<110 bpm) vs strict control
  • No difference in outcomes
  • Lenient control acceptable in stable patients

EAST-AFNET 4 Trial

  • Early rhythm control improved cardiovascular outcomes
  • Shifted modern practice toward earlier rhythm strategies

RACE 7 ACWAS Trial

  • Delayed cardioversion non-inferior to immediate cardioversion
  • Many patients convert spontaneously

Board & ICU Pearls

  • AF = irregularly irregular rhythm without P waves
  • Most thrombi form in the left atrial appendage
  • AF originates from pulmonary veins
  • Goal HR: <110 bpm (lenient control)
  • Diltiazem avoided in HFrEF
  • Beta blockers useful in hyperthyroidism
  • Pre-excited AF = avoid AV nodal blockers
  • Unstable AF → immediate cardioversion
  • All antiarrhythmics are proarrhythmic
  • Amiodarone toxicity is multi-system and long-lasting

Infective Endocarditis: Diagnosis, Duke Criteria, and Treatment Pearls

June 20, 2026 Infectious Disease

Infective endocarditis remains one of the most challenging infectious diseases encountered in clinical medicine. Successful diagnosis requires recognition of bacteremia patterns, Duke criteria, echocardiographic findings, embolic complications, and appropriate antibiotic selection. This article provides a practical bedside framework for approaching endocarditis from admission through treatment completion. :contentReference[oaicite:0]{index=0}

Classification

Infective vs Non-Infective Endocarditis

Type Examples
Infective Endocarditis Bacterial or fungal infection of cardiac valves
Non-Infective Endocarditis Marantic endocarditis, Libman-Sacks endocarditis

Non-infective endocarditis results from sterile fibrin and platelet thrombi and is associated with:

  • Malignancy (especially pancreatic and colon adenocarcinoma)
  • Hypercoagulable states
  • Systemic lupus erythematosus

Non-bacterial thrombotic endocarditis (NBTE) and marantic endocarditis are alternative names commonly used for these conditions. :contentReference[oaicite:1]{index=1}

Culture Positive vs Culture Negative

Another useful classification divides infective endocarditis into:

  • Culture-positive endocarditis
  • Culture-negative endocarditis

Approximately 25–30% of endocarditis cases may initially appear culture-negative. Modern blood culture techniques have improved detection of many previously difficult-to-grow organisms. :contentReference[oaicite:2]{index=2}

Common Organisms

Acute Endocarditis

  • Staphylococcus aureus
  • Viridans streptococci
  • Enterococcus species

Culture-Negative or Subacute Endocarditis

  • Coxiella burnetii (Q fever)
  • Bartonella species
  • Brucella species
  • HACEK organisms
  • Tropheryma whipplei
Staphylococcus aureus remains the most important cause of acute infective endocarditis and bacteremia-associated endocarditis. :contentReference[oaicite:3]{index=3}

Board Pearl: Colonoscopy Indications

  • Streptococcus gallolyticus (formerly S. bovis) bacteremia or endocarditis → Colonoscopy
  • Unexplained Enterococcus faecalis bacteremia in screening-age patients → Consider colonoscopy

Both organisms have important associations with underlying colorectal neoplasia. :contentReference[oaicite:4]{index=4}

Clinical Presentation

Most Common Symptom

Fever is present in approximately 90–93% of patients and remains the most common presenting symptom. :contentReference[oaicite:5]{index=5}

Less Common Findings

Finding Approximate Frequency
Splinter hemorrhages ~27%
Osler nodes ~12%
Janeway lesions ~5%

Classic physical examination findings are much less common than most trainees expect. Their absence does not exclude endocarditis. :contentReference[oaicite:6]{index=6}

Embolic Presentation

Approximately one-quarter of patients initially present because of embolic complications:

  • Stroke
  • Septic emboli
  • Pulmonary embolic phenomena
  • Peripheral embolization

Blood Cultures

When endocarditis is suspected:

Obtain three sets of blood cultures before antibiotics whenever possible.

Each set consists of:

  • 1 aerobic bottle
  • 1 anaerobic bottle

Total: 6 bottles.

Number of Sets Bacteremia Detection Rate
1 set 73%
2 sets 90%
3 sets 98%

This is why endocarditis requires three blood culture sets rather than the two sets often obtained for routine sepsis evaluations. :contentReference[oaicite:7]{index=7}

Updated Duke Criteria (2023)

Major Criteria

  • Typical positive blood cultures
  • Positive echocardiographic findings
  • Positive cardiac imaging
  • PET-CT evidence
  • Surgical evidence
  • Specific laboratory evidence for Coxiella, Bartonella, and Tropheryma

Minor Criteria

  • Predisposing cardiac condition
  • Injection drug use
  • Fever
  • Vascular phenomena
  • Immunologic phenomena
  • Microbiologic evidence not meeting major criteria
  • New regurgitant murmur when imaging unavailable
Diagnosis Requirements
Definite IE 2 major OR 1 major + 3 minor OR 5 minor
Possible IE 1 major + 1 minor OR 3 minor

The 2023 update expanded both imaging and microbiologic pathways to diagnosis. :contentReference[oaicite:8]{index=8}

Immunologic vs Vascular Phenomena

Immunologic Phenomena

  • Osler nodes
  • Roth spots
  • Glomerulonephritis
  • Positive rheumatoid factor

Vascular Phenomena

  • Janeway lesions
  • Splinter hemorrhages
  • Stroke
  • Mycotic aneurysm
  • Septic emboli
  • Pulmonary infarction
Osler nodes are painful. Janeway lesions are classically painless.

Immunologic phenomena may occur with either right-sided or left-sided disease. Most vascular phenomena occur with left-sided disease, although septic pulmonary emboli occur in right-sided endocarditis. :contentReference[oaicite:9]{index=9}

Echocardiography

Test Sensitivity
TTE 40–75%
TEE 90–100%

If Initial Echo Is Negative

Repeat imaging is recommended when suspicion remains high:

  • AHA: Repeat echo in 3–5 days
  • ESC: Repeat echo in 5–7 days

Vegetations may become easier to detect several days into bacteremia. :contentReference[oaicite:10]{index=10}

Treatment Principles

Condition Typical Duration
Uncomplicated MSSA bacteremia 2 weeks
Right-sided endocarditis 2 weeks
Left-sided endocarditis 4–6 weeks
Day 1 of therapy is the first day blood cultures become negative—not the first day antibiotics were started.

Persistent positive blood cultures delay the treatment clock. :contentReference[oaicite:11]{index=11}

Choosing Antibiotics

MSSA Endocarditis

Agent Comments
Nafcillin Preferred initial therapy
Cefazolin Often used after initial stabilization
Vancomycin Not preferred for MSSA

Vancomycin has slower bactericidal activity against MSSA compared with anti-staphylococcal beta-lactams. :contentReference[oaicite:12]{index=12}

Why Start With Nafcillin?

The major reason is the cefazolin inoculum effect:

  • Large bacterial burden may reduce cefazolin effectiveness
  • Nafcillin is less affected
  • Nafcillin also has superior CNS penetration

MRSA Endocarditis

  • Vancomycin = first-line
  • Daptomycin = alternative for intolerance or treatment failure
  • Dalbavancin = potential long-acting option in selected cases

Daptomycin is often combined with ceftaroline initially when treating refractory MRSA bacteremia or endocarditis. :contentReference[oaicite:13]{index=13}

The POET Trial

The POET trial evaluated stable patients with left-sided endocarditis and demonstrated that carefully selected patients could transition from IV therapy to oral therapy without worse outcomes.

Potential Benefits

  • Shorter hospitalization
  • Avoid PICC lines
  • Reduced healthcare costs
  • Improved patient satisfaction

Limitations

  • Very few injection drug use patients
  • No MRSA patients
  • Highly selected population
  • Intensive follow-up

While not yet standard for all patients, POET opened the door to earlier oral transition in selected cases. :contentReference[oaicite:14]{index=14}

Surgical Indications

Left-Sided Endocarditis

  • Heart failure from valve dysfunction
  • Paravalvular abscess
  • Fistula formation
  • Vegetation >10 mm
  • Persistent infection
  • Difficult-to-treat pathogen

Right-Sided Endocarditis

  • Vegetation >20 mm
  • Recurrent septic pulmonary emboli
  • Persistent bacteremia
  • Heart failure
  • Paravalvular abscess
New conduction abnormalities such as first-degree AV block may indicate a perivalvular abscess and require urgent TEE evaluation.

Because the conduction system runs adjacent to the aortic and mitral valves, abscess extension can rapidly progress to complete heart block. :contentReference[oaicite:15]{index=15}

Clinical Pearls

  • Fever is the most common symptom of endocarditis.
  • Always obtain three sets of blood cultures.
  • TEE is far more sensitive than TTE.
  • Repeat echocardiography if suspicion remains high despite an initially negative study.
  • Right-sided endocarditis often requires only 2 weeks of therapy.
  • Left-sided disease typically requires 4–6 weeks of treatment.
  • Count treatment duration from the first negative blood culture.
  • Vancomycin is inferior to beta-lactams for MSSA.
  • No proven role exists for aspirin or anticoagulation to prevent embolic complications.
  • New AV block should raise concern for perivalvular abscess.
  • Persistent fever for 5–7 days after starting therapy is common.
  • Residual vegetations may remain visible on follow-up echocardiography despite successful treatment.

Bottom Line

Endocarditis requires a systematic approach: obtain three blood culture sets, apply the updated Duke criteria, use TEE when suspicion remains high, and tailor therapy based on organism and valve involvement. Right-sided disease often requires 2 weeks of treatment, whereas left-sided disease usually requires 4–6 weeks. Recognizing surgical indications, conduction abnormalities, and embolic complications is essential for improving outcomes. :contentReference[oaicite:16]{index=16}

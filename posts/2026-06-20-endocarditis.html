Infective Endocarditis: Diagnosis, Duke Criteria, and Treatment Pearls

Added June 20, 2026 • Infectious Disease • ~14 min read

Infective endocarditis remains one of the most challenging infectious diseases in clinical medicine. Diagnosis requires recognition of bacteremia patterns, Duke criteria, echocardiographic findings, embolic complications, and appropriate antibiotic selection. This article provides a practical bedside framework for approaching endocarditis from admission through treatment completion.

Classification

Infective vs Non-Infective Endocarditis

Type Examples
Infective Endocarditis Bacterial or fungal infection of cardiac valves
Non-Infective Endocarditis Marantic endocarditis, Libman-Sacks endocarditis

Non-infective endocarditis results from sterile fibrin and platelet thrombi and is associated with malignancy, hypercoagulable states, and systemic lupus erythematosus.

Culture Positive vs Culture Negative

A useful clinical classification divides infective endocarditis into culture-positive and culture-negative disease. Culture-negative endocarditis may occur because antibiotics were given before cultures, the organism is difficult to grow, or specialized testing is required.

Common Organisms

Acute Endocarditis

  • Staphylococcus aureus
  • Viridans streptococci
  • Enterococcus species

Culture-Negative or Subacute Endocarditis

  • Coxiella burnetii
  • Bartonella species
  • Brucella species
  • HACEK organisms
  • Tropheryma whipplei
Staphylococcus aureus is one of the most important causes of acute infective endocarditis, especially when persistent bacteremia is present.

Board Pearl: Colonoscopy Indications

  • Streptococcus gallolyticus bacteremia or endocarditis → colonoscopy
  • Unexplained Enterococcus faecalis bacteremia in screening-age patients → consider colonoscopy

Clinical Presentation

Fever is the most common presenting symptom of infective endocarditis.

Classic Findings

Finding Clinical Meaning
Splinter hemorrhages Vascular phenomenon
Osler nodes Painful immunologic phenomenon
Janeway lesions Painless vascular phenomenon
Roth spots Immunologic phenomenon

Classic physical examination findings are helpful when present, but their absence does not exclude endocarditis. Many patients present with fever, bacteremia, embolic events, new murmur, stroke, heart failure, or persistent positive blood cultures.

Blood Cultures

When endocarditis is suspected, obtain three sets of blood cultures before antibiotics whenever possible.

Each blood culture set usually includes one aerobic bottle and one anaerobic bottle. Three sets improve diagnostic yield and help distinguish true bacteremia from contamination.

Number of Sets Approximate Detection Rate
1 set Lower sensitivity
2 sets Improved sensitivity
3 sets Highest diagnostic yield

Updated Duke Criteria

Major Criteria

  • Typical positive blood cultures
  • Positive echocardiographic findings
  • Positive cardiac imaging
  • PET-CT evidence in selected cases
  • Surgical or pathologic evidence
  • Specific laboratory evidence for organisms such as Coxiella, Bartonella, or Tropheryma

Minor Criteria

  • Predisposing cardiac condition
  • Injection drug use
  • Fever
  • Vascular phenomena
  • Immunologic phenomena
  • Microbiologic evidence not meeting major criteria
  • New regurgitant murmur when imaging is unavailable
Diagnosis Criteria
Definite IE 2 major OR 1 major + 3 minor OR 5 minor
Possible IE 1 major + 1 minor OR 3 minor

Immunologic vs Vascular Phenomena

Immunologic Phenomena

  • Osler nodes
  • Roth spots
  • Glomerulonephritis
  • Positive rheumatoid factor

Vascular Phenomena

  • Janeway lesions
  • Splinter hemorrhages
  • Stroke
  • Mycotic aneurysm
  • Septic emboli
  • Pulmonary infarction
Osler nodes are painful. Janeway lesions are classically painless.

Echocardiography

Test Use
TTE Initial test in many patients; less sensitive
TEE More sensitive; preferred when suspicion remains high or prosthetic valve/device is present

If Initial Echo Is Negative

If the initial echocardiogram is negative but suspicion remains high, repeat imaging should be performed. Vegetations may become more apparent after several days of ongoing bacteremia.

Treatment Principles

Condition Typical Duration
Uncomplicated MSSA bacteremia At least 2 weeks
Right-sided endocarditis Often 2 weeks in selected uncomplicated cases
Left-sided endocarditis Usually 4–6 weeks
Day 1 of therapy is the first day blood cultures become negative, not the first day antibiotics were started.

Persistent positive blood cultures reset the treatment clock and should prompt reassessment for source control, abscess, resistant organism, inadequate antibiotic therapy, or need for surgery.

Choosing Antibiotics

MSSA Endocarditis

Agent Comment
Nafcillin / Oxacillin Preferred anti-staphylococcal beta-lactam options
Cefazolin Often used when appropriate; easier dosing and generally well tolerated
Vancomycin Not preferred for MSSA when beta-lactams can be used

MRSA Endocarditis

  • Vancomycin is commonly used first-line.
  • Daptomycin is an alternative for intolerance, failure, or selected cases.
  • Daptomycin plus ceftaroline may be considered in refractory MRSA bacteremia or endocarditis.
For MSSA bacteremia or endocarditis, use an anti-staphylococcal beta-lactam when possible rather than vancomycin.

The POET Trial

The POET trial showed that carefully selected, clinically stable patients with left-sided endocarditis could transition from IV therapy to oral therapy without worse outcomes.

Potential Benefits

  • Shorter hospitalization
  • Avoidance of PICC-related complications
  • Lower healthcare burden
  • Improved patient convenience

Important Limitations

  • Highly selected patients
  • Limited injection drug use population
  • No MRSA patients
  • Requires close follow-up and reliable adherence

Surgical Indications

Left-Sided Endocarditis

  • Heart failure from valve dysfunction
  • Paravalvular abscess
  • Fistula formation
  • Large vegetation with embolic risk
  • Persistent infection despite appropriate therapy
  • Difficult-to-treat pathogen

Right-Sided Endocarditis

  • Large vegetation
  • Recurrent septic pulmonary emboli
  • Persistent bacteremia
  • Heart failure
  • Paravalvular abscess
New conduction abnormalities, especially new AV block, should raise concern for perivalvular abscess and urgent TEE evaluation.

Clinical Pearls

  • Fever is the most common symptom of endocarditis.
  • Always obtain three sets of blood cultures when possible before antibiotics.
  • TEE is more sensitive than TTE.
  • Repeat echocardiography if suspicion remains high despite an initially negative study.
  • Right-sided endocarditis may require shorter therapy in selected uncomplicated cases.
  • Left-sided disease usually requires 4–6 weeks of therapy.
  • Count treatment duration from the first negative blood culture.
  • Vancomycin is not preferred for MSSA when beta-lactams can be used.
  • No routine role exists for aspirin or anticoagulation solely to prevent embolic complications.
  • New AV block should raise concern for perivalvular abscess.
  • Persistent fever early in treatment can occur, but persistent bacteremia requires reassessment.
  • Residual vegetations may remain visible after successful treatment.

Bottom Line

Endocarditis requires a systematic approach: obtain three blood culture sets, apply Duke criteria, use TEE when clinical suspicion remains high, and tailor antibiotics based on organism, valve involvement, and culture results. Right-sided disease may be shorter-course in selected cases, while left-sided disease usually requires 4–6 weeks. Always watch for surgical indications, embolic complications, persistent bacteremia, and new conduction abnormalities.

Heart Failure with Preserved Ejection Fraction (HFpEF): The Complete Resident Guide

Added June 24, 2026 • Cardiology • ~18 min read

Heart failure with preserved ejection fraction is a clinical syndrome of heart failure symptoms with preserved left ventricular ejection fraction and objective evidence of elevated filling pressures. HFpEF is not simply “diastolic dysfunction”; it is a heterogeneous systemic syndrome involving inflammation, obesity, hypertension, atrial fibrillation, pulmonary hypertension, kidney disease, skeletal muscle dysfunction, and abnormal ventricular filling.

What Is HFpEF?

Heart failure with preserved ejection fraction, or HFpEF, is a clinical syndrome of heart failure with left ventricular ejection fraction at least 50% and objective evidence of elevated left ventricular filling pressures at rest or with exertion.

  • Formerly called diastolic heart failure or diastolic dysfunction
  • Now understood as a heterogeneous systemic syndrome
  • Often involves cardiac and extracardiac abnormalities
  • Requires symptoms plus objective evidence of elevated filling pressures
Think of HFpEF as a systemic inflammatory and cardiometabolic syndrome where the heart is a major target, not simply a disease of “normal EF.”

The Heart Failure Spectrum by Ejection Fraction

Category LVEF Key Concept
HFrEF ≤40% Reduced contractility; multiple therapies improve survival
HFmrEF 41–49% Intermediate phenotype with overlapping features
HFpEF ≥50% Preserved contraction with impaired filling, stiffness, or abnormal reserve
HFimpEF >40% after prior ≤40% Previously reduced EF that improved with therapy
LVEF is a continuous variable. The cutoffs help organize treatment, but patients often exist on a spectrum.

Epidemiology and Risk Factors

HFpEF accounts for approximately half of heart failure cases. Its prevalence is rising because of aging, obesity, hypertension, diabetes, chronic kidney disease, and atrial fibrillation.

Risk Factor Clinical Importance
Age >60 One of the strongest risk factors
Hypertension Most common comorbidity
Obesity Major inflammatory and metabolic driver
Diabetes mellitus Microvascular dysfunction and myocardial stiffness
Atrial fibrillation Common cause and consequence of elevated filling pressures
Female sex Women are overrepresented in many HFpEF cohorts
Chronic kidney disease Sodium handling impairment and volume sensitivity
Sleep apnea Common contributor to pulmonary hypertension and symptoms

Pathophysiology: Beyond “Diastolic Dysfunction”

The older model described HFpEF as isolated left ventricular diastolic dysfunction. The modern model is broader: systemic inflammation and cardiometabolic stress lead to coronary microvascular endothelial dysfunction, myocardial remodeling, fibrosis, impaired relaxation, increased ventricular stiffness, and abnormal exercise reserve.

Multi-Organ Involvement

System Abnormality
Left ventricle Impaired relaxation, stiffness, fibrosis, reduced systolic reserve
Left atrium Enlargement, dysfunction, elevated pressure
Pulmonary circulation Postcapillary pulmonary hypertension and vascular remodeling
Right ventricle Dysfunction is common and worsens prognosis
Pericardium Increased constraint
Arterial system Increased stiffness and impaired microvascular function
Skeletal muscle Reduced mass, reduced function, impaired oxygen extraction
Kidneys Sodium retention and volume sensitivity
Adipose tissue Inflammation and lipotoxicity
HFpEF is often a whole-body syndrome, not just a stiff ventricle.

Clinical Presentation

Two Major Phenotypes

Phenotype Features
Overt congestion Peripheral edema, JVD, pulmonary congestion, elevated filling pressures at rest
Exercise intolerance only Dyspnea on exertion with normal resting pressures but elevated pressures during exercise

Common Symptoms

  • Exertional dyspnea
  • Fatigue
  • Reduced exercise tolerance
  • Orthopnea
  • Paroxysmal nocturnal dyspnea
  • Palpitations from atrial fibrillation
  • Peripheral edema
Older, frail, obese, or female patients may present with dominant fatigue rather than classic dyspnea.

Diagnosis: H₂FPEF and HFA-PEFF Scores

No single test definitively diagnoses HFpEF in every patient. Scoring systems help estimate the probability of HFpEF and determine who needs advanced testing.

H₂FPEF Score

Component Criterion Points
Heavy BMI >30 kg/m² 2
Hypertensive ≥2 antihypertensive medications 1
Atrial fibrillation Paroxysmal or persistent AF 3
Pulmonary hypertension PASP >35 mmHg 1
Elder Age >60 1
Filling pressure E/e′ >9 1

H₂FPEF Interpretation

Score Interpretation
0–1 HFpEF unlikely
2–5 Intermediate probability
≥6 HFpEF likely

HFA-PEFF Score

Step Component
P Pretest assessment: symptoms, risk factors, standard echo
E Echo and natriuretic peptide score
F1 Functional testing if uncertain: exercise echo or invasive hemodynamics
F2 Final etiology: CMR, biopsy, or genetic testing when needed
A high H₂FPEF or HFA-PEFF score supports HFpEF. Intermediate scores should prompt exercise echo or invasive hemodynamic testing when symptoms remain unexplained.

Diagnostic Testing

First-Line Tests

Test Role
BNP / NT-proBNP Supports heart failure diagnosis, but may be lower in obesity
Transthoracic echocardiography Assesses EF, diastolic function, LA volume, pulmonary pressures, valves
ECG Looks for AF, LVH, ischemia, conduction disease
CXR May show congestion, pleural effusions, cardiomegaly, or alternative lung disease
Basic labs Renal function, electrolytes, CBC, TSH, liver tests, iron studies when indicated

Echo Markers of Elevated Filling Pressure

Parameter Suggestive Threshold
Average E/e′ ratio >14
Peak TR velocity >2.8 m/s
Left atrial volume index >34 mL/m²

When Diagnosis Is Uncertain

Test When to Use
Exercise echocardiography Symptoms only with exertion and normal resting filling pressures
Invasive exercise hemodynamics Gold standard when noninvasive testing is inconclusive
Cardiac MRI Concern for amyloidosis, myocarditis, infiltrative disease, or tissue characterization
PYP scan / amyloid workup Concern for transthyretin cardiac amyloidosis
Normal BNP does not exclude HFpEF, especially in obesity.

HFpEF Mimics and Alternative Diagnoses

HFpEF should not be diagnosed until alternative explanations for dyspnea and congestion are considered.

Condition Clinical Clues
Obesity-related dyspnea Deconditioning, sleep apnea, no elevated filling pressures
Pulmonary disease COPD, ILD, abnormal PFTs or CT chest
Anemia Low hemoglobin, fatigue, exertional dyspnea
Deconditioning Low exercise capacity without cardiac limitation
Valvular disease Aortic stenosis, mitral regurgitation, mitral stenosis
Pericardial constriction Septal bounce, ventricular interdependence, discordant RV/LV pressures
Infiltrative cardiomyopathy Low voltage ECG, LV thickening, apical sparing, neuropathy, monoclonal protein
Pulmonary hypertension Dyspnea, RV strain, elevated pulmonary pressures

Guideline-Directed Medical Therapy

Strongest Evidence

Therapy Role Typical Dose
Loop diuretics Symptom relief for congestion Titrate to euvolemia
SGLT2 inhibitors Reduce HF hospitalization and improve outcomes Empagliflozin 10 mg daily or dapagliflozin 10 mg daily

Reasonable in Selected Patients

Therapy Use Monitoring
ARNI May reduce HF hospitalization, especially when EF is closer to mildly reduced range BP, renal function, potassium
ARB Alternative option for BP control and possible hospitalization reduction BP, renal function, potassium
MRA May reduce HF hospitalization in selected patients Potassium and renal function

Not Used for HFpEF Outcome Benefit Alone

  • Beta blockers: use for AF rate control, angina, post-MI indications, or hypertension when needed
  • ACE inhibitors: use for hypertension, CKD, diabetes, or other indications, but not specifically proven for HFpEF outcomes
  • Digoxin: no clear HFpEF outcome benefit
The two practical pillars are congestion control with diuretics and disease-modifying therapy with SGLT2 inhibitors.

Non-Pharmacologic Management

Intervention Best For
Exercise training All stable patients
Weight loss Obesity-predominant HFpEF
Blood pressure control Hypertensive patients
Sodium restriction Patients with congestion or recurrent volume overload
Sleep apnea evaluation Obesity, snoring, daytime sleepiness, resistant hypertension
AF management Rate or rhythm control and anticoagulation when indicated
Exercise and weight loss are especially important in obesity-predominant HFpEF because symptoms often reflect both cardiac and peripheral limitations.

Acute Decompensated HFpEF

Common Triggers

  • Uncontrolled hypertension
  • Rapid atrial fibrillation
  • Infection
  • Anemia
  • Medication nonadherence
  • Dietary sodium excess
  • Renal dysfunction
  • Ischemia

Management

  • IV loop diuretics for congestion
  • Blood pressure control
  • Rate control if AF with rapid ventricular response
  • Identify and treat the precipitating factor
  • Monitor renal function, electrolytes, urine output, and daily weights
Patients with HFpEF can be very preload sensitive. Diurese congestion, but reassess frequently to avoid hypotension, AKI, or underfilling.

Special HFpEF Phenotypes

Obesity-Predominant HFpEF

  • Dominant inflammatory and metabolic features
  • Often lower BNP than expected
  • Weight loss and exercise are especially important

Atrial Fibrillation-Predominant HFpEF

  • AF is both a cause and consequence of elevated LA pressure
  • Rate control is important
  • Rhythm control may improve symptoms in selected patients
  • Anticoagulate based on stroke risk

Pulmonary Hypertension-HFpEF

  • Postcapillary pulmonary hypertension is common
  • Associated with worse prognosis
  • Pulmonary vasodilators are generally not routine therapy for PH-HFpEF

Elderly and Frail Patients

  • Multimorbidity is common
  • Goals include symptom relief, function, quality of life, and avoiding hospitalization
  • Medication tolerance may be limited by hypotension, CKD, and frailty

Prognosis

HFpEF carries substantial morbidity and mortality. Hospitalizations are common, readmissions are frequent, and outcomes may be similar to HFrEF in many cohorts.

Worse Prognostic Feature Why It Matters
Advanced age Higher frailty and comorbidity burden
Renal dysfunction Volume sensitivity and medication limitations
Atrial fibrillation Loss of atrial contribution to filling and higher stroke risk
Pulmonary hypertension Higher RV afterload
Right ventricular dysfunction Strong poor prognostic marker
Recurrent hospitalization Signals advanced disease and instability

Common Clinical Scenarios

Case 1: Classic HFpEF

A 72-year-old woman with BMI 34, hypertension, atrial fibrillation, and dyspnea on exertion has LVEF 55%, E/e′ 12, LAVI 38, and PASP 45.

High H₂FPEF score → HFpEF likely. Treat with SGLT2 inhibitor, diuretics if congested, exercise, weight loss, BP control, and AF management.

Case 2: Diagnostic Uncertainty

A 68-year-old man has exertional dyspnea but a normal resting echo and borderline BNP.

Next step: exercise echocardiography or invasive hemodynamic exercise testing if symptoms remain unexplained.

Case 3: Amyloidosis Mimic

A 75-year-old man has HFpEF symptoms, low-voltage ECG, LV wall thickening, and apical sparing on echo.

Evaluate for cardiac amyloidosis with light chains, immunofixation, cardiac MRI, and technetium pyrophosphate scanning when appropriate.

Rapid Decision Framework

  1. Suspect HFpEF in a patient with dyspnea plus risk factors such as age, hypertension, obesity, AF, diabetes, or CKD.
  2. Confirm preserved EF and look for elevated filling pressures on echo.
  3. Use H₂FPEF or HFA-PEFF score to estimate probability.
  4. Check BNP or NT-proBNP, remembering obesity may lower levels.
  5. If uncertain, pursue exercise echo or invasive hemodynamic testing.
  6. Exclude mimics such as lung disease, anemia, obesity-related dyspnea, valvular disease, constriction, and amyloidosis.
  7. Treat congestion with loop diuretics.
  8. Start SGLT2 inhibitor unless contraindicated.
  9. Optimize BP, weight, exercise, AF, sleep apnea, diabetes, CKD, and ischemic disease.

Clinical Pearls

  • HFpEF requires symptoms plus preserved EF plus evidence of elevated filling pressures.
  • Do not diagnose HFpEF based on EF alone.
  • HFpEF is not just diastolic dysfunction; it is often systemic and multi-organ.
  • Obesity can lower BNP and make HFpEF harder to recognize.
  • AF is heavily weighted in the H₂FPEF score.
  • Exercise-only dyspnea may require exercise echo or invasive hemodynamic testing.
  • Loop diuretics treat congestion but do not reverse the underlying syndrome.
  • SGLT2 inhibitors are first-line disease-modifying therapy in HFpEF.
  • Beta blockers are not used for HFpEF outcome benefit alone.
  • Right ventricular dysfunction and pulmonary hypertension are poor prognostic signs.
  • Always consider amyloidosis in older patients with HFpEF, LV wall thickening, low voltage ECG, or apical sparing.
  • Weight loss and exercise are major treatments in obesity-predominant HFpEF.

Bottom Line

HFpEF is a clinical syndrome of heart failure symptoms, preserved ejection fraction, and elevated filling pressures. It is not simply “diastolic dysfunction,” but a heterogeneous systemic syndrome involving obesity, hypertension, diabetes, atrial fibrillation, kidney disease, vascular dysfunction, pulmonary hypertension, and skeletal muscle abnormalities. Diagnosis relies on clinical suspicion, echo findings, natriuretic peptides, and scoring systems such as H₂FPEF or HFA-PEFF. When uncertain, exercise echo or invasive exercise hemodynamics can confirm the diagnosis. Treatment focuses on SGLT2 inhibitors, diuretics for congestion, exercise, weight loss, blood pressure control, atrial fibrillation management, and aggressive comorbidity optimization.

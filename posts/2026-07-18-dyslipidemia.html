2026 ACC/AHA Dyslipidemia Guideline

A practical clinical summary of modern ASCVD risk assessment, LDL-C goals, biomarkers, coronary calcium, and lipid-lowering therapy

Added: July 18, 2026 • 8-minute read

Overview

The 2026 ACC/AHA multisociety dyslipidemia guideline moves lipid management beyond a simple question of whether a patient qualifies for a statin. Management now combines short-term and long-term risk, absolute lipid goals, percentage LDL-C reduction, risk-enhancing conditions, biomarkers, imaging, and the amount of lipid lowering required.

Central Principle

Lower atherogenic cholesterol earlier, achieve an appropriate treatment goal, and maintain that reduction over time.

The guideline recognizes that atherosclerosis reflects cumulative exposure to ApoB-containing lipoproteins. A younger patient may have a low 10-year event risk while still having substantial lifetime risk from prolonged LDL-C exposure.

Risk Assessment With PREVENT

The guideline uses the American Heart Association PREVENT-ASCVD equations for primary prevention risk assessment. PREVENT estimates both 10-year and 30-year cardiovascular risk.

10-Year PREVENT-ASCVD Risk Risk Category
<3% Low risk
3% to <5% Borderline risk
5% to <10% Intermediate risk
≥10% High risk

Why 30-Year Risk Matters

Thirty-year risk is particularly helpful in younger adults whose short-term risk may appear reassuring. A high long-term risk can support earlier lifestyle intervention and pharmacologic treatment, especially when LDL-C is persistently elevated.

Clinical Pearl: A low 10-year risk should not automatically end the discussion. Consider age, LDL-C exposure, family history, risk enhancers, and 30-year risk.

Primary Prevention Treatment Thresholds

Lipid-lowering therapy may be considered at lower calculated-risk thresholds than in previous cholesterol frameworks.

Clinical Setting General Approach
Low risk: <3% Emphasize lifestyle. Treatment may still be reasonable with substantial lifetime risk, LDL-C 160–189 mg/dL, familial risk, or other important risk enhancers.
Borderline risk: 3% to <5% Discuss lipid-lowering therapy when risk-enhancing factors are present.
Intermediate risk: 5% to <10% Lipid-lowering therapy is generally favored after a clinician-patient risk discussion.
High risk: ≥10% Lipid-lowering therapy is recommended, usually with a more intensive LDL-C reduction strategy.

These thresholds guide clinical discussion rather than replace individualized judgment. The final treatment decision should account for expected benefit, comorbidities, medication burden, adverse effects, patient preference, and competing health risks.

Absolute Lipid Goals and Percentage Reduction

A major change is the return of explicit LDL-C and non-HDL-C treatment goals. Treatment effectiveness should be judged using both the achieved lipid level and the percentage reduction from baseline.

Risk Group LDL-C Goal Non-HDL-C Goal
Borderline or intermediate primary-prevention risk <100 mg/dL <130 mg/dL
High primary-prevention risk <70 mg/dL <100 mg/dL
Very-high-risk ASCVD <55 mg/dL <85 mg/dL

Expected LDL-C Reduction

  • Moderate-intensity therapy: approximately 30% to 49% LDL-C reduction.
  • High-intensity therapy: at least 50% LDL-C reduction.

Do Not Focus on Only One Number

A patient can achieve a large percentage LDL-C reduction and remain above the recommended absolute goal. Conversely, a patient with a relatively low baseline LDL-C may reach the absolute goal without achieving the expected percentage reduction. Both should be considered in context.

Risk-Enhancing Factors

Risk-enhancing factors can support earlier or more intensive treatment when the calculated risk alone does not clearly establish the best approach.

  • Family history of premature ASCVD
  • Persistently elevated LDL-C
  • Chronic inflammatory disorders
  • Metabolic syndrome
  • Persistent hypertriglyceridemia
  • Elevated lipoprotein(a)
  • Elevated ApoB
  • Elevated hs-CRP
  • Premature menopause
  • Adverse pregnancy outcomes
  • South Asian ancestry
  • Evidence of subclinical atherosclerosis

High-Sensitivity C-Reactive Protein

An hs-CRP ≥2 mg/L is a risk-enhancing finding and may support initiation or intensification of statin therapy in an otherwise uncertain primary-prevention decision.

An isolated elevation should be interpreted carefully. Acute infection, trauma, and active inflammatory disease can temporarily increase hs-CRP and may require repeat testing after the acute process resolves.

Advanced Lipid Testing

Lipoprotein(a)

The guideline recommends measuring lipoprotein(a), or Lp(a), at least once during adulthood. Lp(a) is largely genetically determined and is associated with ASCVD and calcific aortic valve disease.

When Lp(a) is elevated, management focuses on aggressive control of modifiable risk factors and more intensive lowering of LDL-C and other ApoB-containing particles. In patients with established ASCVD, a PCSK9 inhibitor may be considered when additional LDL-C lowering is indicated.

Clinical Pearl: Lifestyle modification remains essential for overall cardiovascular risk, but lifestyle changes generally have limited effect on the genetically determined Lp(a) concentration itself.

Apolipoprotein B

ApoB estimates the total number of atherogenic lipoprotein particles. It can be especially useful when LDL-C may underestimate particle burden, including in patients with:

  • Hypertriglyceridemia
  • Diabetes or insulin resistance
  • Metabolic syndrome
  • Obesity
  • Low LDL-C with suspected residual atherogenic risk

Non-HDL Cholesterol

Non-HDL-C represents cholesterol carried by all ApoB-containing lipoproteins. It is calculated as:

Non-HDL-C = Total cholesterol − HDL-C

Non-HDL-C is particularly useful when triglycerides are elevated and should be assessed alongside LDL-C when determining whether therapy is adequate.

Coronary Artery Calcium

Coronary artery calcium scoring is no longer viewed only as a tie-breaker. CAC provides direct evidence of coronary atherosclerosis and can influence whether treatment is started and how intensively LDL-C should be lowered.

CAC Finding Clinical Interpretation
CAC = 0 May support deferring medication in selected primary-prevention patients when no major high-risk condition is present.
CAC 1–99 Confirms coronary atherosclerosis and generally favors lipid-lowering therapy.
CAC ≥100 Indicates a higher atherosclerotic burden and supports more intensive LDL-C lowering.
CAC ≥300 Represents advanced subclinical coronary disease and may justify treatment approaching secondary-prevention intensity.

Coronary calcification reported incidentally on a non-gated chest CT should not be ignored. Although an incidental finding is not always equivalent to a formal Agatston score, it provides evidence of coronary atherosclerosis and should prompt cardiovascular risk assessment.

When CAC = 0 Does Not End the Discussion

A CAC score of zero may be less reassuring in patients with diabetes, cigarette smoking, severe hypercholesterolemia, a strong family history of premature ASCVD, or other high-risk conditions. CAC also does not identify noncalcified plaque.

Direct Treatment Pathways

Some conditions carry sufficient cardiovascular risk that treatment should not depend solely on the PREVENT score.

Severe Hypercholesterolemia

Patients with LDL-C ≥190 mg/dL should generally receive intensive lipid-lowering therapy without relying on a calculated 10-year risk. Evaluate for familial hypercholesterolemia, secondary causes, premature ASCVD, and affected family members.

Diabetes Mellitus

Diabetes remains a major treatment pathway. The intensity of therapy depends on age, duration of diabetes, additional risk factors, end-organ complications, and the presence of clinical ASCVD.

Chronic Kidney Disease

Adults with at least stage 3 chronic kidney disease generally meet criteria for lipid-lowering therapy regardless of their PREVENT estimate, provided that treatment is appropriate for their overall clinical situation.

HIV

Adults with HIV have increased ASCVD risk that may not be fully represented by conventional risk models. Lipid-lowering therapy should be considered through a direct treatment pathway while accounting for potential drug interactions with antiretroviral therapy.

Established ASCVD

Coronary, cerebrovascular, peripheral, and aortic atherosclerotic disease are treated as manifestations of systemic ASCVD. Patients with polyvascular disease or recurrent events are at particularly high risk and generally require intensive LDL-C lowering.

Lipid-Lowering Therapy

Statins Remain First-Line Therapy

Statins remain the foundation of pharmacologic treatment because of their established ability to lower LDL-C and reduce cardiovascular events.

Rosuvastatin 20–40 mg 		≥50%
Moderate intensity Atorvastatin 10–20 mg
Rosuvastatin 5–10 mg

Earlier Use of Combination Therapy

The guideline moves away from unnecessarily prolonged, rigid, stepwise escalation. Therapy may be selected according to the magnitude of LDL-C reduction needed to reach the patient's goal.

Ezetimibe

An oral cholesterol-absorption inhibitor commonly added to a statin when additional LDL-C reduction is required.

PCSK9-Directed Therapy

Provides substantial LDL-C lowering and is particularly useful in very-high-risk ASCVD, familial hypercholesterolemia, or when goals remain unmet with other therapy.

Bempedoic Acid

An oral nonstatin option that may be useful when additional LDL-C lowering is needed or when statin intolerance limits treatment.

Clinical Pearl: When a patient requires a very large LDL-C reduction, consider combination therapy early rather than waiting through repeated months of predictable treatment failure.

Statin-Associated Symptoms

Muscle symptoms should be evaluated systematically rather than automatically labeling the patient as permanently statin intolerant. Consider the timing of symptoms, creatine kinase when clinically indicated, thyroid disease, vitamin deficiencies, drug interactions, strenuous exercise, and other musculoskeletal causes.

Many patients can tolerate a different statin, a lower dose, an alternate dosing schedule, or a statin combined with nonstatin therapy.

Monitoring Response to Therapy

After initiating or changing lipid-lowering therapy, repeat a lipid panel to assess adherence, percentage LDL-C reduction, and whether the absolute treatment goal has been reached.

  1. Obtain a baseline lipid profile and assess for secondary causes when appropriate.
  2. Start therapy based on the patient's risk, lipid level, and required LDL-C reduction.
  3. Repeat lipid testing approximately 4–12 weeks after starting or adjusting treatment.
  4. Reinforce adherence, lifestyle measures, and medication tolerability.
  5. Intensify therapy when the expected reduction or treatment goal has not been achieved.
  6. Continue periodic monitoring once treatment is stable.

Routine creatine kinase or liver-enzyme monitoring is not necessary in every asymptomatic patient. Testing should be guided by the clinical situation, baseline risk, and development of concerning symptoms.

Clinical Summary

  • Use PREVENT to estimate both 10-year and 30-year cardiovascular risk.
  • Do not assume that a low short-term risk means treatment is unnecessary.
  • Consider cumulative LDL-C exposure, especially in younger patients.
  • Assess both the absolute LDL-C goal and percentage reduction from baseline.
  • Use non-HDL-C and ApoB when LDL-C may underestimate atherogenic particle burden.
  • Measure Lp(a) at least once during adulthood.
  • Use CAC and incidental coronary calcification to identify subclinical atherosclerosis and refine treatment intensity.
  • Treat severe hypercholesterolemia, ASCVD, diabetes, stage 3 or greater CKD, and HIV through condition-specific pathways.
  • Consider early combination therapy when statin monotherapy is unlikely to achieve the required LDL-C reduction.

Bottom Line

The 2026 ACC/AHA dyslipidemia guideline shifts practice toward earlier, goal-directed, and individualized lipid management. Treatment decisions incorporate lifetime risk, absolute LDL-C and non-HDL-C goals, percentage reduction, risk-enhancing biomarkers, coronary calcium, and timely combination therapy.

Chest Pain, Pancytopenia, and APML: A Case-Based Approach

Added June 20, 2026 • Hematology / Cardiology • ~18 min read

A case-based review of a patient who presented with chest pain but was ultimately found to have acute promyelocytic leukemia. This article reviews emergent chest pain diagnoses, ACS likelihood ratios, systematic EKG interpretation, STEMI criteria, pancytopenia evaluation, reticulocyte index, problem representation, APML recognition, DIC, and urgent treatment with all-trans retinoic acid.

Case Presentation

A middle-aged man with coronary artery disease status post coronary stent several years earlier presented to the emergency department with chest pain. At first glance, the most urgent concern was acute coronary syndrome because of his prior coronary history.

His chest pain began earlier that morning and woke him from sleep. He described intermittent substernal pressure lasting one to two minutes at a time. There was no radiation, and the pain had resolved by the time he arrived in the emergency department.

Teaching point: A patient may present with one complaint, but the most important diagnosis may be found incidentally during the initial workup.

Emergent Chest Pain Framework

When evaluating chest pain, first rule out life-threatening causes. A useful memory tool is the 4-2-1 rule.

Category Number Emergency Diagnoses
Heart 4 Acute coronary syndrome, aortic dissection, pericarditis, pericardial effusion with tamponade
Lungs 2 Pulmonary embolism, pneumothorax
Esophagus 1 Esophageal perforation
4-2-1 rule: Four cardiac emergencies, two pulmonary emergencies, and one esophageal emergency.

ACS History and Likelihood Ratios

Certain history features increase or decrease the probability of acute coronary syndrome.

Findings That Increase ACS Probability

  • Substernal chest pain
  • Pressure-like quality
  • Exertional symptoms
  • Relief with rest
  • Associated nausea or vomiting
  • Feels similar to prior angina or MI
  • Radiation to the arm or shoulder
High-yield finding: Radiation to the right arm or right shoulder has a particularly high positive likelihood ratio for ACS.

Findings That Decrease ACS Probability

Remember the three Ps.

Feature Effect
Positional Decreases likelihood of ACS
Pleuritic Decreases likelihood of ACS
Reproducible with palpation Decreases likelihood of ACS

Likelihood Ratio Shortcut

Likelihood Ratio Approximate Change in Probability
2 Increases probability by ~15%
5 Increases probability by ~30%
10 Increases probability by ~45%
2510 rule: A likelihood ratio of 2, 5, or 10 increases probability by roughly 15%, 30%, or 45%.

Systematic EKG Interpretation

Every EKG should be read the same way to avoid missing abnormalities.

EKG Approach = Rate → Rhythm → Axis → Intervals → Morphology

Rate

Method How to Use
300 method 300 divided by the number of large boxes between R waves
10-second strip Count QRS complexes and multiply by 6

Rhythm

Sinus rhythm requires a P wave before every QRS and a QRS after every P wave. Lead II is often the best lead to check for upright sinus P waves.

Axis

Lead I Lead II Interpretation
Positive Positive Normal axis
Positive Negative Left axis deviation
Negative Positive Right axis deviation
Negative Negative Extreme axis deviation

Intervals

Interval Normal Meaning
PR interval 120–200 ms AV nodal conduction
QRS duration <120 ms Ventricular conduction
QT interval Usually <50% of R-R interval by quick screen Repolarization

Morphology

Morphology includes atrial enlargement, ventricular hypertrophy, conduction delay, T wave inversions, ST elevations, ST depressions, and ischemic patterns.

In this case, the EKG did not show clear STEMI. There was possible mild conduction delay and possible left atrial enlargement, but no major ischemic pattern.

STEMI Criteria

Lead Group ST Elevation Threshold
All leads except V2–V3 ≥1 mm in contiguous leads
V2–V3 in women ≥1.5 mm
V2–V3 in men age ≥40 ≥2 mm
V2–V3 in men age <40 ≥2.5 mm

ST Elevation Morphology

Morphology Interpretation
Concave / smile-shaped ST elevation Less concerning; can be early repolarization
Convex / frown-shaped ST elevation More concerning for ischemia
Tombstone appearance Highly concerning for STEMI

T Wave Inversions

Sharp, symmetric T wave inversions are more concerning for ischemia. Gradual, asymmetric T wave inversions can be seen with strain patterns, such as severe left ventricular hypertrophy.

Troponin Interpretation

The patient’s troponins remained low and flat without a convincing rise and fall pattern. Combined with a non-ischemic EKG and equivocal symptoms, acute coronary syndrome became unlikely.

A flat troponin trend with reassuring EKG and resolved atypical symptoms helps rule out acute coronary syndrome.

Unexpected Pancytopenia

During the chest pain workup, the CBC showed:

Cell Line Result
White blood cells 0.6
Hemoglobin 9.7
Platelets 70

Eight months earlier, his CBC had been normal. This new pancytopenia shifted the case away from cardiology and toward hematology.

Important shift: Pancytopenia usually suggests a bone marrow production problem until proven otherwise.

4S Framework for Pancytopenia

A useful framework for pancytopenia is the 4S mnemonic.

Category Examples
Systemic disease Lupus, tick-borne illness, severe systemic illness
Substances Chemotherapy, alcohol, heavy metals, linezolid, other marrow-toxic medications
Substance deficiency B12 deficiency, folate deficiency, copper deficiency
Space-occupying marrow disease Lymphoma, leukemia, fungal infection, TB, MAC, granulomatous disease
Stem cell disorders Leukemia, myelodysplastic syndrome, aplastic anemia
A single cytopenia is often peripheral. Two cytopenias can be peripheral or marrow-related. Pancytopenia strongly implicates the bone marrow.

Targeted History

After pancytopenia was discovered, the history changed. He denied fevers, chills, night sweats, nausea, vomiting, shortness of breath, and initially denied weight loss. He had fatigue for several days, a recent dental infection treated with amoxicillin, gum bleeding while brushing his teeth, and hemorrhoidal bleeding.

Relevant History

  • Recent amoxicillin exposure
  • Diffuse mucosal bleeding
  • Severe fatigue
  • Born in the Philippines
  • No recent travel
  • No alcohol use
  • Atrial flutter on apixaban
  • Aspirin use
Medication-induced marrow suppression was considered, but the bleeding severity and later weight loss raised concern for a more serious marrow process.

Initial Workup

The initial workup focused on confirming pancytopenia, looking for hemolysis, assessing nutritional deficiencies, evaluating coagulation, and screening for infection.

Test Result / Meaning
Peripheral smear No schistocytes initially; teardrop cells noted
Folate Normal
Copper Normal
B12 Elevated
Haptoglobin Elevated, arguing against hemolysis
DAT / Coombs Negative
Hepatitis and HIV testing Negative
INR Elevated around 1.5
D-dimer Elevated
Elevated B12 pearl: A markedly elevated B12 level can be associated with malignancy, liver disease, and worse prognosis. Do not always ignore it as “normal enough.”

Peripheral Smear

The peripheral smear is essential in severe thrombocytopenia or pancytopenia.

Emergency Findings to Look For

  • Schistocytes suggesting microangiopathic hemolytic anemia
  • Blasts suggesting acute leukemia
  • Auer rods suggesting AML/APML spectrum
  • Platelet clumping causing pseudothrombocytopenia

Findings in This Case

  • Teardrop cells
  • Anisocytosis
  • Poikilocytosis
  • Ovalocytes
  • Decreased platelets
  • No schistocytes initially
  • No clear blasts initially
Teardrop cells suggest marrow stress or marrow infiltration. A common teaching phrase is that the “bone marrow is crying.”

Nucleated RBCs

Nucleated red blood cells indicate immature red blood cells are being pushed into circulation too early, which also supports marrow stress.

Reticulocyte Index

The reticulocyte count helps determine whether the bone marrow is responding appropriately.

Reticulocyte Index >2 = Appropriate marrow response
Reticulocyte Index <2 = Inadequate marrow response

This patient’s reticulocyte index was approximately 0.6, which was inappropriately low for anemia.

Interpretation: Pancytopenia plus a low reticulocyte index strongly suggests impaired marrow production.

Imaging Findings

Chest X-Ray

The chest X-ray showed cardiomegaly and mild central vascular congestion, suspicious for mild pulmonary edema.

Other Imaging

  • Left upper quadrant ultrasound showed mild splenomegaly.
  • CT chest was negative for pulmonary embolism.
  • CT chest suggested possible pulmonary hypertension and left ventricular hypertrophy.

Initial Hematology Impression

The initial hematology impression favored possible medication-induced pancytopenia because of the temporal relationship with amoxicillin, lack of B symptoms, and initially reassuring smear.

Initial Plan

  • Hold amoxicillin
  • Send EBV testing
  • Send CMV testing
  • Send parvovirus testing
  • Send peripheral flow cytometry
  • Evaluate liver and spleen
  • Consider bone marrow biopsy if pancytopenia persisted
Medication-induced pancytopenia can occur, but persistent pancytopenia, low reticulocyte response, teardrop cells, nucleated RBCs, weight loss, and bleeding should keep marrow malignancy high on the differential.

Problem Representation

A problem representation is a concise summary of the most important features of a case. It helps create illness scripts and improves clinical reasoning.

Four Components

Component Example in This Case
Demographics Middle-aged man
Pertinent history Recent antibiotic exposure, anticoagulation, CAD
Time course Acute to subacute process
Key features Pancytopenia, weight loss, mucosal bleeding, elevated INR
Problem Representation:
Middle-aged man with acute pancytopenia, weight loss, mucosal bleeding, and elevated INR.

Once DIC is considered, the problem representation becomes even more specific.

Middle-aged man with suspected acute leukemia and DIC.
Acute leukemia plus DIC should immediately trigger concern for acute promyelocytic leukemia.

Recognizing APML

Acute promyelocytic leukemia is a subtype of AML that is highly associated with DIC and bleeding. It is rapidly fatal if not recognized and treated early, but outcomes can be excellent with prompt therapy.

Classic Findings

Feature APML Association
Age Often adults, including middle-aged patients
Bleeding Prominent due to DIC
Marrow Promyelocytes / blasts
Smear or marrow Auer rods may be seen
Genetics t(15;17)
Fusion gene PML-RARA
Urgent treatment ATRA
Do not wait: If APML is suspected, start ATRA immediately while confirmatory testing is pending.

APML Treatment

Immediate Therapy

Suspected APML → Start ATRA immediately

All-trans retinoic acid forces abnormal promyelocytes to differentiate, reducing the lethal coagulopathy associated with APML.

Induction Therapy

Medication Role
ATRA Promotes differentiation of promyelocytes
Arsenic trioxide Targets APML cells and improves outcomes
Prednisone Reduces risk of differentiation syndrome

Complications to Monitor

  • DIC and bleeding
  • Tumor lysis syndrome
  • Differentiation syndrome
  • Infection due to neutropenia
  • Transfusion needs
Differentiation syndrome: Watch for fever, weight gain, edema, pulmonary infiltrates, hypoxia, hypotension, and organ dysfunction after starting differentiation therapy.

Hospital Course

The patient’s amoxicillin was held, but pancytopenia persisted. Repeat smear showed a small percentage of presumptive blasts. Bone marrow biopsy later showed Auer rods and approximately 81% blasts, confirming acute promyelocytic leukemia.

He was transferred to the malignant hematology service and started on ATRA, arsenic, and prednisone. He was monitored for tumor lysis syndrome and underwent repeat marrow assessment after induction therapy.

Response

Time Point Finding
Initial marrow Approximately 81% blasts
After induction Approximately 1–2% blasts
Molecular testing Persistent PML-RARA detected
Plan Proceed with consolidation therapy
Patients with acute hematologic malignancy often have long hospital stays due to cytopenias, infection risk, transfusion needs, induction therapy, and repeat marrow assessment.

Clinical Pearls

  1. Use the 4-2-1 rule to remember emergent causes of chest pain.
  2. Radiation to the right arm or shoulder strongly increases the likelihood of ACS.
  3. Positional, pleuritic, and reproducible chest pain decrease the likelihood of ACS.
  4. Use the 2510 rule to estimate the impact of likelihood ratios.
  5. Read EKGs systematically: rate, rhythm, axis, intervals, morphology.
  6. STEMI requires ST elevation in contiguous leads.
  7. V2–V3 have sex- and age-specific STEMI thresholds.
  8. Convex ST elevation is more concerning than concave ST elevation.
  9. A flat troponin trend with reassuring EKG makes ACS less likely.
  10. Pancytopenia strongly suggests a marrow process.
  11. Always order a peripheral smear in severe thrombocytopenia or pancytopenia.
  12. Schistocytes suggest microangiopathic hemolytic anemia.
  13. Teardrop cells suggest marrow stress or infiltration.
  14. Nucleated RBCs suggest immature cells are being pushed out of marrow.
  15. A low reticulocyte index indicates inadequate marrow response.
  16. Medication-induced pancytopenia is possible but should not stop the workup if concerning features persist.
  17. Weight loss and pancytopenia should raise concern for malignancy.
  18. Bleeding out of proportion to platelet count should raise concern for coagulopathy such as DIC.
  19. Acute leukemia plus DIC should trigger concern for APML.
  20. Suspected APML requires immediate ATRA.
  21. APML is associated with t(15;17) and PML-RARA.
  22. APML treatment often includes ATRA, arsenic trioxide, and steroids to reduce differentiation syndrome risk.

Bottom Line

This case began as a chest pain evaluation but became a hematologic emergency. The chest pain workup appropriately focused on emergent diagnoses, ACS probability, EKG interpretation, STEMI criteria, and troponin trends. The unexpected pancytopenia shifted the diagnostic framework toward marrow failure or marrow infiltration. Teardrop cells, nucleated RBCs, low reticulocyte index, mucosal bleeding, weight loss, and elevated INR raised concern for acute leukemia with DIC. The final diagnosis was acute promyelocytic leukemia. The key management pearl is simple: if APML is suspected, start ATRA immediately while awaiting confirmation.

Running a Code Blue: A Practical Guide for Internal Medicine Residents

Added June 17, 2026 • Critical Care • ~20 min read

A practical resident-focused guide to running a Code Blue, including ACLS basics, CPR quality, PEA versus shockable rhythms, medications, team roles, H's and T's, when to call the code, and what to do after ROSC.

Overview

Running your first Code Blue is one of the most intimidating moments in residency. Suddenly, you may be expected to lead a room full of people during cardiac arrest, make rapid decisions, and keep the ACLS algorithm moving.

The key is to remember that a Code Blue is highly protocolized. You do not need to personally perform every task. Your job is to stay calm, keep the team organized, ensure high-quality CPR, recognize the rhythm, give the correct medications, shock when appropriate, and look for reversible causes.

Big picture: A good code leader is not the person doing everything. A good code leader keeps the room organized and makes sure the essentials are happening.

The Code Blue Mindset

Before reviewing the ACLS algorithm, it helps to approach the code with the right mindset. The situation is scary, but your role is manageable if you simplify your priorities.

Three Things to Remember

  1. The patient is already in cardiac arrest. Your actions can only help.
  2. Your calmness and confidence set the tone for the room.
  3. ACLS is protocolized, and the team will help you.
Practical pearl: When you arrive at a code, first check your own pulse. Then take a breath, speak clearly, and start organizing the room.

Your Core Tasks as Code Leader

During a Code Blue, many details can distract you. Focus first on the tasks that matter most.

Task Goal
High-quality CPR Keep compressions deep, fast, and minimally interrupted
Rhythm checks Every 2 minutes
Epinephrine 1 mg IV/IO every 3–5 minutes
Amiodarone For refractory VF/pulseless VT: 300 mg, then 150 mg
Defibrillation Shock VF/pulseless VT as soon as possible
Reversible causes Run through H's and T's
Resident shortcut: CPR, rhythm check, epinephrine, shock if shockable, amiodarone if refractory shockable rhythm, and H's/T's. That is the backbone.

PEA vs Shockable Rhythms

The most important branching point in ACLS is whether the rhythm is shockable or non-shockable.

Rhythm Type Examples Treatment Focus
Non-shockable PEA, asystole CPR + epinephrine + reversible causes
Shockable Ventricular fibrillation, pulseless ventricular tachycardia CPR + defibrillation + epinephrine + amiodarone

PEA

Pulseless electrical activity means the monitor shows organized electrical activity, but the patient has no palpable pulse. Do not shock PEA. Continue CPR, give epinephrine, and search aggressively for reversible causes.

VFib / Pulseless VTach

Ventricular fibrillation and pulseless ventricular tachycardia are shockable rhythms. Defibrillation is the key intervention. Continue CPR while the defibrillator charges, clear the patient, shock, and immediately resume CPR.

High-yield distinction: PEA gets epinephrine only. VFib/pulseless VT gets shocks, epinephrine, and amiodarone.

High-Quality CPR

High-quality CPR is the foundation of resuscitation. Everything else is secondary if compressions are poor.

  • Push hard and fast.
  • Target 100–120 compressions per minute.
  • Minimize pauses in compressions.
  • Rotate compressors every 2 minutes or sooner if fatigued.
  • Use a backboard when possible.
  • Avoid excessive ventilation.

Ventilation During CPR

Airway Status Ventilation Strategy
Not intubated 30 compressions : 2 breaths
Intubated Continuous compressions with breaths every 5–6 seconds

End-Tidal CO₂

End-tidal CO₂ helps estimate CPR quality and may give clues about prognosis and ROSC.

End-Tidal CO₂ Interpretation
<10 mmHg Poor CPR quality or poor prognosis
Persistently <15 mmHg Low likelihood of ROSC
Sudden rise toward 40 mmHg ROSC may be imminent

What to Do When You Arrive

When you arrive at the code, your first moments should be structured. Speak loudly enough for the room to hear you.

  1. Ask: “Is anyone leading this code?”
  2. If no one is leading, say: “I will be the code leader.”
  3. Confirm the patient is pulseless.
  4. Confirm code status if possible.
  5. Start or continue CPR immediately.
  6. Ask for a backboard.
  7. Place defibrillator/pacer pads.
  8. Ensure IV or IO access.
  9. Assign airway management.
  10. Give epinephrine 1 mg IV/IO.
First minute goal: Establish leadership, confirm arrest, start CPR, place pads, secure access, assign airway, and give epinephrine.

Assigning Roles

Many experienced code teams self-assign roles before you arrive. If roles are not clear, assign them directly.

Role Responsibility
Code leader Runs ACLS algorithm and directs team
Compressor team Performs CPR and rotates every 2 minutes
Timer Tracks 2-minute CPR cycles and medication timing
Recorder Documents events, medications, shocks, and pulse checks
Medication nurse Draws up and administers medications
Airway Bags patient and coordinates intubation
Pulse checker Checks pulse during rhythm checks
Ultrasound Assesses for tamponade, pneumothorax, or other causes
Chart review Reviews labs, history, meds, code status, and events
Family communicator Updates family and gathers goals-of-care information
Communication pearl: Use closed-loop communication. Give a clear order, receive confirmation, and confirm completion.

Defibrillation

For VFib or pulseless VTach, defibrillation should happen quickly. Do not delay shockable rhythm treatment.

Suggested Shock Energy

Shock Number Energy
First shock 200 J
Second shock 300 J
Third and later shocks 360 J

How to Shock Safely

  1. Continue CPR while the defibrillator charges.
  2. Announce: “Charging.”
  3. When ready, say: “Clear.”
  4. Make sure no one is touching the patient or bed.
  5. Deliver shock.
  6. Confirm: “Shock delivered.”
  7. Immediately say: “Resume CPR.”

Labs, Access, and Bedside Data

After the first few cycles are running smoothly, obtain labs and bedside data to search for reversible causes.

Common Code Labs

  • ABG or VBG
  • Lactate
  • CMP
  • CBC
  • Troponin
  • Glucose
  • Type and screen

Access

Use existing reliable IV access if present. If access is poor or delayed, place intraosseous access. Do not let lack of IV access delay CPR.

The H's and T's

The H's and T's are reversible causes of cardiac arrest. Once the basics are running, slow down and systematically work through them.

Cause Clues / Evaluation Possible Action
Hypoxia Low O₂, airway issue, difficult ventilation Improve airway and ventilation
Hypovolemia Bleeding, dehydration, shock Fluids, blood products, MTP if needed
Hydrogen ion / acidosis ABG/VBG with severe acidosis Improve ventilation, consider bicarbonate if severe
Hypo-/hyperkalemia CMP, ECG changes, renal failure Calcium, insulin/dextrose, bicarbonate, dialysis if needed
Hypothermia Low temperature Rewarming
Tension pneumothorax Ultrasound, unilateral breath sounds, high pressures Needle decompression / chest tube
Tamponade Bedside ultrasound Pericardiocentesis
Thrombosis - PE Risk factors, echo strain, sudden PEA Consider thrombolysis in selected cases
Thrombosis - ACS ECG, troponin, history Cath lab if ROSC and appropriate
Toxins Medication overdose, drug exposure Specific antidotes/supportive care

Adjunctive Treatments

These are not the core of ACLS, but may be considered depending on the clinical scenario and suspected cause.

Treatment When to Consider
Sodium bicarbonate Severe acidosis, hyperkalemia, certain overdoses
Calcium chloride Hyperkalemia, calcium-channel blocker toxicity
Magnesium sulfate Torsades de pointes or suspected magnesium-responsive arrhythmia
Fluids Hypovolemia or undifferentiated PEA
Blood products / MTP Hemorrhagic shock
Vasopressin Sometimes used during prolonged resuscitation depending on local practice
Keep perspective: Adjuncts should not distract from CPR, rhythm recognition, defibrillation, and epinephrine timing.

When to Call the Code

Calling a code is difficult and depends on context, patient wishes, rhythm, downtime, response to treatment, and team judgment.

General Considerations

  • Prolonged code with no reversible cause identified
  • Persistent end-tidal CO₂ <10 despite high-quality CPR
  • No ROSC after a prolonged resuscitation
  • Poor baseline prognosis or known patient wishes

If you are considering stopping, communicate clearly with the room.

  1. Say you are considering calling the code.
  2. Ask if anyone has concerns or believes resuscitation should continue.
  3. Announce that you will reassess at the next pulse check.
  4. If there is still no pulse, call the time of death clearly.
Leadership pearl: Do not abruptly stop without preparing the team. Give the room a chance to speak before calling the code.

Post-ROSC Care

If return of spontaneous circulation occurs, the code is not over. The next priority is stabilizing the patient and identifying the cause of arrest.

Immediate Post-ROSC Steps

  • Start vasopressors if hypotensive, commonly norepinephrine.
  • Repeat labs, including blood gas and lactate.
  • Obtain ECG.
  • Obtain chest X-ray.
  • Confirm airway and tube position if intubated.
  • Review H's and T's again.
  • Consider cath lab if ACS is suspected.
  • Consider targeted temperature management if comatose after ROSC.
Post-ROSC goal: Stabilize blood pressure, oxygenation, ventilation, and neurologic protection while searching for the cause of arrest.

Key Takeaways

  • Your first job is to stay calm and lead clearly.
  • High-quality CPR is the foundation of every code.
  • Pulse and rhythm checks occur every 2 minutes.
  • Epinephrine dose is 1 mg IV/IO every 3–5 minutes.
  • Shock VFib and pulseless VTach.
  • Amiodarone is used for refractory shockable rhythms: 300 mg first, then 150 mg.
  • PEA and asystole are not shockable.
  • Use end-tidal CO₂ to assess CPR quality and possible ROSC.
  • Assign roles if the team has not already done so.
  • After the basics are running, work through the H's and T's.
  • After ROSC, stabilize, repeat labs, obtain ECG/chest X-ray, and consider targeted temperature management if comatose.

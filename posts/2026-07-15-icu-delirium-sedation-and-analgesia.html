ICU Delirium, Sedation, and Analgesia

Added July 15, 2026 • Pulmonary and Critical Care • ~30 min read

A bedside guide to analgesia-first care, sedation targets, propofol and dexmedetomidine, benzodiazepine limitations, delirium screening, nonpharmacologic prevention, agitation, sleep, early mobility, withdrawal, and safe management of the ventilated patient.

Clinical Overview

Pain, anxiety, agitation, sedation, delirium, immobility, and sleep disruption are linked. Management should prioritize analgesia, the lightest effective sedation, frequent delirium assessment, mobility, and correction of physiologic causes.

Pain First → Light Sedation → Delirium Prevention → Mobility and Sleep

Pain Assessment and Treatment

  • Use patient report when possible.
  • Use validated behavioral tools when communication is impossible.
  • Treat procedural pain proactively.
  • Use multimodal analgesia to reduce opioid exposure.
  • Reassess bowel function, respiratory drive, and withdrawal risk.

Sedation Targets

  • Set and document a daily target such as RASS.
  • Light sedation is preferred for most ventilated patients.
  • Deep sedation is reserved for severe ARDS with paralysis, status epilepticus, intracranial hypertension, dangerous dyssynchrony, or procedures.
  • Pair spontaneous-awakening and breathing trials when appropriate.

Delirium

  • Screen with CAM-ICU or ICDSC.
  • Identify infection, hypoxemia, metabolic disease, withdrawal, pain, urinary retention, constipation, and medications.
  • Promote orientation, hearing/vision aids, daylight, sleep, family presence, and mobility.
  • Antipsychotics do not routinely shorten delirium; use selectively for dangerous agitation after reversible causes are addressed.

Dexmedetomidine Nuance

The 2025 SCCM focused update conditionally favors dexmedetomidine over propofol in selected mechanically ventilated adults where light sedation and delirium considerations are priorities. Individualize for bradycardia, shock, and required depth.

Agitation in a Ventilated Patient

  1. Check oxygenation, ventilation, pain, tube position, bladder, bowel, and withdrawal.
  2. Inspect ventilator waveforms for dyssynchrony.
  3. Reduce environmental distress and communicate.
  4. Use medication matched to the cause.
  5. Do not paralyze an awake or inadequately sedated patient.

Sleep and Mobility

  • Cluster care and reduce nighttime noise and light.
  • Avoid unnecessary overnight procedures.
  • Mobilize early when hemodynamically and neurologically safe.
  • Use an interdisciplinary ICU liberation bundle.
  • The 2025 update conditionally supports enhanced mobilization and melatonin in selected adults.

Withdrawal and Long Exposure

  • Assess opioid, benzodiazepine, alcohol, and alpha-2 agonist withdrawal.
  • Taper prolonged infusions thoughtfully.
  • Convert to enteral agents selectively.
  • Avoid abrupt discontinuation of high-dose dexmedetomidine or opioids.

Common Pitfalls

  • Treating ventilator dyssynchrony with sedation alone
  • No daily target
  • Routine benzodiazepine sedation
  • Antipsychotics as primary delirium treatment
  • Ignoring pain or withdrawal
  • Deep sedation that prevents mobility and extubation

Bottom Line

Use analgesia-first care, a documented light-sedation target, validated delirium screening, nonpharmacologic prevention, sleep promotion, and early mobility. Match sedatives to physiology and reserve deep sedation for clear indications.

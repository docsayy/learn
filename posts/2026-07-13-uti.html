Urinary Tract Infections: The Complete Advanced Resident Guide

Added July 13, 2026 • Infectious Disease • ~36 min read

Urinary tract infections range from uncomplicated bladder infection to bacteremic pyelonephritis, infected urinary obstruction, prostatitis, renal abscess, emphysematous infection, and catheter-associated disease. This guide reviews diagnostic reasoning, urinalysis and culture interpretation, asymptomatic bacteriuria, antimicrobial selection, source control, resistant organisms, treatment duration, oral step-down, recurrent UTI, pregnancy, candiduria, and high-yield board and infectious disease consultation pearls.

Clinical Overview

A urinary tract infection is a symptomatic infection involving one or more structures of the urinary system. The spectrum includes:

  • Urethritis
  • Cystitis
  • Pyelonephritis
  • Prostatitis
  • Renal or perinephric abscess
  • Catheter-associated urinary tract infection
  • Infected obstructed urinary system
  • Urosepsis

The presence of bacteria, pyuria, leukocyte esterase, or an abnormal urine appearance does not independently establish a urinary tract infection.

Compatible Symptoms + Supporting Urine Findings = Urinary Tract Infection

The Major Clinical Questions

  1. Does the patient have urinary symptoms or another compatible syndrome?
  2. Is the infection confined to the bladder or does it involve the kidney, prostate, or bloodstream?
  3. Is there obstruction, retention, a stone, catheter, abscess, or other nidus?
  4. Is the organism likely resistant?
  5. Can the patient receive oral therapy?
  6. What is the shortest effective treatment duration?
Do not diagnose UTI solely because the urine is abnormal. Diagnose a clinical syndrome and use the urinalysis and culture to support it.

Why UTI Is Frequently Overdiagnosed

  • Asymptomatic bacteriuria is common.
  • Pyuria is common in catheterized and older patients.
  • Urine cultures are often ordered without symptoms.
  • Delirium is incorrectly attributed to bacteriuria.
  • Cloudy or foul-smelling urine is mistaken for infection.
  • Positive cultures may distract from the true source of fever.

Definitions and Classification

Term Definition Clinical Importance
Asymptomatic bacteriuria Bacteria in urine without symptoms attributable to UTI Usually should not be treated
Acute cystitis Symptomatic infection confined primarily to the bladder Dysuria, urgency, frequency, suprapubic discomfort
Pyelonephritis Infection involving the renal pelvis and kidney Fever, flank pain, systemic symptoms
Complicated UTI UTI with systemic involvement or factors increasing treatment complexity or failure risk Requires assessment of source, resistance, anatomy, and host factors
Catheter-associated UTI Symptomatic UTI occurring with a urinary catheter or shortly after removal Requires catheter assessment and careful culture interpretation
Recurrent UTI Repeated symptomatic infections, commonly ≥2 in 6 months or ≥3 in 12 months Requires confirmation, prevention strategy, and selective evaluation
Relapse Recurrence with the same organism after apparent treatment Suggests persistent nidus or inadequate eradication
Reinfection New infection after previous resolution, often with a different strain More common than true relapse
Urosepsis Sepsis caused by urinary tract infection Requires rapid antibiotics and source control

Uncomplicated vs Complicated UTI

Traditional definitions classified uncomplicated UTI as cystitis or pyelonephritis in a healthy, nonpregnant woman without relevant structural or functional urinary abnormalities.

Complicated infection may involve:

  • Fever or systemic illness
  • Pyelonephritis
  • Male urinary tract infection
  • Urinary obstruction
  • Stone disease
  • Urinary retention
  • Neurogenic bladder
  • Indwelling catheter
  • Recent urologic procedure
  • Renal transplant
  • Immunocompromise
  • Abnormal urinary anatomy
The practical distinction is whether the infection is limited to the bladder and likely to respond to a short oral regimen, or whether systemic illness, tissue involvement, anatomy, devices, or host factors require broader evaluation.

Anatomy and Pathogenesis

Ascending Infection

Most urinary tract infections begin with colonization of the periurethral area by intestinal organisms. Bacteria then ascend through the urethra into the bladder.

Further ascent through the ureters can produce pyelonephritis.

Hematogenous Infection

Less commonly, organisms reach the kidney through the bloodstream.

Consider Hematogenous Seeding With:

  • Staphylococcus aureus
  • Candida species
  • Mycobacterium tuberculosis
  • Disseminated fungal infection
Staphylococcus aureus in urine without recent urinary instrumentation may indicate hematogenous seeding and should raise concern for S. aureus bacteremia or endocarditis.

Urinary Stasis

Urinary stasis promotes infection by preventing normal washout of bacteria.

Causes

  • Benign prostatic hyperplasia
  • Neurogenic bladder
  • Ureteral obstruction
  • Stone disease
  • Pelvic mass
  • Urethral stricture
  • Vesicoureteral reflux
  • Constipation in selected patients

Biofilm Formation

Urinary catheters and stents rapidly develop biofilm. Organisms within biofilm are difficult to eradicate and may persist despite apparently active antibiotics.

Risk Factors

Risk Factors for Cystitis

  • Female anatomy
  • Sexual activity
  • Spermicide use
  • Previous UTI
  • Menopause
  • Pregnancy
  • Diabetes

Risk Factors for Complicated Infection

  • Urinary obstruction
  • Stone disease
  • Urinary catheter or stent
  • Urinary retention
  • Neurogenic bladder
  • Recent urologic procedure
  • Renal transplant
  • Immunocompromise
  • Male sex
  • Pregnancy
  • Abnormal urinary anatomy

Risk Factors for Resistant Organisms

  • Previous resistant urine culture
  • Recent antibiotic use
  • Recent hospitalization
  • Residence in a healthcare facility
  • Chronic urinary catheter
  • Repeated urinary procedures
  • Travel or healthcare abroad
  • ESBL, AmpC, or CRE colonization
Previous urine cultures are among the most useful predictors of the organism and resistance pattern in the current episode.

Clinical Syndromes

Acute Cystitis

  • Dysuria
  • Urinary frequency
  • Urgency
  • Suprapubic discomfort
  • Hematuria
  • Absence of prominent fever or flank pain

Acute Pyelonephritis

  • Fever
  • Chills
  • Flank pain
  • Costovertebral-angle tenderness
  • Nausea or vomiting
  • Dysuria or urinary frequency may be present or absent
  • Possible bacteremia

Prostatitis

  • Fever
  • Pelvic or perineal pain
  • Dysuria
  • Urinary retention
  • Painful ejaculation
  • Tender or boggy prostate

Urosepsis

  • Fever or hypothermia
  • Hypotension
  • Tachypnea
  • Altered mental status
  • Elevated lactate
  • Acute kidney injury
  • Other organ dysfunction
Urosepsis with urinary obstruction is a source-control emergency.

Diagnostic Approach

Step 1: Identify Symptoms

Determine whether the patient has:

  • Dysuria
  • Urgency
  • Frequency
  • Suprapubic pain
  • Flank pain
  • Fever
  • Rigors
  • Pelvic or perineal pain
  • Urinary retention

Step 2: Look for an Alternative Diagnosis

  • Vaginitis
  • Urethritis
  • Sexually transmitted infection
  • Interstitial cystitis
  • Nephrolithiasis
  • Pelvic inflammatory disease
  • Atrophic vaginitis
  • Bladder malignancy
  • Medication-related dysuria

Step 3: Assess Severity

  • Hemodynamics
  • Ability to take oral medication
  • Vomiting
  • Renal function
  • Sepsis
  • Immune status

Step 4: Assess for Source-Control Problems

  • Obstruction
  • Stone
  • Retention
  • Catheter
  • Stent
  • Abscess
  • Emphysematous infection

Step 5: Obtain Appropriate Testing

Syndrome Suggested Testing
Classic uncomplicated cystitis Urinalysis may be sufficient; culture depending on recurrence, risk, or atypical presentation
Pyelonephritis Urinalysis and urine culture
Complicated UTI Urinalysis, culture, renal function, and selective imaging
Sepsis Urine culture, blood cultures, lactate, organ-function evaluation, imaging when indicated
Recurrent UTI Document symptomatic episodes with urinalysis and culture

Urinalysis Interpretation

Leukocyte Esterase

Leukocyte esterase suggests white blood cells in the urine.

Nitrite

Nitrite is produced when certain gram-negative organisms reduce urinary nitrate.

Nitrite May Be Negative With:

  • Enterococcus
  • Staphylococcus saprophyticus
  • Frequent urination with limited bladder incubation time
  • Low dietary nitrate
  • Dilute urine
A negative nitrite test does not exclude UTI.

Pyuria

Pyuria supports inflammation in the urinary tract but is not specific for bacterial infection.

Pyuria May Occur With:

  • Urinary catheter
  • Stone disease
  • Sexually transmitted infection
  • Interstitial nephritis
  • Genitourinary tuberculosis
  • Malignancy
  • Recent urinary procedure

Bacteriuria

Bacteria on microscopy may reflect:

  • True UTI
  • Asymptomatic bacteriuria
  • Contamination

Squamous Epithelial Cells

Numerous squamous epithelial cells suggest a contaminated specimen and reduce confidence in the urine result.

Hematuria

Microscopic or gross hematuria may occur with cystitis but also requires consideration of:

  • Stone
  • Malignancy
  • Glomerular disease
  • Trauma
  • Anticoagulation-associated bleeding
Pyuria is expected in many chronically catheterized patients and does not distinguish catheter-associated UTI from catheter-associated asymptomatic bacteriuria.

Urine Culture

When to Obtain a Culture

  • Pyelonephritis
  • Complicated UTI
  • Pregnancy
  • Male UTI
  • Recurrent UTI
  • Recent resistant organism
  • Treatment failure
  • Catheter-associated symptoms
  • Sepsis
  • Recent urologic procedure

Specimen Collection

  • Use a clean-catch midstream specimen when possible.
  • Avoid collecting from an old catheter drainage bag.
  • For catheterized patients, obtain urine from the sampling port.
  • If replacing a catheter, obtain the culture from the newly placed catheter.
  • Label the collection method.

Colony Count

The clinical importance of a colony count depends on:

  • Symptoms
  • Collection method
  • Organism
  • Prior antibiotics
  • Hydration
  • Catheter status
Symptomatic patients may have clinically meaningful infection with colony counts below the traditional 100,000 CFU/mL threshold.

Mixed Flora

Mixed growth often indicates contamination, particularly in a poorly collected clean-catch sample.

Polymicrobial infection may be real in:

  • Chronic catheters
  • Urinary fistula
  • Obstruction
  • Recent instrumentation

Culture Follow-Up

  • Review results within 48–72 hours.
  • Stop unnecessary coverage.
  • Narrow to the most focused active agent.
  • Confirm that the drug reaches the urinary tissue involved.

Colonization vs Infection

Finding Interpretation Usual Management
Bacteriuria without symptoms Asymptomatic bacteriuria Usually no antibiotics
Pyuria without symptoms Urinary inflammation or colonization Do not treat solely because of pyuria
Cloudy urine May reflect sediment, cells, crystals, or bacteriuria Not diagnostic of UTI
Foul-smelling urine May reflect concentration, diet, medications, or colonization Not diagnostic of UTI
Candida in catheter urine Often colonization Remove catheter/risk factors when possible
Delirium plus bacteriuria without urinary/systemic symptoms Often unrelated ASB Search for another cause
The more often urine cultures are ordered in asymptomatic patients, the more unnecessary antibiotic treatment will occur.

Asymptomatic Bacteriuria

Asymptomatic bacteriuria is the presence of bacteria in urine without signs or symptoms attributable to urinary infection.

Who Should Usually Be Treated?

Pregnancy • Selected Urologic Procedures With Mucosal Trauma

Pregnancy

Screening and treatment are recommended because untreated bacteriuria increases the risk of pyelonephritis and adverse pregnancy outcomes.

Urologic Procedures

Screen and treat bacteriuria before endoscopic urologic procedures associated with mucosal trauma.

Who Should Usually Not Be Treated?

  • Older community-dwelling adults
  • Nursing-home residents
  • Patients with diabetes
  • Patients with chronic indwelling catheters
  • Patients with spinal cord injury
  • Patients undergoing nonurologic surgery
  • Most kidney-transplant recipients more than one month after transplantation
  • Nonrenal solid-organ transplant recipients

Delirium and Falls

In an older adult with bacteriuria but no urinary symptoms or systemic instability:

  • Do not automatically diagnose UTI.
  • Evaluate medications, dehydration, pain, constipation, hypoxia, metabolic disease, and neurologic causes.
  • Observe carefully for emerging localizing signs.
A positive urine culture does not explain delirium merely because no other cause has yet been identified.

Why Not Treat?

  • No demonstrated clinical benefit in most groups
  • Increased antibiotic resistance
  • Drug toxicity
  • C. difficile infection
  • Rapid recurrence of bacteriuria
  • False attribution of symptoms to the urine

Acute Cystitis

Classic Presentation

  • Dysuria
  • Frequency
  • Urgency
  • Suprapubic pain
  • Possible hematuria
  • No prominent fever, rigors, or flank pain

Diagnostic Probability

In a woman with dysuria and urinary frequency without vaginal discharge or irritation, the probability of cystitis is high.

First-Line Oral Options

Agent Typical Regimen Important Considerations
Nitrofurantoin 100 mg orally twice daily for 5 days Not for pyelonephritis or bacteremia
TMP-SMX DS One tablet orally twice daily for 3 days Use when organism is likely susceptible; monitor potassium and renal function
Fosfomycin 3 g orally once Bladder infection only; not for pyelonephritis
Pivmecillinam Use according to local availability and prescribing guidance Option for uncomplicated lower UTI

Alternative Beta-Lactams

  • Amoxicillin-clavulanate
  • Cefpodoxime
  • Cefdinir
  • Cephalexin

Oral beta-lactams are usually administered for a somewhat longer course than TMP-SMX and may have lower efficacy than preferred first-line agents.

Fluoroquinolones

Fluoroquinolones are effective but should generally be reserved for situations in which safer, narrower options are unsuitable.

Phenazopyridine

Phenazopyridine may provide short-term dysuria relief.

Phenazopyridine treats symptoms but does not treat infection. It may turn urine orange or red.

Acute Pyelonephritis

Clinical Features

  • Fever
  • Flank pain
  • Costovertebral-angle tenderness
  • Nausea or vomiting
  • Systemic illness
  • Urinary symptoms may or may not be present

Required Testing

  • Urinalysis
  • Urine culture with susceptibility
  • Pregnancy testing when applicable
  • Renal function
  • Blood cultures in severe illness, sepsis, or high-risk patients

Outpatient Treatment May Be Appropriate When:

  • Hemodynamically stable
  • Able to take oral medication
  • No major obstruction or complication suspected
  • Reliable follow-up
  • Active oral option available

Hospitalization Indications

  • Sepsis or shock
  • Persistent vomiting
  • Inability to take oral medication
  • Pregnancy
  • Severe pain
  • Acute kidney injury
  • Immunocompromise
  • Concern for obstruction or abscess
  • Failure of outpatient therapy

Imaging Is Not Required for Every First Episode

Consider Imaging When:

  • Sepsis or shock
  • Concern for obstruction or stone
  • Acute kidney injury
  • Known urinary abnormality
  • Diabetes with severe infection
  • Immunocompromise
  • Persistent fever or symptoms after 48–72 hours
  • Recurrent pyelonephritis

Preferred Imaging

  • CT abdomen and pelvis with contrast for abscess or complicated infection
  • Noncontrast CT when stone is the primary concern
  • Ultrasound for hydronephrosis, pregnancy, or when radiation/contrast should be avoided
Nitrofurantoin and oral fosfomycin do not achieve adequate renal parenchymal or serum concentrations and should not be used for pyelonephritis.

Complicated Urinary Tract Infection

Complicated UTI requires a structured assessment of illness severity, likely resistance, anatomy, host factors, and source control.

Four-Step Empiric Assessment

Severity • Resistance Risk • Patient Factors • Local Antibiogram

Features Suggesting Complicated Disease

  • Fever or systemic symptoms
  • Pyelonephritis
  • Male sex
  • Catheter or stent
  • Obstruction or retention
  • Stone disease
  • Recent urologic procedure
  • Renal transplant
  • Immunocompromise
  • Sepsis

Initial Evaluation

  • Urinalysis and urine culture
  • Renal function
  • Previous microbiology
  • Blood cultures when systemically ill
  • Assessment for retention or obstruction
  • Imaging when complications are possible

Reasons for Treatment Failure

  • Inactive antimicrobial
  • Incorrect dose
  • Obstruction
  • Abscess
  • Infected stone
  • Prostatitis
  • Retained catheter or stent
  • Incorrect diagnosis
A patient who does not improve promptly requires evaluation for a drug-bug mismatch or unresolved source, not automatic extension of the same therapy.

Bacteremic Urinary Tract Infection

Common Organisms

  • E. coli
  • Klebsiella
  • Proteus
  • Other Enterobacterales
  • Pseudomonas
  • Enterococcus

Management Principles

  • Start prompt effective therapy.
  • Assess for urinary obstruction.
  • Obtain source control.
  • Narrow therapy based on cultures.
  • Transition to oral therapy when stable and an effective option exists.
  • Use a shorter course in uncomplicated improving disease.

Repeat Blood Cultures

Routine repeat blood cultures are generally unnecessary for an improving patient with uncomplicated gram-negative bacteremia from a controlled urinary source.

Repeat Cultures When:

  • Persistent fever or instability
  • Concern for endovascular infection
  • Inadequate source control
  • Retained infected device
  • Relapse
  • S. aureus or Candida isolated

Oral Step-Down

Appropriate in selected patients who are:

  • Afebrile or clearly improving
  • Hemodynamically stable
  • Able to absorb oral medication
  • Source controlled
  • Infected with an organism susceptible to an oral agent that reaches serum and urinary tissue
Nitrofurantoin and fosfomycin are not appropriate step-down agents for bacteremic UTI because they do not achieve therapeutic bloodstream concentrations.

Empiric Antibiotic Selection

Factors to Consider

  • Sepsis or shock
  • Previous culture results
  • Recent antibiotics
  • Local antibiogram
  • Catheter or urinary hardware
  • Obstruction
  • Renal function
  • Drug allergy
  • Pregnancy

Potential Empiric Options for Hospitalized cUTI

Agent Potential Use Important Limitation
Ceftriaxone Stable community-onset pyelonephritis without major resistance risk No Pseudomonas or Enterococcus coverage
Cefepime Pseudomonas risk or healthcare-associated infection Renal neurotoxicity risk
Piperacillin-tazobactam Broad empiric coverage including Pseudomonas and Enterococcus faecalis Not preferred for many serious invasive ESBL infections
Meropenem Severe illness with known or strongly suspected ESBL infection Broad spectrum; reserve when justified
Ertapenem ESBL infection without Pseudomonas concern No Pseudomonas or Enterococcus coverage
Fluoroquinolone Selected susceptible infection with reliable oral or IV exposure Resistance and major adverse effects
Aminoglycoside Selected resistant infection or one-time adjunctive dose Nephrotoxicity and ototoxicity

Sepsis Due to cUTI

Prioritize early active therapy. Appropriate initial classes may include:

  • Third- or fourth-generation cephalosporin
  • Piperacillin-tazobactam
  • Carbapenem
  • Fluoroquinolone in appropriate settings

When to Cover Enterococcus

  • Previous Enterococcus culture
  • Recent urinary procedure
  • Urinary hardware
  • Healthcare-associated infection
  • Severe infection with compatible risk factors

When to Cover MRSA

Routine MRSA coverage is not needed for most UTI.

Consider it with:

  • Known MRSA urinary infection
  • Recent urinary instrumentation with MRSA risk
  • S. aureus bacteremia
  • Suspected hematogenous renal infection

Definitive Culture-Directed Therapy

Once culture and susceptibility results return:

  1. Confirm that the organism explains the syndrome.
  2. Stop unnecessary antibiotics.
  3. Select the narrowest active drug.
  4. Confirm urinary, renal, prostatic, and serum penetration as needed.
  5. Set the final duration.

Do Not Treat the Susceptibility Report in Isolation

An agent reported susceptible may still be inappropriate because:

  • It does not reach renal parenchyma.
  • It does not reach prostate tissue.
  • It does not achieve bloodstream concentrations.
  • The infection contains an undrained source.
  • The patient cannot absorb the oral medication.

Examples

Culture Result Clinical Syndrome Approach
Susceptible E. coli Cystitis Nitrofurantoin may be appropriate
Same organism Pyelonephritis Do not use nitrofurantoin; choose a renal-tissue agent
Same organism Bacteremic pyelonephritis Choose an agent with serum and renal-tissue exposure
Enterococcus Symptomatic lower UTI Ampicillin/amoxicillin may be used if susceptible
ESBL E. coli Septic bacteremic UTI Carbapenem commonly preferred initially

Oral Antibiotic Options

Agent Useful For Not Appropriate For
Nitrofurantoin Lower cystitis Pyelonephritis, prostatitis, bacteremia
Fosfomycin Selected lower cystitis Pyelonephritis or bacteremia

Oral Beta-Lactam Nuance

Oral beta-lactams may be effective for selected complicated or bacteremic urinary infections when:

  • The organism is susceptible.
  • The drug is appropriately dosed.
  • The patient is improving.
  • Source control has occurred.
  • No better high-bioavailability option is required.
Under-dosing oral beta-lactams is a common reason for treatment failure.

Resistant Urinary Organisms

ESBL-Producing Enterobacterales

ESBL enzymes confer resistance to many penicillins and cephalosporins.

Cystitis Options When Susceptible

  • Nitrofurantoin
  • TMP-SMX
  • Fosfomycin for selected E. coli
  • Other susceptibility-guided agents

Pyelonephritis or Complicated Infection

  • TMP-SMX if susceptible
  • Fluoroquinolone if susceptible
  • Carbapenem
  • Selected other active agents based on susceptibility
Nitrofurantoin and oral fosfomycin should not be used for pyelonephritis or bacteremia.

Piperacillin-Tazobactam and ESBL

Piperacillin-tazobactam is not preferred for serious invasive ESBL bloodstream infection. Urinary-source decisions should be individualized, but carbapenems remain dependable agents for severe ESBL disease.

AmpC-Producing Organisms

Higher-Risk Species

  • Enterobacter cloacae complex
  • Klebsiella aerogenes
  • Citrobacter freundii

Third-generation cephalosporins may select resistance during treatment. Cefepime may be appropriate when susceptible.

CRE

Treatment depends on the resistance mechanism and susceptibility.

  • Ceftazidime-avibactam
  • Meropenem-vaborbactam
  • Imipenem-cilastatin-relebactam
  • Cefiderocol
  • Aztreonam-based strategy for metallo-beta-lactamases

Resistant Pseudomonas

  • Ceftolozane-tazobactam
  • Ceftazidime-avibactam
  • Imipenem-cilastatin-relebactam
  • Cefiderocol
Use newer agents when their spectrum is needed, not merely because the patient is hospitalized.

VRE

Determine whether the patient has true infection rather than colonization.

Potential options depend on site and susceptibility:

  • Ampicillin if susceptible
  • Linezolid
  • Daptomycin
  • Nitrofurantoin for selected lower infection
  • Fosfomycin for selected lower infection

Source Control

Examples

  • Relieve ureteral obstruction
  • Drain pyonephrosis
  • Remove or exchange an infected catheter
  • Remove or exchange an infected stent
  • Drain renal or perinephric abscess
  • Address urinary retention
  • Remove infected stone when appropriate
  • Treat prostatic abscess
Antibiotics + Obstructed Infected Kidney Without Drainage = Incomplete Treatment

Signs of Inadequate Source Control

  • Persistent fever
  • Continued hypotension
  • Persistent bacteremia
  • Worsening kidney function
  • Ongoing flank pain
  • Hydronephrosis
  • Relapse with the same organism

Infected Urinary Obstruction

An infected obstructed collecting system is a urologic emergency.

Common Causes

  • Ureteral stone
  • Stricture
  • Tumor
  • Blood clot
  • Prostatic obstruction
  • Blocked urinary device

Clinical Features

  • Fever
  • Flank pain
  • Sepsis
  • Hydronephrosis
  • Acute kidney injury
  • Oliguria or anuria in severe obstruction

Management

  1. Start broad effective antibiotics.
  2. Obtain blood and urine cultures.
  3. Obtain urgent imaging.
  4. Consult urology or interventional radiology.
  5. Decompress with ureteral stent or percutaneous nephrostomy.
  6. Definitively treat the stone or obstruction after stabilization.
Do not delay decompression while waiting for the antibiotic to work.

Renal and Perinephric Abscess

Risk Factors

  • Diabetes
  • Obstruction
  • Stone disease
  • Recurrent UTI
  • S. aureus bacteremia
  • Immunosuppression

When to Suspect

  • Persistent fever after 48–72 hours
  • Persistent flank pain
  • Bacteremia despite active therapy
  • Renal mass or collection on imaging
  • Unexplained relapse

Diagnosis

Contrast-enhanced CT is generally the preferred diagnostic study.

Management

  • Appropriate antibiotics
  • Drainage for larger or poorly responding abscesses
  • Relief of obstruction
  • Longer individualized treatment

Hematogenous Renal Abscess

Renal cortical abscess caused by S. aureus may arise from hematogenous spread. Evaluate for bacteremia and endocarditis.

Emphysematous Urinary Infections

Emphysematous Pyelonephritis

Emphysematous pyelonephritis is a necrotizing renal infection associated with gas formation in the kidney or surrounding tissues.

Risk Factors

  • Diabetes mellitus
  • Urinary obstruction
  • Female sex
  • Impaired immunity

Common Organisms

  • E. coli
  • Klebsiella
  • Proteus

Diagnosis

CT Abdomen and Pelvis

Management

  • Immediate broad-spectrum antibiotics
  • Glycemic control
  • Fluid and sepsis management
  • Urgent drainage
  • Relief of obstruction
  • Nephrectomy in selected severe or refractory cases

Emphysematous Cystitis

Gas within the bladder wall or lumen may occur in diabetic or obstructed patients.

Management

  • Antibiotics
  • Bladder drainage
  • Glycemic control
  • Assessment for obstruction or fistula
Gas in the urinary tract requires differentiation among infection, recent instrumentation, and enterovesical fistula.

Catheter-Associated Urinary Tract Infection

Major Risk Factor

Duration of Catheterization

Compatible Symptoms

  • Fever without another source
  • Rigors
  • New flank pain
  • Pelvic discomfort
  • Acute hematuria
  • Costovertebral-angle tenderness
  • Symptoms after catheter removal such as dysuria or urgency

Findings That Do Not Diagnose CAUTI

  • Pyuria
  • Cloudy urine
  • Foul-smelling urine
  • Bacteriuria alone
  • Positive culture from a drainage bag

Culture Collection

  • Do not culture from the collection bag.
  • Obtain urine from the sampling port.
  • If the catheter has been in place for an extended period and remains necessary, replace it before collecting the culture.
  • Remove the catheter if no longer needed.

Catheter Replacement

If the catheter has been in place for approximately two weeks or longer and is still required, replacement may improve symptom resolution and culture accuracy.

Treatment Duration

  • Approximately 7 days with prompt response
  • Longer, often 10–14 days, with delayed response or complications
  • Shorter selected regimens may be used in carefully chosen patients

Prevention

  • Avoid unnecessary catheter placement.
  • Use aseptic insertion.
  • Maintain a closed drainage system.
  • Keep the bag below bladder level.
  • Assess catheter need daily.
  • Remove promptly.
The best CAUTI prevention strategy is not inserting an unnecessary catheter and removing an existing catheter as soon as possible.

Acute and Chronic Bacterial Prostatitis

Acute Bacterial Prostatitis

Symptoms

  • Fever and chills
  • Dysuria
  • Urinary frequency
  • Pelvic or perineal pain
  • Painful ejaculation
  • Urinary retention

Examination

The prostate may be tender, swollen, or boggy.

Avoid vigorous prostatic massage in acute bacterial prostatitis because it may worsen bacteremia and pain.

Common Organisms

  • E. coli
  • Other Enterobacterales
  • Pseudomonas
  • Enterococcus
  • Sexually transmitted organisms in selected younger patients

Evaluation

  • Urinalysis and urine culture
  • Blood cultures if febrile or systemically ill
  • Assessment for urinary retention
  • Imaging if abscess is suspected

Treatment

  • Fluoroquinolone when susceptible
  • TMP-SMX when susceptible
  • Initial IV beta-lactam for severe infection
  • Individualized duration, commonly several weeks

Prostatic Abscess

Suspect With:

  • Persistent fever
  • Persistent bacteremia
  • Failure to improve
  • Diabetes
  • Immunosuppression
  • S. aureus infection

Diagnosis

  • Transrectal ultrasound
  • CT
  • MRI

Management

  • Antibiotics
  • Urologic drainage when indicated

Chronic Bacterial Prostatitis

  • Recurrent UTI with the same organism
  • Pelvic discomfort
  • Urinary symptoms
  • Requires a prostate-penetrating antimicrobial
  • Often requires 4–6 weeks or longer

Urinary Tract Infection in Men

UTI in men should prompt assessment for:

  • Prostatitis
  • Urinary retention
  • Benign prostatic hyperplasia
  • Stone disease
  • Structural abnormality
  • Recent instrumentation
  • Sexually transmitted infection

Lower UTI Without Fever

A short oral course may be appropriate in selected men with a clearly lower urinary syndrome and no evidence of prostate involvement.

Febrile UTI

Consider occult prostatic involvement, particularly with:

  • Pelvic or perineal pain
  • Urinary retention
  • Recurrent infection
  • Persistent fever
  • Bacteremia
Men with febrile UTI and suspected prostatitis may require longer therapy than a routine seven-day complicated-UTI course.

Urinary Tract Infection in Pregnancy

Why It Matters

  • Increased risk of pyelonephritis
  • Preterm labor
  • Low birth weight
  • Maternal sepsis

Asymptomatic Bacteriuria

  • Screen during pregnancy according to obstetric guidance.
  • Treat confirmed bacteriuria.
  • Use culture-directed pregnancy-compatible therapy.
  • Consider follow-up culture.

Cystitis

Potential options may include:

  • Nitrofurantoin
  • Cephalexin
  • Amoxicillin-clavulanate
  • Fosfomycin
  • Other culture-directed beta-lactams

Pyelonephritis

  • Hospital evaluation is generally appropriate.
  • Begin IV pregnancy-compatible therapy.
  • Monitor for sepsis and preterm labor.
  • Transition to oral therapy after clinical improvement.
  • Complete an obstetric-guided course.

Medication Considerations

  • Avoid fluoroquinolones in pregnancy.
  • Avoid tetracyclines.
  • TMP-SMX requires gestational and folate-related consideration.
  • Nitrofurantoin should not be used for pyelonephritis.
  • Coordinate treatment with obstetrics.

Recurrent Urinary Tract Infection

Definition

≥2 Symptomatic UTIs in 6 Months or ≥3 in 12 Months

Confirm the Diagnosis

  • Document symptoms.
  • Obtain urinalysis and culture during symptomatic episodes.
  • Distinguish recurrence from persistent symptoms without infection.
  • Review previous organisms and susceptibilities.

Assess for Modifiable Factors

  • Spermicide use
  • Sexual association
  • Incomplete bladder emptying
  • Menopausal genitourinary syndrome
  • Stone disease
  • Constipation
  • Diabetes control
  • Urinary devices

When Routine Imaging Is Usually Unnecessary

A healthy woman with recurrent uncomplicated cystitis and typical culture-confirmed episodes generally does not require routine cystoscopy or upper-tract imaging.

When Further Evaluation Is Appropriate

  • Gross hematuria
  • Persistent microscopic hematuria
  • Relapse with the same organism
  • Recurrent pyelonephritis
  • Stone history
  • Obstruction
  • Unusual organism
  • Poor response to therapy
  • Male recurrent UTI

Nonantibiotic Prevention

  • Adequate hydration
  • Avoid spermicide when possible
  • Postmenopausal vaginal estrogen
  • Manage retention or incomplete emptying
  • Address stone disease
  • Selected cranberry products may provide modest benefit
  • Methenamine may be considered in selected patients

Antibiotic Strategies

  • Patient-initiated therapy for selected reliable patients
  • Postcoital prophylaxis
  • Continuous low-dose prophylaxis
Do not prescribe repeated antibiotics for recurrent dysuria without documenting that episodes are true urinary infections.

Candiduria

Risk Factors

  • Urinary catheter
  • Diabetes
  • Broad-spectrum antibiotics
  • ICU admission
  • Urinary instrumentation
  • Urinary obstruction
  • Immunosuppression

Asymptomatic Candiduria

Usually represents colonization.

Management

  • Remove or replace the urinary catheter.
  • Stop unnecessary antibiotics.
  • Improve glycemic control.
  • Address urinary obstruction.
  • Do not routinely give antifungal therapy.

When Asymptomatic Candiduria May Require Treatment

  • Upcoming urologic manipulation
  • Selected severely immunocompromised or neutropenic patients
  • Very-low-birth-weight infants
  • Concern for disseminated candidiasis

Symptomatic Candida Cystitis

Fluconazole is commonly preferred when the organism is susceptible.

Ascending Candida Pyelonephritis

  • Use an active systemic antifungal.
  • Relieve obstruction.
  • Remove or replace urinary devices when feasible.
  • Evaluate for fungus balls.

Candida in Blood

Candidemia is an invasive bloodstream infection and is not managed as simple candiduria.

Special Populations

Kidney Transplant

  • UTI is common after transplantation.
  • Presentation may be atypical.
  • Consider obstruction, stent, rejection, and drug interactions.
  • Asymptomatic bacteriuria more than one month after transplant is generally not treated.
  • Coordinate complicated infection with transplant specialists.

Spinal Cord Injury and Neurogenic Bladder

Typical dysuria may be absent.

Possible Symptoms

  • Fever
  • New incontinence
  • Autonomic dysreflexia
  • Increased spasticity
  • Malaise
  • Bladder discomfort

Do not treat bacteriuria without a compatible change in clinical status.

Diabetes

  • Do not treat asymptomatic bacteriuria solely because of diabetes.
  • Consider emphysematous infection in severe illness.
  • Glycemic control is important.

Older Adults

  • ASB is common.
  • Classic urinary symptoms may be less prominent.
  • Delirium alone does not establish UTI.
  • Search for alternative causes of nonspecific decline.

Immunocompromised Patients

  • Broader organism differential
  • Lower inflammatory response
  • Greater risk of fungal infection and abscess
  • Need for individualized duration and source-control planning

Treatment Duration

Duration is counted from the first day of effective antimicrobial therapy.

Syndrome Typical Duration Framework Important Qualification
Uncomplicated cystitis Agent specific, usually 1–5 days Depends on drug
Improving cUTI or pyelonephritis 5–7 days with fluoroquinolone or approximately 7 days with effective non-fluoroquinolone Trials excluded many highly complicated patients
Gram-negative bacteremic cUTI Approximately 7 days Requires improvement and source control
CAUTI Approximately 7 days with prompt response Longer with delayed response or complication
Acute bacterial prostatitis Often several weeks Individualize by response and abscess risk
Renal or perinephric abscess Prolonged and individualized Depends on drainage and response
Emphysematous pyelonephritis Individualized prolonged therapy Requires drainage/source control

Patients Underrepresented in Short-Course Trials

  • Septic shock
  • Indwelling catheter
  • Complete urinary obstruction
  • Urinary abscess
  • Severe immune compromise
  • Severe kidney dysfunction
  • Bacterial prostatitis
  • Major structural abnormality
These patients may need an individualized course rather than automatic application of a seven-day regimen.

Do Not Extend Solely Because:

  • Pyuria persists
  • The urine remains cloudy
  • A follow-up culture remains positive without symptoms
  • The patient has a chronic catheter

IV-to-Oral Transition

Eligibility

  • Clinical improvement
  • Hemodynamic stability
  • Ability to take oral medication
  • Functional GI absorption
  • Active oral option
  • Source control achieved

For Bacteremic UTI

The oral drug must achieve therapeutic levels in:

  • Bloodstream
  • Urine
  • Renal or prostatic tissue involved

Common Effective Step-Down Agents When Susceptible

  • TMP-SMX
  • Ciprofloxacin
  • Levofloxacin
  • Selected high-dose oral beta-lactams

Do Not Use for Bacteremic Step-Down

  • Nitrofurantoin
  • Oral fosfomycin
  • Agents with inadequate systemic exposure
An IV antibiotic is not automatically superior to an active, appropriately dosed oral agent in a stable patient with source control.

Prevention

Catheter Prevention

  • Insert only for valid indications.
  • Use aseptic technique.
  • Maintain a closed system.
  • Avoid dependent loops.
  • Keep drainage bag below bladder.
  • Perform daily necessity review.
  • Remove promptly.

Recurrent UTI Prevention

  • Hydration
  • Avoid spermicide
  • Vaginal estrogen after menopause
  • Address retention
  • Treat stones and obstruction
  • Selected prophylactic strategies

Practices That Are Generally Not Recommended

  • Routine screening cultures in asymptomatic patients
  • Routine antimicrobial irrigation of catheters
  • Routine catheter changes at arbitrary intervals without indication
  • Antibiotics solely because a catheter is present
  • Routine treatment of catheter-associated bacteriuria

Daily Inpatient UTI Checklist

  • Does the patient truly have a symptomatic urinary infection?
  • Is this cystitis, pyelonephritis, prostatitis, or urosepsis?
  • What organism is growing?
  • Were urine cultures collected correctly?
  • Are blood cultures positive?
  • Is there urinary obstruction?
  • Is there a stone, abscess, retention, catheter, or stent?
  • Has source control occurred?
  • Does the catheter remain necessary?
  • Can empiric therapy be narrowed?
  • Does the selected drug penetrate the infected site?
  • Can therapy transition to oral?
  • What is the first day of effective treatment?
  • What is the planned stop date?
  • Is follow-up imaging or urologic evaluation required?

How to Present UTI on ID Rounds

  • Urinary syndrome
  • Symptoms and onset
  • Catheter or urinary hardware
  • Previous cultures
  • Current urinalysis and urine culture
  • Blood-culture result
  • Imaging and source-control status
  • Current antibiotic, dose, and start date
  • Renal function
  • Oral step-down option
  • Planned duration
Avoid saying only, “The urine is positive.” State what symptoms support UTI, how the urine was collected, what organism is present, and whether obstruction or another nidus exists.

Common Clinical Scenarios

Case 1: Delirium and Positive Urine Culture

An older nursing-home resident has delirium and a urine culture growing E. coli, but no fever, urinary symptoms, hypotension, or leukocytosis.

Do not automatically treat. This is likely asymptomatic bacteriuria. Evaluate other causes of delirium.

Case 2: Pyelonephritis and Obstructing Stone

A patient has fever, hypotension, hydronephrosis, and an obstructing ureteral stone.

Begin antibiotics and arrange urgent decompression. Antibiotics alone are inadequate.

Case 3: ESBL Bacteremic Pyelonephritis

Blood and urine cultures grow ESBL-producing E. coli in a patient with sepsis.

Use a carbapenem initially. After stabilization and source control, consider oral TMP-SMX or fluoroquinolone step-down if susceptible.

Case 4: Nitrofurantoin for Flank Pain

A patient with fever and costovertebral-angle tenderness is prescribed nitrofurantoin.

Nitrofurantoin does not achieve adequate renal parenchymal concentrations and should not be used for pyelonephritis.

Case 5: Chronic Catheter and Cloudy Urine

A chronically catheterized patient has cloudy urine but no fever, pain, or systemic symptoms.

Do not culture or treat solely because the urine is cloudy.

Case 6: CAUTI With an Old Catheter

A symptomatic patient has a catheter that has been in place for three weeks.

Remove the catheter if unnecessary. If still needed, replace it and obtain the culture from the new catheter.

Case 7: Proteus and Recurrent Infection

A patient has recurrent Proteus mirabilis UTI and alkaline urine.

Evaluate for a struvite stone or persistent urinary nidus.

Case 8: Staphylococcus aureus in Urine

A patient without urinary instrumentation has S. aureus in urine.

Consider hematogenous seeding. Obtain blood cultures and evaluate for invasive S. aureus infection when clinically appropriate.

Case 9: Persistent Fever on Appropriate Therapy

A diabetic patient remains febrile after 72 hours of active treatment for pyelonephritis.

Obtain imaging to evaluate obstruction, renal abscess, perinephric abscess, or emphysematous infection.

Case 10: Candida in Catheter Urine

An asymptomatic ICU patient with a Foley catheter has Candida in urine.

Remove or replace the catheter and address risk factors. Antifungal therapy is usually unnecessary.

Common Pitfalls

  • Diagnosing UTI from pyuria alone
  • Treating cloudy or foul-smelling urine
  • Treating asymptomatic bacteriuria in older adults
  • Attributing delirium to bacteriuria without urinary or systemic findings
  • Obtaining urine cultures from catheter drainage bags
  • Failing to replace an old catheter during CAUTI treatment
  • Using nitrofurantoin for pyelonephritis
  • Using fosfomycin for bacteremic UTI
  • Using nitrofurantoin for prostatitis
  • Failing to evaluate urinary obstruction
  • Delaying drainage of an infected obstructed kidney
  • Missing renal or perinephric abscess
  • Missing emphysematous pyelonephritis in a diabetic patient
  • Continuing broad IV therapy despite an active oral option
  • Automatically treating Candida in urine
  • Ignoring S. aureus bacteriuria without instrumentation
  • Repeating urine cultures as a routine test of cure
  • Extending antibiotics because pyuria persists
  • Failing to consider prostatitis in febrile male UTI
  • Failing to use previous urine cultures to guide empiric treatment

Board and ID Consult Pearls

  • A symptomatic syndrome is required to diagnose UTI.
  • Pyuria does not distinguish infection from colonization in catheterized patients.
  • Nitrite may be negative with Enterococcus or S. saprophyticus.
  • Treat asymptomatic bacteriuria primarily in pregnancy and before selected urologic procedures with mucosal trauma.
  • Do not treat ASB in most older adults, diabetics, nursing-home residents, or chronic-catheter patients.
  • Nitrofurantoin is first-line for uncomplicated cystitis but not pyelonephritis.
  • Fosfomycin is a bladder drug and should not treat renal or bloodstream infection.
  • Previous urine cultures are highly valuable for empiric selection.
  • Seven days is sufficient for many improving complicated and bacteremic UTIs with source control.
  • Short-course evidence is less certain in shock, obstruction, abscess, catheters, prostatitis, and major immune compromise.
  • An infected obstructed collecting system requires urgent decompression.
  • Proteus is associated with urease production and struvite stones.
  • S. aureus bacteriuria may indicate hematogenous infection.
  • Cephalosporins do not reliably treat Enterococcus.
  • ESBL bacteremic infection is commonly treated initially with a carbapenem.
  • Ertapenem does not cover Pseudomonas.
  • Routine repeat blood cultures are unnecessary for improving uncomplicated gram-negative bacteremic UTI.
  • Replace or remove a chronic catheter when treating CAUTI.
  • Cloudy urine and odor are not diagnostic criteria.
  • Acute bacterial prostatitis should not be evaluated with vigorous prostatic massage.
  • Persistent fever should trigger evaluation for obstruction, abscess, or incorrect therapy.
  • Asymptomatic candiduria usually does not require antifungal treatment.
  • Recurrent uncomplicated cystitis does not automatically require cystoscopy or CT.
  • Vaginal estrogen reduces recurrent UTI risk in appropriate postmenopausal patients.

Bottom Line

Urinary tract infection is a clinical diagnosis supported by urine testing. Do not treat pyuria, bacteriuria, cloudy urine, odor, or a positive culture without a compatible syndrome. Asymptomatic bacteriuria should generally be treated only during pregnancy and before selected urologic procedures involving mucosal trauma.

For cystitis, choose a focused short oral regimen. For pyelonephritis, complicated infection, or bacteremia, obtain a culture, assess severity, review previous resistance, and determine whether obstruction, stone, catheter, prostatitis, abscess, or another nidus requires source control.

Many clinically improving complicated and gram-negative bacteremic UTIs can be treated for approximately seven days and transitioned from IV to active oral therapy. Nitrofurantoin and fosfomycin are limited to lower urinary infection and should not be used for pyelonephritis or bacteremia.

An infected obstructed urinary tract requires urgent decompression. Persistent fever or bacteremia despite active therapy should prompt imaging and reassessment rather than automatic extension or broadening of antibiotics.

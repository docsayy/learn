Pneumothorax and Chest Tubes

Added July 15, 2026 • Pulmonary and Critical Care • ~30 min read

A practical guide to tension, primary and secondary spontaneous, traumatic, and iatrogenic pneumothorax, conservative and ambulatory management, needle or finger decompression, chest-tube selection, suction, persistent air leak, and troubleshooting.

Clinical Overview

Management depends on physiologic compromise, symptoms, size, underlying lung disease, mechanism, and access to follow-up. Tension pneumothorax is a clinical emergency; do not wait for imaging.

Tension Pneumothorax

  • Shock or peri-arrest state
  • Severe respiratory distress
  • Unilateral absent breath sounds
  • Rapidly rising airway pressure
  • Distended neck veins may be absent
  • POCUS may support diagnosis but must not delay decompression
Immediate Decompression → Definitive Pleural Drain

Conservative and Ambulatory Care

  • Selected minimally symptomatic stable primary spontaneous pneumothorax can be managed conservatively.
  • Ambulatory one-way-valve pathways require trained services and reliable follow-up.
  • Secondary pneumothorax, hypoxemia, bilateral disease, or significant symptoms usually requires more active management.

Chest-Tube Selection

  • Small-bore drains are effective for many spontaneous pneumothoraces and pleural infections.
  • Larger drains may be needed for major trauma, viscous blood, or large air leak according to local protocol.
  • Use ultrasound for fluid but remember that ultrasound cannot identify a safe route through aerated lung for every pneumothorax procedure.

Drainage-System Basics

  • Keep system below chest level.
  • Assess swinging, bubbling, and tube patency.
  • Continuous bubbling can indicate air leak or a loose connection.
  • Do not routinely clamp a bubbling tube.
  • Check every connection before assuming a bronchopleural fistula.

Suction

Routine immediate suction is not necessary for every spontaneous pneumothorax. Consider low-pressure suction when the lung fails to re-expand or an air leak persists, while recognizing that excessive suction can worsen flow through a fistula.

Persistent Air Leak

  • Confirm tube position and system integrity.
  • Assess for bronchopleural fistula or trapped lung.
  • Reduce injurious ventilator pressures.
  • Involve thoracic surgery or interventional pulmonology early.
  • Options include surgery, blood patch, chemical pleurodesis, or endobronchial valves in selected patients.

Removal

  • Confirm clinical and radiographic resolution.
  • Ensure air leak has stopped.
  • Follow local practice for water seal or clamping trials.
  • Remove during controlled breathing maneuver and apply an occlusive dressing.
  • Monitor for recurrence.

Recurrence, Flying, and Diving

  • Smoking cessation reduces recurrence.
  • Discuss pleurodesis or surgery after recurrent disease and selected high-risk first episodes.
  • Avoid air travel until complete radiographic resolution and guideline-recommended waiting period.
  • Scuba diving is generally contraindicated without definitive bilateral preventive strategy and specialist clearance.

Common Pitfalls

  • Waiting for x-ray in tension physiology
  • Clamping a tube with ongoing air leak
  • Placing a drain through the diaphragm or organ
  • Using suction automatically
  • Missing secondary pneumothorax in COPD
  • Failing to check tubing connections

Bottom Line

Treat physiology first. Stable primary spontaneous pneumothorax may allow conservative pathways, but secondary disease and positive-pressure ventilation demand caution. Every chest tube requires daily assessment of indication, position, patency, leak, and removal plan.

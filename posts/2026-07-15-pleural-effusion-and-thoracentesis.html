Pleural Effusion and Thoracentesis

Clinical Overview

Pleural effusion is a manifestation, not a diagnosis. The decision to sample depends on clinical context, size, laterality, suspected cause, and whether the result will change management.

When to Perform Thoracentesis

  • New unilateral effusion without a clear benign explanation
  • Suspected infection or empyema
  • Possible malignancy
  • Large symptomatic effusion
  • Atypical bilateral effusions
  • Failure to improve with expected therapy

Small bilateral effusions in straightforward decompensated heart failure may be observed during treatment if no atypical features exist.

Ultrasound Before the Procedure

  • Confirm fluid and estimate depth.
  • Identify diaphragm, lung, spleen, and liver.
  • Assess loculations and complexity.
  • Choose a safe window.
  • Mark immediately before or use real-time guidance.
Ultrasound guidance reduces dry taps and pneumothorax and is standard practice for pleural procedures.

Light Criteria

Exudate if any criterion is met: pleural/serum protein >0.5; pleural/serum LDH >0.6; pleural LDH >2/3 upper limit of normal serum LDH

Diuresed heart failure can be misclassified as an exudate. Consider serum–pleural albumin gradient or NT-proBNP when the clinical picture suggests a pseudoexudate.

Core Pleural Fluid Tests

  • Cell count and differential
  • Protein and LDH with simultaneous serum values
  • Glucose and pH when infection is possible
  • Gram stain and culture; inoculate culture bottles at bedside when appropriate
  • Cytology when malignancy is possible
  • Additional tests guided by triglycerides, amylase, ADA, flow cytometry, or hematocrit

Pleural Infection

  • Frank pus requires drainage.
  • Positive culture or Gram stain usually requires drainage.
  • Low pleural pH, low glucose, loculation, and clinical sepsis support complicated infection.
  • Use antibiotics plus timely chest-tube drainage.
  • Consider intrapleural therapy or surgery when drainage is inadequate.

Malignant Effusion

  • Cytology sensitivity varies by tumor type.
  • Repeat sampling may help selectively, but tissue diagnosis is often needed after nondiagnostic cytology.
  • Symptom control options include repeated drainage, indwelling pleural catheter, or pleurodesis.
  • Trapped or nonexpandable lung favors an indwelling catheter.

Chylothorax and Hemothorax

  • Chylothorax: pleural triglyceride elevation or chylomicrons; causes include malignancy and thoracic-duct injury.
  • Hemothorax: pleural hematocrit roughly at least 50% of peripheral blood, although dilution can lower the ratio.
  • Both require cause-directed intervention and often specialist drainage planning.

Thoracentesis Technique and Volume

  • Position and monitor the patient.
  • Use sterile technique and local anesthesia.
  • Enter above the rib while avoiding the neurovascular bundle.
  • Stop for chest pain, persistent cough, respiratory distress, or excessive negative pressure.
  • Routine fixed volume limits are less useful than symptoms, pleural pressure, and clinical context.

Complications

  • Pneumothorax
  • Bleeding
  • Re-expansion pulmonary edema
  • Organ puncture
  • Infection
  • Vasovagal symptoms
  • Persistent air leak

Post-procedure chest radiography is not routinely required after uncomplicated ultrasound-guided thoracentesis in an asymptomatic patient, but obtain imaging when symptoms, difficult procedure, or air aspiration occurs.

Common Pitfalls

  • Calling every diuresed effusion an exudate
  • Sending pH in an inappropriate container or delayed sample
  • Failing to drain frank empyema
  • Repeated blind procedures
  • Ignoring nonexpandable lung
  • Assuming negative cytology excludes malignancy

Bottom Line

Use ultrasound, sample when results matter, interpret fluid in clinical context, and prioritize timely drainage for infected pleural space. Light criteria classify fluid but do not establish the diagnosis.

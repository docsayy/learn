Pressure Ulcers and Sacral Wounds: Practical Inpatient Management

Added June 20, 2026 • Hospital Medicine / Wound Care • ~14 min read

Chronic pressure ulcers, sacral decubitus wounds, and associated chronic osteomyelitis are complex problems that require coordinated care. This guide reviews wound staging, nutrition, pressure offloading, wound care, debridement, infection evaluation, antibiotic decisions, surgical consultation, and discharge planning.

Overview

Chronic pressure ulcers and sacral decubitus wounds are among the most complex inpatient wound problems. Patients often have limited mobility, spinal cord injury, malnutrition, poor social support, recurrent hospitalizations, chronic contamination, and sometimes chronic osteomyelitis.

The hospitalist role is often coordination: wound care, nutrition, pressure offloading, infection evaluation, surgical consultation, discharge planning, and long-term follow-up.

Core principle: Chronic pressure ulcer care is not just antibiotics. Healing requires pressure relief, nutrition, debridement when needed, wound care, and planning for possible future closure.

Pressure Ulcer Staging

Stage Description
Stage 1 Non-blanchable erythema of intact skin
Stage 2 Partial-thickness skin loss with exposed dermis
Stage 3 Full-thickness skin loss; fat may be visible
Stage 4 Full-thickness skin and tissue loss with exposed muscle, tendon, ligament, cartilage, or bone
Unstageable Full-thickness tissue loss obscured by slough or eschar
Large sacral wounds admitted to the hospital are often stage 3 or stage 4 and require multidisciplinary care.

Optimize for Healing

The first inpatient goal is to optimize the patient for eventual healing and possible future surgical closure.

Consult Early

  • Wound care nurse
  • Physical therapy
  • Occupational therapy
  • Dietitian
  • Case management
  • Plastic surgery for stage 4 wounds or flap planning

Pressure Offloading

  • Pressure mapping when available
  • Low-air-loss mattress
  • Wheelchair cushion such as ROHO cushion
  • Frequent repositioning, often every 2 hours
  • Durable medical equipment planning before discharge
Practical point: PT and OT can help assess mobility, wheelchair fit, pressure mapping, and durable medical equipment needs.

Wound Care and Debridement

Wound care consultation is essential for dressing recommendations, staging, pressure offloading plans, and debridement assessment.

Debridement

Necrotic tissue should be removed when appropriate because it impairs healing and can contribute to infection risk.

Situation Approach
Small amount of necrotic tissue May be managed at bedside by wound care or trained clinicians
Extensive necrotic tissue May require surgical debridement in the operating room
Severe pain or deep tissue involvement Consider operative debridement with anesthesia support
Important: If the patient goes to the OR for debridement, ask surgery to obtain deep tissue cultures when infection is suspected.

Nutrition

Nutrition is one of the most important determinants of wound healing.

Nutrition Goals Often Used

Marker Common Goal
Prealbumin >15 mg/dL
Albumin >3 g/dL

Nutrition Interventions

  • Dietitian consultation
  • High-protein diet
  • Oral protein supplements such as Boost or Ensure
  • Consider vitamin C supplementation
  • Consider zinc supplementation
  • Assess need for enteral feeding if oral intake is inadequate
Evidence nuance: Vitamin C and zinc are commonly used to support wound healing, although the evidence is limited. They are often low-risk when used appropriately.
Patients who cannot meet nutrition goals orally may need discussion of NG tube or PEG tube feeding depending on goals of care and expected recovery.

Assessing Infection

Pressure ulcers often look severe, but chronic wounds are not always the source of sepsis. In bedbound or spinal cord injury patients, more common sources such as urinary tract infection or pneumonia must also be evaluated.

Initial Infection Assessment

  • Vital signs
  • Blood cultures if septic
  • Urinalysis and urine culture when appropriate
  • Chest imaging if respiratory symptoms or hypoxia
  • Wound exam for surrounding cellulitis
  • ESR and CRP if concern for osteomyelitis or necrotizing infection
Do not assume the sacral wound is the source of sepsis without evaluating more common causes such as UTI and pneumonia.

Skin and Soft Tissue Infection

Antibiotics are indicated when there is acute surrounding skin and soft tissue infection, not simply because a chronic wound is colonized.

Findings Suggesting Acute Infection

  • Expanding erythema around the wound
  • Warmth
  • Tenderness
  • Purulence with systemic signs
  • Fever or leukocytosis with no better source
  • Rapid wound worsening

Empiric Antibiotics for Infected Pressure Ulcer

Coverage Needed Example Regimen
MRSA Vancomycin
Gram negatives including Pseudomonas Cefepime
Anaerobes Metronidazole
Chronic pressure ulcers can be polymicrobial, especially in patients with frequent healthcare exposure.

Necrotizing Skin and Soft Tissue Infection

If necrotizing infection is suspected, urgent surgical evaluation is required.

Concerning Features

  • Pain out of proportion
  • Rapid progression
  • Crepitus
  • Bullae
  • Skin necrosis
  • Shock or severe toxicity
  • Markedly elevated inflammatory markers

Typical Empiric Antibiotics

Medication Role
Vancomycin MRSA coverage
Piperacillin-tazobactam Broad gram-negative and anaerobic coverage
Clindamycin Toxin suppression
Do not delay surgery: Necrotizing infection is primarily a surgical disease. Antibiotics alone are not enough.

Osteomyelitis

Chronic osteomyelitis is common under long-standing stage 4 pressure ulcers. Management depends on whether there is acute infection, whether closure is planned, and whether source control is possible.

When Acute Osteomyelitis Is Suspected

  • Obtain MRI when appropriate
  • Consult infectious disease
  • Consider IR-guided bone biopsy for culture
  • Avoid superficial swab cultures for definitive guidance
Bone biopsy is preferred over superficial wound swab when culture data are needed to guide osteomyelitis treatment.

Chronic Osteomyelitis With Open Wound

Long-standing chronic osteomyelitis under an open pressure ulcer is often not treated with prolonged antibiotics unless there is a plan for wound closure or there is acute soft tissue infection.

Situation Antibiotic Approach
Open chronic wound with chronic osteomyelitis only Antibiotics often not indicated
Acute cellulitis or soft tissue infection Short course, often 5–14 days depending on severity
Debrided wound with good healing response Consider longer course with ID guidance
Flap closure achieved Often 6 weeks of antibiotics
Common pitfall: Six weeks of antibiotics rarely cures chronic osteomyelitis if the wound remains open and pressure/offloading problems are not corrected.

Fecal and Urinary Diversion

Repeated contamination can prevent wound healing. Diversion may be needed in selected patients.

Fecal Diversion

Consider colorectal surgery consultation when:

  • Wound is within approximately 6 cm of the anus
  • Wound is frequently contaminated by stool
  • Patient has fecal incontinence
  • Future flap closure is being considered

Urinary Diversion

Consider urology consultation when:

  • Urinary incontinence repeatedly contaminates the wound
  • Neurogenic bladder is present
  • Suprapubic catheter may improve wound care
  • Long-term diversion is needed for healing

Plastic Surgery and Flap Closure

Stage 4 pressure ulcers often require plastic surgery evaluation, but flap closure usually requires months of optimization first.

Before Flap Closure

  • Nutrition must improve
  • Pressure offloading plan must be reliable
  • Infection must be controlled
  • Necrotic tissue must be debrided
  • Home support and equipment must be arranged
  • Fecal or urinary contamination must be addressed
Most chronic wounds are not ready for acute inpatient flap closure. The inpatient goal is usually to start optimization and arrange follow-up.

When Acute Surgery May Be Possible

  • New pressure ulcer
  • Rapidly reversible cause
  • Good nutritional status
  • Reliable offloading plan
  • Minimal contamination
  • Plastic surgery feels closure is feasible

Discharge Planning

Discharge planning is one of the most important parts of pressure ulcer care.

Discharge Checklist

  • Wound care clinic referral
  • Home health nursing
  • Home health PT and OT
  • Clear wound care instructions
  • Low-air-loss mattress
  • Wheelchair cushion such as ROHO cushion
  • Turning schedule
  • Nutrition plan and protein supplements
  • Plastic surgery follow-up when appropriate
  • Infectious disease follow-up if antibiotics or osteomyelitis plan needed
Wound care clinic follow-up is often more useful than plastic surgery clinic alone because wound clinics have specialized equipment, staff, and longitudinal wound care expertise.

Initial Dressing While Awaiting Wound Care

A common temporary approach is wet-to-dry dressing changes until the wound care team provides specific recommendations.

Wet-to-dry dressings can help with mechanical debridement, but wound care recommendations should guide the final dressing plan.

Clinical Pearls

  1. Pressure ulcer care is multidisciplinary.
  2. Stage 3 and 4 ulcers require aggressive coordination, not just antibiotics.
  3. Consult wound care early.
  4. Consult PT and OT for mobility, offloading, pressure mapping, and DME needs.
  5. Nutrition is central to wound healing.
  6. Common nutrition goals include prealbumin >15 and albumin >3.
  7. Consider high-protein supplements early.
  8. Necrotic tissue usually needs debridement.
  9. Do not assume a chronic wound is the source of sepsis.
  10. Evaluate common sources such as UTI and pneumonia.
  11. Antibiotics are indicated for acute cellulitis, necrotizing infection, or selected osteomyelitis situations.
  12. Infected pressure ulcers are often polymicrobial.
  13. Ask for deep tissue cultures during operative debridement.
  14. Bone biopsy is preferred when diagnosing osteomyelitis microbiologically.
  15. Chronic osteomyelitis under an open wound often does not improve with antibiotics alone.
  16. Flap closure is often the turning point for prolonged antibiotic treatment.
  17. Consider fecal diversion if stool repeatedly contaminates the wound.
  18. Consider urinary diversion if urine repeatedly contaminates the wound.
  19. Stage 4 ulcers should often have plastic surgery evaluation before discharge.
  20. Discharge planning should include wound clinic, home health, DME, and offloading instructions.

Bottom Line

Chronic pressure ulcers and sacral wounds are rarely solved by one intervention. The hospitalist role is to coordinate pressure offloading, wound care, debridement, nutrition, infection evaluation, surgical planning, and discharge support. Treat acute cellulitis or necrotizing infection promptly, but do not reflexively give prolonged antibiotics for chronic osteomyelitis under an open wound unless source control and closure are planned. The long-term goal is wound optimization, reliable offloading, improved nutrition, contamination control, and eventual flap closure when appropriate.

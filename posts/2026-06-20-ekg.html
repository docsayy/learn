EKG Interpretation: A Systematic Approach for Medical Students and Residents

Added June 20, 2026 • Cardiology • ~20 min read

A practical approach to reading EKGs using the same sequence every time: rate, rhythm, axis, intervals, and morphology. This guide reviews the core interpretation framework plus high-yield findings such as STEMI, pericarditis, hyperkalemia, hypokalemia, atrial fibrillation, flutter, AV blocks, bundle branch blocks, LVH, WPW, Brugada, Wellens, and more.

Overview

Every EKG should be interpreted using the same systematic approach. Even when the tracing looks normal, going step by step prevents missed findings and demonstrates a reliable clinical method.

EKG Approach = Rate → Rhythm → Axis → Intervals → Morphology
Core principle: Do not jump straight to the most obvious abnormality. Start with rate and move through the same sequence every time.

Rate

There are three practical ways to estimate heart rate.

Method How to Use It Best For
Machine read Look at the printed rate on the EKG Quick estimate
300 method 300 ÷ number of large boxes between R waves Regular rhythms
10-second rhythm strip Count QRS complexes and multiply by 6 Irregular rhythms
Regular rhythm: Rate = 300 ÷ large boxes between R waves
Irregular rhythm: Rate = number of QRS complexes in 10 seconds × 6

Rhythm

After calculating rate, determine whether the rhythm is regular and whether it is sinus.

Regular vs Irregular

  • Regular rhythm: QRS complexes occur at predictable intervals.
  • Irregular rhythm: QRS complexes occur at variable intervals.

Sinus Rhythm Criteria

  • Upright P wave in lead II
  • P wave before every QRS
  • QRS after every P wave
  • Consistent PR interval
If there is an upright P wave in lead II before every QRS and a QRS after every P wave, the rhythm is likely sinus.

Axis

Axis describes the overall direction of ventricular depolarization.

Quadrant Method

The simplest bedside method uses lead I and lead II.

Lead I Lead II Axis
Positive Positive Normal axis
Positive Negative Left axis deviation
Negative Positive Right axis deviation
Negative Negative Extreme axis deviation
Thumb method: Lead I is your left thumb and lead II is your right thumb. If both are up, axis is normal. If only one thumb points up, the axis points toward that side.

Why Axis Matters

  • Right axis deviation can suggest right ventricular hypertrophy or right heart strain.
  • Left axis deviation can suggest left ventricular hypertrophy or conduction disease.
  • Axis helps identify fascicular blocks and other conduction abnormalities.

Intervals

The three major intervals to assess are PR interval, QRS duration, and QT interval.

Interval Normal Value Clinical Meaning
PR interval 120–200 ms, or 3–5 small boxes AV nodal conduction
QRS duration <120 ms, or <3 small boxes Ventricular conduction
QT interval <50% of R-R interval by eyeball method Ventricular repolarization

QT Interval Pearl

QT interval shortens with tachycardia and lengthens with bradycardia. Bradycardic patients with prolonged QT are at increased risk for torsades de pointes.

The computer often reports QTc using Bazett’s formula, but visually checking whether QT is less than half the R-R interval is a useful quick screen.

QRS Morphology

QRS width helps determine whether the rhythm originates above or within the ventricles.

QRS Type Meaning
Narrow QRS Usually supraventricular origin with normal conduction
Wide QRS Ventricular origin or aberrant conduction such as bundle branch block

QRS Naming

  • First negative deflection = Q wave
  • First positive deflection = R wave
  • Negative deflection after R wave = S wave
  • Second R wave = R prime
  • Larger waves are capitalized; smaller waves are lowercase

Hypertrophy and Chamber Enlargement

Right Atrial Enlargement

P wave in lead II >2.5 mm = Right atrial enlargement

Right Ventricular Hypertrophy

  • Right axis deviation
  • R wave greater than S wave in V1

Left Atrial Enlargement

  • Broad, bifid, M-shaped P wave in lead II
  • Deep or prolonged negative terminal P wave in V1

Left Ventricular Hypertrophy

Criteria Positive Finding
aVL criterion R wave in aVL >11 mm
Sokolow-Lyon S in V1/V2 + R in V5/V6 >35 mm
Cornell S in V3 + R in aVL >28 mm in men or >20 mm in women
LVH can cause a strain pattern with ST depressions and asymmetric T wave inversions, which must be distinguished from ischemic T wave inversions.

Coronary Territories

Knowing which leads correspond to which cardiac region helps localize ischemia.

Territory Leads
Lateral I, aVL, V5, V6
Inferior II, III, aVF
Septal V1, V2
Anterior V3, V4
Mnemonic: “Big lie, little lie, ass backwards, all.” Leads with L are lateral: I, aVL, V5, V6.

Ischemia and Infarction

Pathologic Q Waves

Q waves suggest prior myocardial infarction when they are large enough or appear in abnormal locations.

  • Pathologic Q wave is often at least one-third the height of the R wave.
  • Q waves in V2–V4 are usually abnormal.
  • Small isolated Q waves in lead III can be normal.
Classic saying: Q wave in lead III, let it be — if isolated and without other ischemic findings.

T Wave Inversions

T wave inversions can suggest ischemia, especially when new and present in contiguous leads.

  • Normal T wave inversion: aVR and sometimes V1
  • New T wave inversions elsewhere are abnormal
  • Always compare with prior EKG

STEMI Criteria

Lead Group ST Elevation Threshold
All leads except V2–V3 ≥1 mm in contiguous leads
V2–V3 in women ≥1.5 mm
V2–V3 in men ≥40 years ≥2 mm
V2–V3 in men <40 years ≥2.5 mm

ST Elevation in aVR

ST elevation in aVR can suggest left main coronary artery occlusion or severe multivessel ischemia.

Pericarditis

Acute pericarditis classically causes diffuse ST elevation and PR depression.

EKG Findings

  • Diffuse ST elevations
  • PR depressions
  • J-point elevation
  • Usually no focal reciprocal changes except aVR/V1 patterns
Diffuse ST elevations plus PR depressions should raise suspicion for acute pericarditis in the right clinical context.

Pulmonary Embolism

Classic Pattern

S1Q3T3 = S wave in lead I + Q wave in lead III + T wave inversion in lead III

S1Q3T3 suggests acute right heart strain but is neither sensitive nor specific for pulmonary embolism.

Most common EKG finding in PE: Sinus tachycardia.

Electrolyte Abnormalities

Hyperkalemia

  • Peaked T waves
  • Progressive QRS widening
  • Sine wave pattern in severe cases
Peaked T waves with widening QRS should be treated as dangerous hyperkalemia until proven otherwise.

Hypokalemia

  • Flattened T waves
  • ST depression
  • Prominent U waves
  • “Camelback” appearance after the T wave
Hyperkalemia = peaked T waves
Hypokalemia = U waves

Atrial Fibrillation and Flutter

Atrial Fibrillation

  • Irregularly irregular rhythm
  • No clear P waves
  • Fibrillatory baseline, often best seen in V1
Atrial fibrillation with heart rate above 100 bpm is often called atrial fibrillation with rapid ventricular response.

Atrial Flutter

  • Sawtooth flutter waves
  • Often regular if fixed conduction
  • Can be irregular with variable AV block
Rhythm Key Finding
Atrial fibrillation Irregularly irregular, no clear P waves
Atrial flutter Sawtooth flutter waves

SVT Patterns

Multifocal Atrial Tachycardia

  • Irregularly irregular rhythm
  • At least 3 different P wave morphologies
  • Often associated with chronic lung disease

Wolff-Parkinson-White

  • Short PR interval
  • Delta wave
  • Wide or slurred QRS upstroke
WPW = Short PR + Delta Wave + Wide QRS

AVNRT

  • Regular narrow complex tachycardia
  • Retrograde P waves may be visible after QRS

AVRT

  • Regular narrow complex tachycardia
  • Associated with accessory pathway physiology

Paroxysmal Atrial Tachycardia

  • Sudden tachycardia
  • Different P wave morphology from baseline
  • Ectopic atrial focus drives the rhythm

PACs, PVCs, and Bigeminy

Premature Atrial Contractions

  • Premature beat
  • Narrow QRS
  • May have abnormal or hidden P wave

Premature Ventricular Contractions

  • Premature beat
  • Wide, bizarre QRS complex
  • No preceding normal P wave
Pattern Meaning
Bigeminy Normal beat, PVC, normal beat, PVC
Trigeminy Two normal beats, then PVC
Quadrigeminy Three normal beats, then PVC

Wide Complex Tachycardias

Ventricular Tachycardia

Ventricular tachycardia is a wide complex tachycardia arising from the ventricles.

Polymorphic VT

Polymorphic VT has varying QRS morphology, suggesting multiple ventricular origins or changing conduction patterns.

Torsades de Pointes

  • Polymorphic VT with twisting morphology
  • Associated with prolonged QT
  • Can be triggered by PVC falling on prolonged repolarization
A wide complex tachycardia should be treated as ventricular tachycardia until proven otherwise, especially in unstable patients.

Escape Rhythms

Escape rhythms are backup pacemakers when higher pacemakers fail.

Rhythm Origin Typical Rate QRS
Sinus rhythm SA node 60–100 bpm Narrow
Junctional rhythm AV junction 40–60 bpm Usually narrow
Ventricular escape rhythm Ventricle 20–40 bpm Wide
Accelerated idioventricular rhythm Ventricle Usually 50–110 bpm Wide
Accelerated idioventricular rhythm is commonly seen after reperfusion following myocardial infarction and is often monitored rather than aggressively treated.

Bundle Branch Blocks

Right Bundle Branch Block

  • Wide QRS
  • RSR′ pattern in V1–V2
  • Deep, slurred S wave in V5–V6

Left Bundle Branch Block

  • Wide QRS
  • Deep S wave in V1–V2
  • Broad, clumsy, or M-shaped R wave in V5–V6
Block V1–V2 V5–V6
RBBB RSR′ / rabbit ears Wide slurred S wave
LBBB Deep S wave Broad M-shaped R wave
If QRS is 100–120 ms with bundle morphology, this is an incomplete bundle branch block. If QRS is wide without specific BBB criteria, it may be called nonspecific intraventricular conduction delay.

AV Blocks

Block EKG Finding Clinical Meaning
First-degree AV block PR interval >200 ms Delayed AV conduction
Mobitz I / Wenckebach Progressively lengthening PR, then dropped QRS Often AV nodal; can be benign
Mobitz II Fixed PR interval with dropped QRS More concerning; infranodal disease
Third-degree AV block P waves and QRS complexes dissociated Complete heart block
Mobitz II and complete heart block are concerning conduction disorders and often require urgent cardiology evaluation.

Fascicular Blocks

Left Anterior Fascicular Block

  • Left axis deviation
  • qR pattern in lateral leads
  • rS pattern in inferior leads

Left Posterior Fascicular Block

  • Right axis deviation
  • rS pattern in lateral leads
  • qR pattern in inferior leads

Bifascicular Block

Bifascicular Block = RBBB + LAFB or LPFB
True trifascicular block is complete heart block. The phrase is often misused to describe first-degree AV block plus bifascicular block.

Classic Board Findings

Finding Suggests
Electrical alternans Large pericardial effusion
Low voltage QRS Obesity, pericardial effusion, amyloidosis, infiltrative disease
PEA Organized electrical rhythm without palpable pulse
Deep symmetric lateral T wave inversions Hypertrophic obstructive cardiomyopathy
Diffuse giant T wave inversions Cerebral T waves from major neurologic injury
Biphasic T waves in V2–V3 Wellens syndrome, critical LAD stenosis
Upsloping ST depression with tall T waves De Winter pattern, LAD occlusion equivalent
Coved or saddleback ST elevation in V1–V3 Brugada pattern
Osborn/J wave Hypothermia
Epsilon wave Arrhythmogenic right ventricular cardiomyopathy
PEA pearl: A monitor can show an organized rhythm while the patient has no pulse. If there is no pulse, start CPR and ACLS.

Clinical Pearls

  1. Use the same EKG sequence every time: rate, rhythm, axis, intervals, morphology.
  2. Use the 300 method for regular rhythms.
  3. Use QRS count ×6 for irregular rhythms on a 10-second strip.
  4. Sinus rhythm requires upright P waves in lead II with P before every QRS.
  5. Use lead I and lead II for quick axis determination.
  6. PR interval should be 120–200 ms.
  7. QRS should be less than 120 ms.
  8. QT should generally be less than half the R-R interval by quick visual estimate.
  9. Wide QRS suggests ventricular origin or aberrant conduction.
  10. R wave in aVL greater than 11 mm supports LVH.
  11. Pathologic Q waves suggest prior infarction.
  12. New T wave inversions in contiguous leads suggest ischemia until proven otherwise.
  13. Diffuse ST elevation plus PR depression suggests pericarditis.
  14. Sinus tachycardia is the most common EKG finding in PE.
  15. Peaked T waves suggest hyperkalemia.
  16. U waves suggest hypokalemia.
  17. Irregularly irregular rhythm without P waves suggests atrial fibrillation.
  18. Sawtooth flutter waves suggest atrial flutter.
  19. Short PR plus delta wave suggests WPW.
  20. Mobitz II and complete heart block are dangerous conduction disorders.
  21. Electrical alternans suggests large pericardial effusion.
  22. Wellens syndrome suggests critical LAD stenosis.
  23. Brugada pattern can appear as coved or saddleback ST elevation in the right precordial leads.

Bottom Line

EKG interpretation should always follow a systematic sequence: rate, rhythm, axis, intervals, and morphology. Rate can be estimated using the 300 method or the 10-second strip method. Rhythm requires assessment of regularity and sinus criteria. Axis can be quickly determined using leads I and II. Intervals include PR, QRS, and QT. Morphology includes hypertrophy, ischemia, arrhythmias, electrolyte changes, conduction disease, and classic board patterns. This approach prevents missed findings and creates a reliable method for interpreting EKGs on rounds, in the ED, and on the wards.

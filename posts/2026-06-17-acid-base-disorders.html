Acid-Base Disorders: The Complete Resident Guide

Added June 17, 2026 • Nephrology / Pulmonary / Critical Care • ~15 min read

A practical, resident-focused guide to acid-base interpretation: ABG vs VBG, identifying the primary disorder, calculating and correcting the anion gap, distinguishing NAGMA from HAGMA, using Winter’s formula, checking the delta-delta gap, and recognizing acute versus chronic respiratory disorders.

Overview

Acid-base disorders are common in hospitalized patients, especially in emergency medicine, intensive care, nephrology, and pulmonary medicine. The goal is to identify the primary disorder, determine whether compensation is appropriate, and then look for additional mixed disorders.

Core idea: Do not stop after naming the first abnormality. After identifying the primary disorder, always ask: Is the patient compensating appropriately? Is there another acid-base disorder present?

Normal pH and the Initial Approach

Normal blood pH is approximately 7.35–7.45. A pH below 7.35 indicates acidemia, while a pH above 7.45 indicates alkalemia.

pH Interpretation
< 7.35 Acidemia
7.35–7.45 Normal range
> 7.45 Alkalemia

Once you know whether the patient is acidemic or alkalemic, look at the PCO₂ and bicarbonate to determine whether the primary process is respiratory or metabolic.

VBG vs ABG

Acid-base status is usually assessed with a venous blood gas (VBG) or arterial blood gas (ABG). A VBG tends to be slightly more acidemic than an ABG.

Practical correction: A VBG pH is often about 0.05 lower than the corresponding ABG pH. For example, a VBG pH of 7.20 roughly corresponds to an ABG pH around 7.25.

For PCO₂, use the value from the ABG or VBG. For bicarbonate, it is often best to use the bicarbonate or CO₂ value from the basic metabolic panel because it is directly measured or more reliable clinically than the calculated bicarbonate on a gas.

Determine the Primary Acid-Base Disorder

The primary disorder is determined by matching the direction of the pH with the direction of PCO₂ and bicarbonate.

Primary Disorder pH PCO₂ HCO₃⁻
Metabolic Acidosis Low Low compensation Low
Respiratory Acidosis Low High High compensation if chronic
Metabolic Alkalosis High High compensation High
Respiratory Alkalosis High Low Low compensation if chronic
Common pitfall: A normal pH does not exclude an acid-base disorder. Mixed disorders can push the pH toward normal.

Anion Gap Metabolic Acidosis

When metabolic acidosis is present, the next step is to determine whether it is an anion gap metabolic acidosis or a non-anion gap metabolic acidosis.

Anion Gap = Na⁺ − Cl⁻ − HCO₃⁻

Example:

Na 140 − Cl 100 − HCO₃ 20 = Anion Gap 20

An anion gap greater than about 12 is generally considered elevated, although the exact normal range varies slightly by lab.

Correcting the Anion Gap for Hypoalbuminemia

Albumin is an important unmeasured anion. When albumin is low, the measured anion gap can appear falsely normal. Correcting the gap can uncover an anion gap metabolic acidosis that would otherwise be missed.

Corrected AG = Measured AG + 2.5 × (4 − Albumin)

Example:

Na 135 − Cl 105 − HCO₃ 20 = AG 10
Albumin = 2
Correction = 2.5 × (4 − 2) = 5
Corrected AG = 10 + 5 = 15
Clinical meaning: A patient with a measured anion gap of 10 and albumin of 2 may actually have a corrected anion gap of 15, consistent with an anion gap metabolic acidosis.

Non-Anion Gap Metabolic Acidosis

The two most common causes of non-anion gap metabolic acidosis are gastrointestinal bicarbonate loss and renal tubular acidosis.

Cause Typical Clue
Diarrhea / GI bicarbonate loss GI history, negative urine anion gap
Renal tubular acidosis Renal acidification problem, positive urine anion gap

Urine Anion Gap

The urine anion gap helps distinguish diarrhea from renal tubular acidosis in non-anion gap metabolic acidosis.

Urine Anion Gap = Urine Na⁺ + Urine K⁺ − Urine Cl⁻
Urine Anion Gap Suggests Reasoning
Negative Diarrhea / GI bicarbonate loss Kidney is appropriately excreting ammonium with chloride
Positive Renal tubular acidosis Impaired ammonium excretion; urine chloride is relatively low
High-yield concept: Urine chloride acts as a surrogate for ammonium excretion. If ammonium excretion is appropriate, urine chloride rises and the urine anion gap becomes negative.

Osmolar Gap

When an anion gap metabolic acidosis has no obvious explanation, calculate the osmolar gap. This is especially useful when toxic alcohol ingestion or glycol exposure is possible.

Osmolar Gap = Measured Serum Osmolality − Calculated Serum Osmolality

A gap greater than about 10 suggests unmeasured osmoles, such as toxic alcohols or glycols.

When to think about it: Unexplained anion gap metabolic acidosis, altered mental status, suspected ingestion, renal failure after possible toxin exposure, or calcium oxalate crystals in urine.

Winter’s Formula

Winter’s formula estimates the expected respiratory compensation in metabolic acidosis. Patients with metabolic acidosis should hyperventilate to lower PCO₂.

Expected PCO₂ = 1.5 × HCO₃⁻ + 8 ± 2

Example: if bicarbonate is 16:

Expected PCO₂ = 1.5 × 16 + 8 ± 2
Expected PCO₂ = 24 + 8 ± 2
Expected PCO₂ = 30–34
Measured PCO₂ Compared With Expected Interpretation
Within expected range Appropriate respiratory compensation
Higher than expected Additional respiratory acidosis
Lower than expected Additional respiratory alkalosis

Delta-Delta Gap

The delta-delta gap helps identify an additional metabolic disorder in a patient who already has an anion gap metabolic acidosis.

Delta-Delta Ratio = (Measured AG − 12) / (24 − Measured HCO₃⁻)
Delta-Delta Ratio Interpretation
< 0.8 Anion gap metabolic acidosis + non-anion gap metabolic acidosis
0.8–2 Pure anion gap metabolic acidosis
> 2 Anion gap metabolic acidosis + metabolic alkalosis

Example 1: Pure Anion Gap Metabolic Acidosis

AG = 16, HCO₃ = 20
(16 − 12) / (24 − 20) = 4 / 4 = 1.0
Interpretation: Pure anion gap metabolic acidosis

Example 2: Added Non-Anion Gap Metabolic Acidosis

Na 135, Cl 105, HCO₃ 10 → AG 20
(20 − 12) / (24 − 10) = 8 / 14 = 0.57
Interpretation: AG metabolic acidosis + non-anion gap metabolic acidosis

Example 3: Added Metabolic Alkalosis

Na 135, Cl 95, HCO₃ 20 → AG 20
(20 − 12) / (24 − 20) = 8 / 4 = 2.0
Interpretation: AG metabolic acidosis with concurrent metabolic alkalosis tendency
Simple memory: If bicarbonate fell more than expected, there is another acidosis. If bicarbonate did not fall enough, there is another alkalosis.

Respiratory Acidosis

Respiratory acidosis occurs when ventilation is inadequate and CO₂ is retained. It is common in COPD exacerbation, severe asthma, sedative overdose, neuromuscular weakness, obesity hypoventilation, and other causes of hypoventilation.

Acute vs Chronic Respiratory Acidosis

The key question is whether the process is acute or chronic. Chronic respiratory acidosis allows the kidneys time to retain bicarbonate.

Feature Acute Respiratory Acidosis Chronic Respiratory Acidosis
pH change pH decreases by ~0.08 for every 10 mmHg rise in PCO₂ pH decreases by ~0.03 for every 10 mmHg rise in PCO₂
HCO₃ change HCO₃ rises by ~1 for every 10 mmHg rise in PCO₂ HCO₃ rises by ~3.5–4 for every 10 mmHg rise in PCO₂
Example pH 7.16, PCO₂ 70, HCO₃ ~27 pH 7.31, PCO₂ 70, HCO₃ ~34–35
Clinical warning: A patient with obstructive lung disease who is initially tachypneic and alkalotic can later fatigue and develop respiratory acidosis. Worsening CO₂ retention can be a sign of impending respiratory failure.

Metabolic Alkalosis

For metabolic alkalosis, the key bedside distinction is whether it is chloride responsive or chloride resistant. Urine chloride is the most useful test.

Urine Chloride Type Common Causes Response to Saline
< 15 Chloride responsive / saline responsive Vomiting, gastric losses, prior diuretic use, volume depletion Improves with normal saline
> 15 Chloride resistant Hyperaldosteronism, Conn syndrome, mineralocorticoid excess Does not correct simply with saline

Respiratory Alkalosis

Respiratory alkalosis occurs when the patient hyperventilates and blows off CO₂. Common causes include pain, anxiety, hypoxemia, sepsis, pregnancy, liver disease, pulmonary embolism, and early asthma exacerbation.

Type Expected Bicarbonate Change
Acute respiratory alkalosis HCO₃ decreases by ~2 for every 10 mmHg decrease in PCO₂
Chronic respiratory alkalosis HCO₃ decreases by ~5 for every 10 mmHg decrease in PCO₂

Final Summary

  1. Check pH first: acidemia, alkalemia, or normal.
  2. Use PCO₂ and bicarbonate to identify the primary disorder.
  3. For metabolic acidosis, calculate the anion gap.
  4. Correct the anion gap for low albumin.
  5. If NAGMA, use urine anion gap to separate diarrhea from RTA.
  6. If HAGMA, consider GOLDMARK/MUDPILES and calculate osmolar gap when ingestion is possible.
  7. Use Winter’s formula to check respiratory compensation in metabolic acidosis.
  8. Use delta-delta ratio to look for additional metabolic disorders.
  9. For respiratory acidosis or alkalosis, decide whether the process is acute or chronic.
  10. For metabolic alkalosis, check urine chloride to determine chloride-responsive vs chloride-resistant disease.
High-yield workflow: pH → PCO₂/HCO₃ → primary disorder → compensation → anion gap if metabolic acidosis → delta-delta if anion gap is elevated → targeted differential.

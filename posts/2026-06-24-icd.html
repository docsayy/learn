Implantable Cardioverter-Defibrillators (ICDs): The Complete Resident Guide

Added June 24, 2026 • Cardiology • ~16 min read

Implantable cardioverter-defibrillators are life-saving devices used to prevent sudden cardiac death from ventricular tachycardia and ventricular fibrillation. This resident guide reviews ICD types, primary and secondary prevention indications, programming strategies, complications, subcutaneous ICDs, wearable defibrillators, follow-up, generator replacement, end-of-life care, and landmark trials.

What Is an ICD?

An implantable cardioverter-defibrillator, or ICD, is a device that continuously monitors cardiac rhythm and delivers therapy to terminate life-threatening ventricular tachyarrhythmias.

  • Primary function: prevent sudden cardiac death from ventricular fibrillation or sustained ventricular tachycardia
  • Therapies: antitachycardia pacing, cardioversion, or defibrillation shocks
  • Secondary functions: bradycardia pacing, heart failure diagnostics, and cardiac resynchronization when combined with CRT
High-yield concept: A pacemaker treats slow rhythms. An ICD prevents sudden cardiac death from dangerous fast ventricular rhythms.

Think of an ICD as a fire extinguisher for the heart: silent most of the time, but potentially life-saving when ventricular tachycardia or ventricular fibrillation occurs.

The ICD Spectrum: Device Types

Device Type Leads Key Features Best For
Single-chamber ICD RV defibrillation lead Defibrillation plus basic bradycardia pacing Patients without atrial pacing needs
Dual-chamber ICD RA + RV leads Atrial sensing improves SVT/VT discrimination Sinus node dysfunction or need for atrial sensing
CRT-D RA + RV + LV lead Defibrillation plus resynchronization Heart failure with LBBB and wide QRS
Subcutaneous ICD No transvenous leads Defibrillation only; no ATP or bradycardia pacing Young patients, infection risk, limited vascular access
Leadless ICD No traditional leads Emerging technology; limited pacing capability Selected patients where lead avoidance is important
Teaching point: Device selection depends on arrhythmia risk, need for pacing, heart failure status, vascular access, infection risk, and patient goals.

Core Indications for ICD Placement

Secondary Prevention

Secondary prevention means the patient has already had a dangerous ventricular arrhythmia or cardiac arrest.

Scenario Recommendation
Survivor of cardiac arrest due to VT/VF without reversible cause ICD indicated
Sustained VT with hemodynamic compromise ICD indicated
Sustained VT without hemodynamic compromise but with structural heart disease ICD indicated
Simple rule: Survivor of VT/VF arrest without a reversible cause → ICD.

Primary Prevention

Primary prevention means the patient has not yet had cardiac arrest, but has high risk for sudden cardiac death.

Population Criteria Recommendation
Ischemic cardiomyopathy LVEF ≤35%, NYHA II–III, ≥40 days post-MI, on GDMT ICD recommended
Non-ischemic dilated cardiomyopathy LVEF ≤35%, NYHA II–III, on GDMT ICD recommended in selected patients
Hypertrophic cardiomyopathy Selected high-risk features Consider ICD
Arrhythmogenic right ventricular cardiomyopathy Selected high-risk features Consider ICD
Long QT, Brugada, and other channelopathies High-risk features or failed medical therapy Consider ICD
Important timing: After acute MI, wait at least 40 days before placing a primary prevention ICD unless there is another clear indication.

When Not to Implant an ICD

  • Cardiac arrest from a clearly reversible cause
  • Acute MI within 48 hours as the cause of VT/VF
  • Severe electrolyte abnormality causing arrhythmia
  • Drug toxicity causing arrhythmia
  • Life expectancy less than one year
  • Severe comorbid illness where shocks would not align with goals of care
  • NYHA class IV refractory heart failure unless CRT-D or advanced therapy is appropriate
  • Patient refusal
Shared decision-making: ICD therapy should match the patient’s prognosis, goals, and willingness to receive shocks.

ICD Programming: The Anti-Shock Strategy

Modern ICD programming aims to reduce unnecessary shocks while preserving protection from sudden cardiac death.

Programming Feature Preferred Strategy Why It Matters
Detection zones Higher-rate cutoff or dual-zone programming Reduces inappropriate shocks for SVT
Detection duration Longer detection before therapy Allows nonsustained VT to self-terminate
ATP before shock Enable ATP in VT zones Terminates VT painlessly
SVT discrimination Use morphology, onset, stability, and atrial sensing Reduces inappropriate shocks

Antitachycardia Pacing

Antitachycardia pacing, or ATP, delivers rapid pacing impulses to interrupt a reentrant ventricular tachycardia circuit.

ATP pearl: ATP can terminate VT without a painful shock.

Inappropriate Shocks

Inappropriate shocks may occur when the ICD mistakes another rhythm or signal for VT/VF.

  • Atrial fibrillation with rapid ventricular response
  • SVT
  • Lead fracture or noise
  • Oversensing
  • T-wave oversensing

ICD Complications and Morbidity

Complication Approximate Frequency Management
Infection Uncommon but serious Antibiotics; extraction if pocket or lead infection
Lead dislodgement Early procedural complication Lead repositioning
Inappropriate shocks Clinically important Reprogramming, rate control, antiarrhythmics, ablation
Psychological distress Common Counseling and shock-anxiety management
Electrical storm Emergency Antiarrhythmics, sedation, beta blockade, ablation
Important: ICD shocks, whether appropriate or inappropriate, are associated with worse quality of life and may signal higher risk.

Electrical Storm

Electrical storm usually refers to recurrent episodes of VT/VF requiring ICD therapy over a short period.

  • Place pads and monitor continuously
  • Correct potassium and magnesium
  • Treat ischemia, hypoxia, acidosis, or decompensated HF
  • Use beta blockers and antiarrhythmics when appropriate
  • Provide sedation if repeated shocks occur
  • Consult electrophysiology early

The Subcutaneous ICD

A subcutaneous ICD avoids transvenous leads by placing the generator and defibrillation coil under the skin rather than inside the venous system.

Advantages

  • No transvenous leads
  • Lower risk of endocarditis
  • No vascular injury from leads
  • Easier extraction if needed
  • Useful in young patients who may need decades of device therapy

Limitations

  • No antitachycardia pacing
  • No chronic bradycardia pacing
  • No CRT capability
  • Larger generator
  • Different implantation technique

Ideal Candidates

  • Young patients with channelopathies
  • High infection risk
  • Limited venous access
  • Prior endocarditis
  • No anticipated pacing need
S-ICD pearl: Consider S-ICD when the patient needs defibrillation but does not need pacing, ATP, or CRT.

The Wearable Cardioverter-Defibrillator

A wearable cardioverter-defibrillator is an external device worn temporarily by patients who are at risk for sudden cardiac death but do not yet have a permanent ICD indication or are awaiting reassessment.

Common Uses

  • Post-MI with reduced EF while awaiting EF reassessment
  • Newly diagnosed non-ischemic cardiomyopathy during GDMT optimization
  • Bridge to ICD implantation
  • Bridge to transplant or LVAD
  • Temporary ICD contraindication such as active infection
Important: A wearable defibrillator is a bridge, not a replacement for a permanent ICD when a permanent ICD is clearly indicated.

ICD Follow-Up and Remote Monitoring

Standard Follow-Up

  • In-person device evaluation every 3–6 months
  • Remote monitoring when available
  • Device interrogation after shocks or suspected malfunction

What to Check

  • Battery status
  • Lead impedance
  • Lead integrity
  • Arrhythmia episodes
  • Delivered therapies
  • Percentage of pacing
  • Atrial arrhythmia burden when applicable

Remote Monitoring Alerts

  • Shock therapies
  • ATP therapies
  • Lead impedance changes
  • Battery depletion
  • Atrial fibrillation burden
  • Possible lead fracture
Resident pearl: Any ICD shock deserves evaluation. Ask whether the patient had syncope, chest pain, palpitations, medication nonadherence, electrolyte abnormalities, or new heart failure symptoms.

Generator Replacement and Device Upgrades

When the ICD battery reaches elective replacement, the indication should be reassessed instead of automatically replacing the generator.

Questions to Ask

  • Has the ejection fraction recovered?
  • Has the patient ever received appropriate ICD therapy?
  • Has the patient’s prognosis changed?
  • Does the patient still want shock therapy?
  • Would CRT-D, CRT-P, pacemaker, or no replacement better match current goals?
Important: Generator replacement is a clinical decision, not just a battery change.

End-of-Life Care and ICD Deactivation

Patients have the ethical and legal right to request deactivation of ICD shock therapy.

When to Discuss Deactivation

  • Terminal illness
  • Hospice enrollment
  • Intractable symptoms
  • Frequent painful shocks
  • Patient or surrogate request
  • Goals of care no longer align with defibrillation

Key Distinction

Deactivating ICD shocks is not euthanasia. It allows natural death without painful device-delivered shocks.

Bradycardia pacing can often remain active while tachyarrhythmia shock therapies are turned off.

Common Clinical Scenarios

Case 1: Post-MI with EF 25%

A 58-year-old patient had an anterior STEMI 10 days ago and now has an EF of 25%.

Management: Wait at least 40 days post-MI, optimize GDMT, reassess EF, and implant ICD if EF remains low and criteria are met.

Case 2: Survivor of Cardiac Arrest

A 45-year-old patient survives VF arrest during exercise and no reversible cause is found.

Management: ICD implantation for secondary prevention.

Case 3: Inappropriate Shocks for AF

A 67-year-old patient with a dual-chamber ICD receives multiple shocks for rapid atrial fibrillation.

Management: Reprogram detection zones, activate SVT discrimination, optimize rate control, and consider ablation strategies if needed.

Case 4: Young Patient with HCM

A 22-year-old patient with hypertrophic cardiomyopathy has family history of sudden cardiac death and syncope.

Management: Perform risk stratification and consider ICD. If no pacing indication exists, S-ICD may be reasonable.

Case 5: End-Stage HF with ICD Firing

A 78-year-old patient with NYHA class IV heart failure is hospice eligible and experiencing recurrent ICD shocks.

Management: Discuss ICD shock deactivation and focus on comfort-directed care.

High-Yield Summary

Concept Key Point
Secondary prevention Survivor of VT/VF arrest without reversible cause → ICD
Primary prevention Cardiomyopathy with EF ≤35% in appropriate patients
Programming High-rate cutoff, prolonged detection, ATP before shock
S-ICD No transvenous leads, but no ATP or bradycardia pacing
Remote monitoring Standard of care for many patients
Deactivation Patient right at end of life; not assisted suicide

Landmark ICD Trials

Trial Year Major Finding
MADIT 1996 ICD improved survival in high-risk post-MI patients
MADIT-II 2002 Supported ICD for primary prevention in post-MI patients with low EF
SCD-HeFT 2005 ICD reduced mortality in ischemic and non-ischemic heart failure
DEFINITE 2004 ICD reduced sudden death in non-ischemic dilated cardiomyopathy
MADIT-RIT 2012 Programming strategies reduced inappropriate shocks
DANISH 2016 Raised debate about ICD benefit in non-ischemic cardiomyopathy with modern GDMT

Rapid Decision Framework

  • Cardiac arrest survivor without reversible cause → ICD
  • Sustained VT with structural heart disease → ICD
  • EF ≤35% with symptomatic HF despite GDMT → consider ICD
  • Wide QRS + LBBB + HF → CRT-D if criteria met
  • Young patient with no pacing need → consider S-ICD
  • Temporary high-risk period → consider wearable defibrillator bridge
  • End-stage illness or hospice → discuss ICD shock deactivation

Bottom Line

ICDs prevent sudden cardiac death by detecting and treating life-threatening ventricular tachyarrhythmias. The most important distinction is secondary prevention versus primary prevention. Survivors of VT/VF arrest without a reversible cause generally need an ICD. Patients with ischemic or non-ischemic cardiomyopathy and persistently reduced EF may qualify for primary prevention ICD placement. Modern programming emphasizes fewer shocks through higher-rate detection, longer detection duration, ATP before shock, and SVT discrimination. Always reassess ICD goals at generator replacement and near the end of life.

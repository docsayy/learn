Cellulitis: Cefazolin vs Vancomycin and IV vs Oral Antibiotics

June 19, 2026 Infectious Disease

Cellulitis is one of the most common skin and soft tissue infections encountered in both outpatient and inpatient medicine. Choosing the correct antibiotic requires understanding whether MRSA coverage is needed, selecting between cefazolin and vancomycin, and determining when intravenous therapy is truly necessary. This review summarizes a practical bedside approach to cellulitis management.

Overview

The majority of cellulitis cases are caused by:

  • Beta-hemolytic streptococci
  • Methicillin-sensitive Staphylococcus aureus (MSSA)

A key management decision is determining whether MRSA coverage is necessary. This distinction largely depends on whether the infection is non-purulent or purulent.

Key Principle: Non-purulent cellulitis usually requires streptococcal and MSSA coverage, whereas purulent cellulitis generally requires MRSA coverage.

Determining Need for MRSA Coverage

Non-Purulent Cellulitis

Non-purulent cellulitis presents with:

  • Erythema
  • Warmth
  • Tenderness
  • Swelling
  • No abscess or drainage

The predominant pathogens are streptococci and MSSA.

Routine MRSA coverage is generally not required.

Purulent Cellulitis

Purulent infections include:

  • Abscesses
  • Purulent drainage
  • Fluctuance
  • Known MRSA history
  • IV drug use

These patients should receive empiric MRSA coverage.

Route Preferred MRSA Options
IV Vancomycin
PO TMP-SMX, Doxycycline, Linezolid
Common Mistake: Doxycycline alone does not reliably cover streptococci. For cellulitis requiring oral MRSA coverage, doxycycline is often paired with amoxicillin.

Recommended Oral MRSA Regimens

  • TMP-SMX + Amoxicillin
  • Doxycycline + Amoxicillin
  • Linezolid monotherapy

Linezolid is usually reserved for patients who cannot tolerate other agents because of cost and potential adverse effects such as thrombocytopenia.

Clindamycin is an alternative but is generally avoided when possible because of increased risk of Clostridioides difficile infection.

Cefazolin vs Vancomycin

For hospitalized patients with non-purulent cellulitis, cefazolin is often the preferred intravenous antibiotic.

Why Cefazolin?

  • Excellent streptococcal coverage
  • Excellent MSSA coverage
  • Rapid bactericidal activity
  • Superior activity against MSSA compared with vancomycin
  • Narrower spectrum than many alternatives

Why Not Ceftriaxone?

Ceftriaxone is commonly used for many infections but is not usually the best antibiotic for uncomplicated cellulitis.

Antibiotic Main Strength Role in Cellulitis
Cefazolin Strong gram-positive activity Preferred for non-purulent cellulitis
Ceftriaxone Broader gram-negative activity Usually not first choice

When Vancomycin Is Preferred

  • Purulent cellulitis
  • Known MRSA infection
  • MRSA colonization
  • IV drug use
  • Severe systemic illness
  • Failure of beta-lactam therapy
If MSSA is the pathogen, cefazolin is generally more effective than vancomycin.

Oral Antibiotic Options

Non-Purulent Cellulitis

Medication Advantages Limitations
Cephalexin Excellent coverage Four times daily dosing
Cefadroxil Twice daily dosing Less commonly prescribed

Purulent Cellulitis

Medication Comments
TMP-SMX Excellent MRSA coverage
Doxycycline Add amoxicillin for streptococcal coverage
Linezolid Excellent oral bioavailability
Clindamycin Avoid when possible due to C. difficile risk

When to Use IV Antibiotics

Many cellulitis patients can be treated successfully with oral antibiotics alone.

Indications for Intravenous Therapy

  • Extensive erythema
  • Rapidly progressing infection
  • Failure of oral antibiotics
  • Inability to tolerate oral medications
  • Severe systemic illness
  • Significant immunocompromise

Transitioning to Oral Therapy

Once clinical improvement is observed and the patient can tolerate oral medications, transition to oral antibiotics is appropriate.

Early switch to oral therapy can shorten hospitalization and reduce complications associated with IV access.

Treatment Duration

Most uncomplicated cellulitis responds to short-course therapy.

Clinical Scenario Recommended Duration
Uncomplicated cellulitis 5–7 days
Slow clinical response 7–14 days
Complicated infection Individualized

Treatment duration should be guided by clinical improvement rather than complete resolution of erythema.

Monitoring Response

Clinical improvement often requires 24–48 hours.

It is common for erythema to remain unchanged or even expand slightly during the first day of treatment despite effective therapy.

Signs of Improvement

  • Reduced pain
  • Reduced tenderness
  • Improved swelling
  • Resolution of fever
  • Improving leukocytosis
  • Improved overall appearance

If the Patient Is Not Improving

Failure to improve should prompt evaluation for:

  • Undrained abscess
  • Incorrect diagnosis
  • Resistant organism
  • Necrotizing infection

Imaging options include:

  • Ultrasound
  • CT scan

Supportive Measures

Elevation of the affected extremity helps:

  • Reduce edema
  • Improve lymphatic drainage
  • Accelerate clinical recovery

Special Situations

Bilateral Lower Extremity Cellulitis

True bilateral cellulitis is uncommon.

The most common alternative diagnosis is:

  • Venous stasis dermatitis
Always reconsider the diagnosis when cellulitis appears bilateral.

Necrotizing Fasciitis

Features concerning for necrotizing fasciitis include:

  • Pain out of proportion to exam
  • Rapid progression
  • Crepitus or soft tissue gas
  • Hypotension
  • Marked systemic toxicity

Immediate surgical consultation is mandatory.

Empiric Antibiotics

  • Vancomycin
  • Piperacillin-Tazobactam
  • Clindamycin

Diabetic Foot Infection

These infections are often polymicrobial and frequently require:

  • Anaerobic coverage
  • Gram-negative coverage
  • Assessment for osteomyelitis

Hand Cellulitis

Hand infections may threaten long-term function and often warrant early surgical consultation.

Groin Cellulitis

Maintain a high index of suspicion for Fournier gangrene, especially in diabetic or immunocompromised patients.

Clinical Pearls

  • Most cellulitis is caused by streptococci and MSSA.
  • Purulence strongly suggests MRSA.
  • Cefazolin is generally preferred over ceftriaxone for uncomplicated IV cellulitis treatment.
  • Cefazolin is more effective than vancomycin for MSSA infections.
  • Doxycycline alone may inadequately cover streptococci.
  • Clinical improvement usually takes 24–48 hours.
  • Elevate affected extremities whenever possible.
  • Bilateral cellulitis is uncommon and should prompt consideration of venous stasis dermatitis.
  • Failure to improve should trigger evaluation for an occult abscess.
  • Always consider necrotizing fasciitis in rapidly progressive or toxic-appearing infections.

Bottom Line

For non-purulent cellulitis, cefazolin (IV) or cephalexin/cefadroxil (PO) are usually preferred because the most common pathogens are streptococci and MSSA. Vancomycin should primarily be reserved for patients with purulent infections, known MRSA risk factors, or severe illness requiring MRSA coverage. Most patients improve within 24–48 hours and can often be transitioned from IV to oral therapy once clinically stable.

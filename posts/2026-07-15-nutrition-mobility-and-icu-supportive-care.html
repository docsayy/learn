Nutrition, Mobility, and ICU Supportive Care

Added July 15, 2026 • Pulmonary and Critical Care • ~30 min read

A practical ICU bundle covering enteral nutrition, protein and calorie delivery, refeeding risk, glycemic control, DVT and stress-ulcer prophylaxis, bowel care, pressure-injury prevention, sleep, early mobility, device removal, and daily de-escalation.

Clinical Overview

Supportive ICU care is not secondary. Nutrition, mobility, sleep, thrombosis prevention, pressure care, bowel function, glucose management, and device removal directly affect ventilator days, delirium, weakness, infection, and recovery.

Nutrition Assessment

  • Assess baseline nutrition risk, weight loss, intake, frailty, and GI function.
  • Start enteral nutrition early in most stable critically ill patients when the gut is usable.
  • Delay or reduce feeding during uncontrolled shock or escalating vasopressors.
  • Advance according to tolerance and refeeding risk.

Energy and Protein

  • Avoid overfeeding during the early acute phase.
  • ASPEN guidance supports a broad early energy range rather than forced full calories.
  • Protein needs are often increased, especially with catabolism, burns, trauma, or CRRT.
  • Use indirect calorimetry when available for prolonged or complex cases.

Refeeding Syndrome

  • Identify malnutrition, prolonged fasting, alcohol use, and low baseline electrolytes.
  • Give thiamine before and during feeding in high-risk patients.
  • Monitor and replace phosphate, potassium, and magnesium.
  • Start calories cautiously and advance slowly.

Glycemic Control

  • Avoid severe hyperglycemia and hypoglycemia.
  • Use a moderate ICU target according to protocol rather than intensive normalization.
  • Adjust insulin for nutrition interruptions, steroids, and renal failure.
  • Treat hypoglycemia immediately and review the cause.

DVT and Stress-Ulcer Prophylaxis

  • Use pharmacologic VTE prophylaxis unless bleeding risk prohibits it.
  • Use mechanical prophylaxis when anticoagulation is unsafe.
  • Stress-ulcer prophylaxis is for patients with meaningful GI-bleeding risk, not every ICU admission.
  • Stop prophylaxis when indications resolve.

Early Mobility

  • Assess daily for sitting, standing, transferring, and walking.
  • Coordinate sedation reduction, ventilator support, lines, and staffing.
  • Mobilization can occur during ventilation in selected stable patients.
  • Enhanced mobilization is conditionally supported by the 2025 PADIS update.

Skin, Bowel, and Device Care

  • Reposition and offload pressure areas.
  • Use moisture and nutrition strategies for skin protection.
  • Use a bowel regimen with opioids and immobility; avoid ineffective routine docusate-only care.
  • Remove urinary catheters, central lines, arterial lines, and restraints as soon as unnecessary.

Sleep and Environment

  • Day-night orientation and natural light
  • Hearing aids and glasses
  • Nighttime noise and interruption reduction
  • Family engagement
  • Melatonin may be considered in selected adults under the 2025 focused update

Daily Supportive-Care Checklist

  • Nutrition route and goal
  • Refeeding electrolytes
  • VTE and GI prophylaxis indication
  • Glucose plan
  • Sedation target and mobility level
  • Bowel movement and urinary retention
  • Skin and line review
  • Sleep and family communication

Common Pitfalls

  • Forcing full feeds during uncontrolled shock
  • Ignoring refeeding risk
  • Keeping stress-ulcer prophylaxis indefinitely
  • Deep sedation preventing mobility
  • No bowel plan with opioids
  • Leaving devices because they are convenient

Bottom Line

High-quality ICU care is a daily bundle: appropriate early nutrition, electrolyte protection, VTE prevention, selective stress-ulcer prophylaxis, light sedation, early mobility, skin and bowel care, sleep promotion, and rapid device removal.

Chest X-Ray Interpretation: A Systematic Approach for Medical Students and Residents

Added June 20, 2026 • Pulmonary / Radiology • ~10 min read

A practical framework for reading chest X-rays using a repeatable system: first determine whether the film is AP or PA, then assess image quality with RIPE, and finally interpret the radiograph using the ABCDE approach.

Overview

Chest X-ray interpretation is a skill every medical student and resident will be asked to perform. The safest approach is to use the same system every time so that quality problems and subtle findings are not missed.

Core approach: First ask whether the film is AP or PA. Then assess quality using RIPE. Then interpret the image using ABCDE.
Step Purpose
AP vs PA Understand projection and limitations
RIPE Assess image quality
ABCDE Systematically interpret the film

AP vs PA Films

Before using RIPE, first determine whether the chest X-ray is an AP or PA film.

Film Type How It Is Taken Clinical Meaning
PA film X-ray enters posteriorly and exits anteriorly Usually higher quality and better for heart size
AP film X-ray enters anteriorly and exits posteriorly Often portable; can falsely magnify the heart

Why PA Films Are Better

PA films usually depict heart size more accurately because the heart is closer to the detector plate. AP portable films can magnify the heart and make cardiomegaly appear worse than it truly is.

Common pitfall: Do not overcall cardiomegaly on an AP portable film. AP projection can make the heart look falsely enlarged.

Why AP Films Are Still Common

AP films are often obtained in hospitalized patients because they can be done portably at the bedside. This is useful for patients who are too ill or immobile to stand for a PA film.

How to tell: Look at the top label of the film. It often says PA, AP, portable, or mobile.

RIPE Quality Checklist

The RIPE mnemonic helps determine whether the film is technically adequate.

Letter Meaning Question
R Rotation Is the patient rotated?
I Inspiration Did the patient take a deep breath?
P Position Was the film angled appropriately?
E Exposure Is the film underexposed, overexposed, or adequate?
You cannot confidently interpret pathology on a poor-quality film without first recognizing the quality limitation.

R: Rotation

Assess rotation by comparing the medial ends of the clavicles to the sternum or spinous processes.

Good Rotation

  • Clavicles are roughly equidistant from the sternum
  • Patient is facing the X-ray beam head-on
  • Mediastinal structures are more reliable

Poor Rotation

  • Clavicles are asymmetric
  • Mediastinum may appear falsely widened
  • Lung fields may look asymmetric
Common artifact: Severe rotation can create the appearance of a widened mediastinum even when the mediastinum is not truly widened.

I: Inspiration

A good inspiratory film should show about 9–10 posterior ribs.

Adequate Inspiration = 9–10 visible posterior ribs

Why Inspiration Matters

Poor inspiration compresses the lungs and can make the film appear more congested. It can also make the heart appear larger and obscure pathology.

Practical point: Low lung volumes can mimic pulmonary edema, atelectasis, or cardiomegaly.

P: Position

Position refers to whether the X-ray was shot at an appropriate angle.

Ideal Position

Clavicles should project between the 3rd and 4th ribs

Abnormal Position

  • Clavicles too low may indicate upward angulation
  • Clavicles too high may indicate downward angulation
  • Angled films can compress lung fields and distort anatomy
Poor positioning makes it harder to interpret lung fields, cardiac size, and mediastinal structures.

E: Exposure

Exposure determines whether the film is too light, too dark, or appropriately penetrated.

Adequate Exposure

  • Vertebral bodies are faintly visible behind the heart
  • Lung fields are not completely burnt out
  • Mediastinal and pulmonary structures remain interpretable
Film Problem Appearance Issue
Underexposed Too white Cannot see vertebral column behind heart
Overexposed Too black Lung fields look burnt out
Well exposed Balanced Vertebral bodies visible behind heart
A good film lets you see the vertebral column behind the heart without burning out the lung fields.

ABCDE Interpretation

After assessing image quality, interpret the film using ABCDE.

Letter Meaning What to Check
A Airway Trachea and central airways
B Bones Clavicles, ribs, shoulders, spine
C Cardiac Heart size, mediastinum, aortic knob
D Diaphragm Costophrenic angles, free air, hemidiaphragms
E Everything else Lung fields, lines, tubes, masses, infiltrates

A: Airway

Start by checking whether the trachea is midline.

What to Look For

  • Midline trachea
  • Tracheal deviation
  • Large mediastinal mass effect
  • Mainstem bronchus abnormalities
Tracheal deviation can suggest mass effect, volume loss, pneumothorax, or other mediastinal pathology depending on the clinical context.

B: Bones

Do not jump straight to the lungs. First inspect the bones.

Check These Areas

  • Clavicles
  • Ribs
  • Shoulders
  • Scapulae
  • Visible spine

Findings You Can Miss

  • Clavicle fracture
  • Rib fractures
  • Lytic lesions
  • Scoliosis or vertebral abnormalities
Practical point: Bone findings are easy to miss if you immediately focus on the lung fields.

C: Cardiac

The cardiac portion includes heart size and mediastinal contour.

Cardiothoracic Ratio

Cardiomegaly = Cardiothoracic Ratio >50% on PA film

This is most reliable on PA films. AP portable films can falsely enlarge the cardiac silhouette.

Mediastinum

Look for mediastinal widening, which can be concerning for serious pathology such as aortic dissection, although quality issues like rotation can mimic widening.

Anatomy Pearls

  • Aortic knob = part of the aortic arch
  • Right heart border = largely right atrium
Important limitation: Cardiothoracic ratio is less reliable on AP portable films.

D: Diaphragm

Evaluate the diaphragms and costophrenic angles.

Free Air

Free air under the diaphragm is concerning for perforated viscus.

Emergency finding: Free intraperitoneal air under the diaphragm should prompt urgent evaluation for bowel perforation.

Costophrenic Angles

The costophrenic angles should be sharp. Blunting suggests pleural fluid.

Finding Suggests
Sharp costophrenic angle Normal
Blunted costophrenic angle Pleural effusion
Free air under diaphragm Perforated viscus

E: Everything Else

After airway, bones, cardiac silhouette, and diaphragm, examine everything else, especially the lung fields.

Look For

  • Opacities
  • Consolidation
  • Atelectasis
  • Pulmonary edema
  • Pneumothorax
  • Masses
  • Lines and tubes
  • Foreign bodies
Use a side-to-side comparison when looking at the lung fields. Subtle asymmetry is often easier to detect than isolated abnormalities.

Clinical Pearls

  1. Always use a systematic approach when reading chest X-rays.
  2. First determine whether the film is AP or PA.
  3. PA films are generally better quality than AP films.
  4. AP films can falsely enlarge the heart.
  5. Portable films are usually AP films.
  6. Use RIPE to assess film quality before making a diagnosis.
  7. Rotation is assessed by comparing clavicle distance from the sternum.
  8. Adequate inspiration usually shows 9–10 posterior ribs.
  9. Poor inspiration can mimic congestion or cardiomegaly.
  10. Good positioning places clavicles between the 3rd and 4th ribs.
  11. Adequate exposure lets you see the vertebral column behind the heart.
  12. Use ABCDE to interpret the film consistently.
  13. A = airway and tracheal position.
  14. B = bones, including clavicles and ribs.
  15. C = cardiac silhouette and mediastinum.
  16. D = diaphragm and costophrenic angles.
  17. E = everything else, including lung fields and masses.
  18. Blunted costophrenic angles suggest pleural effusion.
  19. Free air under the diaphragm suggests perforated viscus.
  20. Cardiothoracic ratio is most reliable on PA films.

Bottom Line

A safe chest X-ray interpretation starts with projection and quality: determine AP versus PA, then assess rotation, inspiration, position, and exposure using RIPE. After that, read the film systematically using ABCDE: airway, bones, cardiac silhouette, diaphragm, and everything else. This approach prevents common mistakes such as overcalling cardiomegaly on AP films, missing fractures, ignoring poor inspiration, or overlooking free air and pleural effusions.

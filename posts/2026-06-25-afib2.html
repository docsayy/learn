Atrial Fibrillation: The Complete Advanced Resident Guide

Added June 25, 2026 • Cardiology • ~22 min read

Atrial fibrillation is the most common sustained arrhythmia in adults and one of the most common rhythm problems encountered in the ED, hospital, ICU, and clinic. This guide reviews AF classification, triggers, acute management, rate control, rhythm control, cardioversion strategy, anticoagulation, antiarrhythmics, ablation, refractory AF, special populations, and high-yield board pearls.

What Is Atrial Fibrillation?

Atrial fibrillation is a supraventricular tachyarrhythmia characterized by chaotic atrial electrical activity and an irregularly irregular ventricular response. It is the most common sustained arrhythmia in adults.

  • Unorganized atrial electrical activity
  • Loss of coordinated atrial contraction
  • Irregularly irregular ventricular rhythm
  • Stasis in the left atrial appendage
  • Increased thromboembolic stroke risk
Think of atrial fibrillation as chaos in the atria that impairs ventricular filling and creates stroke risk.

Major Consequences

  • Palpitations and reduced exercise tolerance
  • Hypotension or pulmonary edema in unstable patients
  • Heart failure exacerbation
  • Tachycardia-induced cardiomyopathy
  • Stroke or systemic embolism

AF Classification

Type Definition Clinical Implication
First Diagnosed Initial detection of AF Evaluate triggers and decide rate vs rhythm strategy
Paroxysmal Terminates spontaneously within 7 days Often symptom-driven; ablation may be considered
Persistent Lasts more than 7 days or requires cardioversion Requires active rhythm control if sinus rhythm is desired
Long-standing Persistent Continuous AF for more than 1 year More difficult to restore and maintain sinus rhythm
Permanent Shared decision to stop pursuing rhythm control Rate control strategy only
“Permanent AF” does not mean the rhythm cannot ever be changed. It means the patient and clinician have decided not to pursue rhythm control.

Pathophysiology and Triggers

The Pulmonary Vein Theory

Many episodes of paroxysmal AF originate from myocardial sleeves around the pulmonary veins in the left atrium. This is why pulmonary vein isolation is the cornerstone of catheter ablation.

AF Begets AF

The longer a patient remains in AF, the more atrial remodeling occurs. Electrical remodeling, structural fibrosis, and atrial dilation make future AF more likely and rhythm control more difficult.

PIRATES Mnemonic for Triggers

Letter Trigger
P Pulmonary disease, PE, post-operative state
I Ischemia
R Rheumatic heart disease
A Anemia, alcohol, “holiday heart”
T Thyroid disease, toxins
E Electrolyte abnormalities, endocarditis
S Sepsis, surgery, sleep apnea
New AF should trigger a search for reversible causes: infection, PE, ACS, thyroid disease, alcohol, anemia, and electrolyte abnormalities.

Diagnosis and Initial Assessment

ECG Findings

  • Irregularly irregular R-R intervals
  • No distinct organized P waves
  • Fibrillatory baseline waves
  • Variable ventricular response

First Questions

  1. Is the patient hemodynamically stable?
  2. Is this definitely AF, or is it another irregular rhythm?
  3. Is there a trigger?
  4. How long has the patient been in AF?
  5. Does the patient need rate control, rhythm control, or urgent cardioversion?
  6. What is the stroke risk?
  7. What is the bleeding risk?

Initial Workup

Test Reason
ECG Confirm rhythm and assess ischemia or pre-excitation
BMP / Magnesium Check potassium, magnesium, renal function
TSH Evaluate thyroid disease
CBC Evaluate anemia and infection
Troponin If ischemic symptoms, chest pain, or high concern for ACS
BNP / CXR If heart failure or volume overload suspected
Echo Evaluate structure, EF, valves, LA size, and cardiomyopathy

Acute Management: The Decision Tree

Step 1: Stable or Unstable?

Clinical Status Management
Unstable Immediate synchronized cardioversion
Stable Rate control or rhythm control depending on symptoms, duration, comorbidities, and goals

Signs of Instability

  • Hypotension or shock
  • Acute ischemic chest pain
  • Acute pulmonary edema
  • Severe heart failure decompensation
  • Altered mental status from poor perfusion
Hemodynamically unstable AF requires immediate synchronized cardioversion regardless of AF duration.

Step 2: If Stable

  • Control the ventricular rate if rapid.
  • Identify and treat triggers.
  • Assess stroke risk with CHA₂DS₂-VASc.
  • Assess bleeding risk and contraindications.
  • Decide whether rhythm control is needed now or later.

Rate Control Strategy

Target Heart Rate

Strategy Target Best For
Lenient Control Resting HR <110 bpm Most stable patients
Strict Control Resting HR <80 bpm Persistent symptoms, tachycardia-induced cardiomyopathy, difficult HF symptoms
Lenient rate control is acceptable for many stable patients, but symptoms and cardiomyopathy risk should guide the target.

Rate Control Agents

Agent Typical IV Dosing Best For Avoid / Use Caution
Metoprolol 2.5–5 mg IV every 5 min, max 15 mg Most patients, hyperthyroidism, CAD Shock, severe decompensated HFrEF, severe bronchospasm
Diltiazem 15–20 mg IV bolus, then 5–15 mg/hr infusion Rapid rate control when EF preserved HFrEF, shock, WPW
Esmolol 500 mcg/kg bolus, then 50–200 mcg/kg/min ICU titration, short half-life needed Shock, severe decompensated HF
Digoxin 0.25 mg IV loading doses Hypotension, HFrEF, sedentary elderly Renal failure, toxicity risk, slow onset
Amiodarone 150 mg IV bolus, then infusion HFrEF or critically ill patients when other agents fail Can cardiovert; consider anticoagulation implications

Rate Control Pearls

  • Diltiazem often works faster than metoprolol but should be avoided in decompensated HFrEF.
  • Beta blockers are useful when AF is driven by hyperthyroidism or adrenergic excess.
  • Digoxin is slower but useful when hypotension limits beta blockers or calcium channel blockers.
  • Correct potassium and magnesium.
  • Magnesium 2 g IV is often used as an adjunct.
For AF with RVR, the best rate-control drug depends on EF, blood pressure, shock, decompensated HF, and pre-excitation.

Rhythm Control Strategy

When to Pursue Rhythm Control

  • Hemodynamic instability
  • Persistent symptoms despite adequate rate control
  • Young or active patient with new AF
  • First episode of AF, especially if duration <48 hours
  • Difficulty achieving rate control
  • Tachycardia-induced cardiomyopathy
  • Heart failure where AF appears to be worsening symptoms
  • Early AF where maintaining sinus rhythm may improve outcomes

Rhythm Control Options

Strategy Use
Electrical cardioversion Fastest and most reliable conversion
Chemical cardioversion Selected stable patients
Antiarrhythmic maintenance Prevents recurrence after conversion
Catheter ablation Symptomatic paroxysmal AF or selected persistent AF
Early rhythm control is increasingly favored in selected patients, especially when AF is recent, symptomatic, or contributing to heart failure.

Cardioversion Strategy

Electrical Cardioversion

  • Use synchronized cardioversion.
  • Common starting energy: 200 J biphasic.
  • Always sedate if the patient is conscious and time allows.
  • Anticoagulate when appropriate.

Duration-Based Strategy

AF Duration Strategy
<48 hours Cardioversion can often proceed without TEE if no recent stroke/TIA and patient is otherwise appropriate
≥48 hours or unknown duration TEE-guided cardioversion OR 3 weeks of therapeutic anticoagulation before cardioversion
After cardioversion Continue anticoagulation for at least 4 weeks when indicated by duration/risk
If AF duration is ≥48 hours or unknown, do not cardiovert electively without adequate anticoagulation or TEE excluding left atrial appendage thrombus.

Chemical Cardioversion Options

Medication Typical Use Key Warning
Flecainide Pill-in-pocket or selected acute cardioversion Avoid in CAD, LV dysfunction, or structural heart disease
Propafenone Pill-in-pocket or selected acute cardioversion Avoid in structural heart disease
Amiodarone Structural heart disease or HFrEF when needed Slower conversion; many long-term toxicities
Ibutilide Acute conversion in monitored setting QT prolongation and torsades risk

Anticoagulation: When, What, and When Not

Risk Factor Points

Stroke / TIA / thromboembolism 2
Vascular disease 1

When to Anticoagulate

Patient Recommendation
Men with score ≥2 Anticoagulation recommended
Women with score ≥3 Anticoagulation recommended
Men with score 1 Consider anticoagulation based on risk/benefit

Very low risk No anticoagulation

Agent Selection

Patient Type Preferred Avoid / Notes
Non-valvular AF DOAC Apixaban, rivaroxaban, dabigatran, edoxaban
Mechanical valve Warfarin DOACs contraindicated
Moderate-severe rheumatic mitral stenosis Warfarin DOACs generally avoided
Severe CKD Warfarin or selected dose-adjusted DOAC Depends on renal function and local guidance
DOACs are preferred for most patients with non-valvular AF because they are easier to use and have lower intracranial bleeding risk than warfarin.

When Not to Anticoagulate

  • Active major bleeding
  • Very recent intracranial hemorrhage
  • Uncorrectable high bleeding risk
  • Patient refusal
  • Goals of care inconsistent with anticoagulation

Peri-Procedural Pearls

  • Low bleeding risk procedures may not require interruption.
  • High bleeding risk procedures usually require holding DOACs based on renal function and agent.
  • Heparin bridging is generally not needed for DOAC interruption.

Long-Term Rhythm Maintenance

Antiarrhythmic Drug Therapy

Drug Class Best For Major Risks
Flecainide IC Paroxysmal AF with normal heart Proarrhythmia; avoid in CAD or structural heart disease
Propafenone IC Paroxysmal AF, pill-in-pocket Same structural heart disease warning
Sotalol III + beta blocker Selected patients with CAD or hypertension QT prolongation, torsades
Dofetilide III HFrEF or structural heart disease Torsades; inpatient initiation required
Amiodarone III Refractory AF or structural heart disease Pulmonary, thyroid, liver, skin, corneal toxicity
Dronedarone III Paroxysmal AF with fewer organ toxicities Contraindicated in HFrEF and permanent AF
Class IC agents such as flecainide and propafenone should be avoided in CAD, LV dysfunction, or structural heart disease.

Amiodarone Toxicities

  • Pulmonary fibrosis
  • Hypothyroidism or hyperthyroidism
  • Hepatotoxicity
  • Corneal deposits
  • Photosensitivity and blue-gray skin discoloration
  • Bradycardia and QT prolongation

Ablation Therapy

Pulmonary Vein Isolation

Pulmonary vein isolation is the main catheter ablation strategy for atrial fibrillation. It electrically isolates triggers arising from pulmonary vein myocardial sleeves.

Who Benefits?

  • Symptomatic paroxysmal AF despite antiarrhythmic therapy
  • Selected patients who prefer first-line ablation
  • AF contributing to heart failure or tachycardia-mediated cardiomyopathy
  • Selected persistent AF patients after shared decision-making

Limitations

  • AF can recur.
  • Repeat ablation may be needed.
  • Success is lower in long-standing persistent AF.
  • Anticoagulation decisions still depend on stroke risk, not just ablation success.
Successful ablation reduces AF burden, but it does not automatically eliminate the need for anticoagulation if stroke risk remains high.

Surgical Maze

Surgical ablation may be performed during cardiac surgery such as valve surgery or CABG, or as a standalone procedure in selected cases.

Refractory AF Management

When rate control fails and rhythm control with medications or ablation is unsuccessful, additional strategies may be needed.

AV Nodal Ablation + Permanent Pacemaker

  • Last-resort rate control strategy
  • Eliminates rapid ventricular response
  • Patient remains in AF
  • Requires permanent pacing
  • Stroke risk remains, so anticoagulation is still based on CHA₂DS₂-VASc

Combination Rate Control

  • Beta blocker plus digoxin
  • Careful monitoring for bradycardia
  • Useful when monotherapy fails

Left Atrial Appendage Occlusion

  • Stroke prevention option when long-term anticoagulation is contraindicated
  • Does not treat AF rhythm itself
  • Requires careful patient selection
Left atrial appendage occlusion is for stroke prevention, not rhythm control.

Special Populations

Post-Cardiac Surgery AF

  • Common after CABG or valve surgery
  • Often self-limited
  • Rate control is commonly first-line
  • Anticoagulate if AF persists or stroke risk warrants it
  • Many patients revert spontaneously within weeks

HFrEF

  • AF can worsen heart failure and cause tachycardia-mediated cardiomyopathy.
  • Avoid diltiazem and verapamil in decompensated HFrEF.
  • Digoxin or amiodarone may be useful when hypotension limits other drugs.
  • Dofetilide and amiodarone are common rhythm-control options in low EF.

CKD

  • Renal function affects DOAC selection and dosing.
  • Dabigatran is more dependent on renal clearance.
  • Warfarin is often used in severe CKD or dialysis depending on the patient.
  • Bleeding risk is higher and requires closer monitoring.

Elderly and Falls

  • Falls alone are not usually a reason to withhold anticoagulation.
  • DOACs are often preferred because of lower intracranial hemorrhage risk.
  • Use bleeding risk scores to identify modifiable risk factors, not to automatically deny anticoagulation.

WPW / Pre-Excited AF

Avoid AV nodal blockers in AF with WPW, including beta blockers, diltiazem, verapamil, digoxin, and adenosine. Use procainamide or cardioversion depending on stability.

Inpatient vs Outpatient Management

Inpatient Indications

  • Hemodynamic instability
  • Acute coronary syndrome
  • Acute decompensated heart failure
  • Stroke or TIA
  • Need for IV rate control
  • Need for urgent cardioversion
  • Severe electrolyte abnormality
  • Initiation of dofetilide
  • Concern for pre-excitation or dangerous rhythm mimic

Outpatient Management

  • Stable patient with controlled rate
  • Reliable follow-up
  • Anticoagulation initiation or monitoring
  • Elective cardioversion planning
  • Antiarrhythmic titration when safe
  • Referral to cardiology or electrophysiology when appropriate

Common Clinical Scenarios

Case 1: New-Onset AF, Less Than 48 Hours, Stable

A 58-year-old patient with hypertension presents with HR 140 and BP 110/70.

Management: IV rate control, evaluate triggers, assess CHA₂DS₂-VASc, start anticoagulation if indicated, and consider rhythm control if symptomatic.

Case 2: AF with RVR and Decompensated HFrEF

A 72-year-old patient with EF 25% presents with HR 160 and flash pulmonary edema.

Avoid diltiazem. Consider digoxin, amiodarone, diuresis, and cardioversion if unstable.

Case 3: AF More Than 48 Hours with TEE Showing LAA Thrombus

The patient is scheduled for cardioversion, but TEE shows left atrial appendage thrombus.

Cancel cardioversion, anticoagulate therapeutically, repeat imaging later, and cardiovert only if thrombus resolves.

Case 4: Young Patient with Symptomatic Paroxysmal AF and Normal Heart

A 35-year-old has monthly episodes of symptomatic AF and a structurally normal heart.

Options include pill-in-pocket flecainide or propafenone after safety evaluation, or early catheter ablation.

Case 5: AF with Mechanical Mitral Valve

A 65-year-old with a mechanical mitral valve has AF.

Warfarin only. Do not use DOACs for mechanical valves.

Landmark Trials

Trial Year Major Finding

ENGAGE AF-TIMI 48 2013 Edoxaban was non-inferior to warfarin

Clinical Pearls

  • Always confirm AF on ECG before treating.
  • The first decision is stable vs unstable.
  • Unstable AF requires synchronized cardioversion.
  • Search for triggers using PIRATES.
  • Lenient rate control is acceptable for many stable patients.
  • Diltiazem works quickly but should be avoided in decompensated HFrEF.
  • Digoxin is useful when hypotension limits other rate-control agents.
  • Correct potassium and magnesium.
  • AF duration determines cardioversion anticoagulation strategy.
  • AF ≥48 hours or unknown duration requires TEE or 3 weeks therapeutic anticoagulation before elective cardioversion.
  • Continue anticoagulation after cardioversion when indicated.
  • DOACs are preferred for most non-valvular AF.
  • Warfarin is required for mechanical valves.
  • Falls alone are not a contraindication to anticoagulation.
  • Class IC agents should be avoided in structural heart disease.
  • Amiodarone is effective but toxic long term.
  • AF with WPW is dangerous: avoid AV nodal blockers.
  • Ablation reduces AF burden but does not automatically remove anticoagulation need.

Bottom Line

Atrial fibrillation management begins with stability. Unstable patients need immediate synchronized cardioversion. Stable patients require rate control or rhythm control based on symptoms, duration, comorbidities, and goals. Always identify reversible triggers, assess stroke risk with CHA₂DS₂-VASc, and choose anticoagulation appropriately. DOACs are preferred for most non-valvular AF, while warfarin is required for mechanical valves and moderate-severe rheumatic mitral stenosis. Rhythm control is especially useful in symptomatic patients, early AF, young patients, and AF-related heart failure. Catheter ablation is most effective for symptomatic paroxysmal AF, but anticoagulation decisions remain based on stroke risk.

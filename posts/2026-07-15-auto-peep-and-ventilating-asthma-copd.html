Auto-PEEP and Mechanical Ventilation in Severe Asthma and COPD

Added July 15, 2026 • Pulmonary and Critical Care • ~30 min read

A practical guide to dynamic hyperinflation, intrinsic PEEP measurement, ventilator settings for obstructive physiology, permissive hypercapnia, hypotension after intubation, external PEEP, bronchodilator delivery, and emergency management of severe air trapping.

Clinical Overview

In obstructive respiratory failure, the central mechanical problem is insufficient time to exhale. The next breath begins before the previous breath has ended, causing dynamic hyperinflation, intrinsic PEEP, rising intrathoracic pressure, hypotension, barotrauma, and difficult triggering.

Reduce Minute Ventilation + Increase Expiratory Time + Treat Obstruction

Recognizing Auto-PEEP

  • Expiratory flow does not return to zero before the next breath.
  • Elevated total PEEP on expiratory hold.
  • High peak pressure with lower plateau pressure.
  • Hypotension that worsens after intubation.
  • Ineffective triggering and patient distress.
  • Hyperinflated chest and reduced breath sounds.

Peak vs Plateau Pressure

Peak pressure reflects airway resistance plus elastic pressure. Plateau pressure approximates alveolar pressure when inspiratory flow is paused. Severe bronchospasm can produce very high peak pressure while plateau pressure remains acceptable.

A high peak pressure alone does not prove alveolar overdistention. Measure plateau pressure.

Initial Ventilator Strategy

  • Low respiratory rate, commonly around 8–12/min depending on physiology.
  • Tidal volume about 6–8 mL/kg predicted body weight.
  • High inspiratory flow to shorten inspiratory time.
  • Long expiratory time, often I:E 1:3 to 1:5 or longer.
  • Minimal effective external PEEP in passive severe asthma; individualized PEEP in COPD and spontaneous triggering.
  • Accept hypercapnia if pH and contraindications permit.

Permissive Hypercapnia

Trying to normalize PaCO₂ can require an unsafe respiratory rate or tidal volume. Accept a higher PaCO₂ while treating bronchospasm and maintaining a tolerable pH.

  • Avoid severe acidemia when possible.
  • Use caution with intracranial hypertension, severe pulmonary hypertension, pregnancy, and unstable arrhythmia.
  • Treat fever, agitation, and metabolic demand that increase CO₂ production.

Post-Intubation Hypotension

  1. Disconnect the ventilator briefly if severe dynamic hyperinflation and peri-arrest hypotension are suspected.
  2. Allow prolonged exhalation while providing manual support carefully.
  3. Reduce respiratory rate and tidal volume.
  4. Treat bronchospasm and exclude tension pneumothorax.
  5. Use vasopressors and judicious fluid according to hemodynamics.
In an intubated obstructive patient with sudden hypotension, think dynamic hyperinflation and tension pneumothorax immediately.

Treating Obstruction

  • Frequent inhaled short-acting beta agonist
  • Inhaled anticholinergic
  • Systemic corticosteroid
  • IV magnesium for severe asthma
  • Treat infection when indicated
  • Remove mucus plugs and optimize humidification
  • Consider ketamine or inhaled anesthetic rescue only in selected refractory cases with specialist support

External PEEP

In a spontaneously triggering COPD patient, external PEEP may reduce the effort needed to overcome intrinsic PEEP. Increase cautiously while watching total PEEP, plateau pressure, expiratory flow, tidal volume, and hemodynamics. In passive severe asthma, unnecessary PEEP may worsen hyperinflation.

Synchrony and Sedation

  • Provide adequate analgesia and sedation during the most unstable phase.
  • Treat double triggering by reducing drive and adjusting timing, not simply increasing tidal volume.
  • Use neuromuscular blockade briefly when dangerous dyssynchrony persists despite correction.
  • Transition toward spontaneous modes as obstruction improves.

Signs of Improvement

  • Expiratory flow returns toward zero.
  • Total PEEP falls.
  • Peak pressure decreases.
  • Wheezing and resistance improve.
  • Ventilator triggering becomes easier.
  • PaCO₂ stabilizes with lower support.

Common Pitfalls

  • High respiratory rate to normalize PaCO₂
  • Low inspiratory flow causing long inspiratory time
  • Treating high peak pressure as alveolar overdistention without plateau measurement
  • Adding PEEP blindly
  • Missing tension pneumothorax
  • Failing to disconnect briefly during hyperinflation-related peri-arrest shock

Bottom Line

The ventilator treatment for severe obstruction is controlled hypoventilation: low rate, high inspiratory flow, long expiratory time, safe tidal volume, and acceptance of hypercapnia while bronchodilation and the underlying cause are treated.

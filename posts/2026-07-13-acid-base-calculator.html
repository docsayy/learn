How the Calculator Interprets Acid–Base Disorders

1. Confirm the sample and internal consistency

Venous pH and bicarbonate often approximate arterial values sufficiently for many metabolic assessments, but venous PCO₂ has wider disagreement with arterial PCO₂. Exact respiratory-compensation assessment may therefore require an ABG when the clinical decision depends on the arterial carbon-dioxide level. PaO₂ and the PaO₂/FiO₂ ratio require an arterial sample.

The calculator also reconstructs bicarbonate from the Henderson–Hasselbalch relationship. A large difference from the reported blood-gas bicarbonate suggests values from different time points, transcription error, or a measurement/calculation discrepancy.

Calculated HCO₃⁻ = 0.03 × PCO₂ × 10(pH − 6.1)

4. Calculate and correct the anion gap

AG = Na⁺ − (Cl⁻ + serum HCO₃⁻)
Albumin-corrected AG = AG + 2.5 × (4 − albumin)

The normal anion gap varies by analyzer and laboratory. This calculator therefore asks for the laboratory's upper-normal AG rather than assuming that every value above a fixed universal number is abnormal. Serum chemistry total CO₂ is preferred for the AG because it was measured with sodium and chloride on the same panel.

5. Use delta analysis cautiously

ΔAG = corrected AG − lab normal AG
ΔHCO₃⁻ = 24 − serum HCO₃⁻
Delta ratio = ΔAG ÷ ΔHCO₃⁻

A ratio below approximately 0.8 supports an additional normal-gap acidosis; a ratio around 0.8–2 can fit an isolated high-gap acidosis; and a ratio above 2 raises concern for concurrent metabolic alkalosis or chronic respiratory acidosis. These are approximate teaching ranges, not absolute diagnostic boundaries. Baseline AG, timing, renal handling of organic anions, and treatment can materially change the ratio.

6. Interpret optional osmolar and urine anion gaps in context

Calculated osmolality = 2 × Na + glucose/18 + BUN/2.8 + ethanol/4.6
Urine AG = urine Na + urine K − urine Cl
  • An osmolar gap above approximately 10 mOsm/kg is often considered elevated, but the reference range and formula vary. A normal gap does not exclude a late toxic-alcohol presentation.
  • In NAGMA, a clearly negative urine AG supports appropriate ammonium excretion and often an extrarenal bicarbonate loss such as diarrhea.
  • A positive urine AG may support impaired renal acid excretion, but interpretation is unreliable with low distal sodium delivery, unusual urinary anions, ketoacid excretion, or recent treatment.

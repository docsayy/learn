Oxygen-Delivery Devices and High-Flow Nasal Cannula

Added July 15, 2026 • Pulmonary and Critical Care • ~30 min read

A bedside guide to selecting oxygen devices, setting oxygen targets, understanding delivered FiO₂, starting and monitoring high-flow nasal cannula, using the ROX index appropriately, and escalating support before respiratory fatigue or peri-intubation collapse occurs.

Clinical Overview

Oxygen is a drug: it has an indication, a target, a delivery system, and toxicity. The correct device must match inspiratory demand, required FiO₂, work of breathing, secretion burden, and the likelihood that noninvasive support will fail.

Target the Physiology—not a Normal Number
  • Correct hypoxemia while treating the cause.
  • Use the least invasive device that reliably achieves the target.
  • Reassess respiratory rate, effort, mentation, gas exchange, and hemodynamics—not saturation alone.

Oxygen Targets

  • Most acutely ill adults: commonly target SpO₂ about 92–96%.
  • Known or suspected chronic hypercapnic respiratory failure: commonly target 88–92% while obtaining blood gas data.
  • Carbon monoxide poisoning, pregnancy, selected neurologic or ischemic states, and peri-intubation care require syndrome-specific targets.
Avoid both untreated hypoxemia and prolonged hyperoxemia. A saturation of 100% on a high FiO₂ can conceal worsening gas exchange.

Why Delivered FiO₂ Is Variable

Low-flow systems provide less flow than many distressed patients inhale. Room air is entrained around the device, so the true FiO₂ falls as inspiratory demand rises. Device flow, mask fit, respiratory pattern, and minute ventilation all matter.

Do not document “6 L equals 44% FiO₂” as an exact value. With low-flow devices, it is only an approximation.

HFNC Physiology

  • Heated humidified flow that can match or exceed inspiratory demand.
  • More stable delivered FiO₂ than conventional low-flow oxygen.
  • Washout of nasopharyngeal dead space.
  • Small flow-dependent positive airway pressure effect.
  • Reduced inspiratory resistance and improved secretion tolerance.

HFNC primarily supports oxygenation and work of breathing. It is not equivalent to pressure-support ventilation and may be inadequate for severe ventilatory pump failure.

Starting HFNC

  • Common starting flow: 40–60 L/min in severe hypoxemia, adjusted for comfort and effort.
  • Set FiO₂ high enough initially to reach the target, then reduce promptly.
  • Use heated humidification and appropriately sized cannula.
  • Treat the underlying disease simultaneously.

Flow and FiO₂ solve different problems: flow better matches inspiratory demand and may reduce effort; FiO₂ corrects oxygen concentration.

Monitoring Response

  • Respiratory rate and accessory-muscle use
  • SpO₂ and FiO₂ requirement
  • Mental status and ability to protect the airway
  • Hemodynamics
  • ABG or VBG when hypercapnia or severe acid-base disease is possible
  • Trajectory during the first 30–120 minutes
ROX Index = (SpO₂ / FiO₂) / Respiratory Rate

The ROX index can support trending in acute hypoxemic respiratory failure, but thresholds vary by timing and population. It must never replace bedside judgment.

Recognizing HFNC Failure

  • Rising respiratory rate or visible fatigue
  • Escalating FiO₂ despite high flow
  • Worsening acidosis or hypercapnia
  • Altered mental status
  • Shock or unstable arrhythmia
  • Inability to clear secretions
  • Persistent severe hypoxemia
Delayed intubation after obvious noninvasive-support failure is associated with difficult, high-risk airway management. Escalate while there is time to prepare.

Special Situations

  • Cardiogenic pulmonary edema: CPAP or bilevel NIV often provides more useful positive pressure than HFNC when blood pressure and mentation permit.
  • COPD with acute respiratory acidosis: bilevel NIV is generally preferred because ventilatory assistance is needed.
  • Immunocompromised hypoxemia: HFNC may improve comfort, but reassess frequently for failure.
  • Apneic oxygenation: HFNC or nasal cannula can remain in place during laryngoscopy when appropriate.

Bedside Checklist

  • Define the oxygen target.
  • Identify whether the problem is oxygenation, ventilation, or both.
  • Check the device connection, flow, reservoir, humidifier, and cannula position.
  • Reassess within minutes after escalation.
  • Document the escalation threshold and airway plan.

Common Pitfalls

  • Treating saturation while ignoring fatigue
  • Leaving FiO₂ at 1.0 after stabilization
  • Using HFNC for profound hypercapnic coma
  • Relying on a single ROX value
  • Assuming conventional-device FiO₂ is exact
  • Failing to prepare intubation during a failing trial

Bottom Line

Choose oxygen support according to inspiratory demand and physiology. HFNC is valuable for acute hypoxemic failure, but success is defined by improving effort and trajectory—not saturation alone. Set explicit reassessment and intubation thresholds.

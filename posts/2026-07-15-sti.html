Sexually Transmitted Infections: The Complete Advanced Resident Guide

Added July 14, 2026 • Infectious Disease • ~52 min read

Sexually transmitted infections are frequently asymptomatic, may involve several anatomic sites simultaneously, and can cause infertility, ectopic pregnancy, chronic pelvic pain, neurologic disease, congenital infection, malignancy, and increased HIV transmission. Effective care requires a nonjudgmental sexual history, testing every exposed site, recognizing syndrome-specific emergencies, using the correct antimicrobial regimen, treating recent partners, arranging repeat testing, reporting required infections, and offering HIV prevention, vaccination, and doxycycline post-exposure prophylaxis when appropriate.

Clinical Overview

Sexually transmitted infections may involve the:

  • Urethra
  • Cervix
  • Vagina
  • Rectum
  • Pharynx
  • Skin
  • Pelvic organs
  • Epididymis and testes
  • Joints
  • Central nervous system

Many infections are asymptomatic. Absence of discharge, dysuria, pelvic pain, ulcers, or rash does not exclude infection.

Sexual Behavior Determines Testing Sites—not Gender Identity Alone

Major Clinical Questions

  1. Which anatomic sites were exposed?
  2. Does the patient have urethritis, cervicitis, vaginitis, ulcer disease, PID, epididymitis, or proctitis?
  3. Is empiric treatment required before test results return?
  4. Could pregnancy alter treatment?
  5. Does the patient need HIV PEP or PrEP?
  6. Which recent partners require evaluation and treatment?
  7. Is expedited partner therapy appropriate and legal locally?
  8. Does the infection require public-health reporting?

Potential Long-Term Complications

  • Pelvic inflammatory disease
  • Tubal infertility
  • Ectopic pregnancy
  • Chronic pelvic pain
  • Epididymal injury
  • Disseminated infection
  • Neurologic and cardiovascular syphilis
  • Congenital infection
  • HPV-associated malignancy
  • Increased HIV acquisition and transmission

Core Clinical Principles

1. Use a Nonjudgmental Approach

Normalize the discussion:

“I ask all patients these questions because sexual health affects screening, vaccines, and treatment.”

2. Test Every Exposed Site

A urine test does not identify every:

  • Rectal infection
  • Pharyngeal infection
  • Cervical infection

3. Treat Syndromes Promptly When Delay Is Harmful

  • Pelvic inflammatory disease
  • Epididymitis
  • Suspected primary syphilis
  • Disseminated gonococcal infection
  • Sexual assault when follow-up is uncertain

4. Treat Partners

Most repeat chlamydia and gonorrhea infections are caused by reinfection from an untreated or newly infected partner rather than antibiotic failure.

5. Retest After Treatment

A test of cure and retesting for reinfection are different:

  • Test of cure: determines whether treatment eradicated the infection.
  • Retesting: detects reinfection several months later.

6. Offer Prevention

  • Condoms and barrier protection
  • HIV PrEP
  • HIV PEP after qualifying exposure
  • Doxycycline PEP for selected patients
  • HPV, hepatitis A, hepatitis B, and mpox vaccination when indicated

Do Not Infer Anatomy

Ask which organs are present and which were exposed. Relevant anatomy may include:

  • Penis
  • Vagina
  • Cervix
  • Rectum
  • Pharynx

Anatomic-Site Testing

Urogenital Testing

  • First-catch urine is commonly used for persons with a penis.
  • A vaginal swab is generally the preferred urogenital NAAT specimen for persons with a vagina.
  • Endocervical specimens may be used when a pelvic examination is performed.

Rectal Testing

Obtain rectal gonorrhea and chlamydia NAAT after receptive anal exposure, regardless of symptoms.

Pharyngeal Testing

Obtain pharyngeal gonorrhea NAAT after oral exposure.

Routine pharyngeal chlamydia screening is generally not recommended, but chlamydia detected while testing for pharyngeal gonorrhea should be treated.

Self-Collected Swabs

Patient-collected vaginal, rectal, and pharyngeal swabs are acceptable for appropriately validated NAAT platforms and may improve screening acceptance.

A negative urine NAAT does not exclude rectal or pharyngeal gonorrhea or chlamydia.

Selected STI Screening Recommendations

Population Screening Framework
Sexually active women younger than 25 Annual chlamydia and gonorrhea screening
Women 25 and older with increased risk Annual chlamydia and gonorrhea screening
Sexually active MSM At least annual gonorrhea, chlamydia, syphilis, and HIV testing at exposed sites
MSM with ongoing increased risk Testing every 3–6 months
Transgender and gender-diverse persons Screen according to anatomy, sexual practices, and exposure
Persons with HIV Screen at entry to care and at least annually; more often according to risk
Pregnancy Early HIV, syphilis, hepatitis B, and hepatitis C screening during every pregnancy
Pregnant persons younger than 25 or at increased risk Chlamydia and gonorrhea screening, with indicated repeat testing

Routine HSV Screening

Routine serologic screening for HSV-2 in the general asymptomatic population is not recommended.

HIV Screening

HIV testing should be offered broadly and repeated according to ongoing exposure risk.

Diagnostic Testing

Nucleic Acid Amplification Tests

NAAT is preferred for:

  • Gonorrhea
  • Chlamydia
  • Trichomoniasis
  • Mycoplasma genitalium
  • HSV from active lesions

Culture

Culture remains important for:

  • Suspected gonorrhea treatment failure
  • Gonococcal antimicrobial susceptibility testing
  • Selected disseminated infections
  • Unusual or refractory disease

Microscopy

  • Gram stain may diagnose gonococcal urethritis in symptomatic men.
  • Wet mount may show motile trichomonads but has limited sensitivity.
  • Clue cells support bacterial vaginosis.
  • Yeast forms may support candidiasis.

Serologic Testing

  • Syphilis requires treponemal and nontreponemal testing.
  • Type-specific HSV serology has selected indications.
  • HIV antigen/antibody testing detects most established infections.
  • HIV RNA testing is useful during suspected acute infection.

When to Treat Empirically

Treat Before Results When:

  • PID is clinically suspected.
  • Epididymitis is likely.
  • Gonococcal urethritis is strongly suspected.
  • Primary syphilis has a compatible lesion and epidemiology.
  • Sexual assault follow-up may be difficult.
  • The patient is unlikely to return.
  • There is a known infected partner.

Consider Waiting for Results When:

  • The patient is stable.
  • Objective urethral or cervical inflammation is absent.
  • Follow-up is reliable.
  • Unnecessary antimicrobial exposure can be avoided.
Whenever possible, collect all indicated anatomic-site specimens before giving empiric antibiotics.

Penicillin and Cephalosporin Allergy

Clarify the Reaction

  • Isolated nausea
  • Remote childhood rash
  • Urticaria
  • Anaphylaxis
  • Stevens–Johnson syndrome
  • DRESS
  • Organ-specific immune injury

Third-Generation Cephalosporins

Cross-reactivity between penicillin allergy and ceftriaxone is low, particularly when the reported penicillin reaction was remote, mild, or non-IgE mediated.

Severe Delayed Reactions

Avoid beta-lactam challenge or routine substitution in patients with Stevens–Johnson syndrome, toxic epidermal necrolysis, DRESS, or severe organ-specific reactions without specialist guidance.

Syphilis in Pregnancy

Penicillin is the only established treatment that prevents congenital syphilis. A pregnant patient with penicillin allergy should undergo desensitization and receive penicillin.

Urethritis

Clinical Features

  • Dysuria
  • Urethral pruritus
  • Mucoid or purulent discharge
  • Meatal erythema

Objective Evidence

  • Visible urethral discharge
  • Positive leukocyte esterase in first-void urine
  • At least 10 WBCs per high-power field in urine sediment
  • Urethral Gram stain showing inflammation

Testing

  • Gonorrhea NAAT
  • Chlamydia NAAT
  • M. genitalium NAAT for recurrent or persistent nongonococcal urethritis
  • Trichomonas testing in selected patients
  • HIV and syphilis testing

Empiric Gonorrhea and Chlamydia Treatment

When urethritis is present and gonorrhea has not been excluded:

Ceftriaxone 500 mg IM Once
+ Doxycycline 100 mg Orally Twice Daily for 7 Days

Use ceftriaxone 1 g when body weight is at least 150 kg.

Nongonococcal Urethritis

Doxycycline 100 mg Orally Twice Daily for 7 Days

Persistent Symptoms

Confirm objective inflammation before giving more antibiotics. Consider:

  • Nonadherence
  • Re-exposure
  • M. genitalium
  • Trichomoniasis
  • HSV
  • Chronic pelvic pain syndrome

Cervicitis

Clinical Features

  • Mucopurulent endocervical discharge
  • Easily induced cervical bleeding
  • Postcoital bleeding
  • Intermenstrual bleeding
  • Abnormal vaginal discharge

Testing

  • Vaginal or cervical gonorrhea and chlamydia NAAT
  • Trichomonas NAAT
  • Evaluation for bacterial vaginosis
  • M. genitalium testing for persistent or recurrent cervicitis
  • Assessment for PID

Recommended Empiric Regimen

Doxycycline 100 mg Orally Twice Daily for 7 Days

Add ceftriaxone when:

  • Gonorrhea risk is substantial.
  • Local prevalence is high.
  • Follow-up is uncertain.
  • Gonorrhea testing is positive.

Alternative

  • Azithromycin 1 g orally once

Persistent Cervicitis

Repeated empiric antibiotics without objective inflammation or targeted testing may cause harm. Consider M. genitalium and noninfectious cervical pathology.

Do Not Diagnose by Appearance Alone

Symptoms overlap considerably. Use:

  • Vaginal pH
  • Microscopy
  • Whiff testing
  • NAAT or molecular panels
  • Cervical examination

Genital Ulcer Disease

Major Infectious Causes

  • HSV-1 or HSV-2
  • Primary syphilis
  • Chancroid
  • Lymphogranuloma venereum
  • Granuloma inguinale

Noninfectious Causes

  • Trauma
  • Behçet disease
  • Fixed drug eruption
  • Malignancy
  • Inflammatory dermatoses

Recommended Evaluation

  • HSV NAAT from the lesion
  • Syphilis serology
  • Direct testing for syphilis when available
  • HIV testing
  • Chancroid testing when epidemiologically plausible
Clinical appearance alone is unreliable. Painful grouped ulcers suggest herpes and a painless chancre suggests syphilis, but atypical presentations and coinfections are common.

Sexually Transmitted Proctitis and Proctocolitis

Symptoms

  • Rectal pain
  • Tenesmus
  • Discharge
  • Bleeding
  • Constipation
  • Perianal ulcers

Evaluation

  • Anoscopy when feasible
  • Rectal gonorrhea and chlamydia NAAT
  • HSV NAAT from ulcers
  • Syphilis testing
  • HIV testing
  • Stool studies when enteritis or proctocolitis is present

Empiric Acute Proctitis Regimen

Ceftriaxone 500 mg IM Once
+ Doxycycline 100 mg Orally Twice Daily for 7 Days

Possible LGV

Extend doxycycline to 21 days when:

  • Rectal chlamydia is detected
  • Severe tenesmus is present
  • Bloody discharge is present
  • Rectal ulcers are present

Possible HSV

Add genital-herpes therapy when painful perianal or rectal ulcers are present.

Sexually Transmitted Enteric Infection

Consider:

  • Shigella
  • Campylobacter
  • Giardia
  • Entamoeba histolytica
  • Cryptosporidium

Gonorrhea

Clinical Syndromes

  • Urethritis
  • Cervicitis
  • Proctitis
  • Pharyngitis
  • PID
  • Epididymitis
  • Conjunctivitis
  • Disseminated infection

Diagnosis

  • NAAT is preferred for routine diagnosis.
  • Test all exposed sites.
  • Obtain culture when treatment failure is suspected.
  • Culture is required for antimicrobial susceptibility testing.

Antimicrobial Resistance

Gonorrhea has developed resistance to:

  • Penicillin
  • Tetracyclines
  • Fluoroquinolones
  • Macrolides
  • Earlier cephalosporin regimens
Ceftriaxone is the preferred treatment for uncomplicated gonorrhea at urogenital, rectal, and pharyngeal sites.

Treatment of Uncomplicated Gonorrhea

Cervical, Urethral, or Rectal Infection

Ceftriaxone 500 mg IM in a Single Dose
for Body Weight Below 150 kg
Ceftriaxone 1 g IM in a Single Dose
for Body Weight 150 kg or Greater

When Chlamydia Has Not Been Excluded

  • Add doxycycline 100 mg orally twice daily for 7 days.
  • Use pregnancy-appropriate chlamydia treatment when pregnant.

If Ceftriaxone Is Not Available

  • Cefixime 800 mg orally once is an alternative.

Cefixime is less reliable for pharyngeal disease and is not preferred when ceftriaxone can be administered.

Severe Cephalosporin Allergy

  • Gentamicin 240 mg IM once
  • Plus azithromycin 2 g orally once

Consult an STI or infectious-disease specialist for severe allergy, pregnancy, pharyngeal infection, or suspected resistance.

Additional Testing

Every person diagnosed with gonorrhea should be tested for:

  • Chlamydia
  • Syphilis
  • HIV

Pharyngeal Gonorrhea

Important Features

  • Frequently asymptomatic
  • More difficult to eradicate
  • Important reservoir for transmission
  • Potential site for antimicrobial-resistance development

Treatment

  • Ceftriaxone 500 mg IM once if below 150 kg
  • Ceftriaxone 1 g IM once if at least 150 kg

Test of Cure

Repeat Culture or NAAT 7–14 Days After Treatment

Testing exactly at seven days may occasionally detect nonviable nucleic acid. If NAAT remains positive:

  • Obtain confirmatory culture when possible.
  • Perform susceptibility testing on positive culture.
  • Assess for reinfection.

Suspected Gonorrhea Treatment Failure

Consider When:

  • Symptoms persist for three to five days after treatment.
  • A test of cure remains positive.
  • The patient reports no sexual re-exposure.
  • Partners were treated.
  • Adherence is confirmed.

Most Apparent Failures Are Reinfections

Before assuming resistance, ask about:

  • Sex before the abstinence period ended
  • Untreated partners
  • New partners
  • Incorrect dose

Required Actions

  • Obtain culture from every infected site.
  • Request antimicrobial susceptibility testing.
  • Obtain simultaneous NAAT.
  • Consult an STI or infectious-disease expert.
  • Notify local or state public health promptly.
  • Ensure partner evaluation.
Do not repeatedly retreat possible resistant gonorrhea using NAAT alone. Culture and susceptibility testing are essential.

Disseminated Gonococcal Infection

Clinical Patterns

Arthritis-Dermatitis Syndrome

  • Fever
  • Migratory polyarthralgia
  • Tenosynovitis
  • Few pustular or vesiculopustular lesions

Localized Septic Arthritis

  • Purulent monoarthritis or oligoarthritis
  • Often without genital symptoms

Diagnostic Evaluation

  • Blood cultures
  • Synovial-fluid cell count and culture
  • NAAT and culture of exposed mucosal sites
  • Evaluation for meningitis or endocarditis when clinically indicated

Initial Treatment

Ceftriaxone 1 g IV or IM Every 24 Hours

Add doxycycline for seven days if chlamydia has not been excluded.

Management

  • Hospitalize initially in many cases.
  • Obtain infectious-disease consultation.
  • Drain purulent joints.
  • Transition to oral therapy only after clinical improvement and susceptibility confirmation.
  • Complete at least seven total days for uncomplicated arthritis-dermatitis disease.

Complicated DGI

Meningitis and endocarditis require longer, syndrome-specific treatment.

Chlamydia

Clinical Syndromes

  • Urethritis
  • Cervicitis
  • PID
  • Epididymitis
  • Proctitis
  • Reactive arthritis
  • Neonatal conjunctivitis and pneumonia

Diagnosis

  • NAAT from every exposed site
  • Vaginal swab preferred for urogenital screening in persons with a vagina
  • First-catch urine commonly used for persons with a penis

Why Rectal Infection Matters

Rectal chlamydia may be present:

  • Without rectal symptoms
  • Without reported receptive anal exposure
  • Alongside urogenital infection

Chlamydia Treatment

Preferred Regimen

Doxycycline 100 mg Orally Twice Daily for 7 Days

Alternatives

  • Azithromycin 1 g orally once
  • Levofloxacin 500 mg orally once daily for 7 days

Why Doxycycline Is Preferred

Doxycycline has greater efficacy for:

  • Rectal chlamydia
  • Urogenital chlamydia in men
  • Potential concomitant rectal infection in women

When Azithromycin May Be Used

  • Major concern for adherence to seven days of doxycycline
  • Pregnancy
  • Doxycycline intolerance

Test of Cure

Routine test of cure is not required in nonpregnant persons unless:

  • Adherence is uncertain.
  • Symptoms persist.
  • Reinfection is suspected.
Do not perform chlamydia NAAT earlier than four weeks after treatment when assessing cure because residual nonviable organisms may cause a false-positive result.

Retesting

  • Retest approximately three months after treatment.

Chlamydia in Pregnancy

Recommended Regimen

Azithromycin 1 g Orally in a Single Dose

Alternative

  • Amoxicillin 500 mg orally three times daily for 7 days

Test of Cure

NAAT Approximately 4 Weeks After Treatment

Additional Follow-Up

  • Retest three months after treatment.
  • Repeat testing later in pregnancy when ongoing risk exists.
  • Ensure partner treatment.

Lymphogranuloma Venereum

Cause

  • Invasive C. trachomatis serovars L1, L2, and L3

Clinical Presentations

  • Proctocolitis
  • Severe rectal pain and tenesmus
  • Bloody or mucous discharge
  • Rectal ulcers
  • Painful inguinal or femoral lymphadenopathy
  • Buboes
  • Transient genital ulcer

Diagnosis

Diagnosis is usually clinical plus:

  • Positive rectal or lesion chlamydia NAAT
  • Exclusion of alternative causes

Treatment

Doxycycline 100 mg Orally Twice Daily for 21 Days

Complications

  • Fistula
  • Stricture
  • Chronic colorectal inflammation
  • Genital lymphedema

Syphilis

Stages

  • Primary
  • Secondary
  • Early latent
  • Late latent or unknown duration
  • Tertiary
  • Neurosyphilis, ocular syphilis, and otosyphilis

Primary Syphilis

  • Classically painless chancre
  • Regional lymphadenopathy
  • Lesions may be multiple, painful, or atypical

Secondary Syphilis

  • Diffuse rash, including palms and soles
  • Generalized lymphadenopathy
  • Condyloma lata
  • Mucous patches
  • Fever and systemic symptoms
  • Patchy alopecia

Latent Syphilis

  • Reactive serology without clinical evidence of active primary, secondary, or tertiary disease

Neurosyphilis Can Occur at Any Stage

Do not assume neurologic syphilis occurs only years after untreated infection.

Syphilis Serologic Testing

Nontreponemal Tests

  • RPR
  • VDRL

These provide a quantitative titer used to:

  • Assess disease activity
  • Monitor treatment response
  • Detect reinfection

Treponemal Tests

  • Treponemal enzyme or chemiluminescence immunoassay
  • TP-PA
  • FTA-ABS

Treponemal tests frequently remain positive for life and should generally not be used to assess treatment response.

Traditional Algorithm

RPR or VDRL → Confirm With Treponemal Test

Reverse Algorithm

Treponemal Screening Test → Quantitative RPR → Second Treponemal Test if Discordant

Prozone Effect

Very high antibody concentrations may produce a falsely negative nontreponemal test. Ask the laboratory to dilute the sample when clinical suspicion is high.

Clinically Meaningful Titer Change

Fourfold Change = Two Dilution Steps
Example: 1:32 to 1:8

Primary, Secondary, and Early Latent Syphilis

Recommended Regimen

Benzathine Penicillin G 2.4 Million Units IM Once

Additional Doses Are Not Beneficial

Do not give three weekly doses for properly staged:

  • Primary syphilis
  • Secondary syphilis
  • Early latent syphilis

Nonpregnant Penicillin Allergy

A possible alternative for primary or secondary syphilis is:

  • Doxycycline 100 mg orally twice daily for 14 days

Close clinical and serologic follow-up is required.

Follow-Up

  • Clinical and RPR or VDRL assessment at 6 and 12 months
  • More frequent follow-up when reinfection risk is high

Treatment Failure or Reinfection

Consider with:

  • Recurrent symptoms
  • Sustained fourfold titer increase
  • Inadequate titer decline in the appropriate context

Latent Syphilis

Early Latent Syphilis

Evidence of acquisition within the preceding 12 months may include:

  • Documented seroconversion
  • Fourfold sustained rise in titer
  • Recent primary or secondary symptoms
  • Partner with early syphilis
  • Only possible exposure occurring during the prior year

Early Latent Treatment

  • Benzathine penicillin G 2.4 million units IM once

Late Latent or Unknown Duration

Benzathine Penicillin G 2.4 Million Units IM Weekly for 3 Doses

Total dose:

  • 7.2 million units

Do Not Stage by Titer Alone

A high RPR titer may suggest recent infection but cannot by itself establish early latent syphilis.

Follow-Up

  • Quantitative nontreponemal titers at 6, 12, and 24 months

Delayed Weekly Dose

An interval of 7–9 days is preferred. In pregnancy, a delay exceeding nine days requires restarting the complete series.

Neurosyphilis

Potential Presentations

  • Meningitis
  • Cranial neuropathy
  • Stroke
  • Altered mental status
  • Tabes dorsalis
  • General paresis
  • Sensory abnormalities

Who Needs CSF Evaluation?

Perform lumbar puncture when syphilis is accompanied by:

  • Cranial nerve dysfunction
  • Meningitis
  • Stroke
  • Altered mental status
  • Motor or sensory neurologic deficits

Routine Lumbar Puncture Is Not Required

CSF abnormalities may occur during early syphilis without neurologic symptoms. Routine CSF testing is not recommended in neurologically asymptomatic patients.

Treatment

Aqueous Crystalline Penicillin G
18–24 Million Units per Day IV
for 10–14 Days

Administer as:

  • 3–4 million units IV every four hours
  • Or continuous infusion

After Neurosyphilis Therapy

One to three weekly doses of benzathine penicillin G may be considered afterward to provide a duration comparable with latent-syphilis treatment.

Ocular Syphilis and Otosyphilis

Ocular Manifestations

  • Uveitis
  • Iritis
  • Optic neuritis
  • Neuroretinitis
  • Vision loss

Management

Suspected ocular syphilis requires urgent ophthalmologic evaluation and should be treated using the neurosyphilis regimen even when CSF findings are normal.

CSF Evaluation in Ocular Syphilis

CSF examination may be unnecessary when:

  • Ocular abnormalities are confirmed
  • Neurologic and cranial-nerve examination is normal

Otosyphilis

  • Sensorineural hearing loss
  • Tinnitus
  • Vertigo

Treat using the neurosyphilis regimen and involve otolaryngology.

CSF evaluation usually does not alter management in isolated otosyphilis with a normal neurologic examination.

Syphilis in Pregnancy

Core Principle

Penicillin Is the Only Proven Treatment for Preventing Congenital Syphilis

Penicillin Allergy

  • Desensitize.
  • Treat with the stage-appropriate penicillin regimen.

Do Not Use as Substitutes

  • Doxycycline
  • Azithromycin
  • Unvalidated alternative regimens

Maternal Screening

  • Screen at the first prenatal visit.
  • Repeat around 28 weeks and at delivery when individual or geographic risk is present.
  • Follow applicable state requirements.

Delayed Late-Latent Doses

Pregnant patients with more than nine days between weekly benzathine penicillin doses should restart the complete three-dose series.

Fetal Evaluation

Second-half pregnancy infection may warrant fetal ultrasound for:

  • Hepatomegaly
  • Ascites
  • Hydrops
  • Placental thickening

Ultrasound should not delay maternal treatment.

Jarisch–Herxheimer Reaction

Clinical Features

  • Fever
  • Chills
  • Myalgia
  • Headache
  • Transient worsening of lesions

Timing

  • Usually begins during the first 24 hours after syphilis treatment

Mechanism

This is an inflammatory response to treatment and is not an allergic reaction to penicillin.

Management

  • Supportive care
  • Antipyretics
  • Clinical observation
The Jarisch–Herxheimer reaction may precipitate contractions or fetal distress during pregnancy, but this risk is not a reason to delay syphilis treatment.

Genital Herpes

Organisms

  • HSV-2
  • HSV-1

Clinical Features

  • Painful vesicles or ulcers
  • Burning or tingling prodrome
  • Dysuria
  • Tender lymphadenopathy
  • Systemic symptoms during first episode

HSV-1 vs HSV-2

  • Genital HSV-2 recurs and sheds more frequently.
  • Genital HSV-1 generally recurs less often and shedding decreases more rapidly.

Asymptomatic Shedding

HSV can be transmitted when no lesions or prodromal symptoms are present.

Herpes Diagnosis

Active Lesion

HSV NAAT or PCR From the Lesion

Viral Culture

Culture sensitivity decreases as lesions heal and is lower for recurrent disease.

Type-Specific Serology

May be useful when:

  • Recurrent genital symptoms occur with negative lesion testing.
  • A clinical diagnosis was made without laboratory confirmation.
  • A partner has genital herpes.

Limitations

  • False-positive HSV-2 results occur, especially at low index values.
  • Confirmatory testing may be needed.
  • HSV IgM testing is not recommended.

First Clinical Episode of Genital Herpes

Treat Every First Episode

Acyclovir 400 mg Orally Three Times Daily for 7–10 Days

Or:

  • Valacyclovir 1 g orally twice daily for 7–10 days
  • Famciclovir 250 mg orally three times daily for 7–10 days

Incomplete Healing

  • Extend treatment beyond 10 days if lesions have not healed.

Severe or Disseminated Disease

  • Acyclovir 5–10 mg/kg IV every eight hours
  • Adjust for renal function
  • Provide IV hydration and monitor renal function

HSV Meningitis

A common framework is:

  • IV acyclovir until clinical improvement
  • Then valacyclovir 1 g orally three times daily
  • Total treatment approximately 10–14 days

HSV encephalitis requires a longer IV course.

Recurrent Genital Herpes

Episodic Treatment

Treatment is most effective when started:

  • During the prodrome
  • Within one day of lesion onset

Examples

  • Valacyclovir 500 mg orally twice daily for 3 days
  • Valacyclovir 1 g orally once daily for 5 days
  • Acyclovir 800 mg orally twice daily for 5 days

Suppressive Therapy

  • Acyclovir 400 mg orally twice daily
  • Valacyclovir 500 mg orally once daily
  • Valacyclovir 1 g orally once daily
  • Famciclovir 250 mg orally twice daily

Benefits of Suppression

  • Reduces recurrences by approximately 70%–80% in patients with frequent outbreaks
  • Improves quality of life
  • Reduces HSV-2 transmission risk

Frequent Recurrences

Valacyclovir 500 mg daily may be less effective when there are at least 10 episodes per year.

Counseling

Common Clinical Scenarios

Case 1: Dysuria With Negative Urinalysis

A young adult has dysuria and urethral discharge but minimal pyuria on routine urinalysis.

Obtain gonorrhea and chlamydia NAAT and treat objective urethritis rather than assuming uncomplicated cystitis.

Case 2: Negative Urine Test After Receptive Anal Exposure

A patient has rectal pain and discharge but a negative urine gonorrhea and chlamydia NAAT.

Obtain rectal NAAT. A negative urine test does not exclude rectal infection.

Case 3: Uncomplicated Gonorrhea

A patient weighing 80 kg has urethral gonorrhea and chlamydia has not been excluded.

Give ceftriaxone 500 mg IM once plus doxycycline 100 mg twice daily for seven days.

Case 4: Pharyngeal Gonorrhea

A patient is asymptomatic but has a positive pharyngeal gonorrhea NAAT.

Treat with ceftriaxone and arrange test of cure in 7–14 days.

Case 5: Positive Gonorrhea NAAT After Treatment

A patient has recurrent symptoms after treatment and denies further sexual contact.

Obtain culture and susceptibility testing before repeated empiric retreatment and notify public health if cephalosporin failure is suspected.

Case 6: Rectal Chlamydia

A patient has asymptomatic rectal chlamydia.

Use doxycycline 100 mg twice daily for seven days rather than relying on single-dose azithromycin.

Case 7: Pregnancy With Chlamydia

A pregnant patient has a positive chlamydia NAAT.

Treat with azithromycin 1 g once and arrange a test of cure in approximately four weeks.

Case 8: Painless Genital Ulcer

A patient has a painless genital ulcer but initial RPR is negative.

Early primary syphilis remains possible. Obtain treponemal testing or direct lesion testing, repeat serology when indicated, and treat empirically when clinical probability is high.

Case 9: New Visual Symptoms With Syphilis

A patient with positive syphilis serology develops blurred vision and photophobia.

Arrange urgent ophthalmologic evaluation and treat ocular syphilis with the neurosyphilis regimen.

Case 10: Syphilis During Pregnancy With Penicillin Allergy

A pregnant patient reports immediate urticaria and respiratory symptoms with penicillin.

Desensitize and treat with penicillin. Doxycycline and azithromycin do not adequately prevent congenital syphilis.

Case 11: First Genital Herpes Episode

A patient has extensive painful genital ulcers and a positive HSV-2 PCR.

Treat every first episode for 7–10 days and extend treatment if healing is incomplete.

Case 12: Recurrent Nongonococcal Urethritis

Symptoms recur after doxycycline, and gonorrhea and chlamydia testing are negative.

Confirm inflammation and test for M. genitalium. Use resistance-guided sequential therapy when available.

Case 13: Pelvic Pain and Cervical-Motion Tenderness

A patient has lower-abdominal pain and cervical-motion tenderness, but cervical gonorrhea and chlamydia results are pending.

Treat presumptively for PID. Waiting for NAAT results can increase reproductive complications.

Case 14: Acute Scrotal Pain

A teenager has sudden severe unilateral scrotal pain and nausea.

Assume possible testicular torsion and obtain immediate urologic evaluation rather than treating empirically for epididymitis.

Case 15: Recent HIV Exposure

A patient presents 30 hours after a substantial sexual HIV exposure.

Start three-drug HIV PEP immediately. Do not wait for complete laboratory results.

Case 16: Recent Bacterial STI in a Patient on PrEP

A man who has sex with men uses HIV PrEP and was treated for syphilis three months ago.

Discuss doxy PEP, provide 200 mg within 72 hours after sex when selected, and repeat site-specific STI testing every 3–6 months.

Common Pitfalls

  • Assuming asymptomatic patients do not have an STI
  • Testing urine alone after rectal or pharyngeal exposure
  • Inferring sexual practices from identity or relationship status
  • Failing to provide confidential, nonjudgmental care
  • Treating dysuria as UTI without considering urethritis
  • Failing to collect specimens before empiric treatment
  • Using ceftriaxone 500 mg in a patient weighing at least 150 kg
  • Omitting chlamydia treatment when infection has not been excluded
  • Using cefixime as preferred therapy when ceftriaxone is available
  • Failing to perform test of cure for pharyngeal gonorrhea
  • Using NAAT without culture in suspected gonorrhea treatment failure
  • Assuming persistent gonorrhea always represents resistance rather than reinfection
  • Using single-dose azithromycin as preferred therapy for rectal chlamydia
  • Performing chlamydia test of cure too early
  • Failing to retest gonorrhea or chlamydia at three months
  • Staging syphilis based only on RPR titer
  • Giving three weekly penicillin doses for correctly staged early syphilis
  • Using benzathine penicillin alone for neurosyphilis
  • Failing to ask about visual, auditory, or neurologic symptoms in syphilis
  • Using doxycycline for syphilis during pregnancy
  • Mislabeling a Jarisch–Herxheimer reaction as penicillin allergy
  • Ordering HSV IgM
  • Relying on lesion appearance without HSV and syphilis testing
  • Using topical antiviral therapy for genital herpes
  • Using single-dose metronidazole as preferred trichomoniasis treatment in women
  • Using metronidazole vaginal gel for trichomoniasis
  • Giving azithromycin 1 g alone for M. genitalium
  • Failing to treat PID because cervical NAAT is negative
  • Omitting metronidazole from the standard outpatient PID regimen
  • Failing to reassess PID after 72 hours
  • Missing ectopic pregnancy or testicular torsion
  • Using fluoroquinolone monotherapy for epididymitis before excluding gonorrhea
  • Failing to treat or notify recent partners
  • Using EPT without written safety instructions
  • Failing to offer HIV testing, PrEP, PEP, or vaccination
  • Starting HIV PEP after waiting for the source person’s results
  • Using doxy PEP outside an evidence-supported population without shared decision-making
  • Assuming an undetectable HIV viral load prevents other STIs

Board and ID Consult Pearls

  • Sexual practices determine the anatomic sites that require testing.
  • A negative urine NAAT does not exclude rectal or pharyngeal infection.
  • Patient-collected vaginal, rectal, and pharyngeal swabs are acceptable for validated NAATs.
  • Screen sexually active women younger than 25 annually for gonorrhea and chlamydia.
  • Screen sexually active MSM at least annually at all exposed sites and every 3–6 months with ongoing increased risk.
  • Gonorrhea is treated with ceftriaxone 500 mg IM once when weight is below 150 kg.
  • Use ceftriaxone 1 g when body weight is at least 150 kg.
  • Add doxycycline when chlamydia has not been excluded.
  • Pharyngeal gonorrhea requires test of cure in 7–14 days.
  • Suspected gonorrhea treatment failure requires culture and susceptibility testing.
  • Doxycycline for seven days is preferred for uncomplicated chlamydia.
  • Azithromycin is preferred for chlamydia during pregnancy.
  • Pregnant patients treated for chlamydia require test of cure at approximately four weeks.
  • Retest gonorrhea and chlamydia approximately three months after treatment.
  • LGV requires doxycycline for 21 days.
  • Primary, secondary, and early latent syphilis require one dose of benzathine penicillin G.
  • Late latent syphilis or unknown duration requires three weekly doses.
  • Do not stage latent syphilis from RPR titer alone.
  • A fourfold RPR change equals two dilution steps.
  • Routine lumbar puncture is not required for syphilis without neurologic findings.
  • Neurosyphilis requires IV aqueous penicillin for 10–14 days.
  • Ocular and otosyphilis are treated using the neurosyphilis regimen.
  • Penicillin is required for syphilis during pregnancy.
  • Jarisch–Herxheimer reaction is not penicillin allergy.
  • HSV PCR from an active lesion is the preferred herpes test.
  • Do not order HSV IgM.
  • Treat every first genital-herpes episode.
  • Suppressive herpes treatment reduces recurrence and HSV-2 transmission risk.
  • Trichomoniasis in women is treated with metronidazole 500 mg twice daily for seven days.
  • Trichomoniasis in men is treated with metronidazole 2 g once.
  • Single-dose azithromycin should not be used alone for M. genitalium.
  • PID is a clinical diagnosis and should be treated with a low threshold.
  • Negative cervical gonorrhea and chlamydia tests do not exclude PID.
  • Standard outpatient PID therapy includes ceftriaxone, doxycycline, and metronidazole.
  • PID should improve clinically within 72 hours.
  • Pregnancy and tubo-ovarian abscess are indications for PID hospitalization.
  • Testicular torsion must be excluded in acute scrotal pain.
  • HIV PEP should begin as soon as possible and no later than 72 hours after exposure.
  • HIV PEP is continued for 28 days.
  • HIV PrEP options include oral tenofovir-based therapy and long-acting injectable cabotegravir or lenacapavir.
  • Doxy PEP is 200 mg within 72 hours after sex, with a maximum of 200 mg per 24 hours.
  • Doxy PEP should be reassessed every 3–6 months.
  • Partner treatment is essential to prevent reinfection.
  • EPT is useful for chlamydia and selected gonorrhea partners where legally permitted.
  • Every STI encounter is an opportunity for HIV prevention and vaccination.

Bottom Line

Sexually transmitted infection care begins with a confidential, nonjudgmental sexual history and testing of every exposed anatomic site. Urine testing alone misses rectal, pharyngeal, and some cervical infections. Many STIs are asymptomatic, so appropriate screening is as important as symptom-based testing.

Treat uncomplicated gonorrhea with weight-based ceftriaxone and add doxycycline when chlamydia has not been excluded. Pharyngeal gonorrhea requires test of cure. Suspected gonorrhea treatment failure requires culture, susceptibility testing, specialist input, and public-health notification.

Doxycycline for seven days is preferred for uncomplicated chlamydia, while azithromycin is preferred during pregnancy. Retest chlamydia and gonorrhea approximately three months after treatment because reinfection is common. Treat recent partners and use expedited partner therapy when appropriate and legally permitted.

Correct syphilis staging determines treatment. Primary, secondary, and early latent syphilis require one dose of benzathine penicillin G, whereas late latent disease or unknown duration requires three weekly doses. Neurosyphilis, ocular syphilis, and otosyphilis require IV aqueous penicillin. Penicillin desensitization is required for pregnant patients with syphilis and penicillin allergy.

Genital herpes requires lesion PCR when lesions are present, antiviral therapy for every first episode, and counseling about recurrence and asymptomatic shedding. Trichomoniasis, M. genitalium, PID, epididymitis, proctitis, and genital-wart treatment require syndrome-specific management rather than interchangeable empiric antibiotics.

Every STI encounter should include HIV testing, assessment for acute HIV, consideration of HIV PrEP or time-sensitive 28-day PEP, vaccination review, partner treatment, public-health reporting, and planned follow-up. Selected MSM and transgender women with a recent bacterial STI should be offered doxycycline PEP through shared decision-making, using 200 mg within 72 hours after sex and no more than 200 mg per day.

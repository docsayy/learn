Nephrotic vs Nephritic Syndrome: Practical Approach to Glomerulonephritis

June 20, 2026 Nephrology

Glomerulonephritis is one of the most intimidating topics in nephrology because of the enormous number of diseases involved. Fortunately, most cases can be approached using a simple framework: first determine whether the patient has nephrotic syndrome or nephritic syndrome, then narrow the differential from there. This article focuses on the practical bedside approach to glomerular disease. :contentReference[oaicite:0]{index=0}

Overview

The first step when evaluating glomerulonephritis is deciding whether the patient has:

  • Nephrotic syndrome
  • Nephritic syndrome

These syndromes arise from different pathophysiologic mechanisms and therefore have very different differential diagnoses. :contentReference[oaicite:1]{index=1}

Feature Nephrotic Syndrome Nephritic Syndrome
Proteinuria > 3.5 g/day Usually < 3.5 g/day
Hematuria Minimal Prominent
RBC Casts Absent Present
Edema Marked Variable
Albumin Low Mildly low or normal
Hyperlipidemia Common Uncommon
Mechanism Podocyte injury Inflammation and immune injury

Nephrotic Syndrome

Pathophysiology

Nephrotic syndrome results primarily from podocyte injury and effacement of podocyte foot processes. Loss of this filtration barrier allows large amounts of protein to leak into the urine. :contentReference[oaicite:2]{index=2}

Diagnostic Criteria

  • Proteinuria > 3.5 g/day
  • Hypoalbuminemia
  • Generalized edema (anasarca)
  • Often hyperlipidemia

Classic Findings

  • Foamy or frothy urine
  • Diffuse edema
  • Low serum albumin
  • Hyperlipidemia
  • Hypercoagulability
Loss of antithrombin III contributes to the increased risk of venous thrombosis seen in nephrotic syndrome.

Primary Nephrotic Syndromes

1. Minimal Change Disease (MCD)

Minimal change disease is the most common cause of nephrotic syndrome in children but can occur in adults as well. :contentReference[oaicite:3]{index=3}

Finding Characteristic
Light Microscopy Normal appearance
Electron Microscopy Podocyte foot process effacement
Treatment Glucocorticoids

2. Membranous Nephropathy

Membranous nephropathy is strongly associated with anti-PLA2R antibodies and is one of the most common causes of nephrotic syndrome in adults. :contentReference[oaicite:4]{index=4}

Finding Characteristic
Light Microscopy Thickened basement membrane
Electron Microscopy Subepithelial deposits ("spike and dome")
Complication Renal vein thrombosis

Treatment

  • Low risk → Observation and supportive care
  • Moderate/high risk → Rituximab preferred
  • Progressive disease → Immunosuppression

3. Focal Segmental Glomerulosclerosis (FSGS)

FSGS refers to sclerosis affecting only some glomeruli (focal) and only portions of affected glomeruli (segmental). :contentReference[oaicite:5]{index=5}

Treatment

  • Glucocorticoids
  • Calcineurin inhibitors when appropriate

Secondary Nephrotic Syndromes

Diabetic Nephropathy

The most common secondary cause of nephrotic syndrome.

Biopsy Findings

  • Mesangial expansion
  • Nodular glomerulosclerosis
  • Kimmelstiel-Wilson nodules

Amyloidosis

  • Congo red staining
  • Apple-green birefringence

Secondary Membranous Nephropathy

Common associations:

  • Hepatitis B
  • Autoimmune disease
  • Malignancy
  • NSAIDs
  • Light-chain disorders

Membranoproliferative GN (MPGN)

  • Often associated with hepatitis C
  • Classic "tram-track" appearance

Secondary FSGS

Associated with:

  • Obesity
  • Diabetes
  • HIV
  • Heroin use
  • NSAIDs
  • Bisphosphonates (especially pamidronate)
Secondary FSGS is often treated by addressing the underlying disease and reducing intraglomerular pressure with ACE inhibitors or ARBs.

These secondary causes account for a large proportion of nephrotic syndrome encountered in adult medicine. :contentReference[oaicite:6]{index=6}

Nephritic Syndrome

Pathophysiology

Nephritic syndrome results from inflammation within the glomerulus, often driven by immune complexes or autoantibodies. The inflammatory injury allows leakage of red blood cells and inflammatory cells into the urine. :contentReference[oaicite:7]{index=7}

Classic Findings

  • Hematuria
  • Dysmorphic RBCs
  • RBC casts
  • Pyuria
  • Proteinuria (usually sub-nephrotic)
  • Acute kidney injury

Three Major Categories

Category Approximate Frequency
Pauci-immune ~60%
Immune complex mediated ~20%
Anti-GBM disease ~10%

This three-part framework is one of the most useful approaches to nephritic syndrome. :contentReference[oaicite:8]{index=8}

Pauci-Immune Glomerulonephritis

Pauci-immune GN is most commonly caused by ANCA-associated vasculitis.

Disease Associated Antibody
Granulomatosis with Polyangiitis (GPA) PR3 / c-ANCA
Microscopic Polyangiitis (MPA) MPO / p-ANCA
Eosinophilic GPA (EGPA) Often p-ANCA
Complement levels are generally normal in ANCA-associated vasculitis.

ANCA serologies are therefore a critical part of the nephritic workup. :contentReference[oaicite:9]{index=9}

Immune Complex Glomerulonephritis

Normal Complement Causes

  • IgA nephropathy
  • IgA vasculitis (Henoch-Schönlein purpura)
IgA nephropathy affects the kidneys alone, whereas IgA vasculitis also involves the skin, GI tract, and joints.

Low Complement Causes

  • Lupus nephritis
  • Post-infectious GN
  • Cryoglobulinemia
  • Infective endocarditis

Timing Pearl

Condition Timing After URI
IgA Nephropathy ~3 days
Post-Infectious GN ~14 days

A useful memory aid is that "IgA" has 3 letters, reminding you that hematuria often appears within 3 days of infection. Post-streptococcal GN occurs later, typically around 1–2 weeks after infection. :contentReference[oaicite:10]{index=10}

Lupus Nephritis

Lupus nephritis has six classes:

  • Class I-II → Usually monitored and treated conservatively
  • Class III-V → Immunosuppression
  • Class VI → Advanced sclerosis and kidney failure

Treatment commonly includes:

  • Corticosteroids
  • Mycophenolate mofetil
  • Cyclophosphamide
  • Calcineurin inhibitors

Anti-GBM Disease

Anti-glomerular basement membrane disease is caused by antibodies directed against the basement membrane. :contentReference[oaicite:11]{index=11}

Goodpasture Syndrome

Anti-GBM disease + pulmonary involvement = Goodpasture syndrome.

Important Facts

  • May overlap with ANCA positivity
  • Can cause pulmonary hemorrhage
  • Requires anti-GBM antibody testing

Do Not Confuse With Alport Syndrome

Alport syndrome results from a type IV collagen mutation and is associated with:

  • Hematuria
  • Progressive kidney disease
  • Sensorineural hearing loss

Rapidly Progressive Glomerulonephritis (RPGN)

Rapidly progressive glomerulonephritis is a clinical syndrome rather than a specific disease.

Features

  • Rapidly rising creatinine
  • Rapid loss of kidney function
  • Crescent formation on biopsy
Crescents involving more than 50% of glomeruli should raise concern for RPGN and warrant urgent nephrology involvement.

Because irreversible kidney injury can occur quickly, these patients require expedited evaluation and treatment. :contentReference[oaicite:12]{index=12}

Clinical Pearls

  • Always start by deciding whether the patient has nephrotic or nephritic syndrome.
  • Nephrotic syndrome = proteinuria >3.5 g/day, edema, hypoalbuminemia.
  • Nephritic syndrome = hematuria, dysmorphic RBCs, RBC casts, inflammation.
  • Minimal change disease, membranous nephropathy, and FSGS are the major primary nephrotic syndromes.
  • Diabetes is the most common secondary nephrotic syndrome.
  • ANCA vasculitis is the most common cause of pauci-immune GN.
  • IgA nephropathy presents within days of a URI.
  • Post-infectious GN usually occurs 1–2 weeks after infection.
  • Lupus nephritis typically causes low complement levels.
  • Goodpasture syndrome combines anti-GBM disease with pulmonary involvement.
  • Rapidly rising creatinine and crescents suggest RPGN.
  • Complement levels, ANCA serologies, anti-GBM antibodies, ANA, anti-dsDNA, and kidney biopsy are key diagnostic tools.

Bottom Line

The simplest approach to glomerulonephritis is to first distinguish nephrotic syndrome from nephritic syndrome. Nephrotic disease is primarily a podocyte problem characterized by heavy proteinuria and edema, whereas nephritic disease is an inflammatory process characterized by hematuria and glomerular injury. For nephrotic syndrome, think primary versus secondary causes. For nephritic syndrome, think pauci-immune, immune-complex mediated, and anti-GBM disease. This framework allows rapid organization of an otherwise overwhelming differential diagnosis. :contentReference[oaicite:13]{index=13}

Nephrotic vs Nephritic Syndrome: Practical Approach to Glomerulonephritis

Added June 20, 2026 • Nephrology • ~15 min read

Glomerulonephritis is one of the most intimidating topics in nephrology because of the enormous number of diseases involved. Fortunately, most cases can be approached using a simple framework: first determine whether the patient has nephrotic syndrome or nephritic syndrome, then narrow the differential from there. This article focuses on the practical bedside approach to glomerular disease.

Overview

The first step when evaluating glomerulonephritis is deciding whether the patient has:

  • Nephrotic Syndrome
  • Nephritic Syndrome

These syndromes arise from different pathophysiologic mechanisms and therefore have very different differential diagnoses.

Feature Nephrotic Syndrome Nephritic Syndrome
Proteinuria > 3.5 g/day Usually < 3.5 g/day
Hematuria Minimal Prominent
RBC Casts Absent Present
Edema Marked Variable
Albumin Low Mildly low or normal
Hyperlipidemia Common Uncommon
Mechanism Podocyte Injury Inflammation and Immune Injury

Nephrotic Syndrome

Pathophysiology

Nephrotic syndrome results primarily from podocyte injury and effacement of podocyte foot processes. Loss of this filtration barrier allows large amounts of protein to leak into the urine.

Diagnostic Criteria

  • Proteinuria > 3.5 g/day
  • Hypoalbuminemia
  • Generalized edema (anasarca)
  • Often hyperlipidemia

Classic Findings

  • Foamy or frothy urine
  • Diffuse edema
  • Low serum albumin
  • Hyperlipidemia
  • Hypercoagulability
High-Yield Pearl: Loss of antithrombin III contributes to the increased risk of venous thrombosis seen in nephrotic syndrome.

Primary Nephrotic Syndromes

Minimal Change Disease (MCD)

Minimal change disease is the most common cause of nephrotic syndrome in children but can occur in adults as well.

Finding Characteristic
Light Microscopy Normal Appearance
Electron Microscopy Foot Process Effacement
Treatment Glucocorticoids

Membranous Nephropathy

Membranous nephropathy is strongly associated with anti-PLA2R antibodies and is one of the most common causes of nephrotic syndrome in adults.

Finding Characteristic
Light Microscopy Thickened Basement Membrane
Electron Microscopy Subepithelial Deposits ("Spike and Dome")
Complication Renal Vein Thrombosis

Treatment

  • Low Risk → Observation and Supportive Care
  • Moderate/High Risk → Rituximab Preferred
  • Progressive Disease → Immunosuppression

Focal Segmental Glomerulosclerosis (FSGS)

FSGS refers to sclerosis affecting only some glomeruli (focal) and only portions of affected glomeruli (segmental).

  • Primary FSGS
  • Secondary FSGS
  • Genetic FSGS

Treatment

  • Glucocorticoids
  • Calcineurin Inhibitors
  • RAAS Blockade

Secondary Nephrotic Syndromes

Diabetic Nephropathy

The most common secondary cause of nephrotic syndrome.

  • Mesangial expansion
  • Kimmelstiel-Wilson nodules
  • Progressive proteinuria

Amyloidosis

  • Congo Red Positive
  • Apple-Green Birefringence

Secondary Membranous Nephropathy

  • Hepatitis B
  • Malignancy
  • Autoimmune Disease
  • NSAIDs
  • Monoclonal Gammopathy

Membranoproliferative GN (MPGN)

  • Often associated with Hepatitis C
  • Classic Tram-Track Appearance

Secondary FSGS

  • Obesity
  • Diabetes
  • HIV
  • Heroin Use
  • NSAIDs
  • Bisphosphonates
Secondary FSGS treatment focuses on treating the underlying disease and reducing intraglomerular pressure with ACE inhibitors or ARBs.

Nephritic Syndrome

Pathophysiology

Nephritic syndrome results from inflammation within the glomerulus, often driven by immune complexes or autoantibodies.

Classic Findings

  • Hematuria
  • Dysmorphic RBCs
  • RBC Casts
  • Pyuria
  • Proteinuria (usually sub-nephrotic)
  • Acute Kidney Injury

Three Major Categories

Category Approximate Frequency
Pauci-Immune ~60%
Immune Complex Mediated ~20%
Anti-GBM Disease ~10%

Pauci-Immune Glomerulonephritis

Pauci-immune GN is most commonly caused by ANCA-associated vasculitis.

Disease Associated Antibody
Granulomatosis with Polyangiitis (GPA) PR3 / c-ANCA
Microscopic Polyangiitis (MPA) MPO / p-ANCA
Eosinophilic GPA (EGPA) Often p-ANCA
Complement levels are generally normal in ANCA-associated vasculitis.

Immune Complex Glomerulonephritis

Normal Complement Causes

  • IgA Nephropathy
  • IgA Vasculitis

Low Complement Causes

  • Lupus Nephritis
  • Post-Infectious GN
  • Cryoglobulinemia
  • Infective Endocarditis

Timing Pearl

Condition Timing After URI
IgA Nephropathy ~3 Days
Post-Infectious GN ~14 Days
IgA has 3 letters → think hematuria within 3 days of an upper respiratory infection.

Lupus Nephritis

  • Class I-II → Conservative management
  • Class III-V → Immunosuppression
  • Class VI → Advanced sclerosis

Common treatments include corticosteroids, mycophenolate mofetil, cyclophosphamide, and calcineurin inhibitors.

Anti-GBM Disease

Anti-glomerular basement membrane disease is caused by antibodies directed against the glomerular basement membrane.

Anti-GBM Disease + Pulmonary Hemorrhage = Goodpasture Syndrome

Important Features

  • May overlap with ANCA positivity
  • Can cause pulmonary hemorrhage
  • Requires anti-GBM antibody testing

Alport Syndrome (Do Not Confuse)

  • Type IV collagen mutation
  • Hematuria
  • Progressive kidney disease
  • Sensorineural hearing loss

Rapidly Progressive Glomerulonephritis (RPGN)

Rapidly progressive glomerulonephritis is a clinical syndrome characterized by rapid loss of kidney function.

Classic Features

  • Rapidly rising creatinine
  • Progressive renal failure
  • Crescent formation on biopsy
Crescents involving more than 50% of glomeruli should raise concern for RPGN and require urgent nephrology involvement.

Clinical Pearls

  1. Always begin by distinguishing nephrotic from nephritic syndrome.
  2. Nephrotic syndrome = heavy proteinuria, edema, hypoalbuminemia.
  3. Nephritic syndrome = hematuria, RBC casts, inflammation.
  4. Minimal change disease, membranous nephropathy, and FSGS are major primary nephrotic syndromes.
  5. Diabetes is the most common secondary nephrotic syndrome.
  6. ANCA vasculitis is the most common pauci-immune GN.
  7. IgA nephropathy presents within days of URI symptoms.
  8. Post-infectious GN typically occurs 1–2 weeks later.
  9. Lupus nephritis commonly causes low complement levels.
  10. Goodpasture syndrome combines anti-GBM disease with pulmonary hemorrhage.
  11. Rapidly rising creatinine and crescents suggest RPGN.
  12. Kidney biopsy remains one of the most important diagnostic tools.

Bottom Line

The simplest approach to glomerulonephritis is to first distinguish nephrotic syndrome from nephritic syndrome. Nephrotic disease is primarily a podocyte problem characterized by heavy proteinuria and edema, whereas nephritic disease is an inflammatory process characterized by hematuria and glomerular injury. For nephrotic syndrome, think primary versus secondary causes. For nephritic syndrome, think pauci-immune disease, immune-complex mediated disease, and anti-GBM disease. This framework rapidly organizes an otherwise overwhelming differential diagnosis.

Fever of Unknown Origin: The Complete Advanced Resident Guide

Added July 14, 2026 • Infectious Disease • ~54 min read

Fever of unknown origin is not merely fever without an obvious source. It is a prolonged, objectively documented febrile illness that remains unexplained after an appropriate standardized evaluation. The differential includes occult infection, noninfectious inflammatory disease, malignancy, medication reactions, thrombosis, endocrine disease, and factitious fever. Successful evaluation depends on repeatedly identifying potential diagnostic clues, avoiding indiscriminate testing, using CT and FDG-PET/CT strategically, obtaining the highest-yield tissue sample, and withholding empiric treatment when the patient is stable so that the underlying disease remains diagnosable.

Clinical Overview

Fever of unknown origin is a syndrome rather than a single disease. It describes prolonged fever that remains unexplained despite a deliberate minimum evaluation.

FUO should not be diagnosed merely because the admitting team has not identified a source within the first several days. Most acute inpatient fevers are better classified as:

  • Undifferentiated acute fever
  • Hospital-acquired fever
  • Sepsis without an established source
  • Febrile neutropenia
  • Prolonged fever not yet meeting FUO criteria
FUO = Prolonged Documented Fever + Appropriate Minimum Evaluation + No Explanation

The Five Major Diagnostic Categories

  • Infection
  • Noninfectious inflammatory disease
  • Malignancy
  • Miscellaneous conditions
  • Undiagnosed illness

Major Clinical Questions

  1. Has the fever been objectively confirmed?
  2. Does the patient truly meet FUO criteria?
  3. Is the patient immunocompetent or immunocompromised?
  4. Which potential diagnostic clues are present?
  5. Has the minimum evaluation been completed correctly?
  6. Which diagnosis can be confirmed by a targeted tissue sample?
  7. Would empiric treatment obscure the diagnosis?
  8. Is observation safer than another low-yield invasive test?
Most FUO diagnoses result from atypical presentations of recognizable diseases rather than from extraordinarily rare diseases.

Modern Definition of Classic FUO

Current international consensus supports classifying classic FUO in an immunocompetent patient when there is:

  • Fever lasting more than three weeks
  • Documented fever on multiple occasions
  • No explanation after completion of a defined minimum diagnostic evaluation
More Than 3 Weeks of Fever + Minimum Standard Evaluation Unrevealing

Temperature Threshold

The traditional threshold is at least 38.3°C or 100.9°F on several occasions.

Some modern clinicians use 38.0°C as a clinically meaningful threshold, particularly when:

  • The fever is repeatedly documented
  • The patient has objective inflammation or anemia
  • Baseline temperature is normally low
  • Antipyretics or age may blunt the temperature elevation

However, the 2024 consensus group retained the historical concept and did not reach full agreement on lowering the formal threshold.

Immunocompetent Population

Classic FUO definitions are intended primarily for patients without a major immune defect.

Patients with the following generally require a separate diagnostic framework:

  • ANC below 500 cells/µL
  • Uncontrolled HIV with CD4 below 200 cells/mm³
  • Solid-organ or stem-cell transplantation
  • Significant hypogammaglobulinemia
  • Recent biologic immunosuppression
  • Prolonged systemic corticosteroid therapy

Historical Definition

Petersdorf and Beeson Definition

The classic 1961 definition required:

  • Temperature above 38.3°C on several occasions
  • Illness lasting at least three weeks
  • No diagnosis after one week of hospital investigation

Why the Definition Changed

Modern diagnostic evaluation frequently occurs in the outpatient setting. Requiring one full week of hospitalization is no longer clinically appropriate.

The current concept emphasizes:

  • A defined minimum evaluation
  • Objective documentation
  • Patient immune status
  • Geography and epidemiology
  • Potential diagnostic clues

Durack and Street Categories

The 1991 framework divided FUO into:

  • Classic FUO
  • Nosocomial FUO
  • Neutropenic FUO
  • HIV-associated FUO
These categories remain clinically useful, but neutropenic and advanced-HIV fever require urgent syndrome-specific management rather than waiting passively for formal FUO criteria.

FUO Categories

Category Clinical Framework
Classic FUO More than three weeks of fever in an immunocompetent patient after an unrevealing minimum evaluation
Nosocomial FUO Fever developing during hospitalization, not present or incubating at admission, remaining unexplained after an appropriate inpatient evaluation
Neutropenic FUO Fever with severe neutropenia in which no clinical or microbiologic source is identified
HIV-associated FUO Prolonged unexplained fever in a person with HIV, particularly uncontrolled or advanced infection
Inflammation of unknown origin Persistent objective inflammation without documented high fever after an unrevealing minimum evaluation

Why Classification Matters

The likely diagnoses and urgency differ substantially:

  • Classic FUO frequently includes inflammatory disease, occult infection, and malignancy.
  • Nosocomial FUO emphasizes devices, medications, thrombosis, C. difficile, and postoperative complications.
  • Neutropenic fever requires immediate empiric antipseudomonal treatment.
  • Advanced-HIV FUO emphasizes opportunistic infection and lymphoma.

Inflammation of Unknown Origin

Inflammation of unknown origin describes patients with persistent objective inflammation but without temperatures meeting the classic FUO threshold.

Consensus Framework

  • Symptoms or inflammatory abnormalities lasting more than three weeks
  • Elevated inflammatory markers
  • No documented fever at or above approximately 38.3°C
  • No explanation after the same minimum evaluation used for FUO

Common Presentations

  • Weight loss
  • Fatigue
  • Anemia
  • Elevated ESR or CRP
  • Night sweats
  • Myalgia
  • Functional decline

Diagnostic Approach

The diagnostic strategy for inflammation of unknown origin is similar to FUO, including potential use of FDG-PET/CT and targeted biopsy.

What Is Not Yet FUO?

Examples

  • Three days of fever without a source
  • A single temperature elevation
  • Fever without completion of basic testing
  • Neutropenic fever awaiting cultures
  • Septic shock with an unidentified source
  • Fever after recent surgery before targeted evaluation
  • Subjective fever without objective documentation

Why Premature Labeling Is Harmful

  • Encourages indiscriminate rare-disease testing
  • May distract from common diagnoses
  • May lead to unnecessary PET/CT or biopsy
  • May lead to premature corticosteroids
  • May obscure a rapidly evolving acute infection
FUO is a diagnosis of a clinical syndrome after an adequate evaluation—not a substitute phrase for “we do not yet know why the patient is febrile.”

Core Diagnostic Principles

1. Confirm That Fever Is Real

Document the temperature, measurement site, instrument, frequency, and relationship to antipyretics.

2. Search for Potential Diagnostic Clues

Every symptom, exposure, examination finding, and laboratory abnormality should be evaluated for its ability to narrow the differential.

3. Repeat the History and Examination

Diagnostic clues may appear only after the illness evolves.

4. Test From a Differential, Not From a List

Order tests that can confirm or meaningfully exclude a plausible diagnosis.

5. Obtain Tissue When Tissue Is the Issue

A high-quality lymph-node, temporal-artery, bone-marrow, liver, skin, or other targeted biopsy may outperform dozens of serologies.

6. Avoid Premature Empiric Treatment

Antibiotics and corticosteroids can sterilize cultures, alter pathology, and temporarily suppress lymphoma, vasculitis, or inflammatory disease.

Potential Diagnostic Clue → Targeted Test → Confirmatory Tissue or Microbiology

Potential Diagnostic Clues

Potential diagnostic clues are findings that meaningfully increase or decrease the likelihood of a disease.

Examples From History

  • New headache or jaw claudication
  • Animal exposure
  • Recent dental procedure
  • Travel or migration
  • Unpasteurized dairy consumption
  • Tick exposure
  • New medication
  • Injection-drug use
  • Prosthetic valve or vascular graft
  • Intermittent rash with fever

Examples From Examination

  • Temporal-artery abnormality
  • New murmur
  • Lymphadenopathy
  • Splenomegaly
  • Synovitis
  • Oral ulcer
  • Retinal lesion
  • Nail-bed embolic finding
  • Subtle skin nodule
  • Spinal tenderness

Examples From Laboratory Testing

  • Cholestatic liver-test pattern
  • Cytopenias
  • Marked ferritin elevation
  • Eosinophilia
  • Monoclonal protein
  • Hematuria with proteinuria
  • Persistent bacteremia
  • Elevated alkaline phosphatase
The highest-yield FUO strategy is repeated clue-directed evaluation—not repeating a broad standard panel every day.

Verify the Fever

Objective Verification Is Particularly Important When:

  • Only home measurements are reported
  • Inflammatory markers are normal
  • The patient appears well despite extreme temperatures
  • Temperature recordings are inconsistent
  • There is unexplained discrepancy among measurement sites
  • Factitious fever is possible

Document:

  • Temperature threshold
  • Anatomic measurement site
  • Measurement device
  • Timing
  • Antipyretic exposure
  • Associated heart rate and symptoms

Do Not Overinterpret Fever Patterns

Daily, intermittent, relapsing, nocturnal, and periodic patterns may generate clues but rarely establish a diagnosis by themselves.

Relative Bradycardia

A lower-than-expected heart rate for the temperature has been associated with several infectious and noninfectious illnesses but is neither sufficiently sensitive nor specific to identify the cause of FUO.

Minimum Standard Evaluation

The 2024 international consensus proposed a defined minimum evaluation before classifying an immunocompetent patient as having FUO.

Basic Laboratory Studies

  • CBC with differential
  • Comprehensive metabolic panel
  • Calcium
  • Liver tests
  • ESR
  • CRP
  • Ferritin
  • TSH

Selected Immunologic Studies

  • Antinuclear antibody
  • Rheumatoid factor
  • Antineutrophil cytoplasmic antibodies

Microbiology and Infectious Screening

  • Three blood-culture sets
  • HIV-1/2 testing
  • Urinalysis with microscopy
  • Urine culture when clinically appropriate
  • IGRA or tuberculin skin test

Foundational Imaging

When available, contrast-enhanced CT of the chest, abdomen, and pelvis is preferred as the foundational imaging component.

Where CT is not available or contraindicated, evaluation may include:

  • Posteroanterior and lateral chest radiographs
  • Abdominal ultrasonography

Minimum Does Not Mean Maximum

This standard evaluation establishes the FUO syndrome. It does not mean every patient requires every conceivable serology, culture, or imaging test.

History

Fever History

  • Exact onset
  • Objective temperature records
  • Continuous vs episodic fever
  • Relationship to medications or procedures
  • Rigors, sweats, or chills
  • Effect of antipyretics

Geographic History

  • Country of birth
  • Previous long-term residence
  • Recent travel
  • Rural vs urban exposures
  • Known local endemic infections

Exposure History

  • TB exposure
  • Animals and livestock
  • Cats, dogs, birds, rodents, and reptiles
  • Ticks and insects
  • Freshwater exposure
  • Raw or unpasteurized food
  • Occupational exposure
  • Homelessness, incarceration, or shelter exposure

Medical History

  • Valvular disease
  • Prosthetic valves or vascular grafts
  • Joint prostheses
  • Prior malignancy
  • Inflammatory disease
  • Immune suppression
  • Previous TB treatment
  • Recent surgery or dental work

Medication History

Review:

  • Prescription medications
  • Recently discontinued drugs
  • Over-the-counter products
  • Herbal supplements
  • Intermittent injections
  • Recreational substances

Social and Behavioral History

  • Sexual exposures
  • Injection-drug use
  • Alcohol use
  • Housing conditions
  • Occupational risks
  • Potential intentional temperature manipulation

Physical Examination

General Examination

  • Weight trend
  • Temporal wasting
  • Diaphoresis
  • Functional decline
  • Measured temperature and pulse

Eyes and Fundus

  • Conjunctival petechiae
  • Uveitis
  • Retinal emboli
  • Choroidal lesions
  • CMV or other opportunistic retinal disease

Oral and Head Examination

  • Dental infection
  • Oral ulcers
  • Thrush
  • Temporal-artery tenderness or reduced pulsation
  • Sinus tenderness

Lymphatic Examination

  • Cervical nodes
  • Supraclavicular nodes
  • Axillary nodes
  • Inguinal nodes
  • Node size, mobility, tenderness, and consistency

Cardiovascular Examination

  • New or changing murmur
  • Peripheral embolic findings
  • Vascular-graft tenderness
  • Pacemaker or ICD pocket changes

Abdominal Examination

  • Hepatomegaly
  • Splenomegaly
  • Localized tenderness
  • Abdominal mass
  • Vascular bruit

Skin and Musculoskeletal Examination

  • Evanescent rash
  • Palpable purpura
  • Erythema nodosum
  • Subcutaneous nodules
  • Janeway-type lesions
  • Synovitis
  • Spinal tenderness
  • Proximal muscle weakness
Examine the skin during fever when possible. The rash of adult-onset Still disease may disappear when the temperature falls.

Repeat History and Examination

Why Repetition Matters

FUO is an evolving syndrome. Findings absent during the first evaluation may appear later.

Repeat Attention to:

  • New lymph nodes
  • Changing murmur
  • New rash
  • Joint symptoms
  • Headache or visual symptoms
  • Back pain
  • Abdominal localization
  • Medication exposure

Bedside Observation

During a febrile episode, reassess:

  • Rash
  • Rigors
  • Pulse response
  • Blood pressure
  • Joint symptoms
  • Neurologic changes
Repeated careful bedside assessment may be more diagnostically productive than repeating an unchanged laboratory panel.

Interpreting Laboratory Patterns

Cytopenias

Consider:

  • Hematologic malignancy
  • HLH
  • Disseminated infection
  • Bone-marrow infiltration
  • Hypersplenism
  • Medication toxicity

Eosinophilia

  • Drug reaction
  • Helminth infection
  • Allergic disease
  • Eosinophilic granulomatosis with polyangiitis
  • Adrenal insufficiency
  • Selected malignancies

Cholestatic Liver-Test Pattern

  • Granulomatous hepatitis
  • Disseminated infection
  • Lymphoma
  • Infiltrative malignancy
  • Drug reaction
  • Adult-onset Still disease

Hematuria and Proteinuria

  • Endocarditis-associated glomerulonephritis
  • ANCA-associated vasculitis
  • Systemic lupus erythematosus
  • Infection-related glomerulonephritis

Ferritin

Marked ferritin elevation may support:

  • Adult-onset Still disease
  • HLH or macrophage-activation syndrome
  • Severe infection
  • Liver injury
  • Malignancy
No ferritin concentration independently diagnoses Still disease or HLH. Interpret it with the entire clinical and laboratory syndrome.

Normal ESR and CRP

Repeatedly normal inflammatory markers should prompt reconsideration of:

  • Whether true fever has been documented
  • Factitious fever
  • Intermittent autoinflammatory disease
  • Measurement error

However, normal markers do not absolutely exclude important disease.

Blood Cultures

Minimum FUO Evaluation

Three Properly Collected Blood-Culture Sets

Collection Principles

  • Collect before new antibiotics when feasible.
  • Use separate peripheral venipuncture sites.
  • Obtain adequate blood volume.
  • Do not require a prolonged interval between sets.
  • Notify the laboratory when unusual organisms are suspected.

Special Culture Considerations

Standard blood cultures may not reliably detect:

  • Coxiella burnetii
  • Bartonella species
  • Brucella with inadequate incubation or laboratory warning
  • Mycobacteria
  • Selected fungi
  • Tropheryma whipplei

Prior Antibiotics

Antibiotics may sterilize blood cultures and complicate endocarditis diagnosis. When the patient is stable, consider whether antimicrobial therapy can safely be held while repeat cultures are obtained.

Culture-negative endocarditis is not synonymous with negative blood cultures alone. It requires a compatible endocardial syndrome and targeted investigation.

Targeted Microbiology

Testing Should Reflect Epidemiology

Potential targeted tests include:

  • TB culture and NAAT
  • Q fever serology
  • Bartonella serology or PCR
  • Brucella serology and culture
  • Histoplasma antigen
  • Cryptococcal antigen
  • Malaria blood films or rapid testing
  • Babesia smear and PCR
  • Strongyloides testing before corticosteroids

Specimen Choice Matters

  • Sputum or tissue for TB
  • Excised lymph node for lymphoma and infection
  • Valve tissue for endocarditis PCR and culture
  • Bone marrow for selected disseminated infections
  • Liver tissue for granulomatous hepatitis

Notify the Laboratory With:

  • Brucella concern
  • Francisella concern
  • Mycobacterial disease
  • Fungal culture request
  • Potential highly hazardous organisms

Serologies and Molecular Tests

Problems With Broad Serologic Panels

  • False-positive results
  • Evidence of remote rather than active infection
  • Cross-reactivity
  • Low positive predictive value
  • Incidental autoantibodies

Use Serology When:

  • A specific exposure is present.
  • The disease is difficult to culture.
  • Paired acute and convalescent testing has diagnostic value.
  • The result can change management.

Broad Molecular Sequencing

Cell-free DNA or metagenomic sequencing may be considered when:

  • Infection remains strongly suspected.
  • Conventional cultures and targeted tests are unrevealing.
  • The result is interpreted by specialists familiar with assay limitations.

Limitations of Broad Molecular Testing

  • Detection of clinically irrelevant microbial DNA
  • False-negative results
  • Limited susceptibility information
  • High cost
  • Risk of replacing rather than guiding tissue diagnosis
Broad molecular testing is an adjunct—not a replacement for high-quality cultures, imaging, pathology, and clinical reasoning.

Imaging Strategy

First-Line Cross-Sectional Imaging

Contrast-enhanced CT of the chest, abdomen, and pelvis can identify:

  • Occult abscess
  • Lymphadenopathy
  • Solid-organ lesion
  • Aortic or vascular abnormality
  • Granulomatous disease
  • Malignancy
  • Deep infection

Second-Line Functional Imaging

FDG-PET/CT identifies sites of increased glucose metabolism associated with:

  • Infection
  • Vasculitis
  • Inflammatory disease
  • Malignancy

Targeted Imaging

Use symptom-directed imaging for:

  • Spinal infection
  • CNS disease
  • Vascular-graft infection
  • Cardiac infection
  • Sinus or dental disease
  • Joint infection

CT Chest, Abdomen, and Pelvis

Role

When available, CT chest, abdomen, and pelvis is favored over chest radiography plus abdominal ultrasound as the foundational imaging component of the minimum FUO evaluation.

Potential Findings

  • Deep lymphadenopathy
  • Pulmonary nodules
  • Abscess
  • Splenic or hepatic lesions
  • Renal mass
  • Bowel inflammation
  • Aortic inflammation
  • Occult malignancy

Contrast

IV contrast generally improves evaluation of:

  • Abscesses
  • Solid organs
  • Vascular structures
  • Inflammatory bowel disease
  • Malignancy

Limitations

  • Early vasculitis may be missed.
  • Small endocardial lesions are not evaluated.
  • Small-vessel inflammatory disease may be occult.
  • Residual nonspecific lymph nodes may generate low-yield biopsies.

FDG-PET/CT

When to Consider It

Minimum FUO Evaluation Unrevealing → Consider Early FDG-PET/CT

FDG-PET/CT is particularly useful when:

  • No strong potential diagnostic clue exists.
  • Inflammatory markers are elevated.
  • Large-vessel vasculitis is possible.
  • Lymphoma or occult malignancy is possible.
  • Deep infection or vascular-graft infection is possible.
  • A biopsy target is needed.

Potential Benefits

  • Whole-body assessment
  • Identification of clinically silent disease
  • Selection of the highest-yield biopsy site
  • Detection of large-vessel vasculitis
  • Detection of metabolically active lymphoma
  • Prognostic reassurance when negative in selected stable patients

Limitations

  • FDG uptake is not disease specific.
  • Recent surgery can cause false-positive uptake.
  • Muscle activity and hyperglycemia reduce image quality.
  • Corticosteroids may reduce vasculitis uptake.
  • Low-grade malignancies may show limited uptake.
  • Availability and cost vary.

Preparing for PET/CT

  • Review glucose control.
  • Avoid unnecessary strenuous exercise beforehand.
  • Coordinate timing relative to corticosteroids.
  • Provide the interpreting physician with the complete clinical question.
PET/CT is most valuable when it directs a confirmatory biopsy or targeted diagnostic procedure.

Echocardiography

Indications

Obtain echocardiography when endocarditis is plausible because of:

  • Persistent bacteremia
  • New murmur
  • Prosthetic valve
  • Cardiac implantable device
  • Embolic phenomena
  • Injection-drug use
  • Culture-negative endocarditis risk

Transthoracic Echocardiography

TTE is generally the initial test but has limited sensitivity for:

  • Small vegetations
  • Prosthetic-valve infection
  • Cardiac-device infection
  • Perivalvular abscess

Transesophageal Echocardiography

TEE is preferred or required with:

  • High clinical suspicion despite negative TTE
  • Prosthetic valve
  • Intracardiac device
  • Persistent bacteremia
  • Suspected perivalvular extension

Repeat Imaging

A negative early echocardiogram does not completely exclude evolving endocarditis. Repeat imaging may be appropriate when clinical suspicion remains high.

Other Targeted Imaging

MRI Spine

Use with:

  • Back pain
  • Persistent bacteremia
  • Neurologic deficits
  • Spinal tenderness

MRI Brain

  • Focal neurologic deficit
  • Headache with embolic or inflammatory concern
  • Possible CNS malignancy
  • Endocarditis with neurologic symptoms

Vascular Imaging

  • CT angiography for infected aneurysm
  • Ultrasound for thrombophlebitis
  • PET/CT for vascular graft or large-vessel inflammation

Dental and Sinus Imaging

Use only when symptoms, examination findings, or specific risk factors suggest an occult source.

Tagged-White-Cell and Gallium Scans

These may be considered where FDG-PET/CT is unavailable, but PET/CT is generally favored for modern FUO evaluation.

Role of Biopsy

Core Principle

Biopsy the Most Abnormal, Accessible, and Diagnostically Informative Site

Before Biopsy

  • Review imaging with radiology.
  • Clarify the differential diagnosis.
  • Notify pathology and microbiology.
  • Divide tissue appropriately.
  • Avoid placing all tissue in formalin when cultures or molecular studies are needed.

Potential Studies

  • Histopathology
  • Flow cytometry
  • Aerobic and anaerobic culture
  • Fungal culture
  • Mycobacterial culture
  • Targeted PCR
  • Cytogenetics and molecular oncology testing
A poorly planned biopsy may permanently lose the opportunity for microbiologic diagnosis.

Lymph-Node Biopsy

Excisional Biopsy

Excisional biopsy is often preferred when lymphoma is possible because it preserves:

  • Nodal architecture
  • Immunophenotyping
  • Molecular testing
  • Microbiologic sampling

Core Biopsy

Core biopsy may be appropriate for:

  • Deep inaccessible nodes
  • Image-guided sampling
  • Patients who cannot undergo surgery

Fine-Needle Aspiration

FNA may diagnose metastatic carcinoma or suppurative infection but may be insufficient for lymphoma classification.

Choose the Node Carefully

  • Prefer the most metabolically active accessible node.
  • Avoid an inguinal node when a more specific target exists.
  • Communicate lymphoma and infection concerns before the procedure.

Temporal-Artery and Large-Vessel Evaluation

Consider Giant-Cell Arteritis With:

  • Age above 50
  • New headache
  • Jaw or tongue claudication
  • Visual symptoms
  • Scalp tenderness
  • Polymyalgia symptoms
  • Marked inflammatory-marker elevation
  • Unexplained anemia or constitutional symptoms

Diagnostic Options

  • Temporal-artery ultrasound
  • Temporal-artery biopsy
  • FDG-PET/CT for large-vessel disease
  • CT or MR angiography

Visual Symptoms

When visual ischemia from giant-cell arteritis is possible, begin corticosteroids immediately. Do not delay treatment while arranging biopsy.

Biopsy Timing

Temporal-artery histopathology may remain diagnostic after corticosteroids begin, but obtain the biopsy as promptly as feasible.

Skip Lesions

GCA may involve the artery segmentally. An adequate specimen length and expert pathologic review improve yield.

Bone-Marrow Biopsy

Higher-Yield Settings

  • Unexplained cytopenias
  • Abnormal peripheral smear
  • Splenomegaly
  • Marked ferritin elevation
  • Suspected lymphoma or leukemia
  • Advanced HIV
  • Possible disseminated fungal or mycobacterial infection

Lower-Yield Setting

Routine blind bone-marrow biopsy in a patient with normal blood counts and no hematologic clue has lower diagnostic yield.

Send Appropriate Studies

  • Histopathology
  • Flow cytometry
  • Cytogenetics
  • Mycobacterial culture
  • Fungal culture
  • Targeted molecular testing

Hemophagocytosis

Hemophagocytosis may support HLH but is neither required nor specific. Absence of hemophagocytosis does not exclude HLH.

Liver Biopsy

Consider With:

  • Persistent cholestatic liver-test abnormalities
  • Hepatomegaly
  • Granulomatous disease concern
  • Infiltrative malignancy concern
  • Unexplained hepatic PET uptake

Potential Diagnoses

  • Granulomatous hepatitis
  • Disseminated mycobacterial or fungal infection
  • Sarcoidosis
  • Lymphoma
  • Drug reaction
  • Primary biliary or autoimmune disease

Microbiology Planning

When infection is possible, ensure that fresh tissue is submitted for fungal and mycobacterial culture rather than sending the entire specimen in formalin.

Skin and Muscle Biopsy

Skin Findings Worth Biopsying

  • Palpable purpura
  • Subcutaneous nodules
  • Persistent unexplained rash
  • Ulcers
  • Retiform purpura
  • Possible intravascular lymphoma lesions

Random Skin Biopsy

Random deep skin biopsy may occasionally help diagnose intravascular large B-cell lymphoma when there are:

  • Systemic symptoms
  • High LDH
  • Cytopenias
  • Neurologic or cutaneous clues
  • No accessible lymph node

Muscle Biopsy

Consider with:

  • Myalgia or weakness
  • Elevated muscle enzymes
  • MRI or PET abnormalities
  • Suspected vasculitis or inflammatory myopathy

Differential Framework

Category Representative Diagnoses
Infection TB, endocarditis, occult abscess, zoonoses, fungal disease, infected prosthetic material
Noninfectious inflammatory GCA, PMR, adult Still disease, vasculitis, connective-tissue disease, sarcoidosis
Malignancy Lymphoma, leukemia, renal-cell carcinoma, hepatocellular carcinoma, metastatic cancer
Miscellaneous Drug fever, thrombosis, thyroiditis, factitious fever, autoinflammatory disease
Undiagnosed No cause after adequate evaluation and follow-up

Epidemiology Changes the Differential

  • Infectious causes remain more prominent in many high-TB-burden regions.
  • Inflammatory disease and undiagnosed cases are increasingly common in high-resource settings.
  • Older adults have a higher likelihood of GCA, PMR, malignancy, and endocarditis.
  • Immune status substantially changes the likely diagnoses.

Infectious Causes

Common Important Infectious Diagnoses

  • Tuberculosis
  • Culture-negative endocarditis
  • Occult intra-abdominal or pelvic abscess
  • Vertebral osteomyelitis
  • Vascular-graft infection
  • Q fever
  • Bartonellosis
  • Brucellosis
  • Disseminated fungal infection

Why Infection Becomes Occult

  • Deep anatomic location
  • Prior antibiotics
  • Low organism burden
  • Difficult-to-culture organism
  • Immunosuppression producing atypical presentation
  • Biofilm on prosthetic material

Do Not Assume Every FUO Is Infectious

The longer a stable immunocompetent patient remains undiagnosed after a high-quality evaluation, the less appropriate indiscriminate broad-spectrum antibiotics become.

Tuberculosis

Potential FUO Presentations

  • Miliary TB
  • Extrapulmonary TB
  • Lymph-node TB
  • Genitourinary TB
  • Spinal TB
  • Peritoneal TB
  • TB in advanced HIV

Clues

  • Birth or residence in a high-incidence region
  • Prior exposure
  • Weight loss and night sweats
  • Lymphadenopathy
  • Granulomas
  • Cholestatic liver tests
  • Miliary nodules
  • Sterile pyuria
  • Exudative lymphocytic effusion

IGRA Limitations

A negative IGRA does not exclude active TB, particularly with severe illness, advanced HIV, immunosuppression, or disseminated disease.

Diagnosis

  • Respiratory AFB smear, NAAT, and culture
  • Lymph-node or tissue biopsy
  • Mycobacterial blood culture in advanced HIV
  • Urine mycobacterial studies when genitourinary disease is suspected
  • Bone-marrow or liver sampling in selected disseminated disease

Infective Endocarditis

Potential FUO Presentation

Subacute endocarditis may present with:

  • Prolonged fever
  • Weight loss
  • Anemia
  • Elevated inflammatory markers
  • Microscopic hematuria
  • Splenomegaly
  • Back pain

Risk Factors

  • Prosthetic valve
  • Previous endocarditis
  • Congenital heart disease
  • Intracardiac device
  • Injection-drug use
  • Recent bacteremia
  • Hemodialysis

Culture-Negative Endocarditis

Consider:

  • Prior antibiotic exposure
  • Coxiella burnetii

Nosocomial FUO

Historical Framework

Nosocomial FUO refers to fever that:

  • Develops during hospitalization
  • Was not present or incubating at admission
  • Remains unexplained after an appropriate evaluation of approximately three days
  • Includes adequate microbiologic culture incubation

Common Causes

  • Drug fever
  • Catheter-related bloodstream infection
  • C. difficile infection
  • Postoperative abscess or leak
  • DVT or pulmonary embolism
  • Pressure-wound infection
  • Acalculous cholecystitis
  • Transfusion reaction
  • Hospital-acquired pneumonia
  • Fungal infection

Approach

  1. Reconstruct the hospital timeline.
  2. Review every device.
  3. Review every new medication.
  4. Repeat a complete skin and wound examination.
  5. Search for postoperative and thrombotic complications.
  6. Use targeted cultures and imaging.
Nosocomial FUO substantially overlaps with the structured approach to persistent fever in a hospitalized patient.

Neutropenic FUO

Febrile Neutropenia Definition

Single Oral Temperature ≥38.3°C or ≥38.0°C Sustained for at Least 1 Hour

with:

ANC <500 Cells/µL or Expected to Decline Below 500 Cells/µL

FUO in Neutropenia

The term neutropenic FUO is used when fever persists without a clinically or microbiologically documented source.

Do not wait several days to begin treatment. Febrile neutropenia is an emergency requiring immediate empiric antipseudomonal antibiotics.

Initial Evaluation

  • Peripheral and central-line blood cultures
  • Focused examination
  • Chest imaging when respiratory findings exist
  • Urine testing when indicated
  • Assessment of skin, oral cavity, perineum, and catheter sites

Persistent Neutropenic Fever

Reassess for:

  • Invasive mold infection
  • Resistant bacterial infection
  • Catheter infection
  • Typhlitis
  • Drug fever
  • Viral infection

Antifungal Strategy

Empiric or preemptive mold-active therapy may be appropriate in high-risk patients with:

  • Prolonged profound neutropenia
  • Persistent or recurrent fever
  • Compatible CT or fungal biomarkers
  • No alternative source

HIV-Associated FUO

Historical Definition

Traditional HIV-associated FUO frameworks used:

  • Temperature above 38.3°C on several occasions
  • More than four weeks of outpatient fever or more than three days in the hospital
  • No diagnosis after an appropriate evaluation

Modern Context

In a person with durable viral suppression and preserved CD4 count, the differential increasingly resembles that of the general population.

With uncontrolled advanced HIV, prioritize:

  • Disseminated MAC
  • Tuberculosis
  • Cryptococcosis
  • Histoplasmosis
  • CMV disease
  • Pneumocystis
  • Lymphoma
  • Kaposi sarcoma

Empiric Treatment in FUO

General Rule

Stable Classic FUO → Avoid Empiric Therapy Until Diagnostic Evaluation Is Exhausted

Why Withhold Treatment?

  • Antibiotics sterilize cultures.
  • Corticosteroids alter pathology.
  • Temporary response may be misleading.
  • Adverse effects may create new diagnostic confusion.
  • Broad therapy may delay tissue diagnosis.

Exceptions

  • Hemodynamic instability
  • Neutropenia
  • Threatened vision from GCA
  • Strongly suspected TB with life-threatening disease
  • Rapidly progressive organ injury
  • Another condition in which delay creates substantial harm

Empiric Antibiotics

Usually Avoid in Stable Classic FUO

Indiscriminate antibiotics may:

  • Reduce blood-culture yield
  • Partially treat endocarditis
  • Cause drug fever
  • Cause C. difficile
  • Select resistant organisms

Use When:

  • Sepsis or shock is present.
  • A high-probability bacterial syndrome exists.
  • Neutropenic fever is present.
  • Delay would create immediate danger.

Stop When Unsupported

If the evaluation does not support bacterial infection and the patient is stable, stop empiric therapy rather than continuing indefinitely because fever persists.

Empiric Antituberculous Therapy

Potential Indications

  • Life-threatening suspected TB
  • TB meningitis
  • Miliary disease with deterioration
  • High epidemiologic likelihood when tissue diagnosis is unsafe or repeatedly unsuccessful

Why Avoid Routine Therapeutic Trials?

  • Clinical response may be slow and nonspecific.
  • Drug toxicity may be substantial.
  • Partial or inadequate treatment may select resistance.
  • Alternative diagnoses may temporarily improve.

Obtain Specimens First

Whenever feasible, collect respiratory, blood, CSF, lymph-node, bone-marrow, or other tissue specimens before starting empiric TB treatment.

Empiric Corticosteroids

Major Risks

  • Worsening occult infection
  • Strongyloides hyperinfection
  • Reduced lymphoma-biopsy yield
  • Reduced PET/CT uptake
  • Temporary nonspecific symptom improvement
  • Hyperglycemia and other toxicity

Appropriate Emergency Use

  • Threatened vision from giant-cell arteritis
  • Life-threatening vasculitis
  • HLH or macrophage-activation syndrome
  • Adrenal crisis
  • Severe inflammatory organ injury

Before Steroids When Possible

  • Obtain cultures.
  • Obtain needed biopsy tissue.
  • Evaluate TB and fungal risk.
  • Assess Strongyloides exposure.
  • Document the clinical rationale.
A dramatic corticosteroid response does not prove an inflammatory diagnosis. Lymphoma, infection, and other disorders may temporarily improve.

The Naproxen Test

Historically, rapid defervescence with naproxen was proposed as evidence supporting neoplastic fever.

Limitations

  • Inflammatory diseases may also respond.
  • Infections may show partial temperature improvement.
  • Response is not sufficiently specific.
  • NSAID toxicity may occur.
Do not use the naproxen response as a stand-alone diagnostic test for tumor fever.

Undiagnosed FUO

When the Evaluation Remains Negative

Reassess whether:

  • FUO criteria were truly met.
  • Fever was objectively documented.
  • Important exposures were missed.
  • A potential diagnostic clue was ignored.
  • A biopsy target exists.
  • Empiric treatment has obscured the diagnosis.

Observation May Be Appropriate

For a clinically stable patient with:

  • No progressive organ dysfunction
  • No high-risk immune defect
  • Negative appropriate imaging
  • No compelling biopsy target
  • Reliable outpatient follow-up

Follow-Up Plan

  • Temperature diary
  • Weight monitoring
  • Repeat history and examination
  • Repeat CBC and inflammatory markers
  • Immediate reassessment for new focal symptoms
  • Planned interval review rather than open-ended testing

Prognosis

General Principle

Patients with undiagnosed classic FUO who remain clinically stable after a thorough evaluation often have a favorable prognosis and may experience spontaneous resolution.

Worse Prognostic Features

  • Progressive organ dysfunction
  • Severe weight loss
  • Cytopenias
  • Very high LDH
  • Persistent bacteremia
  • Major immune suppression
  • Abnormal PET/CT requiring unresolved evaluation

Negative PET/CT

A negative FDG-PET/CT in a clinically stable patient may support a lower probability of focal inflammatory, malignant, or infectious disease and is associated in observational studies with a greater likelihood of spontaneous remission.

A favorable prognosis does not mean no follow-up is needed. The diagnosis may become apparent only when a new clue develops.

Practical FUO Diagnostic Algorithm

Step 1: Confirm FUO Criteria

  • Objective fever
  • More than three weeks
  • Multiple episodes
  • Immunocompetent status for classic FUO
  • Minimum evaluation completed

Step 2: Reconstruct the Entire Illness

  • Timeline
  • Travel and residence
  • Exposures
  • Medications
  • Devices and procedures
  • Previous antimicrobial treatment

Step 3: Repeat Complete Examination

  • Skin
  • Eyes
  • Oral cavity
  • Temporal arteries
  • Lymph nodes
  • Heart
  • Abdomen
  • Joints and spine

Step 4: Complete Minimum Testing

  • CBC, CMP, calcium, liver tests
  • ESR, CRP, ferritin, TSH
  • ANA, RF, ANCA
  • HIV, TB screening, urinalysis
  • Three blood-culture sets
  • CT chest, abdomen, and pelvis

Step 5: Follow Potential Diagnostic Clues

  • Targeted cultures
  • Targeted serology
  • Echocardiography
  • MRI
  • Specialist examination

Step 6: Consider FDG-PET/CT

Particularly when:

  • Minimum testing is unrevealing
  • Inflammatory markers remain elevated
  • No obvious biopsy target exists

Step 7: Obtain Confirmatory Tissue

  • Excisional lymph node
  • Temporal artery
  • Bone marrow
  • Liver
  • Skin or muscle
  • Other PET-directed tissue

Step 8: Avoid Empiric Therapy Unless Necessary

If stable and no diagnosis is established, structured observation may be safer than blind antibiotics or corticosteroids.

Daily FUO Checklist

  • Has fever been objectively documented?
  • Does the patient truly meet classic FUO criteria?
  • Is the patient immunocompetent?
  • What are today’s potential diagnostic clues?
  • Was the complete history repeated?
  • Were the skin, eyes, oral cavity, lymph nodes, heart, abdomen, joints, and spine examined?
  • Have all medications been reviewed?
  • Were blood cultures obtained correctly before antibiotics?
  • Has CT chest, abdomen, and pelvis been completed?
  • Is echocardiography indicated?
  • Is FDG-PET/CT appropriate?
  • Is there a biopsy target?
  • Were pathology, microbiology, and flow-cytometry needs discussed before biopsy?
  • Could this be TB or culture-negative endocarditis?
  • Could this be GCA, Still disease, vasculitis, or sarcoidosis?
  • Could this be lymphoma or another malignancy?
  • Could this be drug fever, thrombosis, thyroiditis, or factitious fever?
  • Is empiric treatment truly necessary?
  • Is structured observation safe?

  • Geography, travel, and key exposures
  • Potential diagnostic clues
  • Minimum evaluation completed

Common Clinical Scenarios

Case 1: Seven Days of Unexplained Fever

A stable patient has had daily fever for one week with negative initial cultures.

This is prolonged undifferentiated fever, not yet classic FUO. Continue targeted evaluation rather than prematurely ordering every rare-disease test.

Case 2: Three Weeks of Fever Without CT Imaging

A patient has had fever for four weeks, but only CBC, urinalysis, and chest radiography were completed.

The patient has not yet completed the modern minimum evaluation. Obtain appropriate foundational laboratory testing and CT chest, abdomen, and pelvis.

Case 3: FUO With No Diagnostic Clues

CT, cultures, and minimum testing are unrevealing, but ESR and CRP remain elevated.

FDG-PET/CT is an appropriate early next diagnostic test and may identify a biopsy target.

Case 4: Older Patient With Fever and Shoulder Stiffness

A 74-year-old patient has fever, anemia, high ESR, and new shoulder-girdle stiffness without headache.

Evaluate for polymyalgia rheumatica and occult giant-cell arteritis, including large-vessel disease.

Case 5: Fever, Murmur, and Negative Cultures

A patient received antibiotics before cultures and has a prosthetic valve, splenomegaly, hematuria, and persistent fever.

Evaluate for culture-negative endocarditis with TEE and targeted testing for organisms such as Coxiella and Bartonella.

Case 6: FUO With Cervical Lymphadenopathy

PET/CT shows an intensely active accessible cervical lymph node.

Arrange excisional biopsy with pathology, flow cytometry, bacterial, fungal, and mycobacterial studies as appropriate.

Case 7: High Ferritin and Cytopenias

A patient has persistent fever, splenomegaly, thrombocytopenia, hepatitis, triglyceride elevation, and ferritin above 20,000 ng/mL.

Urgently evaluate for HLH and its trigger, including infection and lymphoma. Do not wait for every criterion if the patient is deteriorating.

Case 8: Fever and Evanescent Rash

A patient develops a salmon-colored rash during daily fever spikes with arthritis and neutrophilic leukocytosis.

Adult-onset Still disease is possible after infection and malignancy have been adequately excluded.

Case 9: Positive ANCA and Hematuria

A febrile patient has positive ANCA, hematuria, and a new murmur.

Do not assume primary vasculitis. Endocarditis can cause positive ANCA and glomerulonephritis.

Case 10: Fever After New Antibiotic

A clinically improving patient develops persistent fever, rash, eosinophilia, and liver-test abnormalities after two weeks of antibiotics.

Stop the suspected drug and evaluate for DRESS rather than broadening antimicrobial therapy.

Case 11: Advanced HIV With FUO

A patient with CD4 count 18 has fever, weight loss, anemia, and elevated alkaline phosphatase.

Prioritize disseminated MAC, TB, histoplasmosis, lymphoma, and other advanced-HIV opportunistic disease. Obtain appropriate blood cultures, antigen tests, imaging, and tissue.

Case 12: Fever During Profound Neutropenia

A patient has ANC 100 cells/µL and no defined source.

This is febrile neutropenia and requires immediate antipseudomonal antibiotics. Do not wait for the prolonged classic-FUO evaluation.

Case 13: Negative PET/CT and Stable Clinical Course

A stable patient has an appropriate negative minimum evaluation and negative PET/CT without weight loss or organ dysfunction.

Structured observation with close follow-up may be safer than blind biopsy, antibiotics, or corticosteroids.

Case 14: Extreme Temperatures With Normal Inflammatory Markers

A patient reports temperatures above 41°C but appears well, has normal ESR and CRP, and is never febrile during supervised measurement.

Verify the fever objectively and consider measurement manipulation or factitious illness using a careful nonconfrontational approach.

Case 15: Visual Symptoms During FUO Evaluation

An older patient with prolonged fever and elevated inflammatory markers develops transient monocular vision loss.

Treat immediately for possible giant-cell arteritis while arranging confirmatory vascular imaging or biopsy.

Common Pitfalls

  • Calling three days of unexplained fever FUO
  • Failing to verify the temperature objectively
  • Using FUO as a substitute for an incomplete evaluation
  • Applying classic FUO criteria to a profoundly immunocompromised patient
  • Ignoring geography, migration, and travel history
  • Failing to repeat the history and examination
  • Ordering broad serologic panels without a clinical hypothesis
  • Interpreting low-titer autoantibodies as a diagnosis
  • Using a negative IGRA to exclude active TB
  • Missing culture-negative endocarditis after prior antibiotics
  • Failing to obtain adequate blood-culture volume
  • Performing only TTE despite high endocarditis probability
  • Delaying FDG-PET/CT until after months of low-yield testing
  • Treating PET uptake as a final diagnosis
  • Biopsying an incidental low-yield site rather than the best PET target
  • Sending all biopsy tissue in formalin
  • Using fine-needle aspiration alone when lymphoma is suspected
  • Performing blind bone-marrow biopsy without hematologic clues
  • Diagnosing sarcoidosis from granulomas before excluding infection
  • Starting corticosteroids before lymphoma biopsy
  • Starting corticosteroids before assessing TB, fungal, and Strongyloides risk
  • Using a steroid response to prove inflammatory disease
  • Using the naproxen response to diagnose tumor fever
  • Giving indiscriminate broad-spectrum antibiotics to a stable FUO patient
  • Waiting to treat febrile neutropenia until a source is found
  • Missing GCA in an older patient without headache
  • Attributing extreme ferritin solely to Still disease
  • Ignoring drug fever because rash or eosinophilia is absent
  • Prematurely accusing a patient of factitious fever
  • Continuing invasive testing when structured observation is safer

Board and ID Consult Pearls

  • Classic FUO requires more than three weeks of unexplained fever after a minimum standard evaluation.
  • The traditional fever threshold is at least 38.3°C on several occasions.
  • Classic FUO definitions are intended for immunocompetent patients.
  • Neutropenic and advanced-HIV fever require separate urgent frameworks.
  • Inflammation of unknown origin uses a similar diagnostic strategy without documented high fever.
  • Most FUO cases are unusual presentations of recognizable diseases.
  • Potential diagnostic clues should drive testing.
  • Repeat history and physical examination are essential.
  • Geographic residence and travel history materially change the differential.
  • The minimum evaluation includes CBC, metabolic and liver tests, inflammatory markers, ferritin, TSH, selected autoantibodies, HIV testing, TB screening, urinalysis, and blood cultures.
  • Three properly collected blood-culture sets are part of the minimum evaluation.
  • CT chest, abdomen, and pelvis is preferred foundational imaging when available.
  • FDG-PET/CT is an important early next study after an unrevealing minimum evaluation.
  • PET/CT identifies a biopsy target but rarely supplies the final diagnosis alone.
  • A negative PET/CT may support a favorable prognosis in a stable patient.
  • TEE is required when endocarditis probability remains high despite negative TTE.
  • Negative blood cultures do not exclude culture-negative endocarditis.
  • Coxiella and Bartonella are important causes of culture-negative endocarditis.
  • A negative IGRA does not exclude active or disseminated TB.
  • Excisional lymph-node biopsy is preferred when lymphoma is possible.
  • Coordinate tissue handling before biopsy so cultures and flow cytometry are not lost.
  • Bone-marrow biopsy is highest yield with cytopenias, splenomegaly, abnormal smear, or disseminated-disease concern.
  • Granulomas require exclusion of mycobacterial and fungal infection.
  • GCA may present with FUO without headache.
  • Visual symptoms from possible GCA require immediate corticosteroids.
  • Adult-onset Still disease often causes spiking fever, evanescent rash, arthritis, and very high ferritin.
  • Ferritin elevation is not specific for Still disease or HLH.
  • Endocarditis can produce positive ANCA and glomerulonephritis.
  • Lymphoma is a major malignant cause of FUO.
  • Avoid corticosteroids before lymphoma biopsy when clinically safe.
  • Drug fever can occur without rash or eosinophilia.
  • Febrile neutropenia requires immediate antipseudomonal therapy.
  • Stable classic FUO generally should not receive blind antibiotics or corticosteroids.
  • Factitious fever requires objective evidence and a nonconfrontational multidisciplinary approach.
  • Many undiagnosed stable FUO cases resolve spontaneously and have a favorable prognosis.

Bottom Line

Fever of unknown origin is a prolonged, objectively documented febrile syndrome that remains unexplained after completion of a defined minimum evaluation. Classic FUO generally requires more than three weeks of fever in an immunocompetent patient. Acute undifferentiated fever, sepsis without a source, postoperative fever, and febrile neutropenia should not be prematurely labeled FUO.

The evaluation begins by verifying fever, reconstructing the entire timeline, repeating a detailed history and physical examination, and identifying potential diagnostic clues. The foundational workup includes blood counts, metabolic and liver testing, inflammatory markers, ferritin, TSH, selected autoimmune testing, HIV and TB evaluation, urinalysis, three properly collected blood-culture sets, and CT of the chest, abdomen, and pelvis.

When the initial evaluation remains unrevealing, FDG-PET/CT is an important early next test, particularly when inflammatory markers are elevated and no biopsy target is apparent. PET findings must be confirmed through targeted microbiology, pathology, echocardiography, vascular imaging, or tissue biopsy.

Important causes include tuberculosis, culture-negative endocarditis, deep abscesses, zoonotic infections, giant-cell arteritis, adult-onset Still disease, systemic vasculitis, lymphoma, drug fever, thrombosis, thyroiditis, HLH, and factitious fever. Age, geography, travel, immune status, medication exposure, and implanted devices substantially alter the differential.

Stable patients with classic FUO should generally not receive blind broad-spectrum antibiotics or corticosteroids because empiric treatment may sterilize cultures, alter pathology, obscure lymphoma, worsen occult infection, and generate misleading clinical responses. Exceptions include instability, febrile neutropenia, threatened vision from giant-cell arteritis, severe inflammatory organ injury, and other time-critical syndromes. When an appropriate investigation remains negative and the patient is stable, structured observation with close follow-up is often safer than continued indiscriminate testing.

Goals of Care, Time-Limited Trials, and Post-ICU Syndrome

Added July 15, 2026 • Pulmonary and Critical Care • ~30 min read

A practical framework for serious-illness communication, code status, prognosis, surrogate decision-making, time-limited trials, conflict, palliative care, symptom management, ICU survivorship, post-intensive-care syndrome, family burden, rehabilitation, and follow-up.

Clinical Overview

Goals-of-care work aligns medical treatment with the patient’s values, acceptable outcomes, and prognosis. It is not synonymous with stopping treatment. Palliative care can occur alongside full intensive treatment.

Medical Facts + Patient Values + Recommendation + Reassessment

Prepare for the Conversation

  • Clarify decision-maker and capacity.
  • Review diagnosis, trajectory, best- and worst-case outcomes, and uncertainty.
  • Identify decisions that must be made now versus later.
  • Use professional interpreters.
  • Include nursing, consultants, social work, chaplaincy, and palliative care as appropriate.

Eliciting Values

  • What abilities make life worth living?
  • What outcomes would be unacceptable?
  • How much burden would the patient accept for a chance of recovery?
  • What prior experiences shape these preferences?
  • What did the patient say before losing capacity?

Code Status

Code status addresses treatment during cardiac or respiratory arrest; it does not define the rest of the care plan. A patient can be DNR and still receive vasopressors, antibiotics, surgery, NIV, or ICU care when those treatments match the goals.

Making a Recommendation

After understanding values, clinicians should offer a recommendation rather than presenting every intervention as an isolated menu choice.

“Based on what you told me about independence and avoiding prolonged machines, I recommend a time-limited trial of ICU treatment without CPR if the heart stops.”

Time-Limited Trials

  • Define the treatment being tried.
  • Define the hoped-for clinical changes.
  • Set a time or event for reassessment.
  • Define what happens if the goals are met, partly met, or not met.
  • Document the plan and communicate it across teams.

Conflict and Uncertainty

  • Name the disagreement respectfully.
  • Check for misunderstanding, grief, guilt, cultural or religious concerns, and inconsistent messages.
  • Use a structured second opinion or ethics consultation.
  • Avoid declaring treatment futile casually.
  • Maintain symptom relief and respect during conflict.

Symptom Management

  • Treat pain, dyspnea, anxiety, delirium, thirst, secretions, and nausea.
  • Opioids are appropriate for dyspnea when titrated to comfort.
  • Do not use neuromuscular blockade without adequate sedation.
  • During withdrawal of ventilation, prepare medications and explain expected changes to family.

Post-Intensive-Care Syndrome

  • New physical weakness and reduced endurance
  • Cognitive impairment
  • Anxiety, depression, or PTSD
  • Sleep disturbance
  • Chronic pain
  • Family psychological and financial burden

Risk increases with prolonged ventilation, delirium, immobility, severe sepsis, and preexisting frailty.

ICU Survivorship Plan

  • Medication reconciliation and device removal
  • Rehabilitation and mobility plan
  • Swallow, nutrition, and oxygen assessment
  • Cognitive and mental-health screening
  • Follow-up for kidney, lung, and cardiac injury
  • Primary-care and specialist handoff
  • Family support and clear return precautions

Common Pitfalls

  • Asking “do you want everything?”
  • Treating DNR as do-not-treat
  • No clinician recommendation
  • Different teams giving conflicting prognoses
  • Time-limited trial without endpoints
  • Ignoring family distress and post-ICU disability

Bottom Line

Good ICU decision-making combines honest prognosis with the patient’s values and a clear recommendation. Use time-limited trials when uncertainty is real, provide symptom relief throughout, and plan for the physical, cognitive, and psychological consequences of critical illness.

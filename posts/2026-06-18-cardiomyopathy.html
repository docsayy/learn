Cardiomyopathy: A Practical Guide for Internal Medicine Residents

Added June 18, 2026 • Cardiology • ~12 min read

A practical review of cardiomyopathy classification, causes, diagnostic approach, echocardiographic patterns, cardiac MRI utility, and management principles for residents and clinicians.

Overview

Cardiomyopathy refers to diseases of the heart muscle that lead to abnormal cardiac structure, impaired contraction, impaired relaxation, arrhythmias, or heart failure.

For clinical reasoning, the most useful question is:

Is the ventricle dilated, hypertrophied, stiff/restrictive, or affected by a specific acquired process such as ischemia, myocarditis, toxins, stress, or infiltrative disease?

Major Types of Cardiomyopathy

Type Classic Pattern Common Associations
Dilated cardiomyopathy Large ventricle, reduced systolic function Ischemic disease, alcohol, viral myocarditis, genetic, chemotherapy, tachycardia
Hypertrophic cardiomyopathy Thickened ventricle, often asymmetric septal hypertrophy Genetic sarcomere mutations, sudden cardiac death risk
Restrictive cardiomyopathy Stiff ventricle, impaired filling, preserved or mildly reduced EF Amyloidosis, sarcoidosis, hemochromatosis, radiation
Arrhythmogenic cardiomyopathy Fibrofatty replacement, ventricular arrhythmias Often genetic, classically right ventricular involvement
Takotsubo cardiomyopathy Transient stress-induced LV dysfunction Emotional or physical stressor

Dilated Cardiomyopathy

Dilated cardiomyopathy is characterized by ventricular dilation and impaired systolic function. It commonly presents as heart failure with reduced ejection fraction.

Common Causes

  • Ischemic cardiomyopathy
  • Alcohol-related cardiomyopathy
  • Viral myocarditis
  • Genetic cardiomyopathy
  • Chemotherapy-related cardiomyopathy, especially anthracyclines
  • Tachycardia-mediated cardiomyopathy
  • Peripartum cardiomyopathy
  • Thyroid disease
  • Hemochromatosis

Clinical Clues

  • Progressive dyspnea
  • Orthopnea or paroxysmal nocturnal dyspnea
  • Volume overload
  • S3 gallop
  • Reduced ejection fraction on echocardiogram
Tachycardia-mediated cardiomyopathy is important because it may be reversible after rate or rhythm control.

Hypertrophic Cardiomyopathy

Hypertrophic cardiomyopathy, or HCM, is usually an inherited disorder involving abnormal myocardial hypertrophy. The classic pattern is asymmetric septal hypertrophy.

Key Features

  • Left ventricular hypertrophy not explained by hypertension or aortic stenosis
  • May cause dynamic LV outflow tract obstruction
  • Can present with exertional dyspnea, chest pain, syncope, or sudden cardiac death
  • Family history is important

Murmur Clue

The murmur of obstructive HCM typically increases with maneuvers that decrease preload.

Maneuver Effect on HCM Murmur
Valsalva Increases murmur
Standing Increases murmur
Squatting Decreases murmur
Handgrip Usually decreases murmur
Avoid aggressive preload reduction in obstructive HCM because this can worsen outflow tract obstruction.

Restrictive Cardiomyopathy

Restrictive cardiomyopathy is characterized by impaired ventricular filling due to a stiff myocardium. Systolic function may be preserved until late.

Common Causes

  • Amyloidosis
  • Sarcoidosis
  • Hemochromatosis
  • Radiation-induced heart disease
  • Endomyocardial fibrosis

Clinical Pattern

  • Right-sided heart failure symptoms may predominate
  • Peripheral edema
  • Ascites
  • Elevated JVP
  • Preserved or mildly reduced EF
  • Biatrial enlargement

Amyloidosis Clues

  • Increased wall thickness on echo
  • Low voltage ECG despite thickened ventricles
  • Carpal tunnel syndrome
  • Neuropathy
  • Proteinuria
  • Heart failure with preserved EF
Thick walls on echo plus low voltage on ECG should make you think about cardiac amyloidosis.

Arrhythmogenic Cardiomyopathy

Arrhythmogenic cardiomyopathy is a genetic disorder involving replacement of myocardium with fibrofatty tissue. It classically affects the right ventricle, but left-sided and biventricular forms occur.

Clinical Clues

  • Palpitations
  • Syncope
  • Ventricular tachycardia
  • Family history of sudden cardiac death
  • Young patient with unexplained ventricular arrhythmia
Exercise can increase arrhythmia risk in arrhythmogenic cardiomyopathy. Patients need cardiology guidance on activity restriction.

Takotsubo Cardiomyopathy

Takotsubo cardiomyopathy is a transient stress-induced cardiomyopathy that can mimic acute coronary syndrome.

Classic Pattern

  • Often triggered by emotional or physical stress
  • Chest pain or dyspnea
  • Troponin elevation may occur
  • ECG changes may mimic MI
  • Coronary angiography often shows no obstructive culprit lesion
  • Classic echo pattern is apical ballooning
Takotsubo is also called stress cardiomyopathy or broken heart syndrome.

Myocarditis

Myocarditis is inflammation of the myocardium and may present with chest pain, heart failure, arrhythmias, or cardiogenic shock.

Causes

  • Viral infections
  • Autoimmune disease
  • Immune checkpoint inhibitors
  • Toxins or medications
  • Giant cell myocarditis

Clinical Clues

  • Recent viral syndrome
  • Chest pain with troponin elevation
  • New heart failure
  • Arrhythmias
  • Cardiac MRI with myocardial edema or late gadolinium enhancement
Fulminant myocarditis can deteriorate quickly and may require ICU-level care, mechanical circulatory support, and advanced heart failure consultation.

Diagnostic Workup

Initial Evaluation

  • History and family history
  • Medication and toxin review
  • Alcohol and stimulant use history
  • ECG
  • Transthoracic echocardiogram
  • BNP or NT-proBNP
  • Troponin if ischemia or myocarditis is suspected
  • Basic metabolic panel, magnesium, liver tests, CBC
  • TSH
  • Iron studies if hemochromatosis is possible

When to Consider Ischemic Evaluation

Ischemic cardiomyopathy is common and should be considered in patients with reduced EF, regional wall motion abnormalities, angina, coronary risk factors, or unexplained new heart failure.

Role of Echocardiography

Echo Finding Possible Diagnosis
Dilated LV with reduced EF Dilated cardiomyopathy, ischemic cardiomyopathy, toxic, viral, tachycardia-mediated
Asymmetric septal hypertrophy Hypertrophic cardiomyopathy
Thick walls with diastolic dysfunction Amyloidosis, hypertensive heart disease, HCM
Biatrial enlargement with restrictive filling Restrictive cardiomyopathy
Regional wall motion abnormality Ischemia, infarction, stress cardiomyopathy

Role of Cardiac MRI

Cardiac MRI is useful when echocardiography does not fully explain the diagnosis or when myocarditis, infiltrative disease, scar pattern, or arrhythmogenic cardiomyopathy is suspected.

  • Can assess myocardial edema
  • Can detect scar and fibrosis
  • Can support myocarditis diagnosis
  • Can suggest infiltrative cardiomyopathy
  • Can help risk stratify some cardiomyopathies

Management Principles

Dilated Cardiomyopathy with Reduced EF

Management generally follows guideline-directed medical therapy for heart failure with reduced ejection fraction.

  • ARNI or ACE inhibitor/ARB when appropriate
  • Evidence-based beta blocker
  • Mineralocorticoid receptor antagonist
  • SGLT2 inhibitor
  • Diuretics for congestion
  • ICD consideration if EF remains reduced after optimized therapy
  • CRT consideration if criteria are met

Hypertrophic Cardiomyopathy

  • Avoid dehydration
  • Avoid excessive preload reduction in obstructive physiology
  • Beta blockers are commonly used for symptoms
  • Non-dihydropyridine calcium channel blockers may be considered in selected patients
  • Assess sudden cardiac death risk
  • Family screening is important
  • Refer to cardiology or HCM specialist when possible

Restrictive or Infiltrative Cardiomyopathy

  • Treat the underlying disease when possible
  • Use diuretics carefully for congestion
  • Evaluate for amyloidosis, sarcoidosis, or hemochromatosis when suggested by the clinical picture
  • Consider advanced imaging and specialist referral

Myocarditis

  • Supportive care
  • Avoid intense exercise during recovery
  • Treat heart failure if present
  • Monitor for arrhythmias
  • Consider advanced heart failure support in fulminant cases

Clinical Pearls

  • Dilated cardiomyopathy means a big ventricle with poor squeeze.
  • Hypertrophic cardiomyopathy means thick muscle, sometimes with dynamic obstruction.
  • Restrictive cardiomyopathy means stiff ventricles with impaired filling.
  • Low voltage ECG plus thick ventricular walls should suggest amyloidosis.
  • Tachycardia-mediated cardiomyopathy can improve after rhythm or rate control.
  • Takotsubo can mimic ACS but is usually transient.
  • Cardiac MRI is helpful for myocarditis, infiltrative disease, scar, and unclear cardiomyopathy.
  • Always consider ischemic disease in new reduced EF.
  • Family history matters, especially in HCM and arrhythmogenic cardiomyopathy.
  • Unexplained syncope, ventricular arrhythmias, or sudden death family history should prompt urgent cardiology evaluation.

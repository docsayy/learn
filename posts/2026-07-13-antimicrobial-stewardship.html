Antimicrobial Stewardship: The Complete Advanced Resident Guide

Added July 13, 2026 • Infectious Disease • ~32 min read

Antimicrobial stewardship is the clinical discipline of choosing the correct antimicrobial only when it is needed, at the appropriate dose, route, and duration, while minimizing toxicity, resistance, unnecessary broad-spectrum exposure, and disruption of the microbiome. This guide reviews the practical reasoning expected of a PGY-3 medicine resident on infectious disease consultation rounds and on ABIM-style examinations.

What Is Antimicrobial Stewardship?

Antimicrobial stewardship is the coordinated effort to improve how antimicrobial medications are selected, prescribed, administered, monitored, and discontinued. Although the term is frequently used interchangeably with antibiotic stewardship, antimicrobial stewardship also includes antifungal, antiviral, and antiparasitic therapy.

Stewardship is not simply using fewer antibiotics. The goal is to provide effective treatment to patients who truly have infection while reducing unnecessary exposure and avoiding preventable harm.

Right Diagnosis • Right Drug • Right Dose • Right Route • Right Duration

Why Stewardship Matters

  • Improves the likelihood of clinical cure
  • Reduces medication toxicity and drug interactions
  • Reduces Clostridioides difficile infection
  • Reduces selection of resistant organisms
  • Preserves the patient's microbiome
  • Reduces unnecessary IV access and hospitalization
  • Improves transition from IV to oral therapy
  • Reduces cost without compromising care
  • Preserves antimicrobial effectiveness for future patients
Stewardship means giving broad therapy rapidly when it is needed, then narrowing or stopping therapy as soon as the clinical and microbiologic data allow.

Antibiotics Are Not Benign

Every antimicrobial prescription creates potential benefit and potential harm. Even an apparently well-tolerated antibiotic may cause allergic reactions, nephrotoxicity, hepatotoxicity, cytopenias, QT prolongation, drug interactions, microbiome disruption, resistance, or C. difficile infection.

The question is therefore not simply:

“Could this be an infection?”

The more useful questions are:

  • How likely is bacterial infection?
  • What is the most likely source?
  • Which organisms are plausible?
  • What host factors change the differential?
  • How sick is the patient?
  • Is source control required?
  • What is the narrowest effective treatment?

The Stewardship Framework

The Five D's of Antimicrobial Stewardship

Principle Clinical Question
Diagnosis Does the patient truly have a bacterial, fungal, viral, or parasitic infection?
Drug Which antimicrobial has the necessary spectrum and tissue penetration?
Dose Is the dose appropriate for weight, renal function, severity, and site?
Duration What is the shortest evidence-supported course?
De-escalation Can therapy be narrowed, changed to oral treatment, or stopped?

The Three Moments of Antibiotic Decision-Making

Moment Questions
Before starting Does the patient have an infection requiring antibiotics? What cultures and imaging are needed first?
After initiation Is the empiric regimen appropriate for the syndrome, host, severity, and local resistance?
At 48–72 hours Can treatment be stopped, narrowed, converted to oral therapy, or assigned a definitive duration?
The most important stewardship action performed by a medicine resident is the structured 48–72-hour reassessment.

  • Preauthorization for selected broad-spectrum agents
  • Facility-specific clinical pathways
  • Antibiotic time-outs
  • Automatic IV-to-oral conversion
  • Renal dose adjustment
  • Pharmacokinetic dosing services
  • Penicillin allergy evaluation
  • Rapid diagnostic interpretation
  • Syndrome-specific interventions
  • Duration and stop-date protocols
The strongest stewardship programs combine infectious disease physician leadership, pharmacy expertise, microbiology support, infection prevention, and bedside clinician engagement.

Initial Clinical Approach Before Prescribing

Step 1: Define the Clinical Syndrome

Do not begin with the antibiotic. Begin with the syndrome.

  • Pneumonia
  • Bloodstream infection
  • Meningitis or encephalitis
  • Urinary infection
  • Skin and soft-tissue infection
  • Intra-abdominal infection
  • Bone or joint infection
  • Endovascular infection
  • Febrile neutropenia
  • Undifferentiated sepsis

Step 2: Assess Severity

Feature Stewardship Implication
Septic shock Immediate broad empiric treatment is appropriate; do not delay for nonessential testing.
Organ dysfunction Use doses appropriate for severe infection and altered pharmacokinetics.
Hemodynamic stability Allows more targeted evaluation before exposing the patient to broad therapy.
Immunocompromised host Broader differential, lower threshold for treatment, and different organisms.
Localized mild syndrome Narrower therapy or observation may be appropriate.

Step 3: Identify Host Factors

  • Age
  • Pregnancy
  • Renal or hepatic dysfunction
  • Neutropenia
  • HIV and CD4 count
  • Transplantation
  • Chronic corticosteroids or biologic therapy
  • Asplenia
  • Recent surgery or hospitalization
  • Indwelling devices or prosthetic material
  • Prior resistant organisms
  • Recent antimicrobial exposure
  • Drug allergy history

Step 4: Review Previous Microbiology

Previous cultures may be more useful than population-level risk factors. Review urine, respiratory, wound, blood, and surveillance cultures from prior admissions.

A patient previously colonized or infected with an ESBL-producing organism, CRE, MRSA, or resistant Pseudomonas has increased risk of recurrence, especially after recent antibiotic exposure.

Step 5: Decide Whether Antibiotics Are Needed Now

Situation Approach
Septic shock or bacterial meningitis suspected Give effective empiric therapy immediately.
Stable patient with uncertain diagnosis Obtain focused diagnostic information before committing to treatment.
Likely viral syndrome Avoid antibiotics unless bacterial coinfection is reasonably suspected.
Colonization without symptoms Do not treat.
Drainable infection Arrange source control in addition to antibiotics.

Cultures and Diagnostic Stewardship

Diagnostic stewardship means ordering the correct test, from the correct site, at the correct time, and interpreting the result in the clinical context.

General Culture Principles

  • Obtain cultures before antibiotics when this does not meaningfully delay treatment.
  • Culture the actual site of infection whenever safely possible.
  • Avoid superficial swabs when deep tissue or aspirate is needed.
  • Document whether cultures were obtained before or after antibiotics.
  • Interpret results based on specimen quality and collection method.
  • Do not treat a laboratory result without a compatible clinical syndrome.

Blood Cultures

When Blood Cultures Are High Yield

  • Sepsis or septic shock
  • Suspected endocarditis
  • Suspected vascular catheter infection
  • Severe pneumonia
  • Possible vertebral osteomyelitis or epidural abscess
  • Febrile neutropenia
  • Severe immunocompromise
  • Cholangitis or severe intra-abdominal infection
  • Concern for Staphylococcus aureus bacteremia

When Repeat Blood Cultures Are Usually Needed

  • Staphylococcus aureus bacteremia
  • Candidemia
  • Suspected or confirmed endocarditis
  • Persistent fever or hemodynamic instability
  • Retained infected intravascular device
  • Concern for inadequate source control
  • Persistent bacteremia with selected multidrug-resistant organisms

When Repeat Cultures Are Often Unnecessary

  • Uncomplicated gram-negative bacteremia with clear clinical improvement
  • Transient bacteremia with controlled source and no endovascular concern
  • Routine surveillance after clinical resolution

Urine Cultures

A positive urine culture does not equal urinary tract infection.

  • Send urine cultures when symptoms or systemic signs suggest infection.
  • Avoid reflex cultures for isolated pyuria or abnormal urinalysis.
  • Pyuria is common with catheters and does not prove infection.
  • Cloudy or foul-smelling urine alone is not diagnostic of UTI.
  • Do not culture urine solely because a patient is confused without a compatible syndrome.

Respiratory Cultures

  • Assess sputum quality before interpreting culture results.
  • Endotracheal aspirates frequently show colonizing organisms.
  • Candida in respiratory cultures almost always represents colonization.
  • A positive multiplex respiratory PCR must be interpreted with symptoms and imaging.
  • A negative MRSA nasal test can help discontinue empiric MRSA therapy in appropriate pneumonia patients.

Wound Cultures

  • Avoid superficial swabs of chronic ulcers.
  • Obtain deep tissue, aspirate, or bone culture when possible.
  • Chronic wounds are frequently colonized with multiple organisms.
  • Treat the patient and the invasive infection, not every organism reported from a surface swab.
Poor-quality specimens create misleading results, unnecessary broadening, and prolonged treatment.

Colonization vs Infection

Finding Likely Interpretation Usual Approach
Asymptomatic bacteriuria Colonization Do not treat except pregnancy or selected urologic procedures.
Candida in sputum Airway colonization Do not treat as Candida pneumonia.
Candida in urine without symptoms Often colonization Remove risk factors; treat only selected high-risk situations.
Positive catheter urine culture without symptoms Colonization Do not treat.
Coagulase-negative staphylococcus in one blood culture bottle Possible contamination Interpret with devices, number of sets, time to positivity, and symptoms.
Staphylococcus aureus in blood True bacteremia until proven otherwise Full evaluation and treatment.
Candida in blood True invasive infection Never dismiss as contamination.
Positive C. difficile PCR without diarrhea Colonization Do not treat or test asymptomatic patients.

Signs Supporting True Infection

  • Compatible symptoms and examination findings
  • Objective inflammation or organ dysfunction
  • Imaging consistent with infection
  • High-quality specimen from a sterile site
  • Repeated recovery of the same organism
  • Clinical response to targeted therapy
  • Evidence of invasion rather than surface colonization

Empiric Antibiotic Selection

Empiric therapy is treatment initiated before the causative organism and susceptibilities are known. The regimen should cover the most likely pathogens while avoiding unnecessary spectrum.

Four Inputs for Empiric Therapy

Syndrome + Severity + Host + Local Resistance

Questions Before Choosing Empiric Therapy

  1. What is the likely source?
  2. Which organisms commonly cause this syndrome?
  3. Does the patient need MRSA coverage?
  4. Does the patient need antipseudomonal coverage?
  5. Are anaerobes likely clinically important?
  6. Is there a risk of ESBL, AmpC, CRE, or other resistance?
  7. Does the drug reach the site?
  8. What previous cultures are available?
  9. What allergies, toxicities, and interactions matter?
  10. Is source control required?

When Broad Empiric Therapy Is Appropriate

  • Septic shock
  • Neutropenic fever
  • Bacterial meningitis
  • Severe healthcare-associated infection
  • Known recent multidrug-resistant colonization
  • Severe immunocompromise
  • Unclear source with rapidly progressive organ dysfunction

When Narrow Empiric Therapy Is Reasonable

  • Stable community-acquired syndrome
  • Low risk for resistant organisms
  • Clear localized source
  • Reliable follow-up
  • Previous cultures showing predictable susceptible organisms
Broad empiric therapy is a temporary strategy, not a permanent plan.

Atypical Coverage

  • Azithromycin
  • Clarithromycin
  • Doxycycline
  • Levofloxacin
  • Moxifloxacin

Antipseudomonal Agents

  • Piperacillin-tazobactam
  • Cefepime
  • Ceftazidime
  • Aztreonam
  • Meropenem
  • Imipenem-cilastatin
  • Ciprofloxacin
  • Levofloxacin
  • Aminoglycosides
  • Selected newer beta-lactam/beta-lactamase inhibitor agents
Ertapenem does not cover Pseudomonas.

Antibiograms and Resistance Risk

An antibiogram summarizes local susceptibility patterns. It is designed to guide empiric therapy, not replace patient-specific culture results.

How to Use an Antibiogram

  • Use the antibiogram for empiric selection before susceptibilities are known.
  • Use the most specific unit or population data available.
  • Recognize that ICU resistance may differ from general medical wards.
  • Review organism-specific susceptibility rather than relying on overall hospital percentages.
  • Once patient-specific susceptibilities return, use those results instead.

Risk Factors for Resistant Organisms

  • Previous infection or colonization with the organism
  • Recent broad-spectrum antimicrobial exposure
  • Recent hospitalization
  • Prolonged hospitalization
  • Residence in a healthcare facility
  • Chronic dialysis
  • Invasive devices
  • Immunocompromise
  • Frequent healthcare contact
  • Travel or hospitalization in areas with high resistance
Previous patient-specific cultures are often more predictive than the label “healthcare-associated.”

Do Not Use Resistance Risk as an Excuse for Permanent Broad Therapy

It may be appropriate to begin with broad therapy in a severely ill patient with resistant-organism risk. Once cultures, imaging, clinical response, and source-control data become available, narrow therapy promptly.

Pharmacokinetics, Pharmacodynamics, and Optimized Dosing

Pharmacokinetics

Pharmacokinetics describes what the body does to the drug:

  • Absorption
  • Distribution
  • Metabolism
  • Elimination

Pharmacodynamics

Pharmacodynamics describes the relationship between drug exposure and antimicrobial activity.

Drug Group Major PK/PD Driver Dosing Strategy
Beta-lactams Time above MIC Frequent dosing, prolonged infusion, or continuous infusion in selected severe infections
Aminoglycosides Peak concentration relative to MIC High-dose extended-interval dosing when appropriate
Fluoroquinolones AUC relative to MIC Ensure adequate total exposure
Vancomycin AUC relative to MIC AUC-guided dosing for serious MRSA infection
Daptomycin Concentration-dependent activity Higher weight-based dosing may be needed for severe bacteremia or endovascular disease

Vancomycin Monitoring

For serious MRSA infections, AUC-guided monitoring is preferred over trough-only monitoring. A commonly used target AUC/MIC is 400–600 when the MIC is assumed to be 1 mg/L.

Vancomycin is appropriate empirically for suspected MRSA, but definitive MSSA bacteremia should generally be treated with cefazolin, nafcillin, or oxacillin rather than vancomycin.

Extended-Infusion Beta-Lactams

Prolonged infusion may increase the amount of time that free drug concentrations remain above the organism's MIC. This may be useful for:

  • Severe sepsis or septic shock
  • High bacterial burden
  • Organisms with higher but susceptible MICs
  • Augmented renal clearance
  • Critically ill patients with altered volume of distribution

Loading Doses

A loading dose depends primarily on volume of distribution rather than renal clearance. Critically ill patients may still require a full loading dose even when maintenance doses need renal adjustment.

Special Dosing Situations

  • Obesity
  • Severe renal dysfunction
  • Augmented renal clearance
  • Intermittent hemodialysis
  • Continuous renal replacement therapy
  • Extracorporeal membrane oxygenation
  • Burns
  • Major fluid resuscitation
  • Hypoalbuminemia
A creatinine that appears “normal” does not guarantee normal drug clearance in a patient with low muscle mass.

The Antibiotic Time-Out

An antibiotic time-out is a structured reassessment usually performed within 48–72 hours after initiation.

Questions to Ask

  1. Does the patient still appear to have an infection?
  2. What is the most likely source?
  3. Are the cultures meaningful?
  4. Is the current therapy active against the identified organism?
  5. Can MRSA coverage be stopped?
  6. Can antipseudomonal coverage be stopped?
  7. Can therapy be narrowed?
  8. Can the patient change to oral therapy?
  9. Has source control occurred?
  10. What is the planned stop date?

Possible Outcomes of the Time-Out

Decision Example
Stop therapy Viral infection confirmed with no evidence of bacterial coinfection
Narrow therapy Vancomycin plus cefepime changed to cefazolin for MSSA
Change route IV antibiotic converted to a highly bioavailable oral agent
Optimize dose Renally adjusted or changed to prolonged infusion
Broaden therapy Culture reveals resistance not covered by the initial regimen
Pursue source control Persistent fever due to undrained abscess

De-escalation

De-escalation means reducing antimicrobial spectrum or intensity once diagnostic information becomes available.

Forms of De-escalation

  • Stopping unnecessary agents
  • Changing from combination therapy to monotherapy
  • Stopping MRSA coverage
  • Stopping antipseudomonal coverage
  • Changing from a carbapenem to a narrower beta-lactam
  • Changing from IV to oral therapy
  • Assigning a shorter course

Examples

Initial Therapy Final Data Stewardship Decision
Vancomycin + cefepime Blood cultures grow MSSA Change to cefazolin or antistaphylococcal penicillin.
Vancomycin + piperacillin-tazobactam Urine culture grows susceptible E. coli Stop vancomycin and narrow gram-negative therapy.
Cefepime + metronidazole No abscess; uncomplicated biliary infection with source control Narrow and define a short course.
Vancomycin for pneumonia MRSA nasal test negative and no other MRSA evidence Discontinue vancomycin if clinical context supports it.
Meropenem Organism susceptible to ceftriaxone without resistant mechanism Change to ceftriaxone.
De-escalation is not undertreatment. It is the transition from uncertainty to precision.

Treatment Duration

Longer treatment is not automatically safer. Excess duration increases toxicity, resistance, C. difficile, line complications, and medication burden.

Typical Duration Framework

Infection Common Duration in Selected Patients Important Qualification
Community-acquired pneumonia At least 5 days Patient must be clinically stable and improving.
Hospital-acquired / ventilator-associated pneumonia Approximately 7 days May differ with slow response, abscess, empyema, or complications.
Uncomplicated cellulitis Approximately 5 days Extend if there is inadequate improvement.
Uncomplicated cystitis Agent-specific short course Depends on drug, sex, pregnancy, anatomy, and host factors.
Pyelonephritis Agent- and severity-dependent Shorter courses may be used with selected high-bioavailability agents.
Complicated intra-abdominal infection Approximately 4 days after adequate source control Persistent symptoms should trigger evaluation for failed source control, not automatic extension.
Uncomplicated gram-negative bacteremia Approximately 7 days in selected stable patients Requires source control, clinical stability, and no endovascular focus.
Uncomplicated S. aureus bacteremia At least 14 days All strict uncomplicated criteria must be met.
Complicated S. aureus bacteremia Usually 4–6 weeks Depends on endocarditis, bone, joint, device, or metastatic infection.
Native vertebral osteomyelitis Often approximately 6 weeks Longer therapy may be required in selected cases.
Endocarditis Usually several weeks Organism-, valve-, and complication-specific.
C. difficile infection Usually 10 days for an initial standard course Recurrence strategies differ.

What Determines Duration?

  • Site of infection
  • Organism
  • Source control
  • Presence of bacteremia
  • Endovascular involvement
  • Prosthetic material
  • Immune status
  • Clinical response
  • Ability to use active oral therapy
Persistent fever does not always mean antibiotics should be extended. Consider drug fever, thrombosis, inflammatory disease, tissue necrosis, resistant infection, or inadequate source control.

When Does Day 1 Begin?

The treatment clock may begin from:

  • The first day of effective therapy
  • The first negative blood culture
  • The date of source control
  • The date infected hardware is removed

The correct starting point depends on the infection. Document the intended treatment anchor clearly.

IV-to-Oral Conversion

IV therapy is not inherently stronger than oral therapy. The relevant question is whether the oral agent achieves adequate exposure at the site of infection.

Criteria for Oral Step-Down

  • Hemodynamic stability
  • Clinical improvement
  • Functional gastrointestinal absorption
  • Ability to take and adhere to oral medication
  • Active oral agent based on susceptibility
  • Adequate source control
  • No uncontrolled endovascular or CNS infection

Highly Bioavailable Oral Agents

Agent Approximate Clinical Role
Fluoroquinolones Selected susceptible gram-negative infections; weigh toxicity and resistance.
Linezolid Oral therapy achieves exposure similar to IV treatment.
Metronidazole Excellent oral absorption.
TMP-SMX Useful for susceptible urinary, skin, and selected invasive infections.
Doxycycline Excellent oral absorption for susceptible infections.
Clindamycin Good oral absorption when susceptibility is reliable.
Fluconazole Excellent oral bioavailability for susceptible fungal infection.

Potential Benefits of Oral Therapy

  • Removes IV-line infection and thrombosis risk
  • Facilitates discharge
  • Reduces nursing burden
  • Improves mobility
  • Reduces cost
  • Avoids outpatient IV complications

Situations Requiring Greater Caution

  • Bacterial meningitis
  • Uncontrolled endocarditis
  • Persistent bacteremia
  • Undrained abscess
  • Severe malabsorption
  • Hemodynamic instability
  • Unreliable adherence
  • Organism without a dependable oral option
Some traditionally IV-treated infections may be eligible for oral step-down in carefully selected patients, but complex endovascular, bone, joint, and hardware infections should be discussed with infectious disease specialists.

Antibiotic Allergy Assessment

A penicillin allergy label frequently leads to broader, less effective, more toxic, or more expensive treatment. Most patients who report penicillin allergy are not truly allergic when formally evaluated.

Questions to Ask

  • Which drug caused the reaction?
  • What exactly happened?
  • How soon after the dose did symptoms begin?
  • How long ago did the reaction occur?
  • Was treatment required?
  • Has the patient tolerated penicillins or cephalosporins since?
  • Was the reaction a side effect rather than allergy?

Low-Risk Histories

  • Isolated gastrointestinal upset
  • Headache
  • Fatigue
  • Family history of penicillin allergy
  • Remote vague childhood reaction
  • Pruritus without rash or systemic symptoms

High-Risk Immediate Reactions

  • Anaphylaxis
  • Angioedema
  • Bronchospasm
  • Hypotension
  • Immediate urticaria

Severe Delayed Reactions

  • Stevens-Johnson syndrome
  • Toxic epidermal necrolysis
  • DRESS
  • Acute generalized exanthematous pustulosis
  • Drug-induced hepatitis
  • Interstitial nephritis
  • Hemolytic anemia
Do not perform a routine oral challenge in a patient with a history of severe cutaneous adverse reaction or serious organ involvement.

Stewardship Approach

Risk Potential Approach
Low risk Structured history and, when appropriate, supervised direct oral challenge
Moderate risk Allergy testing or specialist assessment
High-risk IgE-mediated history Avoid culprit drug; consider testing or desensitization when essential
Severe delayed reaction Avoid culprit and related agents based on specialist guidance

Biomarkers and Rapid Diagnostics

Procalcitonin

Procalcitonin may support antimicrobial discontinuation in selected patients when interpreted with clinical improvement. It should not be used as the sole reason to withhold initial antibiotics from a patient with severe suspected bacterial infection.

Potential Uses

  • Supporting discontinuation in clinically improving sepsis patients
  • Helping distinguish bacterial from viral lower respiratory disease in selected contexts
  • Following trends rather than relying on a single value

Limitations

  • May be low early in infection
  • May be low in localized infection
  • May be elevated after trauma, surgery, burns, or shock
  • May be difficult to interpret in severe renal dysfunction
  • Does not replace examination, imaging, and cultures
Do not withhold initial therapy for radiographically confirmed community-acquired pneumonia solely because procalcitonin is low.

Rapid Molecular Diagnostics

  • Blood-culture identification panels
  • Respiratory pathogen panels
  • Meningitis/encephalitis panels
  • Gastrointestinal pathogen panels
  • MRSA nasal PCR
  • Carbapenemase gene detection
  • Resistance marker identification

Stewardship Principle

Rapid tests improve care only when results are reviewed promptly and translated into action. A rapid result that does not change therapy provides limited stewardship value.

MRSA Nasal Testing

A negative MRSA nasal PCR or culture has a high negative predictive value for MRSA pneumonia in many settings and may support early discontinuation of vancomycin or linezolid when the rest of the clinical picture is compatible.

A positive MRSA nasal result indicates colonization and has a much lower positive predictive value for MRSA pneumonia.

Source Control

Source control means physically removing, draining, debriding, or correcting the site that sustains infection.

Examples

  • Drain an abscess
  • Remove an infected central line
  • Debride necrotic tissue
  • Operate on necrotizing fasciitis
  • Relieve an obstructed infected urinary system
  • Perform ERCP for obstructed cholangitis
  • Remove infected prosthetic material when required
  • Drain empyema
  • Exchange or remove an infected urinary catheter
Antibiotics Cannot Reliably Sterilize an Undrained Abscess

Clues That Source Control Is Inadequate

  • Persistent bacteremia
  • Persistent fever despite active therapy
  • Worsening organ dysfunction
  • Increasing leukocytosis
  • Continued purulent drainage
  • Progressive imaging findings
  • Relapsing infection after treatment
When a patient does not improve despite an apparently active antibiotic, first reconsider the diagnosis and source control before automatically broadening therapy.

Approach to Resistant Organisms

ESBL-Producing Enterobacterales

Extended-spectrum beta-lactamases hydrolyze many penicillins and cephalosporins.

General Principles

  • Carbapenems are preferred for many invasive ESBL infections, especially outside the urinary tract.
  • Urinary infections may have additional options depending on site and susceptibility.
  • Oral TMP-SMX or fluoroquinolone step-down may be reasonable in selected stable patients when susceptible and source control is adequate.
  • Avoid using susceptibility results without considering infection site and resistance mechanism.

AmpC-Producing Enterobacterales

Certain organisms may induce AmpC beta-lactamase production during therapy, creating resistance despite initial susceptibility.

Common Higher-Risk Organisms

  • Enterobacter cloacae complex
  • Klebsiella aerogenes
  • Citrobacter freundii

Cefepime is often used when susceptible because of greater stability against AmpC. Third-generation cephalosporins may select resistance during therapy in invasive infection.

Carbapenem-Resistant Enterobacterales

Treatment depends on the carbapenemase mechanism.

Mechanism Potential Active Strategy
KPC Selected newer beta-lactam/beta-lactamase inhibitor agents
Metallo-beta-lactamase Aztreonam-based strategy with beta-lactamase inhibition or cefiderocol
OXA-48-like Ceftazidime-avibactam may be active
CRE therapy should be guided by susceptibility testing, resistance mechanism, infection site, and infectious disease consultation.

Difficult-to-Treat Resistant Pseudomonas

Potential options include:

  • Ceftolozane-tazobactam
  • Ceftazidime-avibactam
  • Imipenem-cilastatin-relebactam
  • Cefiderocol

Use susceptibility testing and avoid unnecessary combination therapy once a reliably active beta-lactam is identified.

MRSA

Syndrome Common Preferred Options
Bacteremia / endocarditis Vancomycin or daptomycin
Pneumonia Vancomycin or linezolid
Purulent SSTI Drainage plus active oral or IV therapy based on severity
Osteomyelitis Agent selected by susceptibility, surgery, and clinical context

VRE

  • Linezolid
  • Daptomycin
  • Selected organism- and site-specific alternatives

Stenotrophomonas maltophilia

This organism frequently colonizes the respiratory tract and hospital environment. Before treating, determine whether it is causing true infection. Potential active agents may include TMP-SMX, minocycline, levofloxacin, or cefiderocol depending on susceptibility and severity.

Acinetobacter baumannii

Carbapenem-resistant Acinetobacter requires mechanism-aware therapy and infectious disease input. Sulbactam-based treatment is central to many current approaches.

Newer agents should not be used simply because they are broad. Reserve them for infections in which their activity is needed.

Metronidazole Neuropathy with prolonged therapy Neurologic symptoms and duration

Carbapenems Seizures and reduced valproate levels Neurologic risk and medication interactions
Amphotericin B AKI, hypokalemia, hypomagnesemia, anemia Creatinine, potassium, magnesium, CBC

Syndrome-Based Stewardship

Community-Acquired Pneumonia

  • Confirm a compatible clinical syndrome and imaging finding.
  • Do not automatically cover MRSA or Pseudomonas.
  • Use validated patient-specific risk factors and previous cultures.
  • Stop vancomycin when MRSA is unlikely and testing supports de-escalation.
  • Avoid unnecessary anaerobic coverage for uncomplicated aspiration pneumonia.
  • Use a short course when clinically stable.

Hospital-Acquired and Ventilator-Associated Pneumonia

  • Distinguish true pneumonia from colonization and ventilator-associated events.
  • Use local ICU susceptibility data.
  • Obtain respiratory cultures before antibiotics when feasible.
  • Use dual antipseudomonal therapy only when justified by resistance or mortality risk.
  • De-escalate based on cultures and response.
  • A standard course is often approximately 7 days.

Urinary Tract Infection

  • Do not treat asymptomatic bacteriuria except in established indications.
  • Do not diagnose UTI from pyuria alone.
  • Remove or exchange a chronic catheter when appropriate.
  • Look for obstruction, abscess, stone, or prostatitis when response is poor.
  • Narrow therapy to urine culture results.
  • Use oral step-down when feasible.

Skin and Soft-Tissue Infection

  • Incision and drainage is the primary treatment for many abscesses.
  • Nonpurulent cellulitis usually requires streptococcal coverage.
  • Do not add MRSA coverage to every cellulitis case.
  • Bilateral lower-extremity erythema is frequently a noninfectious mimic.
  • Necrotizing infection requires immediate surgery.
  • Do not rely on superficial wound swabs.

Intra-Abdominal Infection

  • Source control is essential.
  • Choose therapy based on community versus healthcare exposure and severity.
  • Avoid redundant anaerobic coverage.
  • Do not continue antibiotics indefinitely after effective drainage.
  • Persistent illness after source control should prompt reevaluation for ongoing infection or another diagnosis.

Bloodstream Infection

  • Identify whether the isolate is a pathogen or contaminant.
  • Repeat cultures selectively.
  • Remove infected lines when indicated.
  • Search for endovascular or metastatic infection.
  • Define the start date and duration clearly.
  • Use narrow definitive therapy.

Staphylococcus aureus Bacteremia

  • Never dismiss as contamination.
  • Repeat blood cultures until clearance.
  • Evaluate for endocarditis.
  • Search for metastatic infection.
  • Remove infected intravascular devices.
  • Use cefazolin or antistaphylococcal penicillin for MSSA when appropriate.
  • Do not use a short course unless all uncomplicated criteria are fulfilled.

Bacterial Meningitis

  • Do not delay treatment for CT or LP when suspicion is high.
  • Use age- and host-specific empiric therapy.
  • Give dexamethasone before or with the first antibiotic dose when indicated.
  • Narrow once organism and susceptibilities are known.
  • Use CNS-penetrating doses.

Febrile Neutropenia

  • Start an antipseudomonal beta-lactam rapidly.
  • Do not add vancomycin routinely.
  • Add MRSA coverage for defined indications such as catheter infection, pneumonia, severe mucositis, or hemodynamic instability.
  • Reassess persistent fever based on duration, neutrophil recovery, and fungal risk.

Clostridioides difficile

  • Test only patients with clinically significant unformed stool.
  • Do not perform a test of cure.
  • Stop unnecessary inciting antibiotics.
  • Use guideline-directed therapy for initial and recurrent disease.
  • Do not treat PCR positivity without a compatible syndrome.

Antimicrobial Prophylaxis

Surgical Prophylaxis

  • Administer before incision at the recommended interval.
  • Select the narrowest agent that covers expected organisms.
  • Redose during prolonged procedures or major blood loss when indicated.
  • Do not continue prophylaxis simply because a drain remains in place.
  • Most prophylaxis should stop within the recommended perioperative window.

Medical Prophylaxis

  • PCP prophylaxis based on HIV CD4 count or immunosuppression
  • Toxoplasmosis prophylaxis in appropriate HIV patients
  • Antiviral prophylaxis after transplant or during selected chemotherapy
  • Antifungal prophylaxis in defined high-risk populations
  • Postexposure prophylaxis after meningococcal exposure
  • Rabies postexposure prophylaxis
  • HIV postexposure prophylaxis
Prophylaxis should have a defined indication, start point, dose, and stop point.

Common Prophylaxis Errors

  • Continuing surgical antibiotics for several postoperative days without infection
  • Using unnecessarily broad prophylaxis
  • Failing to adjust for weight
  • Incorrect timing before incision
  • Continuing prophylaxis after the risk period has ended

Prospective Audit and Feedback

The stewardship team reviews active antimicrobial therapy and provides recommendations to the treating team.

  • Change to oral treatment

  • Shorten duration
  • Clarify allergy

Education Is Necessary but Not Sufficient

Lectures alone rarely produce sustained prescribing improvement. Effective programs combine education with clinical pathways, order-set design, audit and feedback, diagnostic support, and prescriber accountability.

Daily Inpatient Antimicrobial Checklist

  • What infection are we treating?
  • Is infection still the most likely diagnosis?
  • What is the presumed source?
  • What cultures and imaging are available?
  • Were cultures obtained before antibiotics?
  • Is the organism colonization, contamination, or true infection?
  • Does the patient still need every antimicrobial?
  • Can MRSA coverage stop?
  • Can antipseudomonal coverage stop?
  • Can anaerobic coverage stop?
  • Is the dose correct for renal function and weight?
  • Does the drug reach the infected site?
  • Has source control occurred?
  • Can therapy change to oral?
  • What adverse effects require monitoring?
  • What is the treatment start date?
  • What is the planned stop date?

How to Present Antibiotics on ID Rounds

A strong resident presentation should include:

  • Drug name
  • Dose
  • Route
  • Start date
  • Renal function
  • Culture data
  • Reason for selection
  • Planned duration
  • Potential oral step-down
  • Monitoring plan
Avoid saying only, “The patient is on vancomycin and Zosyn.” Explain what each drug is covering and why that coverage remains necessary.

Common Clinical Scenarios

Case 1: Broad Therapy Continued Despite Negative Data

A stable patient was admitted with fever and started on vancomycin plus cefepime. Blood cultures remain negative, chest imaging shows no infiltrate, urinalysis is unremarkable, and the patient has a positive influenza test.

Stewardship decision: Stop antibacterial therapy unless there is a specific concern for bacterial coinfection.

Case 2: MSSA Bacteremia on Vancomycin

Blood cultures identify MSSA. The patient remains on vancomycin because the original regimen was “working.”

Stewardship decision: Change to cefazolin, nafcillin, or oxacillin unless a specific contraindication exists. Vancomycin is not preferred definitive therapy for MSSA bacteremia.

Case 3: Asymptomatic Bacteriuria

An older nursing-home resident is admitted after a fall. Urine culture grows E. coli, but there are no urinary symptoms, fever, hypotension, or leukocytosis.

Stewardship decision: Do not treat the urine culture. Search for other causes of the fall.

Case 4: Persistent Fever After Abscess Treatment

A patient with an intra-abdominal abscess remains febrile despite meropenem. Imaging shows an enlarging fluid collection.

Stewardship decision: This is primarily a source-control failure. Broader antibiotics will not replace drainage.

Case 5: Negative MRSA Nasal PCR in Pneumonia

A patient with community-acquired pneumonia is receiving ceftriaxone, azithromycin, and vancomycin. MRSA nasal PCR is negative, cultures show no MRSA, and the patient is improving.

Stewardship decision: Discontinue vancomycin if no other strong MRSA evidence exists.

Case 6: Positive Candida Respiratory Culture

Candida grows from an endotracheal aspirate in an intubated patient.

Stewardship decision: Candida in respiratory cultures almost always represents colonization. Do not start antifungal therapy solely for this finding.

Case 7: Cefepime Neurotoxicity

A patient with CKD receiving cefepime develops confusion and myoclonus.

Stewardship decision: Review renal dosing and consider cefepime neurotoxicity rather than assuming worsening sepsis.

Case 8: Prolonged IV Therapy Despite Oral Option

A clinically stable patient with susceptible gram-negative pyelonephritis is tolerating food and has negative follow-up blood cultures.

Stewardship decision: Evaluate for oral step-down rather than maintaining a central line solely for IV antibiotics.

Common Stewardship Pitfalls

  • Treating a positive culture instead of a clinical infection
  • Using vancomycin for definitive MSSA treatment
  • Continuing MRSA coverage without evidence of MRSA
  • Continuing antipseudomonal therapy in a low-risk patient
  • Treating asymptomatic bacteriuria
  • Treating Candida in respiratory cultures
  • Treating chronic wound surface cultures
  • Using antibiotics for a viral syndrome
  • Adding anaerobic coverage when it is already present
  • Using two beta-lactams without a clear indication
  • Failing to adjust for renal function
  • Failing to use a loading dose in critical illness
  • Continuing antibiotics because leukocytosis persists
  • Extending antibiotics instead of obtaining source control
  • Assuming IV therapy is always superior to oral therapy
  • Failing to document a stop date
  • Labeling every cephalosporin as unsafe in penicillin allergy
  • Using procalcitonin as the sole diagnostic test
  • Ignoring previous culture results
  • Using a newer antibiotic when a narrower older agent is active

Board and ID Consult Pearls

  • Antimicrobial stewardship is not simply reducing antibiotic use; it is optimizing treatment.
  • Start broad therapy rapidly in septic shock, then de-escalate when data return.
  • Obtain cultures before antibiotics when doing so does not delay urgent treatment.
  • A positive culture does not always represent infection.
  • Staphylococcus aureus and Candida in blood should never be casually dismissed.
  • Previous patient cultures are among the strongest predictors of resistance.
  • Vancomycin is inferior to beta-lactams for definitive MSSA bacteremia.
  • Daptomycin should not be used for pneumonia.
  • Ertapenem does not cover Pseudomonas.
  • Aztreonam covers aerobic gram-negative organisms but not gram-positive organisms or anaerobes.
  • Cefepime can cause neurotoxicity in renal dysfunction.
  • Beta-lactams are time-dependent antibiotics.
  • Aminoglycosides are concentration-dependent antibiotics.
  • For serious MRSA infection, vancomycin AUC-guided dosing is preferred.
  • Do not double cover anaerobes without a specific reason.
  • Do not treat asymptomatic bacteriuria except in defined circumstances.
  • Candida in respiratory cultures usually represents colonization.
  • A negative MRSA nasal test can support stopping MRSA pneumonia coverage.
  • Source control may be more important than broadening antibiotics.
  • Shorter courses are effective for many uncomplicated infections.
  • IV therapy is not automatically superior to highly bioavailable oral therapy.
  • A reported penicillin allergy should be clarified rather than accepted without history.
  • Procalcitonin is an adjunct, not a substitute for clinical judgment.
  • Every antibiotic order should have an indication and planned stop date.
  • The 48–72-hour antibiotic time-out is one of the highest-yield stewardship interventions.

Bottom Line

Antimicrobial stewardship begins with an accurate diagnosis. Before prescribing, define the syndrome, assess severity, review host factors, obtain appropriate cultures, and identify prior resistant organisms. In severe infection, give effective empiric therapy promptly. Once microbiology and clinical data return, stop unnecessary agents, narrow coverage, optimize dosing, obtain source control, convert to oral therapy when appropriate, and choose the shortest effective duration.

The strongest medicine resident does not simply know which antibiotic is broadest. The strongest resident can explain why an antimicrobial is needed, what organisms it covers, what it does not cover, how it reaches the infected site, how it should be dosed, when it can be narrowed, and exactly when it should stop.

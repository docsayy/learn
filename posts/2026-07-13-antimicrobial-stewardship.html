The ID Approach to Antibiotics

Antibiotic selection is not simply choosing a drug that appears active. Define the syndrome, estimate likely organisms, assess host and resistance factors, obtain useful cultures, identify source-control needs, and select the narrowest regimen with adequate exposure at the infected site.

Syndrome → Host → Organisms → Source Control → Drug → Dose → Duration → Reassessment
  • Confirm infection rather than colonization or sterile inflammation.
  • Obtain cultures before antibiotics when this will not create unsafe delay.
  • Use prior cultures, recent antibiotics, healthcare exposure, devices, and local antibiogram data.
  • Account for renal/hepatic function, body size, allergy history, interactions, pregnancy, and tissue penetration.
  • Reassess at 48–72 hours and narrow, stop, or convert to oral therapy.

High-Yield Spectrum

Target Commonly Useful Agents Key Limitation
MSSA Cefazolin; nafcillin/oxacillin Vancomycin is inferior definitive therapy when a beta-lactam can be used.
MRSA Vancomycin; linezolid; daptomycin depending on site Daptomycin is inactivated by pulmonary surfactant and does not treat pneumonia.
Enterococcus faecalis Ampicillin when susceptible Cephalosporins do not reliably treat Enterococcus.
Enterococcus faecium / VRE Linezolid or daptomycin High-dose daptomycin is often needed for serious bloodstream infection.
Pseudomonas Cefepime, piperacillin-tazobactam, ceftazidime, meropenem, newer agents Use susceptibility and severity; avoid unnecessary double coverage.
Anaerobes Metronidazole, beta-lactam/beta-lactamase inhibitors, carbapenems Do not add metronidazole to piperacillin-tazobactam or a carbapenem.
Atypical respiratory pathogens Azithromycin, doxycycline, respiratory fluoroquinolone Beta-lactams alone do not cover atypicals.

Resistance Mechanisms Residents Should Recognize

Mechanism Clue Practical Consequence
ESBL Resistance to many penicillins and cephalosporins in Enterobacterales Carbapenem often preferred for severe nonurinary infection; use susceptibility-guided options.
AmpC Enterobacter cloacae complex, Klebsiella aerogenes, Citrobacter freundii Avoid vulnerable agents in serious infection; cefepime may be appropriate when susceptible.
CRE Carbapenem resistance Use mechanism- and susceptibility-directed newer therapy with ID input.
MRSA Altered penicillin-binding protein Use MRSA-active agents; most cephalosporins do not cover MRSA.
VRE Vancomycin resistance genes Linezolid or daptomycin are common options.

Antibiotic Toxicities and Monitoring

Drug Major Issue Monitoring Pearl
Vancomycin AKI; infusion reaction Use AUC-guided dosing for serious MRSA infections when available.
Cefepime Encephalopathy, myoclonus, seizures Renally adjust, especially in older or critically ill patients.
TMP-SMX Hyperkalemia, creatinine rise, AKI, cytopenias Monitor potassium, creatinine, and CBC.
Linezolid Thrombocytopenia, neuropathy, serotonin toxicity Monitor CBC during prolonged therapy and review serotonergic drugs.
Daptomycin Myopathy, eosinophilic pneumonia Check CK and review statin use.
Fluoroquinolones QT prolongation, tendinopathy, neuropathy, dysglycemia, C. difficile Avoid when safer narrow options exist.
Rifampin Hepatotoxicity; strong inducer Never use alone for active bacterial infection; review all interactions.
Carbapenems Seizure risk; profound valproate interaction Avoid combining with valproate when possible.

IV-to-Oral Conversion

Oral therapy can be definitive when the patient is clinically improving, absorbs medications, has adequate source control, and an active oral agent reaches the infected site.

  • High-bioavailability options include fluoroquinolones, linezolid, TMP-SMX, metronidazole, doxycycline, clindamycin, and fluconazole.
  • Dose oral beta-lactams adequately for the indication.
  • S. aureus bacteremia, endovascular disease, CNS infection, and some hardware infections require specialist-directed decisions.

Duration and Source Control

Core principle: The correct duration often starts after adequate source control and, for many bloodstream infections, after documented clearance—not automatically on admission day.
  • Drain abscesses and infected collections.
  • Remove infected lines when indicated.
  • Relieve urinary or biliary obstruction.
  • Debride necrotic tissue.
  • Do not compensate for absent source control by extending or broadening antibiotics indefinitely.

Common Pitfalls

  • Treating colonization, asymptomatic bacteriuria, or a positive superficial wound culture.
  • Continuing vancomycin after MSSA is identified.
  • Using daptomycin for pneumonia.
  • Duplicating anaerobic coverage.
  • Failing to renal-dose cefepime or acyclovir.
  • Choosing a duration without defining source control and clinical endpoints.

Board and Consult Pearls

  • MSSA bacteremia: cefazolin or an antistaphylococcal penicillin when possible.
  • Daptomycin treats bloodstream infection but not pneumonia.
  • A positive culture is not synonymous with infection.
  • The narrowest adequate regimen is usually the safest regimen.
  • Source control is an antimicrobial intervention.

Shock and Hemodynamic Assessment

Added July 15, 2026 • Pulmonary and Critical Care • ~30 min read

A practical framework for recognizing shock, separating distributive, cardiogenic, hypovolemic, obstructive, and mixed physiology, integrating bedside examination, lactate, capillary refill, urine output, echocardiography, vasopressors, and source-specific therapy.

Clinical Overview

Shock is inadequate effective tissue perfusion. Blood pressure is only one component; a patient may have occult shock with preserved pressure or low pressure without tissue hypoperfusion.

Perfusion = Flow × Oxygen Content × Effective Distribution

Recognizing Shock

  • Altered mentation
  • Cool or mottled skin, or warm vasodilated extremities
  • Delayed capillary refill
  • Oliguria
  • Elevated or rising lactate
  • Hypotension relative to baseline
  • Narrow pulse pressure or bounding pulses depending on physiology

Shock Categories

TypePrimary ProblemExamples

MixedMore than one mechanismSepsis with cardiomyopathy, RV failure with vasodilation

First Minutes

  1. Stabilize airway and oxygenation.
  2. Place monitors and obtain IV/IO access.
  3. Assess perfusion, not pressure alone.
  4. Obtain ECG, bedside ultrasound, lactate, CBC, chemistry, and targeted cultures or imaging.
  5. Treat immediately reversible causes while defining physiology.

Bedside Hemodynamic Examination

  • Pulse pressure and heart rate
  • Jugular venous pressure
  • Skin temperature and capillary refill
  • Lung crackles or wheeze
  • Peripheral edema and venous congestion
  • New murmur
  • Unilateral breath sounds
  • Abdominal or pelvic bleeding clues

POCUS Integration

  • LV systolic function and gross filling
  • RV size and pressure-overload pattern
  • Pericardial effusion and tamponade physiology
  • Lung B-lines, consolidation, and pneumothorax
  • IVC as one clue—not a stand-alone volume test
  • Abdominal aorta and free fluid when relevant

Lactate and Capillary Refill

Lactate can rise from hypoperfusion, adrenergic stimulation, seizures, liver dysfunction, or drugs. Trend it alongside capillary refill, mental status, urine output, and hemodynamics. A normal lactate does not exclude shock.

Vasopressors and Inotropes

  • Norepinephrine is first-line for most vasodilatory shock.
  • Add vasopressin when norepinephrine dose is escalating.
  • Epinephrine can provide additional vasopressor and inotropic support.
  • Dobutamine is considered for persistent low output with adequate pressure.
  • Phenylephrine has limited roles because pure alpha stimulation can reduce stroke volume.

Cause-Directed Priorities

  • Sepsis: antimicrobials, source control, targeted fluid, norepinephrine.
  • Hemorrhage: control bleeding and balanced blood-component resuscitation.
  • MI-related shock: urgent reperfusion and mechanical-complication assessment.
  • PE: reperfusion strategy when high risk.
  • Tamponade or tension pneumothorax: immediate decompression.

Reassessment

  • MAP and pulse pressure
  • Capillary refill and skin perfusion
  • Urine output
  • Lactate trajectory
  • Stroke volume or VTI response
  • Vasopressor trend
  • Signs of pulmonary or venous congestion
Every intervention should have a predicted physiologic effect and a measured response.

Common Pitfalls

  • Equating normal pressure with normal perfusion
  • Giving repeated fluid without responsiveness testing
  • Using IVC size alone
  • Missing obstructive shock
  • Treating septic cardiomyopathy with escalating vasoconstrictor alone
  • Delaying source control

Bottom Line

Shock management is repeated bedside physiology: identify the dominant mechanism, restore perfusion, treat the cause, and reassess after every fluid, vasopressor, inotrope, or procedure.

Pulmonary Nodules and Lung Cancer

Added July 15, 2026 • Pulmonary and Critical Care • ~30 min read

A structured guide to incidental solid and subsolid nodules, malignancy probability, CT surveillance, PET-CT, biopsy selection, lung-cancer screening, staging principles, tissue acquisition, molecular testing, and avoiding both overtesting and missed cancer.

Clinical Overview

Most pulmonary nodules are benign, but management must reliably identify malignancy without causing unnecessary radiation, procedures, and anxiety. Nodule size, attenuation, growth, morphology, and patient risk determine the pathway.

First Questions

  • Is this a true nodule?
  • Is prior imaging available?
  • Solid, part-solid, or ground-glass?
  • What is the average diameter and growth?
  • Is the patient immunocompromised or known to have cancer?
  • Was the nodule found during screening or incidentally?

Malignancy Risk

  • Age and smoking history
  • Prior cancer
  • Upper-lobe location
  • Spiculation
  • Emphysema or fibrosis
  • Larger size and documented growth
  • Persistent part-solid component

Using Nodule Guidelines

Fleischner recommendations apply to many incidentally detected nodules but not every patient. Screening nodules use Lung-RADS or the relevant screening program. Known cancer, immune suppression, or suspected infection requires individualized management.

Solid Nodules

  • Very small low-risk nodules may require no follow-up.
  • Intermediate nodules generally undergo interval low-dose CT based on size and risk.
  • Nodules above about 8 mm require malignancy-probability assessment and consideration of PET-CT, biopsy, or resection.

Subsolid Nodules

  • Confirm persistence because transient inflammatory ground-glass nodules are common.
  • Persistent subsolid nodules may grow slowly but carry meaningful adenocarcinoma risk.
  • The solid component drives concern in part-solid nodules.
  • Surveillance often extends longer than for solid nodules.

Lung-Cancer Screening

  • Use current national eligibility criteria for annual low-dose CT.
  • Screen only when the patient is willing and fit enough for diagnostic evaluation and treatment.
  • Smoking cessation is part of every screening program.
  • Do not substitute chest radiography for low-dose CT screening.

Staging and Molecular Testing

  • Stage using TNM principles and appropriate PET-CT/brain imaging.
  • Obtain adequate tissue for histology and molecular markers.
  • Avoid biopsy of an inaccessible primary when a safer metastatic or nodal site provides diagnosis and stage.
  • Coordinate through a multidisciplinary thoracic oncology team.

Common Pitfalls

  • Applying Fleischner guidance to a patient with known cancer
  • Ignoring prior imaging
  • Calling two-year stability reassuring for every subsolid nodule
  • Ordering PET for very small nodules
  • Biopsying before estimating probability and operability
  • Losing follow-up during transitions

Bottom Line

Risk-stratify by nodule and patient features, compare prior imaging, and use the correct incidental or screening pathway. For higher-risk nodules, choose the diagnostic site and method that provides the safest path to diagnosis and stage.

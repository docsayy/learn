ACLS: The Complete Resident Guide to Running a Code

Added June 25, 2026 • Critical Care • ~24 min read

ACLS is organized around 2-minute cycles of high-quality CPR, rhythm assessment, defibrillation when indicated, epinephrine, antiarrhythmics, and active search for reversible causes. This guide gives residents a practical framework for running a code, understanding shockable versus non-shockable rhythms, using medications correctly, interpreting ETCO₂, managing post-ROSC care, and knowing when to escalate or terminate resuscitation.

The ACLS Algorithm: The Clock of Cardiac Arrest

Cardiac arrest is managed in 2-minute cycles. Every rhythm check, pulse check, medication decision, and shock decision should fit into this rhythm.

Time Action
Minute 0–2 Start CPR, attach monitor/defibrillator, assess rhythm
Minute 2–4 Defibrillate if shockable, immediately resume CPR
Minute 4–6 Give epinephrine when indicated, continue CPR
Minute 6–8 Give antiarrhythmic if refractory shockable rhythm, continue CPR
Repeat Continue 2-minute cycles until ROSC or termination
The golden rule of ACLS is simple: minimize interruptions in chest compressions.

High-Quality CPR: The Physiology

During cardiac arrest, the heart is not functioning as an active pump. Chest compressions create intrathoracic pressure changes that move blood through the body.

How Compressions Generate Flow

Phase Physiology
Compression Increases intrathoracic pressure and ejects blood from the heart and aorta
Recoil Decreases intrathoracic pressure and allows venous return to the heart
Coronary Perfusion Pressure = Aortic Diastolic Pressure − Right Atrial Pressure

Coronary perfusion pressure is essential for achieving ROSC. Every pause in compressions causes perfusion pressure to fall.

High-Quality CPR Targets

Component Target Why It Matters
Rate 100–120/min Too slow reduces perfusion; too fast reduces depth and recoil
Depth At least 2 inches / 5 cm in adults Generates forward blood flow
Recoil Full chest recoil Allows venous return
Interruptions Less than 10 seconds Maintains coronary perfusion pressure
Ventilation Avoid hyperventilation Excess ventilation increases intrathoracic pressure and reduces venous return
Compressor switch Every 2 minutes Fatigue rapidly reduces CPR quality
Do not lean on the chest between compressions. Incomplete recoil decreases venous return and worsens perfusion.

Defibrillation: The Reset Button

Defibrillation is an unsynchronized shock used for pulseless shockable rhythms.

Shockable Rhythms

Rhythm ECG Appearance Mechanism
Ventricular fibrillation Chaotic electrical activity with no organized QRS complexes Multiple re-entry circuits with no coordinated contraction
Pulseless ventricular tachycardia Wide-complex tachycardia without a pulse Organized ventricular arrhythmia without effective mechanical output

Why Defibrillation Works

Defibrillation depolarizes a critical mass of myocardium at once, interrupting re-entry circuits and allowing the heart’s intrinsic pacemakers to regain control if viable.

Energy Selection

Patient Initial Energy Subsequent Shocks
Adult, biphasic defibrillator 200 J or manufacturer recommendation Same or higher energy
Adult, monophasic defibrillator 360 J 360 J
Pediatric 2–4 J/kg Escalate per pediatric algorithm
After any shock in cardiac arrest, immediately resume CPR for 2 minutes before checking rhythm again.

Defibrillation vs Cardioversion

Feature Defibrillation Cardioversion
Synchronization Unsynchronized Synchronized to R-wave
Used for VF and pulseless VT Unstable SVT, AF, atrial flutter, VT with pulse
Pulse present? No Yes
Reason for timing Patient is pulseless, so shock immediately Avoid shocking during T-wave and causing VF

Non-Shockable Rhythms: Asystole and PEA

Non-shockable rhythms are treated with CPR, epinephrine, and aggressive search for reversible causes.

Asystole

  • No organized electrical activity
  • Confirm leads are attached
  • Increase gain if needed
  • Check another lead
  • Fine VF can mimic asystole
  • Treatment: CPR + epinephrine + reversible causes
Do not shock true asystole. There is no organized electrical activity to reset.

PEA: Pulseless Electrical Activity

PEA means electrical activity is present but there is no palpable pulse. It is a syndrome of mechanical pump failure, not a single rhythm diagnosis.

PEA Pattern Possible Clues
Narrow-complex PEA Tamponade, tension pneumothorax, massive PE, hypovolemia
Wide-complex PEA Hyperkalemia, sodium-channel blocker toxicity, severe acidosis
Bradycardic PEA Severe metabolic issue, hypoxia, terminal rhythm
Tachycardic PEA Massive PE, hypovolemia, tamponade, tension pneumothorax
PEA is not solved by shocking. It is solved by CPR, epinephrine, and finding the cause.

Cardiac Arrest Pharmacology

Epinephrine

Feature Details
Dose 1 mg IV/IO every 3–5 minutes
Mechanism Alpha-1 vasoconstriction increases aortic diastolic pressure and coronary perfusion pressure
Best timing Early in non-shockable arrest; after shocks have started in shockable arrest
Epinephrine improves the chance of ROSC, but neurologically intact survival depends heavily on high-quality CPR, early defibrillation, arrest cause, and downtime.

Antiarrhythmics for Refractory VF/pVT

Medication Dose Notes
Amiodarone 300 mg IV/IO bolus, then 150 mg if recurrent Common first choice for refractory VF/pVT
Lidocaine 1–1.5 mg/kg IV/IO, then 0.5–0.75 mg/kg Alternative to amiodarone; useful especially in ischemic VT/VF

Calcium

Calcium is not given routinely in cardiac arrest. Give it only for specific indications.

  • Hyperkalemia with ECG changes
  • Calcium channel blocker overdose
  • Hypocalcemia
  • Hypermagnesemia
  • Magnesium toxicity in obstetric patients
Formulation Dose Comment
Calcium chloride 1 g IV More bioavailable, more caustic to veins
Calcium gluconate 3 g IV Safer peripherally, less elemental calcium

Sodium Bicarbonate

Bicarbonate is not routine ACLS therapy. Consider it only in selected cases.

  • Severe metabolic acidosis with hemodynamic compromise
  • Tricyclic antidepressant overdose with QRS widening
  • Hyperkalemia as a temporizing measure
  • Severe salicylate poisoning

Magnesium

Indication Dose
Torsades de pointes 2 g IV
Suspected hypomagnesemia-related arrhythmia 2 g IV
Atropine and vasopressin are no longer part of routine cardiac arrest ACLS for asystole or PEA.

The Hs and Ts: Reversible Causes

The Hs and Ts are the causes you can actually treat during CPR.

The 5 Hs

Cause How to Suspect Treatment
Hypovolemia Trauma, GI bleed, sepsis, flat IVC, narrow-complex PEA Fluids, blood products, hemorrhage control
Hypoxia Respiratory arrest, airway obstruction, cyanosis, low oxygen Oxygenation, ventilation, airway management
Hydrogen ion / acidosis Sepsis, DKA, uremia, prolonged arrest Ventilation, treat cause, bicarbonate only if selected severe cases
Hyperkalemia / hypokalemia Dialysis patient, renal failure, overdose, ECG changes Calcium for hyperK, insulin/glucose, albuterol, bicarbonate, dialysis; replace potassium if hypoK
Hypothermia Exposure, drowning, cold environment Rewarming; prolonged resuscitation may be appropriate

The 5 Ts

Cause How to Suspect Treatment
Tension pneumothorax Trauma, ventilation, unilateral breath sounds, JVD, PEA Immediate needle decompression or finger thoracostomy/chest tube
Tamponade Trauma, cancer, uremia, pericarditis, JVD, clear lungs Pericardiocentesis or thoracotomy depending on cause
Toxins Overdose, pill bottles, QRS widening, bradycardia, hypotension Specific antidotes, sodium bicarbonate for TCA, calcium/high-dose insulin for CCB, naloxone for opioids
Thrombosis: pulmonary Sudden collapse, DVT risk, RV strain, narrow-complex PEA Systemic thrombolysis, ECMO, embolectomy when appropriate
Thrombosis: coronary Known CAD, STEMI, shockable rhythm PCI after ROSC; consider advanced support if refractory
During PEA, ultrasound can rapidly evaluate tamponade, RV strain, pneumothorax, severe hypovolemia, and cardiac standstill.

Airway Management During CPR

In early cardiac arrest, compressions and defibrillation matter most. Airway is important, but it should not interrupt high-quality CPR.

Airway Options

Device When to Use Caveats
Bag-mask ventilation Initial airway management Works best with two-person technique; avoid hyperventilation
Supraglottic airway When BVM inadequate or prolonged resuscitation Does not fully protect from aspiration
Endotracheal tube Definitive airway, post-ROSC, prolonged arrest, oxygenation failure Do not pause compressions longer than 10 seconds

ETCO₂: The Most Useful Monitor

ETCO₂ Finding Interpretation
10–20 mmHg during CPR Reasonable CPR quality
Persistently <10 mmHg Poor compressions, prolonged downtime, massive PE, tamponade, or poor prognosis
Sudden rise to >35 mmHg ROSC likely
Flat waveform after intubation Esophageal intubation until proven otherwise
Avoid hyperventilation. Too much ventilation increases intrathoracic pressure and decreases venous return.

Mechanical CPR and Advanced Interventions

Mechanical CPR

Mechanical compression devices can provide consistent compressions and allow transport or procedures during CPR.

Best Uses

  • Prolonged resuscitation
  • Cardiac catheterization lab
  • Transport during CPR
  • Limited staff situations
  • High-risk environments where manual CPR is difficult

ECMO / ECPR

Extracorporeal CPR may be considered in selected refractory arrests.

Good Candidate Features Poor Candidate Features
Young patient Unwitnessed prolonged arrest
Witnessed arrest Prolonged no-flow time
Bystander CPR Severe irreversible comorbidity
Shockable rhythm Unwitnessed asystole
Potentially reversible cause No reversible cause identified

Double Sequential Defibrillation

Double sequential defibrillation may be considered for refractory VF after multiple failed shocks and antiarrhythmic therapy depending on local protocol.

  • Two defibrillators
  • Two pad vectors
  • One anterior-lateral set
  • One anterior-posterior set
  • Shocks delivered nearly simultaneously

Post-ROSC Care

ROSC is only the beginning. Post-arrest care focuses on oxygenation, perfusion, coronary evaluation, temperature control, and neurologic protection.

Immediate Priorities

  1. Confirm pulse and perfusing rhythm.
  2. Use ETCO₂ rise as a clue to ROSC.
  3. Start with 100% oxygen, then titrate to avoid hyperoxia.
  4. Target oxygen saturation around 94–98%.
  5. Maintain MAP ≥65 mmHg or SBP ≥90 mmHg.
  6. Obtain 12-lead ECG.
  7. Activate cath lab for STEMI or high suspicion of coronary occlusion.
  8. Prevent fever and use temperature management when indicated.

Post-ROSC Targets

Parameter Target
Oxygenation Avoid hypoxia and hyperoxia; SpO₂ 94–98%
Blood pressure MAP ≥65 mmHg or SBP ≥90 mmHg
Ventilation Avoid severe hypercapnia or hypocapnia
Temperature Prevent fever; consider targeted temperature management if comatose
ECG Look for STEMI or ischemia

Neuroprognostication

Do not prognosticate too early. Use multimodal assessment after confounders such as sedation, hypothermia, and metabolic derangements are addressed.
  • Clinical exam
  • Pupillary and corneal reflexes
  • EEG
  • Somatosensory evoked potentials
  • MRI brain
  • Biomarkers when used locally

When to Stop: Termination of Resuscitation

Termination decisions depend on arrest context, witnessed status, rhythm, downtime, reversible causes, patient wishes, comorbidities, and institutional policy.

Factors Supporting Continued Resuscitation

  • Witnessed arrest
  • Bystander CPR
  • Shockable rhythm
  • Reversible cause identified
  • Hypothermia
  • Young patient
  • ECMO candidate
  • Intermittent signs of life or recurrent ROSC

Factors Supporting Termination

  • Unwitnessed arrest with prolonged downtime
  • No bystander CPR
  • Persistent asystole
  • No reversible cause found
  • No ROSC despite prolonged high-quality ACLS
  • Severe irreversible underlying disease
  • Known goals of care against aggressive resuscitation
Family communication should be clear, compassionate, and focused on what has been done, what is happening physiologically, and whether ongoing resuscitation can still achieve the patient’s goals.

The Complete ACLS Algorithm

Text Algorithm

No pulse → Activate code → Start CPR → Attach monitor/defibrillator → Analyze rhythm

Shockable Pathway: VF / Pulseless VT

  1. Shock immediately.
  2. Resume CPR for 2 minutes.
  3. Check rhythm.
  4. Shock again if still shockable.
  5. Resume CPR.
  6. Give epinephrine 1 mg IV/IO every 3–5 minutes.
  7. After recurrent shockable rhythm, give amiodarone or lidocaine.
  8. Search for Hs and Ts.
  9. Continue until ROSC or termination.

Non-Shockable Pathway: PEA / Asystole

  1. Start CPR immediately.
  2. Give epinephrine 1 mg IV/IO as soon as possible.
  3. Continue CPR for 2 minutes.
  4. Recheck rhythm.
  5. If still non-shockable, continue CPR and epinephrine every 3–5 minutes.
  6. Aggressively search for and treat Hs and Ts.
  7. If rhythm becomes shockable, move to shockable pathway.

Critical Decision Points

Do I Intubate Now?

Situation Answer
First 5–10 minutes of typical arrest Usually no; prioritize compressions and defibrillation
Hypoxic or asphyxial arrest Airway becomes more urgent
After ROSC Consider definitive airway if unable to protect airway or oxygenate
Prolonged CPR Consider supraglottic airway or intubation without interrupting compressions

Do I Give Calcium or Bicarbonate?

Situation Give?
Routine arrest No
Hyperkalemia Yes, calcium; consider bicarbonate as temporizing measure
TCA overdose Yes, sodium bicarbonate
Severe metabolic acidosis Consider bicarbonate if severe and clinically relevant
Calcium channel blocker overdose Yes, calcium plus targeted overdose therapy

Do I Call ECMO?

  • Call early for young, witnessed, refractory VF/pVT with bystander CPR and suspected reversible cause.
  • ECMO is generally less useful for prolonged unwitnessed asystole without reversible cause.

Common Clinical Scenarios

Scenario 1: VF After Initial 200 J Shock Fails

Resume CPR immediately. Continue 2-minute cycle. Shock again at same or higher energy. Give epinephrine and antiarrhythmic per algorithm.

Scenario 2: PEA in a Dialysis Patient

Think hyperkalemia. Give calcium immediately if ECG or clinical context supports it, then insulin/glucose, bicarbonate, albuterol, and arrange emergent dialysis if ROSC.

Scenario 3: Narrow-Complex PEA After Sudden Collapse

Think massive PE, tamponade, tension pneumothorax, or hypovolemia. Use bedside ultrasound if available without delaying CPR.

Scenario 4: Arrest with ETCO₂ Suddenly Rising from 12 to 42

This likely indicates ROSC. Finish the cycle if needed, then check rhythm and pulse promptly.

Scenario 5: Torsades Degenerating Into Pulseless Arrest

Defibrillate if pulseless, give magnesium 2 g IV, correct potassium and magnesium, stop QT-prolonging medications, and consider overdrive pacing if recurrent after ROSC.

Clinical Pearls

  • Run codes in 2-minute cycles.
  • High-quality CPR is the most important intervention.
  • Minimize compression pauses to less than 10 seconds.
  • Shock VF and pulseless VT immediately.
  • Do not shock asystole or PEA.
  • Resume CPR immediately after every shock.
  • Epinephrine is 1 mg IV/IO every 3–5 minutes.
  • Amiodarone or lidocaine can be used for refractory VF/pVT.
  • Atropine is not used for asystole or PEA in modern ACLS.
  • Calcium and bicarbonate are not routine arrest medications.
  • Use calcium for hyperkalemia, CCB overdose, hypocalcemia, or hypermagnesemia.
  • Use bicarbonate for TCA overdose, severe acidosis, hyperkalemia, or salicylate poisoning.
  • PEA is a syndrome, not a single rhythm diagnosis.
  • Use Hs and Ts aggressively during PEA.
  • ETCO₂ is one of the best monitors of CPR quality and ROSC.
  • A sudden ETCO₂ rise suggests ROSC.
  • Do not hyperventilate during CPR.
  • Post-ROSC care should avoid hypoxia, hyperoxia, hypotension, and fever.
  • Do not prognosticate neurologic outcome too early after arrest.

Bottom Line

ACLS is built around high-quality CPR, early defibrillation for shockable rhythms, epinephrine, antiarrhythmics for refractory VF/pVT, and aggressive treatment of reversible causes. Shock VF and pulseless VT. Do not shock PEA or asystole. Keep compression pauses short, switch compressors every 2 minutes, avoid hyperventilation, and use ETCO₂ to monitor CPR quality and detect ROSC. After ROSC, focus on oxygenation, blood pressure, ECG/cath lab decisions, temperature management, and delayed multimodal neurologic prognostication.

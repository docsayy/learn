Healthcare-Associated and Device-Related Infections: The Complete Advanced Resident Guide

Added July 14, 2026 • Infectious Disease • ~50 min read

Healthcare-associated infections develop during or soon after medical care and are frequently associated with invasive devices, surgery, mechanical ventilation, antibiotic exposure, environmental contamination, and transmission of multidrug-resistant organisms. This guide reviews the clinical approach to central-line bloodstream infection, catheter-associated urinary infection, hospital-acquired and ventilator-associated pneumonia, surgical-site infection, dialysis-catheter infection, CSF-device infection, implanted cardiac and vascular-device infection, diagnostic stewardship, device removal, antimicrobial treatment, isolation precautions, outbreak recognition, and evidence-based prevention.

Clinical Overview

A healthcare-associated infection is an infection acquired while receiving care in a hospital, nursing facility, dialysis center, clinic, surgical facility, or another healthcare environment. Some infections become clinically apparent only after discharge.

Major Categories

  • Central-line-associated bloodstream infection
  • Catheter-related bloodstream infection
  • Catheter-associated urinary tract infection
  • Hospital-acquired pneumonia
  • Ventilator-associated pneumonia
  • Surgical-site infection
  • Clostridioides difficile infection
  • Device-associated ventriculitis and meningitis
  • Dialysis-catheter infection
  • Cardiac-device infection
  • Vascular-graft infection
  • Prosthetic-joint and orthopedic-hardware infection
  • Transmission of multidrug-resistant organisms
Remove Unnecessary Devices + Obtain Meaningful Cultures + Control the Source + Use the Narrowest Effective Therapy

The Major Clinical Questions

  1. Does the patient truly have an infection?
  2. Is the positive culture contamination, colonization, or invasive disease?
  3. Is an invasive device the source?
  4. Can the device be removed?
  5. Is there an undrained collection, infected thrombus, vegetation, or metastatic focus?
  6. Which resistant organisms are plausible from previous cultures and healthcare exposure?
  7. Can empiric therapy be narrowed or discontinued?
  8. Which infection-prevention actions are required now?

Time-Critical Situations

  • Septic shock with a suspected infected central catheter
  • Staphylococcus aureus bacteremia with an intravascular device
  • Candidemia with a central venous catheter
  • Pseudomonas bloodstream infection involving a catheter
  • Necrotizing or organ-space surgical infection
  • Ventilator-associated pneumonia with shock
  • Infected prosthetic vascular graft
  • Pacemaker or ICD infection with bacteremia
  • CSF-shunt infection with neurologic deterioration
  • Hospital transmission of Candida auris or a carbapenemase-producing organism
Many healthcare-associated infections cannot be cured with antibiotics alone because bacteria or fungi persist within biofilm on foreign material.

Core Definitions

Term Practical Meaning
Healthcare-associated infection Infection related to healthcare exposure, procedure, device, surgery, or transmission within a care environment
Colonization Organism present without tissue invasion or attributable clinical illness
Contamination Organism introduced into a specimen during collection or processing
Biofilm Microbial community attached to foreign material and protected by an extracellular matrix
Source control Removal, drainage, debridement, repair, or replacement of the infected anatomic source
Surveillance definition Standardized criteria used for quality measurement, reporting, and interfacility comparison
Clinical diagnosis Bedside determination used to decide whether and how to treat an individual patient

Device Associated Does Not Always Mean Device Caused

A patient may have a central line and bloodstream infection from pneumonia, urinary infection, abdominal infection, or another source. The presence of a device alone does not establish causality.

Colonization Is Common

Colonization frequently occurs with:

  • Urinary catheters
  • Endotracheal tubes
  • Tracheostomies
  • Chronic wounds
  • Long-standing drains
  • Central-line hubs
  • Dialysis catheters
  • Skin and mucosal surfaces
Treating colonization exposes the patient to toxicity, C. difficile infection, drug interactions, and selection of increasingly resistant organisms.

Surveillance Definitions vs Clinical Diagnosis

Why the Distinction Matters

National Healthcare Safety Network definitions are designed to:

  • Standardize reporting
  • Measure hospital performance
  • Compare infection rates
  • Track quality-improvement programs

They are not designed to replace individualized clinical reasoning.

CLABSI

Central-line-associated bloodstream infection is primarily a surveillance designation based on bloodstream infection occurring in a patient who meets specified central-line timing criteria without another recognized source.

CRBSI

Catheter-related bloodstream infection is a clinical diagnosis supported by microbiologic evidence that the intravascular catheter is the source.

CLABSI = Surveillance Concept
CRBSI = Clinical and Microbiologic Diagnosis

Why the Diagnoses May Differ

A bloodstream infection may:

  • Meet CLABSI surveillance criteria without definitive proof that the catheter caused it
  • Be clinically catheter related but not satisfy a particular surveillance definition
  • Be secondary to another source despite the presence of a central line

Use Current Definitions

NHSN criteria are updated periodically. Infection-prevention teams should use the current reporting manual rather than relying on remembered historical definitions.

Major Risk Factors

Patient Factors

  • Critical illness
  • Advanced age
  • Neutropenia
  • Transplantation
  • Malignancy
  • Diabetes
  • Renal failure
  • Severe malnutrition
  • Extensive burns or wounds
  • Prolonged hospitalization

Device Factors

  • Central venous catheter
  • Urinary catheter
  • Mechanical ventilation
  • External ventricular drain
  • CSF shunt
  • Dialysis catheter
  • Pacemaker or defibrillator
  • Vascular graft
  • Orthopedic hardware
  • Long-standing surgical drain

Healthcare-Exposure Factors

  • Recent broad-spectrum antibiotics
  • Recent surgery
  • Repeated invasive procedures
  • Prolonged ICU stay
  • Residence in a long-term care facility
  • Previous colonization with an MDRO
  • Inadequate hand hygiene
  • Improper device maintenance
  • Environmental contamination
  • Breakdown in sterilization or disinfection

The Most Modifiable Risks

Unnecessary Device Days + Unnecessary Antibiotic Days

  • MRSA
  • VRE
  • ESBL-producing Enterobacterales

  • Multidrug-resistant Pseudomonas
  • Carbapenem-resistant Acinetobacter
  • Azole-resistant Candida

Use the Patient’s Previous Cultures

The patient’s own previous resistant isolates are often more useful for empiric selection than a generic hospital antibiogram.

Initial Approach to a Suspected HAI

Step 1: Assess Severity

  • Hemodynamics
  • Lactate
  • Oxygenation
  • Urine output
  • Mental status
  • New organ dysfunction

Step 2: Review Every Device

  • Type of device
  • Insertion date
  • Indication
  • Recent manipulation
  • Local erythema, tenderness, drainage, or malfunction
  • Whether the device is still needed

Step 3: Search for Non-Device Sources

  • Lungs
  • Urinary tract
  • Abdomen and biliary tract
  • Skin and wounds
  • Joints
  • Endocardium
  • Sinuses
  • Medications and transfusion reactions
  • Thrombosis and other noninfectious causes

Step 4: Obtain Targeted Cultures

Obtain cultures that can answer a specific question and that are collected from an appropriate site.

Step 5: Begin Empiric Therapy When Indicated

Start promptly with:

  • Septic shock
  • Rapidly progressive infection
  • Neutropenia
  • Suspected meningitis or ventriculitis
  • Severe HAP or VAP
  • High-risk prosthetic-device infection

Step 6: Remove or Control the Source

  • Remove an unnecessary catheter.
  • Drain an abscess.
  • Open an infected incision.
  • Extract infected cardiac hardware.
  • Remove or externalize an infected CSF shunt.
  • Debride necrotic tissue.

Culture Strategy

Blood Cultures

For suspected bloodstream infection:

  • Obtain at least two appropriately collected culture sets.
  • Use separate venipuncture sites when possible.
  • Obtain cultures before antibiotics when this does not delay urgent therapy.
  • Clearly label cultures drawn from a catheter and from a peripheral vein.

Line Cultures

When evaluating a central catheter, obtain:

  • A peripheral blood-culture set
  • A blood-culture set from each relevant catheter lumen

Urine Cultures

  • Collect only when compatible symptoms or systemic findings exist.
  • Collect from the sampling port or from a newly replaced catheter.
  • Never collect from the drainage bag.

Respiratory Cultures

  • Evaluate specimen quality.
  • Use endotracheal aspirate or BAL in ventilated patients according to clinical circumstances.
  • Interpret chronic tracheostomy cultures cautiously because colonization is common.

Wound Cultures

  • Prefer deep tissue, aspirate, or operative material.
  • Avoid superficial swabs of chronic wounds when deeper infection is suspected.
  • Do not culture a closed, uninfected surgical incision.

Chronic Drain Cultures

Cultures obtained from long-standing drains often represent colonization and should not automatically trigger broader therapy.

Diagnostic Stewardship

Core Principle

Test Only When the Result Can Change Management

Common Low-Value Tests

  • Urine cultures for isolated cloudy urine
  • Urine cultures for delirium without urinary or systemic features
  • Repeated blood cultures for uncomplicated gram-negative bacteremia after improvement
  • Routine culture of chronic wounds without infection
  • Routine endotracheal cultures without clinical pneumonia
  • Repeat C. difficile testing during the same diarrheal episode
  • C. difficile tests of cure
  • Routine central-line tip culture after noninfectious removal

Consequences of Over-Testing

  • Detection of colonization
  • False-positive results
  • Unnecessary antibiotics
  • Unnecessary line removal
  • Drug toxicity
  • Longer hospitalization
  • Incorrect HAI labeling

Reflex Testing

Useful stewardship strategies may include:

  • Urinalysis with reflex culture only when pyuria criteria are met
  • Restriction of C. difficile testing after recent laxatives
  • Rejection of formed stool
  • Blood-culture contamination tracking
  • Respiratory specimen-quality review
A positive result is most meaningful when the patient had an appropriate pretest probability before the specimen was collected.

General Principles of Device Infection

Biofilm

Organisms attach to device surfaces and form biofilm, which:

  • Protects organisms from host immunity
  • Reduces antimicrobial killing
  • Allows persistent or relapsing infection
  • Promotes resistance

Signs Suggesting Device Infection

  • Local erythema or drainage
  • Device malfunction
  • Persistent bacteremia
  • Relapse with the same organism
  • Infection without another source
  • Vegetation or peri-device collection

Factors Favoring Removal

  • Unstable patient
  • S. aureus
  • Pseudomonas
  • Candida
  • Mycobacteria
  • Persistent bloodstream infection
  • Tunnel or pocket infection
  • Suppurative thrombophlebitis
  • Endocarditis
  • Metastatic infection
  • Mechanical device failure

Factors That May Permit Salvage

  • Stable patient
  • Long-term device with limited alternative access
  • Low-virulence susceptible organism
  • No tunnel, pocket, thrombotic, endocardial, or metastatic infection
  • Rapid clearance of cultures
  • Ability to use systemic and local lock therapy
“Difficult access” alone is not a sufficient reason to retain an infected device when the organism or complications make cure unlikely.

CLABSI and Catheter-Related Bloodstream Infection

Central-Line Types

  • Nontunneled central venous catheter
  • Tunneled catheter
  • Implanted port
  • Peripherally inserted central catheter
  • Hemodialysis catheter
  • Pulmonary-artery catheter

Common Pathogens

  • Coagulase-negative staphylococci
  • Staphylococcus aureus
  • Enterococcus
  • Enterobacterales
  • Pseudomonas aeruginosa
  • Candida species
  • Less commonly Bacillus, Corynebacterium, Cutibacterium, or mycobacteria

Clinical Features

  • Fever or rigors during catheter infusion
  • Unexplained bacteremia
  • Exit-site erythema or purulence
  • Tunnel tenderness
  • Port-pocket inflammation
  • Septic shock without another source
  • Persistent or relapsing bacteremia

Normal Insertion Site Does Not Exclude Infection

Many catheter-related bloodstream infections occur without visible erythema, drainage, or tenderness.

Evaluating a Suspected Line Infection

Before Antibiotics

  1. Obtain at least one peripheral blood-culture set.
  2. Obtain cultures from relevant catheter lumens.
  3. Label collection sites and times accurately.
  4. Examine the exit site, tunnel, and pocket.
  5. Search for another bloodstream source.

Catheter-Tip Culture

Culture the catheter tip when:

  • The catheter is removed because infection is suspected
  • The result may clarify the source

Do not routinely culture catheter tips removed for noninfectious reasons.

One Positive Bottle With Skin Flora

A single culture with coagulase-negative staphylococcus or another skin organism may represent contamination. Before committing to prolonged treatment:

  • Repeat cultures
  • Review the number of positive bottles
  • Assess timing and collection sites
  • Consider the patient’s devices and immune status

S. aureus Is Different

Staphylococcus aureus in blood should not be dismissed as a contaminant. It requires repeat blood cultures, source evaluation, and assessment for endocarditis and metastatic infection.

Differential Time to Positivity

Principle

When equal-volume blood samples are collected simultaneously from a catheter and a peripheral vein, earlier positivity from the catheter may support catheter-related infection.

Catheter Culture Positive ≥2 Hours Before Peripheral Culture → Supports CRBSI

Limitations

  • Blood volumes must be similar.
  • Collection times must be accurately documented.
  • Antibiotics may reduce reliability.
  • Not every laboratory reports the needed timing.
  • The method is less useful when only catheter cultures are obtained.

Quantitative Cultures

Quantitative blood-culture methods may support catheter-source diagnosis but are less commonly used in routine practice.

When to Remove the Catheter

Remove a Short-Term Catheter With:

  • S. aureus bacteremia
  • Pseudomonas infection
  • Candidemia
  • Mycobacterial infection
  • Severe sepsis or shock
  • Persistent bacteremia despite active therapy
  • Suppurative thrombophlebitis
  • Endocarditis
  • Metastatic infection
  • Exit-site purulence or tunnel infection

Long-Term Catheter Removal Is Strongly Favored With:

  • Tunnel infection
  • Port-pocket infection
  • Catheter malfunction associated with infection
  • S. aureus, Pseudomonas, Candida, or mycobacteria
  • Persistent positive blood cultures after approximately 72 hours of active therapy
  • Endocarditis or infected thrombus

Send the Tip

When an infected catheter is removed, send the catheter tip for culture if this will assist diagnosis.

New Line Placement

When possible, delay placement of a new long-term central catheter until:

  • Blood cultures are negative
  • The patient is clinically improving
  • The original source has been controlled
Do not routinely exchange an infected central catheter over a guidewire. This can contaminate the new catheter and preserve the infected tract.

Catheter Salvage

Potential Candidates

  • Long-term catheter
  • Stable patient
  • Coagulase-negative staphylococcus or another relatively low-virulence susceptible organism
  • No tunnel or pocket infection
  • No endocarditis
  • No suppurative thrombosis
  • No metastatic infection
  • Limited alternative vascular access

Requirements

  • Active systemic antimicrobial therapy
  • Antibiotic-lock therapy when appropriate
  • Repeat blood cultures
  • Close clinical monitoring
  • Immediate removal if cultures fail to clear

Salvage Is Usually Inappropriate With:

  • S. aureus
  • Pseudomonas
  • Candida
  • Mycobacteria
  • Severe sepsis
  • Persistent bacteremia
  • Tunnel or pocket infection

Antibiotic-Lock Therapy

What It Is

A high-concentration antimicrobial solution is instilled into the catheter lumen and allowed to dwell to treat intraluminal biofilm.

Use

  • Selected long-term catheter infections
  • Catheter salvage attempt
  • Used with systemic therapy for true bloodstream infection

Not Appropriate As Sole Therapy With:

  • Sepsis
  • Endocarditis
  • Metastatic infection
  • Extraluminal or tunnel infection
  • High-risk organisms requiring removal

Lock Selection

Selection depends on:

  • Organism and susceptibility
  • Catheter material
  • Compatibility with heparin or citrate
  • Required dwell time
  • Pharmacy preparation
Antibiotic-lock therapy should be coordinated with infectious disease, pharmacy, nephrology, oncology, or the relevant device team.

Organism-Specific Catheter Infection

Coagulase-Negative Staphylococci

  • Confirm true infection rather than contamination.
  • Remove unnecessary catheters.
  • Uncomplicated infection after removal may be treated for approximately 5–7 days.
  • If a long-term catheter is retained, systemic treatment plus lock therapy commonly continues approximately 10–14 days.

Staphylococcus aureus

  • Remove the catheter.
  • Repeat blood cultures until negative.
  • Evaluate for endocarditis.
  • Search for vertebral, joint, epidural, or other metastatic infection.
  • Treat most patients for 4–6 weeks when infection is complicated.

Shorter S. aureus Treatment

A shorter course of at least approximately 14 days may be considered only when strict uncomplicated criteria are satisfied, including:

  • Catheter removed
  • Rapid fever and culture clearance
  • No endocarditis
  • No prosthetic intravascular material
  • No metastatic infection
  • No major immunosuppression
  • Reliable follow-up

Gram-Negative Bacilli

  • Remove short-term infected catheters.
  • Remove long-term catheters with Pseudomonas, severe illness, persistent bacteremia, or complications.
  • Uncomplicated infection is often treated for approximately 7–14 days.
  • Use susceptibility-guided therapy and source control.

Enterococcus

  • Remove an unnecessary catheter.
  • Use ampicillin for susceptible isolates when appropriate.
  • Use vancomycin when beta-lactam resistance or intolerance requires it.
  • Use linezolid or daptomycin for selected VRE infection.
  • Uncomplicated disease commonly requires approximately 7–14 days.

Candida

Remove the Catheter + Start Systemic Antifungal Therapy
  • Use an echinocandin initially in many hospitalized patients.
  • Repeat blood cultures until clearance.
  • Evaluate for metastatic complications.
  • Treat uncomplicated candidemia for at least 14 days after the first negative blood culture and resolution of attributable symptoms.

Bacillus, Corynebacterium, and Related Organisms

These organisms may represent contamination but are more likely clinically significant when:

  • Multiple cultures are positive
  • An intravascular device is present
  • The patient is immunocompromised
  • Bacteremia persists

Device removal is often required for persistent true infection.

Complicated Catheter-Related Bloodstream Infection

Complications

  • Endocarditis
  • Suppurative thrombophlebitis
  • Septic pulmonary emboli
  • Vertebral osteomyelitis
  • Septic arthritis
  • Epidural abscess
  • Persistent bacteremia

Suppurative Thrombophlebitis

Suspect with:

  • Persistent bacteremia despite active therapy
  • Swelling or pain along the catheterized vein
  • Ultrasound or CT evidence of thrombus
  • S. aureus, Candida, or gram-negative bacteremia

Management

  • Remove the catheter.
  • Use prolonged pathogen-directed therapy.
  • Evaluate for endocarditis and embolic complications.
  • Individualize anticoagulation.

Echocardiography

TEE is particularly important with:

  • S. aureus bacteremia
  • Persistent bacteremia
  • Prosthetic valve
  • Cardiac implantable device
  • Clinical evidence of emboli
  • Poor-quality or nondiagnostic transthoracic study

Peripheral IV Catheter Infection

Clinical Features

  • Local erythema
  • Tenderness
  • Palpable venous cord
  • Purulent drainage
  • Fever or bacteremia

Management

  • Remove the catheter immediately.
  • Culture purulent drainage when present.
  • Obtain blood cultures with systemic illness.
  • Evaluate for suppurative thrombophlebitis if bacteremia persists.

S. aureus Bacteremia

Hospital-onset S. aureus bacteremia may arise from a peripheral IV even when the insertion site appears only mildly inflamed.

Prevention

  • Aseptic insertion
  • Appropriate skin antisepsis
  • Daily assessment
  • Prompt removal when no longer needed
  • Replacement based on clinical indication rather than arbitrary routine replacement when facility policy permits

Hemodialysis Catheter-Related Infection

Clinical Features

  • Fever or rigors during dialysis
  • Exit-site drainage
  • Tunnel tenderness
  • Unexplained bacteremia
  • Catheter malfunction

Initial Management

  • Obtain blood cultures from the catheter and peripherally when feasible.
  • Begin empiric gram-positive and gram-negative therapy based on severity and local resistance.
  • Assess whether the catheter should be removed, exchanged, or salvaged.

Remove the Catheter With:

  • S. aureus
  • Pseudomonas
  • Candida
  • Tunnel infection
  • Septic shock
  • Persistent bacteremia
  • Endocarditis or metastatic infection

Guidewire Exchange

In selected stable patients with rapidly resolving symptoms, no tunnel infection, and susceptible lower-risk organisms, exchange over a guidewire may be considered using dialysis and infectious-disease protocols.

Catheter Salvage

Systemic antibiotics plus antibiotic-lock therapy may be considered for selected lower-risk organisms when vascular access options are limited.

Prevention

An arteriovenous fistula or graft generally has lower infection risk than a long-term hemodialysis catheter.

Catheter-Associated Urinary Tract Infection

Why It Is Commonly Overdiagnosed

  • Bacteriuria develops rapidly after catheter placement.
  • Pyuria is common.
  • Urine may become cloudy or odorous.
  • Hospitalized patients frequently have fever or delirium from another cause.

Potential Symptoms and Signs

  • New fever or rigors without another source
  • Suprapubic pain
  • Flank pain or costovertebral-angle tenderness
  • Acute hematuria
  • Pelvic discomfort
  • Dysuria, urgency, or frequency after catheter removal
  • Sepsis without another source

Nonspecific Findings

The following do not independently diagnose CAUTI:

  • Pyuria
  • Bacteriuria
  • Cloudy urine
  • Odorous urine
  • Positive nitrite
  • Delirium without urinary or systemic findings
Most patients with long-term urinary catheters eventually develop bacteriuria. Treat symptoms and invasive infection—not the culture alone.

Diagnosing CAUTI

Required Clinical Reasoning

  1. Identify compatible urinary or systemic features.
  2. Exclude another source of fever or sepsis.
  3. Assess catheter necessity and duration.
  4. Obtain urine correctly.
  5. Interpret bacterial quantity and organism with the clinical syndrome.

Pyuria

Pyuria supports urinary inflammation but:

  • Does not distinguish infection from catheter-associated bacteriuria
  • Should not be used alone to start antibiotics
  • Its absence makes bacterial UTI less likely in many circumstances

Candida in Urine

Candiduria commonly represents colonization, particularly with:

  • Urinary catheter
  • Diabetes
  • Antibiotic exposure
  • Critical illness

Remove or replace the catheter and treat only when an accepted indication exists, such as:

  • Symptomatic Candida UTI
  • Neutropenia in selected circumstances
  • Upcoming urologic manipulation
  • Invasive candidiasis

Correct Urine-Culture Collection

If the Catheter Is Still Needed

When a catheter has been present for approximately two weeks or longer and CAUTI is suspected:

  1. Replace the catheter before starting antibiotics when feasible.
  2. Obtain the culture from the newly placed catheter.

If the Catheter Is Not Needed

  • Remove it.
  • Obtain a clean-catch specimen if clinically necessary.

Sampling Technique

  • Disinfect the catheter sampling port.
  • Aspirate urine using sterile technique.
  • Do not disconnect the closed drainage system unnecessarily.
  • Do not collect urine from the collection bag.

Why Replacement Helps

  • Removes colonized biofilm
  • Improves clinical response
  • Produces a more representative culture
  • Reduces early recurrence

CAUTI Treatment

Empiric Therapy

Base empiric therapy on:

  • Severity
  • Previous urine cultures
  • Previous resistant organisms
  • Recent antibiotics
  • Local susceptibility
  • Renal function
  • Presence of obstruction or upper-tract infection

Potential Agents

  • Ceftriaxone for stable lower-resistance-risk infection
  • Cefepime or piperacillin-tazobactam when Pseudomonas risk exists
  • Carbapenem when severe ESBL infection is likely
  • Culture-directed oral therapy after stabilization

Duration

Clinical Response Common Duration Framework
Prompt clinical improvement Approximately 7 days
Delayed response Approximately 10–14 days
Selected nonseverely ill patient A shorter fluoroquinolone regimen may be considered when susceptible and safe

Complicated Disease

Longer or individualized treatment may be required with:

  • Prostatitis
  • Renal or perinephric abscess
  • Obstruction
  • Infected stone
  • Persistent bacteremia
  • Emphysematous infection

Source Control

  • Remove the catheter.
  • Relieve obstruction.
  • Exchange an infected ureteral stent or nephrostomy when indicated.
  • Drain abscesses.

Asymptomatic Bacteriuria

Do Not Treat In:

  • Most catheterized patients
  • Older adults with bacteriuria but no urinary or systemic symptoms
  • Residents of long-term care facilities
  • Patients with spinal-cord injury without compatible symptoms
  • Most patients with diabetes

Accepted Treatment Indications

  • Pregnancy
  • Before selected invasive urologic procedures involving mucosal trauma

Delirium and Falls

Bacteriuria in an older adult with delirium or a fall does not establish UTI when urinary symptoms, fever, hemodynamic instability, or another compatible feature is absent.

Why Avoid Treatment?

  • No clinical benefit in most populations
  • Resistance selection
  • C. difficile infection
  • Adverse drug reactions
  • Rapid recurrence of bacteriuria

CAUTI Prevention

Use a Catheter Only for an Appropriate Indication

  • Acute urinary retention or obstruction
  • Accurate urine measurement in selected critically ill patients
  • Selected perioperative indications
  • Assistance with healing of severe sacral or perineal wounds in incontinent patients
  • Prolonged immobilization for selected injuries
  • Comfort-focused end-of-life care

Inappropriate Indications

  • Convenience
  • Routine incontinence management
  • Routine collection of urine specimens
  • Continued use after the acute indication has ended

Alternatives

  • External urinary collection device
  • Intermittent catheterization
  • Bedside commode
  • Scheduled toileting
  • Bladder ultrasound

Maintenance Bundle

  • Aseptic insertion
  • Closed drainage system
  • Unobstructed urine flow
  • Bag positioned below bladder level
  • Avoid dependent loops
  • Daily necessity assessment
  • Prompt removal
The most effective CAUTI-prevention intervention is avoiding unnecessary catheter placement and removing catheters early.

Hospital-Acquired and Ventilator-Associated Pneumonia

Hospital-Acquired Pneumonia

HAP develops at least approximately 48 hours after hospital admission and was not incubating at admission.

Ventilator-Associated Pneumonia

VAP develops after at least approximately 48 hours of endotracheal intubation.

Common Pathogens

  • S. aureus, including MRSA
  • Pseudomonas aeruginosa
  • Enterobacterales
  • Acinetobacter
  • Other hospital gram-negative bacilli

Risk Factors for Resistance

  • Recent IV antibiotics
  • Prolonged hospitalization
  • Septic shock
  • ARDS before VAP
  • Renal replacement therapy
  • Known colonization with resistant organisms
  • High unit-level resistance prevalence

Diagnosis of HAP and VAP

Clinical Syndrome

Diagnosis generally requires a new or progressive pulmonary infiltrate plus compatible features such as:

  • Fever or hypothermia
  • Leukocytosis or leukopenia
  • Purulent respiratory secretions
  • Worsening oxygenation
  • Increased ventilator support

Important Mimics

  • Pulmonary edema
  • Atelectasis
  • Pulmonary embolism
  • Diffuse alveolar hemorrhage
  • ARDS progression
  • Aspiration pneumonitis
  • Drug-induced lung disease
  • Organizing pneumonia

Chest Radiography

Portable radiographs are imperfect. CT may help when:

  • The diagnosis remains unclear
  • A complication is suspected
  • Empyema, abscess, or obstruction is possible
  • The patient fails to improve

Fever Alone Is Not Pneumonia

A positive respiratory culture in a ventilated patient does not diagnose VAP without a compatible clinical and radiographic syndrome.

Respiratory Cultures

Before Antibiotics

When feasible, obtain respiratory cultures before starting or changing antibiotics.

Potential Specimens

  • Expectorated sputum
  • Endotracheal aspirate
  • Bronchoalveolar lavage
  • Protected specimen brush in selected settings

Noninvasive Sampling

Endotracheal aspirate with semiquantitative culture is adequate for many patients with suspected VAP.

Bronchoscopy

Consider bronchoscopy when:

  • The diagnosis is uncertain
  • The patient is immunocompromised
  • Airway obstruction is possible
  • Alternative diagnoses require evaluation
  • Noninvasive sampling is nondiagnostic

Interpretation

  • Evaluate organism burden and specimen quality.
  • Review prior airway colonization.
  • Use Gram stain and culture concordance.
  • De-escalate when cultures do not support the empiric regimen.

Blood Cultures

Obtain blood cultures in severe HAP or VAP because they may identify:

  • Bacteremic pneumonia
  • An alternative source
  • A resistant organism

Empiric Antibiotics for HAP and VAP

Core Coverage

Empiric therapy generally includes activity against:

  • S. aureus
  • Pseudomonas
  • Other hospital gram-negative bacilli

Potential Antipseudomonal Beta-Lactams

  • Cefepime
  • Piperacillin-tazobactam
  • Meropenem
  • Imipenem
  • Ceftazidime in selected settings

MRSA Coverage

  • Vancomycin
  • Linezolid

Add empiric MRSA therapy when:

  • Previous IV antibiotics increase resistance risk
  • MRSA prevalence is high or unknown
  • Previous MRSA colonization or infection exists
  • The patient has high mortality risk

Double Antipseudomonal Coverage

Two agents from different classes may be appropriate when:

  • Septic shock or high mortality risk is present
  • Prior IV antibiotics increase resistance risk
  • Local susceptibility is poor or unknown
  • The patient has structural lung disease with resistant Pseudomonas risk

Do Not Continue Double Coverage After Susceptibilities Return

Once the organism and susceptibility are known, use one active definitive antipseudomonal agent in most patients.

Renal and Neurologic Toxicity

  • Adjust beta-lactams for renal function.
  • Monitor cefepime-associated neurotoxicity.
  • Use AUC-guided vancomycin monitoring when appropriate.
  • Monitor linezolid-related cytopenias and interactions.

Duration and De-Escalation of HAP/VAP Therapy

Typical Duration

Approximately 7 Days

Seven days is appropriate for most improving HAP or VAP, including many infections caused by Pseudomonas or MRSA.

Individualize With:

  • Empyema
  • Lung abscess
  • Necrotizing pneumonia
  • Endocarditis
  • Persistent bacteremia
  • Severe immunocompromise
  • Slow or inadequate clinical response

De-Escalation

  • Stop MRSA coverage when unsupported.
  • Stop the second antipseudomonal agent.
  • Narrow to the most targeted active agent.
  • Stop antibiotics when an alternative noninfectious diagnosis becomes more likely.

Procalcitonin

Procalcitonin should not replace clinical judgment when deciding whether to begin therapy. Serial values may support discontinuation in selected patients when interpreted with the clinical course.

Aspiration Pneumonitis vs Aspiration Pneumonia

Aspiration Pneumonitis

Chemical injury after aspiration of gastric contents may cause:

  • Sudden hypoxemia
  • Fever
  • Leukocytosis
  • Dependent infiltrates
  • Rapid improvement over 24–48 hours

Antibiotics are not routinely required when the syndrome is clearly chemical pneumonitis and the patient improves promptly.

Aspiration Pneumonia

Bacterial aspiration pneumonia is more likely with:

  • Persistent or progressive symptoms
  • Purulent sputum
  • Delayed presentation
  • Poor dentition
  • Chronic aspiration
  • Lung abscess or empyema

Anaerobic Coverage

Routine additional anaerobic coverage is not required for every aspiration pneumonia. It is most appropriate with lung abscess, empyema, necrotizing infection, or major periodontal disease.

Prevention of HAP, VAP, and Ventilator-Associated Events

Avoid Intubation When Safe

  • High-flow nasal oxygen
  • Noninvasive ventilation in appropriate syndromes
  • Early treatment of reversible respiratory failure

Minimize Ventilator Duration

  • Daily sedation assessment
  • Spontaneous awakening trials
  • Spontaneous breathing trials
  • Early mobility
  • Prompt extubation

Positioning

  • Elevate the head of the bed approximately 30–45 degrees unless contraindicated.

Oral Care

  • Provide regular oral hygiene.
  • Include toothbrushing when safe.
  • Routine chlorhexidine oral care solely for VAP prevention is not recommended for all ventilated adults.

Subglottic Secretion Drainage

Use an endotracheal tube with subglottic secretion drainage when prolonged ventilation is expected and implementation is feasible.

Ventilator Circuit

  • Do not change circuits routinely unless visibly soiled or malfunctioning.
  • Drain condensate safely without directing it toward the patient.

Nonventilator Hospital Pneumonia

Prevention includes:

  • Oral hygiene
  • Mobility
  • Swallow assessment
  • Aspiration precautions
  • Reducing unnecessary acid suppression and sedation

Surgical-Site Infection

Clinical Features

  • Increasing incision pain
  • Erythema
  • Purulent drainage
  • Wound separation
  • Fever
  • Failure to recover as expected
  • Deep tenderness or fluctuance
  • Organ dysfunction from deeper infection

Common Organisms

  • S. aureus
  • Coagulase-negative staphylococci with prosthetic material
  • Streptococci
  • Enterobacterales and anaerobes after gastrointestinal surgery
  • Enterococcus and Candida in selected postoperative abdominal infection
  • Pseudomonas or other resistant gram-negative organisms after healthcare exposure

Postoperative Fever Is Not Automatically an SSI

Alternative causes include:

  • Expected postoperative inflammation
  • Pneumonia
  • UTI
  • Line infection
  • Drug fever
  • Venous thromboembolism
  • Hematoma
  • Transfusion reaction

SSI Classification

Superficial Incisional

  • Skin and subcutaneous tissue
  • Purulent drainage or localized infection

Deep Incisional

  • Fascia or muscle
  • Deep wound dehiscence
  • Abscess or deep purulence

Organ-Space

  • Infection involving an organ or space manipulated during the operation
  • Examples include intra-abdominal abscess, mediastinitis, prosthetic-joint infection, or empyema

Surveillance Windows

Formal SSI surveillance periods vary according to procedure and prosthetic implantation. Use current NHSN criteria for reporting.

Treatment of Surgical-Site Infection

Superficial Infection

Open the Incision + Drain Purulence + Provide Wound Care

Systemic antibiotics may be unnecessary after adequate drainage when:

  • Infection is localized
  • No systemic inflammatory response exists
  • No deep or organ-space infection is present

Add Systemic Antibiotics With:

  • Fever or systemic illness
  • Rapidly spreading cellulitis
  • Deep infection
  • Immunocompromise
  • Prosthetic material
  • Organ-space infection

Empiric Coverage

Select according to the surgery:

  • Clean surgery: staphylococci and streptococci
  • GI or pelvic surgery: gram-negative organisms and anaerobes
  • Healthcare-associated postoperative infection: consider resistant gram-negative organisms, Enterococcus, MRSA, and Candida according to risk

Necrotizing Infection

Pain out of proportion, bullae, crepitus, shock, rapidly spreading erythema, or tissue necrosis requires immediate surgical exploration. Do not wait for imaging when the clinical diagnosis is strongly suspected.

Organ-Space and Postoperative Deep Infection

Clues

  • Persistent tachycardia
  • Prolonged ileus
  • New organ dysfunction
  • Increasing deep pain
  • Unexpected drain output
  • Persistent bacteremia
  • Failure to improve after surgery

Evaluation

  • Contrast-enhanced CT
  • Ultrasound in selected spaces
  • Deep aspiration or operative culture
  • Assessment for anastomotic leak
  • Assessment of prosthetic material

Management

  • Percutaneous drainage
  • Operative washout
  • Debridement
  • Repair of a leak
  • Removal or exchange of infected hardware
  • Short targeted antimicrobial course after source control when appropriate

Prevention of Surgical-Site Infection

Preoperative Antibiotics

  • Select an agent appropriate for the procedure.
  • Administer within approximately 60 minutes before incision.
  • Begin vancomycin or a fluoroquinolone earlier because infusion requires more time.
  • Use weight-based dosing.
  • Redose during prolonged procedures or major blood loss.

Stop After Incision Closure

For clean and clean-contaminated surgery, prophylactic antibiotics should be stopped after incision closure. The presence of a drain is not an indication to continue prophylaxis.

Skin Preparation

  • Use an alcohol-containing antiseptic solution unless contraindicated.
  • Allow the preparation to dry.

Hair Removal

  • Do not shave the operative site with a razor.
  • If hair removal is required, use clippers immediately before surgery.

Additional Prevention

  • Maintain perioperative normothermia.
  • Control perioperative hyperglycemia.
  • Optimize oxygenation.
  • Encourage smoking cessation.
  • Use appropriate MRSA or S. aureus decolonization for selected procedures.
  • Use mechanical bowel preparation plus oral antibiotics for selected elective colorectal surgery.

External Ventricular Drains and CSF Shunt Infections

Risk Factors

  • Recent neurosurgery
  • Prolonged external drain duration
  • CSF leak
  • Repeated device access
  • Device obstruction
  • Prior infection

Clinical Features

  • Fever
  • Headache
  • Altered mental status
  • Meningismus
  • Shunt malfunction
  • Erythema or tenderness along shunt tubing
  • Abdominal symptoms with a peritoneal shunt

Diagnosis

  • Obtain CSF from a sampling port using sterile technique.
  • Do not culture fluid from the drainage bag.
  • Send cell count, glucose, protein, Gram stain, and culture.
  • Hold cultures adequately for slow-growing Cutibacterium.
Normal or mildly abnormal CSF cell count, protein, and glucose do not exclude device-associated ventriculitis.

Empiric Therapy

Vancomycin + Cefepime, Ceftazidime, or Meropenem

Source Control

  • Remove an infected external drain.
  • Remove and externalize an infected shunt.
  • Use temporary external drainage when needed.
  • Repeat CSF cultures until sterile.

Intraventricular Therapy

Consider for selected resistant infections or poor response to systemic therapy with neurosurgery, infectious disease, and pharmacy guidance.

Cardiac Implantable Electronic Device Infection

Devices

  • Permanent pacemaker
  • Implantable cardioverter-defibrillator
  • Cardiac-resynchronization device
  • Leadless or epicardial systems in selected patients

Clinical Syndromes

  • Pocket infection
  • Generator erosion
  • Lead infection
  • Device-related endocarditis
  • Unexplained bacteremia in a patient with a device

Signs of Pocket Infection

  • Erythema
  • Warmth
  • Fluctuance
  • Drainage
  • Skin erosion
  • Exposed generator or lead
An exposed generator or lead is infected even when purulence or systemic symptoms are absent.

Evaluation

  • Multiple blood-culture sets
  • TEE
  • Focused examination of the pocket
  • FDG-PET/CT or other advanced imaging in selected uncertain cases
  • Evaluation for metastatic infection

Management

Complete Device and Lead Extraction + Pathogen-Directed Antibiotics

Antibiotics without extraction are associated with high relapse when the device is truly infected.

Treatment Duration Framework

  • Localized pocket infection after complete extraction: often approximately 10–14 days
  • Bloodstream or lead infection without valve involvement: commonly several weeks
  • Valve endocarditis or metastatic infection: generally 4–6 weeks or longer

Reimplantation

  • Confirm that the device is still needed.
  • Wait for bloodstream clearance.
  • Use a new site or alternative system.
  • Individualize timing by organism and extent of infection.

Vascular-Graft and Endovascular-Prosthesis Infection

Clinical Features

  • Persistent bacteremia
  • Localized pain
  • Wound drainage
  • Pseudoaneurysm
  • Perigraft gas or fluid
  • Embolic events
  • Aortoenteric fistula or GI bleeding

Diagnosis

  • Blood cultures
  • CT angiography
  • FDG-PET/CT in selected cases
  • Operative tissue cultures
  • Evaluation for fistula or pseudoaneurysm

Management

  • Urgent vascular-surgery consultation
  • Broad empiric therapy when unstable
  • Removal or reconstruction when feasible
  • Debridement and drainage
  • Prolonged pathogen-directed therapy

Retained Graft

When complete removal is impossible:

  • Relapse risk remains high.
  • Prolonged or lifelong suppressive therapy may be considered.
  • Close clinical and imaging follow-up is required.
S. aureus or gram-negative bacteremia in a patient with an aortic graft should not be assumed to be transient until graft infection has been considered.

Orthopedic Hardware and Prosthetic-Joint Infection

Clinical Features

  • Chronic pain
  • Joint swelling
  • Wound drainage
  • Sinus tract
  • Early postoperative erythema or dehiscence
  • Acute hematogenous joint symptoms during bacteremia

Diagnosis

  • ESR and CRP
  • Joint aspiration before antibiotics when the patient is stable
  • Synovial cell count and differential
  • Aerobic and anaerobic cultures
  • Multiple operative tissue cultures

Do Not Swab a Sinus Tract

A sinus tract communicating with a prosthesis establishes infection, but superficial swab culture may not identify the true deep pathogen.

Surgical Strategies

  • Debridement, antibiotics, and implant retention
  • One-stage exchange
  • Two-stage exchange
  • Resection arthroplasty
  • Amputation in selected uncontrolled infection

Rifampin

Rifampin should never be used alone for staphylococcal device infection. Resistance develops rapidly. Use it only in combination when the organism is susceptible and the surgical strategy supports biofilm-active therapy.

Chronic Suppression

Suppressive antibiotics may be used when:

  • Curative surgery is not possible
  • The prosthesis must be retained
  • The organism remains susceptible
  • Long-term toxicity is acceptable

Healthcare-Associated Clostridioides difficile

Risk Factors

  • Antibiotic exposure
  • Hospitalization
  • Older age
  • Previous CDI
  • Immunocompromise
  • Gastrointestinal surgery
  • Environmental spore exposure

Diagnostic Stewardship

  • Test only clinically significant unformed stool.
  • Review laxative administration.
  • Do not test asymptomatic patients.
  • Do not order a test of cure.
  • Do not repeatedly test during the same episode.

Isolation

  • Initiate contact precautions when CDI is suspected.
  • Use gloves and gowns.
  • Use dedicated equipment.
  • Use sporicidal environmental cleaning.

Hand Hygiene

Soap and water physically remove spores and is particularly important after caring for patients with CDI, during outbreaks, and when hands are visibly soiled.

Antimicrobial Stewardship

Reducing unnecessary antibiotics is one of the most important CDI-prevention interventions.

Multidrug-Resistant Organisms

Important Organisms

  • MRSA
  • VRE
  • ESBL-producing Enterobacterales
  • Carbapenemase-producing Enterobacterales
  • Multidrug-resistant Pseudomonas
  • Carbapenem-resistant Acinetobacter
  • Candida auris

Colonization vs Infection

Patients may remain colonized for months or years. Colonization:

  • Increases future infection risk
  • Increases transmission risk
  • Does not itself require systemic antimicrobial treatment

Prevention

  • Hand hygiene
  • Contact precautions according to organism and facility policy
  • Dedicated or disinfected equipment
  • Environmental cleaning
  • Antimicrobial stewardship
  • Interfacility communication
  • Screening of contacts during selected outbreaks

Empiric Therapy

Previous colonization should influence empiric therapy only when:

  • The current syndrome is compatible
  • The prior isolate is recent and clinically relevant
  • The patient is severely ill
  • There are few safer alternatives
A history of resistant colonization should not lead to lifelong automatic use of the broadest antibiotic for every fever.

Candida auris

Why It Is Important

  • Often resistant to multiple antifungal classes
  • Persists on skin and environmental surfaces
  • Spreads in healthcare facilities
  • May be misidentified by older laboratory systems
  • Can cause invasive bloodstream and deep infection

Colonization

C. auris may colonize:

  • Axilla
  • Groin
  • Skin
  • Wounds
  • Respiratory tract
  • Urinary tract
Do not treat C. auris colonization. Treat invasive infection.

Infection Prevention

  • Promptly notify infection prevention.
  • Use contact precautions or enhanced barrier precautions according to setting.
  • Use dedicated equipment.
  • Use an EPA-registered disinfectant effective against C. auris.
  • Communicate status during transfer.
  • Coordinate screening with public health during transmission investigations.

Treatment of Invasive Infection

  • An echinocandin is commonly used initially.
  • Obtain susceptibility testing.
  • Remove infected central catheters when feasible.
  • Escalate therapy when resistance or clinical failure occurs.

Persistent Colonization

Patients may remain colonized for prolonged periods even after repeated negative surveillance specimens. Follow public-health and facility guidance rather than declaring permanent clearance based on a single negative test.

Isolation Precautions

Standard Precautions

Use for every patient:

  • Hand hygiene
  • Risk-based PPE
  • Safe injection practices
  • Respiratory hygiene
  • Sharps safety
  • Appropriate equipment and environmental cleaning

Contact Precautions

  • Gown and gloves
  • Dedicated equipment
  • Appropriate room placement

Examples

  • C. difficile
  • C. auris
  • Selected MDROs
  • Uncontained draining wounds

Droplet Precautions

  • Surgical mask
  • Eye protection according to exposure risk
  • Patient masking during transport

Examples

  • Influenza
  • Meningococcal disease
  • Pertussis

Airborne Precautions

  • Negative-pressure room
  • Fit-tested N95 or higher respiratory protection
  • Limit transport

Examples

  • Pulmonary tuberculosis
  • Measles
  • Airborne varicella or disseminated zoster

Syndromic Precautions

Begin appropriate precautions based on the suspected syndrome before the final laboratory diagnosis is available.

Hand Hygiene

Perform Hand Hygiene:

  • Before touching a patient
  • Before an aseptic procedure
  • After body-fluid exposure risk
  • After touching a patient
  • After touching the patient environment
  • After glove removal

Alcohol-Based Hand Rub

Preferred in most routine clinical situations when hands are not visibly soiled.

Soap and Water

Use particularly when:

  • Hands are visibly soiled
  • After caring for a patient with C. difficile
  • During selected enteric outbreaks
  • After restroom use

Gloves Do Not Replace Hand Hygiene

Hands may become contaminated:

  • During glove removal
  • Through microscopic glove defects
  • Before gloves are applied

Artificial Nails

Healthcare personnel caring for high-risk patients should avoid artificial fingernails when facility policy identifies a transmission risk.

Environmental Cleaning and Equipment

High-Touch Surfaces

  • Bed rails
  • Call buttons
  • Door handles
  • Bedside tables
  • Infusion pumps
  • Monitors
  • Toilets and commodes
  • Mobile devices

Shared Equipment

  • Stethoscopes
  • Blood-pressure cuffs
  • Ultrasound probes
  • Thermometers
  • Glucometers
  • Wheelchairs

Disinfection Must Match the Organism

  • Routine hospital disinfectant for many bacteria and viruses
  • Sporicidal product for C. difficile
  • C. auris-active EPA-registered product for C. auris

Cleaning vs Disinfection

  • Cleaning removes soil and organic material.
  • Disinfection inactivates microorganisms on surfaces.
  • Sterilization eliminates all viable organisms, including spores.

Reusable Medical Equipment

Equipment must be cleaned and disinfected according to its intended use and manufacturer instructions before use on another patient.

Device and Procedure Prevention Bundles

Central-Line Bundle

  • Hand hygiene
  • Maximal sterile barrier precautions
  • Chlorhexidine-alcohol skin antisepsis
  • Optimal insertion-site selection
  • Avoid femoral access when another safe site is preferable
  • Sterile dressing care
  • Hub and connector disinfection
  • Daily necessity review
  • Prompt removal

Urinary-Catheter Bundle

  • Avoid unnecessary insertion
  • Aseptic insertion
  • Closed system
  • Unobstructed flow
  • Daily indication review
  • Prompt removal
  • Use alternatives

Ventilator Bundle

  • Avoid intubation when possible
  • Minimize sedation
  • Spontaneous awakening and breathing trials
  • Head-of-bed elevation
  • Oral hygiene and toothbrushing
  • Early mobility
  • Subglottic secretion drainage in selected patients

Surgical Bundle

  • Correct prophylactic antibiotic
  • Correct timing and dose
  • Redose when needed
  • Alcohol-based skin preparation
  • Clip rather than shave hair
  • Maintain normothermia
  • Control glucose
  • Stop prophylaxis after closure

Bundle Compliance

Bundles work when every component is reliably performed. Partial compliance produces less benefit than consistent standardized practice.

Antimicrobial Stewardship

Every Antibiotic Order Should Have:

  • A documented indication
  • An appropriate dose
  • A planned duration
  • A review date
  • A de-escalation plan

Antibiotic Time-Out

After approximately 48–72 hours, reassess:

  • Does the patient have an infection?
  • What is the source?
  • What do cultures show?
  • Can treatment be narrowed?
  • Can IV therapy become oral?
  • What is the stop date?

Common Stewardship Opportunities

  • Stop vancomycin when MRSA is unsupported.
  • Stop double antipseudomonal coverage.
  • Stop antibiotics for asymptomatic bacteriuria.
  • Stop antibiotics for aspiration pneumonitis that is rapidly improving.
  • Stop prolonged surgical prophylaxis.
  • Use seven days for most improving HAP/VAP.
  • Use short courses after adequate source control.

Broadest Is Not Always Safest

Excessive treatment increases:

  • Drug toxicity
  • Acute kidney injury
  • C. difficile infection
  • Fungal infection
  • Resistance
  • Drug interactions

Outbreak Recognition and Response

Possible Outbreak Clues

  • Unusual clustering of the same organism
  • Unexpected increase in infection rate
  • Rare resistance mechanism
  • Multiple infections linked to one unit or procedure
  • Shared equipment or medication exposure
  • Identical environmental and patient isolates

Immediate Actions

  • Notify infection prevention.
  • Notify microbiology.
  • Preserve isolates.
  • Apply appropriate precautions.
  • Identify exposed patients.
  • Review common staff, equipment, medications, and procedures.
  • Coordinate with public health.

Potential Sources

  • Contaminated sinks or drains
  • Reusable equipment
  • Compounded medications
  • Injection practices
  • Endoscopes
  • Dialysis equipment
  • Water systems
  • Healthcare personnel transmission

Do Not Wait for Perfect Proof

When transmission of a high-consequence organism is plausible, initiate containment measures while the investigation proceeds.

Transitions of Care

Communicate:

  • Current infection
  • Colonization with MDRO or C. auris
  • Required isolation precautions
  • Device type and insertion date
  • Pending cultures
  • Antibiotic regimen and stop date
  • Need for device removal or follow-up

Discharge Planning

  • Remove unnecessary devices before discharge.
  • Confirm outpatient antibiotic access.
  • Arrange laboratory monitoring.
  • Provide wound and catheter-care education.
  • Schedule ID, surgical, device, or specialty follow-up.

Long-Term Care and Dialysis Transfer

Communicate resistant-organism status clearly without using colonization as a reason to deny medically appropriate transfer.

Home Infusion

  • Confirm line necessity.
  • Use the least invasive access needed.
  • Educate on aseptic technique.
  • Provide a plan for fever, line pain, or drainage.

  • Does the patient truly have an infection?
  • What is the exact source?
  • Could the culture represent contamination or colonization?
  • Which devices are present?
  • Does each device remain necessary?
  • Is there erythema, drainage, tenderness, or malfunction?
  • Were cultures obtained correctly?
  • Are repeat blood cultures required?
  • Is the device removable?

  • Does the patient need MRSA coverage?
  • Does the patient need antipseudomonal coverage?
  • Does previous resistance affect empiric therapy?
  • Can therapy be narrowed today?
  • Can IV therapy stop or become oral?
  • What is the treatment stop date?
  • Are isolation precautions correct?
  • Has infection prevention been notified when needed?

How to Present on ID Rounds

  • Hospital day and procedure timeline
  • Device type and insertion date
  • Clinical syndrome
  • Culture sites and collection times
  • Time to positivity
  • Source-control status
  • Previous resistant organisms
  • Current antibiotics and doses
  • Culture-clearance status
  • Device-removal plan
  • Duration and follow-up plan
Avoid saying only, “The patient has a line infection.” State whether the diagnosis is surveillance CLABSI or clinical CRBSI, what cultures support the catheter source, which organism is present, whether the line is removed, and whether complications have been excluded.

Common Clinical Scenarios

Case 1: Fever With a Central Line

A hospitalized patient with a PICC develops fever. Blood cultures have not yet been obtained.

Obtain paired peripheral and catheter cultures before antibiotics when the patient is stable. Do not assume the PICC is the source without evaluating other sites.

Case 2: S. aureus From a Central Line

Peripheral and catheter cultures both grow S. aureus.

Remove the catheter, repeat blood cultures, obtain echocardiographic evaluation, and search for metastatic infection.

Case 3: Coagulase-Negative Staphylococcus in One Bottle

One of four culture bottles grows coagulase-negative staphylococcus in a stable patient.

Contamination is possible. Repeat cultures and assess the device before diagnosing bloodstream infection.

Case 4: Candidemia With a Central Catheter

Blood cultures grow Candida in a patient receiving parenteral nutrition through a central line.

Start systemic antifungal therapy, remove the catheter when feasible, and repeat blood cultures until clearance.

Case 5: Long-Term Catheter With Pseudomonas

A long-term tunneled catheter and peripheral cultures grow Pseudomonas.

Catheter salvage is generally inappropriate. Remove the catheter and evaluate for complicated infection.

Case 6: Cloudy Urine in a Catheterized Patient

A patient has cloudy urine but no fever, urinary symptoms, leukocytosis, or hemodynamic instability.

Do not culture or treat solely because urine is cloudy. Reassess catheter necessity and remove it if no longer needed.

Case 7: CAUTI With a Three-Week-Old Catheter

A patient has fever, flank pain, pyuria, and bacteriuria with a catheter in place for three weeks.

Replace the catheter, obtain culture from the new catheter, and begin severity-appropriate empiric therapy.

Case 8: Positive Urine Culture and Delirium

An older patient has delirium and bacteriuria but no fever, urinary symptoms, hypotension, or leukocytosis.

Do not diagnose CAUTI from delirium and bacteriuria alone. Evaluate medication effects, dehydration, metabolic disease, pain, and other causes.

Case 9: Ventilated Patient With Positive Sputum

An intubated patient has a positive endotracheal culture but stable oxygen needs and no new infiltrate.

This likely represents airway colonization. A positive culture alone does not diagnose VAP.

Case 10: VAP With Septic Shock

A ventilated patient develops a new infiltrate, purulent secretions, worsening oxygenation, and shock after recent IV antibiotics.

Begin MRSA coverage and two antipseudomonal agents from different classes, obtain cultures, and de-escalate when susceptibilities return.

Case 11: Postoperative Wound Drainage

A patient has a small superficial incision with purulent drainage but no fever or surrounding cellulitis.

Open and drain the incision. Systemic antibiotics may be unnecessary after adequate local treatment.

Case 12: Antibiotics Continued Because a Drain Remains

A stable postoperative patient is still receiving cefazolin five days after surgery because a drain is present.

A drain is not an indication to continue surgical prophylaxis. Prophylactic antibiotics should generally stop after incision closure.

Case 13: Pacemaker Pocket Erosion

A pacemaker generator is visible through eroded skin, but the patient has no fever.

The device is infected. Obtain blood cultures and arrange complete system extraction.

Case 14: C. auris in Urine

A long-term care resident has C. auris detected in urine without urinary symptoms or invasive disease.

Do not treat colonization. Notify infection prevention and use appropriate transmission-based precautions and environmental disinfection.

Case 15: Persistent Fever After Line Removal

A catheter is removed for S. aureus bacteremia, but blood cultures remain positive after 72 hours.

Evaluate for endocarditis, suppurative thrombophlebitis, vertebral osteomyelitis, epidural abscess, septic arthritis, and other metastatic infection.

Common Pitfalls

  • Using a surveillance CLABSI label as proof that the catheter caused infection
  • Assuming every bloodstream infection in a patient with a line is CRBSI
  • Failing to obtain paired peripheral and catheter blood cultures
  • Drawing all blood cultures through the same catheter
  • Failing to label the culture source
  • Dismissing S. aureus bacteremia as contamination
  • Retaining a catheter infected with S. aureus, Pseudomonas, or Candida
  • Trying catheter salvage despite tunnel or pocket infection
  • Using antibiotic-lock therapy without systemic therapy for bloodstream infection
  • Failing to repeat cultures after S. aureus bacteremia or candidemia
  • Failing to evaluate persistent bacteremia for endocarditis or thrombophlebitis
  • Collecting urine from the drainage bag
  • Treating pyuria without symptoms
  • Treating cloudy or odorous urine
  • Treating asymptomatic bacteriuria in a catheterized patient
  • Failing to replace an old catheter during true CAUTI
  • Treating respiratory colonization as VAP
  • Diagnosing pneumonia without a compatible infiltrate and clinical syndrome
  • Continuing double antipseudomonal coverage after susceptibility results
  • Using prolonged HAP or VAP treatment without a complication
  • Treating chemical aspiration pneumonitis automatically
  • Failing to drain a surgical-site abscess
  • Continuing surgical prophylaxis after incision closure
  • Continuing prophylaxis because a drain remains
  • Using superficial wound swabs to diagnose deep infection
  • Treating chronic-drain cultures
  • Retaining infected pacemaker or defibrillator hardware
  • Treating C. auris colonization
  • Failing to communicate MDRO or C. auris status during transfer
  • Using gloves as a substitute for hand hygiene
  • Failing to remove unnecessary invasive devices

Board and ID Consult Pearls

  • CLABSI is a surveillance definition; CRBSI is a clinical microbiologic diagnosis.
  • Paired peripheral and catheter blood cultures help identify a catheter source.
  • Catheter cultures becoming positive at least two hours earlier support CRBSI.
  • A normal catheter insertion site does not exclude bloodstream infection.
  • S. aureus in blood is not a contaminant until proven otherwise.
  • Remove catheters infected with S. aureus, Pseudomonas, Candida, or mycobacteria.
  • Persistent bacteremia after catheter removal suggests a complicated infection.
  • Antibiotic-lock therapy is reserved for selected catheter-salvage situations.
  • Do not routinely culture catheter tips removed for noninfectious reasons.
  • Urinary-catheter bacteriuria and pyuria are common and do not establish CAUTI.
  • Cloudy or foul-smelling urine does not diagnose UTI.
  • Never obtain a urine culture from the drainage bag.
  • Replace a catheter present for at least approximately two weeks when true CAUTI is treated and catheterization must continue.
  • Do not treat catheter-associated asymptomatic bacteriuria except for accepted special indications.
  • HAP develops after approximately 48 hours of hospitalization.
  • VAP develops after approximately 48 hours of intubation.
  • A positive endotracheal culture alone does not diagnose VAP.
  • Seven days is sufficient for most improving HAP and VAP.
  • De-escalate MRSA and double antipseudomonal coverage promptly.
  • Aspiration pneumonitis usually does not require antibiotics.
  • Regular oral care and toothbrushing help prevent hospital-acquired pneumonia.
  • Routine chlorhexidine oral care is not required for every ventilated adult.
  • Superficial surgical-site infection is primarily treated by opening and draining the incision.
  • Organ-space SSI requires source control.
  • Stop surgical prophylaxis when the incision is closed.
  • The presence of a surgical drain does not justify continued prophylactic antibiotics.
  • Use clippers rather than razors for preoperative hair removal.
  • CSF cultures from a device sampling port are more useful than cultures from a drainage bag.
  • Normal CSF parameters do not exclude device-associated ventriculitis.
  • Infected CSF shunts generally require removal and external drainage.
  • Pacemaker or ICD pocket infection requires complete system extraction.
  • Exposed cardiac-device hardware is infected even without fever.
  • Vascular-graft infection often requires surgery and prolonged therapy.
  • Rifampin must never be used alone for staphylococcal prosthetic-device infection.
  • C. auris colonization should not be treated.
  • Use a C. auris-active environmental disinfectant.
  • Hand hygiene is required before and after patient contact and after glove removal.
  • The most effective device-infection prevention measure is removing unnecessary devices.
  • Every antibiotic course should have a documented indication, review date, and stop date.

Bottom Line

Healthcare-associated infections should be approached by distinguishing true infection from contamination and colonization, identifying the anatomic source, reviewing every invasive device, obtaining correctly collected cultures, and achieving source control. The presence of a catheter, urinary device, endotracheal tube, wound, or implant does not by itself prove that the device is infected.

Central-line bloodstream infection requires paired blood cultures and careful source assessment. Remove infected catheters when S. aureus, Pseudomonas, Candida, mycobacteria, tunnel infection, persistent bacteremia, endocarditis, thrombophlebitis, or severe sepsis is present. Catheter salvage and antibiotic-lock therapy are reserved for selected stable patients with lower-risk organisms and no complicated infection.

Catheter-associated urinary infection is frequently overdiagnosed. Pyuria, bacteriuria, cloudy urine, and delirium alone do not establish infection. Remove unnecessary urinary catheters, replace older catheters when true CAUTI is treated, collect urine from the sampling port or new catheter, and avoid treating asymptomatic bacteriuria except in pregnancy or before selected urologic procedures.

HAP and VAP require a compatible clinical and radiographic syndrome rather than a positive respiratory culture alone. Use empiric MRSA and antipseudomonal therapy according to severity and resistance risk, then de-escalate promptly. Approximately seven days is adequate for most improving cases.

Surgical-site and implanted-device infections require drainage, debridement, extraction, or other source control when indicated. Prophylactic surgical antibiotics should stop after incision closure, even when a drain remains. Preventing healthcare-associated infection depends on hand hygiene, correct isolation, environmental disinfection, antimicrobial stewardship, evidence-based insertion and maintenance bundles, and daily removal of unnecessary devices.

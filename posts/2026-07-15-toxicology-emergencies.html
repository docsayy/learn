Clinical Overview

The poisoned patient is managed by physiology first. Identify life-threatening airway, ventilation, dysrhythmia, shock, seizure, temperature, and glucose problems while gathering medication, timing, dose, formulation, and co-ingestion history.

Supportive Care + Toxidrome + ECG + Targeted Antidote

Immediate Approach

  • Airway and ventilation; use capnography.
  • Glucose, temperature, ECG, and continuous monitoring.
  • IV access and focused laboratory testing.
  • Acetaminophen level in significant intentional ingestion.
  • Pregnancy testing when relevant.
  • Contact poison control early.

Decontamination

  • Activated charcoal may help selected early ingestions with protected airway and adsorbable toxin.
  • Whole-bowel irrigation is reserved for selected sustained-release, metal, or body-packer exposures.
  • Gastric lavage is rarely indicated.
  • External contamination requires clothing removal and washing before routine care areas.

ECG Toxicology

  • Wide QRS or terminal R in aVR suggests sodium-channel blockade.
  • QT prolongation increases torsades risk.
  • Bradycardia and AV block occur with beta-blockers, calcium-channel blockers, digoxin, and others.
  • Serial ECGs are often more useful than one tracing.

Sodium Bicarbonate

  • Use for TCA and other sodium-channel blocker toxicity with QRS widening, ventricular dysrhythmia, or hypotension.
  • Use in severe salicylate poisoning to alkalinize serum and urine.
  • Monitor sodium, potassium, pH, and volume status.

High-Yield Poisonings

  • Acetaminophen: start N-acetylcysteine based on nomogram or clinical risk; do not delay in late or uncertain severe ingestion.
  • Salicylates: avoid intubation when possible; alkalinize and dialyze early for severe disease.
  • Toxic alcohols: fomepizole and dialysis according to acid-base status, levels, and organ injury.
  • Opioids: titrated naloxone and ventilatory support.
  • Calcium-channel blocker: calcium, high-dose insulin, vasopressors, and specialist rescue.
  • Beta-blocker: glucagon, high-dose insulin, vasopressors, and agent-specific therapy.

The Dangerous Toxicologic Airway

Intubation can be hazardous in salicylate poisoning and severe metabolic acidosis because loss of compensatory hyperventilation causes abrupt acidemia. If unavoidable, minimize apnea and match the pre-intubation minute ventilation immediately.

Lipid Emulsion and ECMO

IV lipid emulsion is used as rescue for local-anesthetic systemic toxicity and selected severe lipophilic cardiotoxic overdoses after standard therapy. VA-ECMO can bridge refractory cardiogenic shock from reversible poisoning in experienced centers.

Extracorporeal Treatment

  • Consider dialyzability, severity, organ failure, level, and expected natural clearance.
  • Classic dialyzable toxins include salicylate, methanol, ethylene glycol, lithium, valproate, and selected others.
  • Consult nephrology and poison control early rather than waiting for collapse.

Common Pitfalls

  • No acetaminophen level in intentional overdose
  • Intubating salicylate toxicity without matching ventilation
  • Using flumazenil in mixed or seizure-prone overdose
  • Delaying dialysis
  • Treating a tox screen rather than the patient
  • Missing extended-release formulations

Bottom Line

Stabilize physiology, obtain an ECG and targeted levels, recognize toxidromes, call poison control early, and use antidotes or extracorporeal therapy when they change outcome. The most dangerous error is destroying compensatory ventilation during intubation.

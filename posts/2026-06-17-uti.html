Urinary Tract Infections: A Practical Guide for Internal Medicine Residents

Added June 17, 2026 • Infectious Disease • ~15 min read

A practical, resident-focused guide to urinary tract infections, including simple cystitis, complicated UTI, pyelonephritis, inpatient and outpatient antibiotic selection, urinalysis interpretation, asymptomatic bacteriuria, catheter-associated UTI, recurrent infections, ESBL organisms, and high-yield clinical pearls.

Overview

Urinary tract infections are among the most common infections treated by internal medicine physicians. The key to managing them well is not simply choosing an antibiotic. The key is first deciding what type of urinary infection the patient has.

The most important first branch point is whether the patient has simple cystitis or a complicated UTI. This distinction determines the likely severity of illness, the need for IV therapy, whether imaging should be considered, and the duration of antibiotics.

Resident framework: First decide whether the infection is limited to the bladder or whether there are signs that infection has extended beyond the bladder.

Simple Cystitis vs Complicated UTI

Simple cystitis refers to infection localized to the bladder. Complicated UTI refers to infection with systemic features, upper tract involvement, pyelonephritis, resistant organisms, or higher-risk host factors.

Feature Simple Cystitis Complicated UTI / Pyelonephritis
Primary location Bladder Upper tract or systemic infection
Dysuria Common May be present
Frequency / urgency Common May be present
Suprapubic tenderness Common May be present
Fever / chills Usually absent Common
Flank pain Absent Suggests upper tract disease
CVA tenderness Absent Suggests pyelonephritis
Systemic illness Absent May be present

Clinical Features

Simple Cystitis

  • Dysuria
  • Urinary frequency
  • Urinary urgency
  • Suprapubic tenderness
  • No fever, flank pain, or systemic toxicity

Complicated UTI / Pyelonephritis

  • Fever or chills
  • Flank pain
  • CVA tenderness
  • Nausea or vomiting
  • Systemic signs of infection
  • Possible bacteremia or sepsis
Clinical warning: Fever, flank pain, CVA tenderness, hypotension, or systemic illness should move you away from simple cystitis and toward complicated UTI or pyelonephritis.

Inpatient Treatment

Hospitalized patients with UTI are commonly started on IV antibiotics, then narrowed once urine culture and susceptibility data return.

Common IV Options

Scenario Common Initial Antibiotic Notes
Typical inpatient UTI Ceftriaxone Often 1 g IV every 24 hours
Bacteremia suspected or confirmed Ceftriaxone Often increased to 2 g IV every 24 hours
Pseudomonas risk Cefepime Anti-pseudomonal coverage
MDR organism risk Piperacillin-tazobactam or meropenem Broaden based on history and severity
Critically ill patient Broad gram-negative coverage ± vancomycin Consider imaging and early source evaluation

Pseudomonas Risk Factors

  • Recent antibiotic use within the last 3 months
  • Recent hospitalization
  • Nursing home or healthcare exposure
  • Travel to areas with high antimicrobial resistance
  • Prior urine culture with resistant organisms
Practical approach: Start broad when appropriate, but narrow to the most targeted oral or IV antibiotic once culture susceptibilities are available.

Outpatient Simple Cystitis

For uncomplicated outpatient cystitis, oral therapy is usually sufficient. Antibiotic choice should account for renal function, allergies, prior cultures, local resistance patterns, pregnancy status, and medication interactions.

Medication Typical Duration Key Notes
Nitrofurantoin 5 days Common first-line option; avoid if eGFR <30; bladder only
TMP-SMX 3 days Useful if susceptible; watch AKI, hyperkalemia, and resistance
Fosfomycin Single dose Useful option, especially in selected resistant organisms
Beta-lactams 5–7 days Examples: cephalexin, cefpodoxime, amoxicillin-clavulanate
Fluoroquinolones Usually 3 days for simple cystitis Reserve when needed due to adverse effects and resistance concerns
Nitrofurantoin pearl: Nitrofurantoin is appropriate for simple cystitis but should not be used for pyelonephritis because it does not achieve adequate kidney tissue penetration.

Outpatient Complicated UTI and Pyelonephritis

Patients with pyelonephritis or complicated UTI who are stable enough for outpatient treatment often receive oral therapy with agents that achieve adequate urinary and renal tissue concentrations.

Scenario Potential Regimen Typical Duration
Pyelonephritis, low resistance risk Ciprofloxacin or levofloxacin 5–7 days
High fluoroquinolone resistance One dose IV ceftriaxone, then oral beta-lactam 7–10 days
Alternative if susceptible TMP-SMX 7–10 days
High MDR risk Initial IV broad agent, then narrow based on culture Depends on organism and syndrome
Fluoroquinolone pearl: Ciprofloxacin and levofloxacin penetrate the urinary tract well. Moxifloxacin is considered more of a respiratory fluoroquinolone and is not preferred for UTIs.

Diagnosis

The diagnosis of UTI requires both supportive testing and compatible symptoms. A positive urinalysis alone does not automatically mean the patient has a UTI that requires treatment.

The major diagnostic tools are urinalysis and urine culture. Urinalysis provides rapid supportive evidence, while urine culture identifies the organism and susceptibilities.

Urinalysis Interpretation

A urinalysis should be interpreted in context. The most important findings include nitrites, leukocyte esterase, white blood cells, bacteria, and squamous epithelial cells.

Finding Meaning Clinical Pearl
Nitrites Suggest gram-negative bacteria Enterococcus may be nitrite-negative
Leukocyte esterase Enzyme from WBCs Suggests inflammation
Pyuria WBCs in urine Supports inflammation but is not diagnostic alone
Bacteria Organisms seen in urine Culture determines colony count and organism
Squamous epithelial cells Contamination marker Many squamous cells suggest poor clean-catch sample

Culture Thresholds

  • Traditional threshold: ≥100,000 CFU/mL
  • Catheter-associated UTI threshold: ≥1,000 CFU/mL
Language pearl: Avoid saying “dirty UA” because some clinicians use it to mean contamination, while others use it to mean a positive UA. Be specific: contaminated sample, positive nitrites, pyuria, bacteria, or many squamous cells.

Catheter-Associated UTI

Patients with chronic Foley catheters commonly have colonization and asymptomatic bacteriuria. This does not automatically require treatment.

  • Colonization is common with chronic catheters.
  • Do not treat asymptomatic bacteriuria just because the urine culture is positive.
  • If a true catheter-associated UTI is suspected, replace the catheter before collecting a new urine culture.
  • The culture threshold for catheter-associated UTI is lower than standard clean-catch thresholds.
Important: A urine culture from an old Foley can reflect colonization of the catheter rather than true bladder infection. Replace the catheter and obtain a clean sample when possible.

Candida in the Urine

Candida in the urine is common in patients with chronic catheters and does not usually require treatment.

When Treatment May Be Considered

  • Clear symptoms attributable to Candida UTI
  • Severe neutropenia or marked immunocompromise
  • Selected high-risk procedural scenarios

Renal Transplant Patients

UTIs in renal transplant patients require special caution because infection can threaten the transplanted kidney. These patients are immunosuppressed and may need broader therapy and longer treatment duration.

  • Consider broad-spectrum antibiotics such as piperacillin-tazobactam or meropenem depending on severity and prior culture history.
  • Treatment duration may be extended compared with routine UTI.
  • Have a lower threshold for specialist involvement.
High-risk host: Inadequately treated infection in a renal transplant patient may threaten graft function.

Asymptomatic Bacteriuria

Asymptomatic bacteriuria means bacteria are present in the urine without symptoms of cystitis, pyelonephritis, or systemic infection. Most patients do not need antibiotics.

Population Treat?
Pregnancy Yes
Before invasive urologic procedure Yes
Most older adults without urinary symptoms No
Chronic Foley without symptoms No
Men without symptoms No
Board pearl: Treat asymptomatic bacteriuria in pregnancy and before invasive urologic procedures. Most other patients do not need treatment.

UTIs in Men

UTIs are less common in men because of the longer urethra, but men can still develop simple cystitis, complicated UTI, or asymptomatic bacteriuria.

  • UTI in a man is not automatically complicated in every case.
  • Men can have asymptomatic bacteriuria.
  • Persistent or recurrent UTI symptoms should raise concern for prostatitis.
  • Prostatitis often requires longer antibiotic courses with agents that penetrate the prostate, such as fluoroquinolones or TMP-SMX when appropriate.

Bacteremia and UTI

Severe pyelonephritis can lead to gram-negative bacteremia. In many cases, bacteremia from a urinary source does not automatically require extending antibiotics beyond the usual recommended duration if the patient responds well and source control is adequate.

Practical pearl: Do not reflexively extend duration just because blood cultures are positive. Consider the organism, response to therapy, source control, immune status, and clinical stability.

Staphylococcus aureus in the Urine

Staphylococcus aureus is not expected urinary flora. When it appears in the urine, consider the possibility of occult Staphylococcus aureus bacteremia with secondary seeding of the urinary tract.

Do not ignore this: Staph aureus in the urine should prompt blood cultures and evaluation for bacteremia.

Recurrent UTIs

Recurrent UTIs are common and require a broader strategy than simply repeating short antibiotic courses indefinitely.

Common Definitions

  • Three or more UTIs in one year
  • Two or more UTIs in six months
  • Recent UTI treated with antibiotics within the last three months

Prevention Strategies

  • Increase fluid intake when appropriate
  • Post-coital urination
  • Consider vaginal estrogen in post-menopausal patients
  • Consider post-coital antibiotic prophylaxis for clearly sex-associated UTIs
  • Consider continuous prophylaxis only after careful risk-benefit discussion
Antibiotic stewardship: Chronic prophylactic antibiotics can help selected patients, but they also increase risks of resistance, adverse effects, and microbiome disruption.

ESBL and Resistant Organisms

Extended-spectrum beta-lactamase organisms are resistant mechanisms that can make common cephalosporins unreliable.

Even if an ESBL organism appears susceptible to cefepime on a report, many clinicians avoid cefepime because beta-lactamases can hydrolyze cephalosporins. Carbapenems such as meropenem are commonly used for serious ESBL infections.

High-yield pearl: ESBL infection generally pushes treatment toward carbapenem therapy for serious infection rather than cefepime, even when cefepime susceptibility appears favorable.

Cloudy or Smelly Urine

Cloudy or foul-smelling urine alone is not enough to diagnose a UTI. Diet, dehydration, colonization, and catheter-related changes can all alter urine appearance or smell.

Treatment rule: Do not treat isolated cloudy or smelly urine unless the patient also has compatible urinary symptoms or systemic signs of infection.

Altered Mental Status and UTI

Altered mental status in older adults is one of the most common situations where UTIs are overdiagnosed. A positive urinalysis in an older patient does not automatically prove that the urine is the cause of delirium.

When urinary symptoms are absent, consider other causes of delirium first, including medications, dehydration, metabolic abnormalities, pain, sleep disruption, constipation, urinary retention, hypoxia, and environmental factors.

  • Do not order UA reflexively in the absence of urinary symptoms.
  • A positive UA without symptoms may represent asymptomatic bacteriuria.
  • Watchful waiting and correction of other delirium triggers may be appropriate in stable patients.
  • Treat when urinary symptoms, fever, sepsis, or strong clinical evidence supports infection.
Stewardship warning: Treating asymptomatic bacteriuria as “UTI causing confusion” can lead to unnecessary antibiotics, resistance, C. difficile infection, and missed alternative diagnoses.

Key Takeaways

  • First decide whether the patient has simple cystitis or complicated UTI.
  • Simple cystitis is localized to the bladder.
  • Fever, flank pain, CVA tenderness, or systemic illness suggests complicated UTI or pyelonephritis.
  • Ceftriaxone is a common inpatient starting point, but broaden if Pseudomonas, MDR risk, or critical illness is present.
  • Nitrofurantoin is for bladder infection only, not pyelonephritis.
  • Interpret UA in context; pyuria or bacteria alone does not equal UTI.
  • Many squamous epithelial cells suggest sample contamination.
  • Do not treat asymptomatic bacteriuria except in pregnancy or before invasive urologic procedures.
  • Staph aureus in urine should prompt blood cultures.
  • Do not treat isolated cloudy or smelly urine.
  • In older adults with altered mental status, look for other delirium causes before blaming the urine.

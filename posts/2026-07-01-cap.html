Community-Acquired Pneumonia: Outpatient Treatment and High-Value Workup

Added July 1, 2026 • Infectious Disease • ~16 min read

Community-acquired pneumonia is usually diagnosed clinically with compatible symptoms and a new infiltrate on chest imaging. In an otherwise healthy outpatient without comorbidities or MRSA/Pseudomonas risk factors, high-dose oral amoxicillin is first-line therapy. This article reviews outpatient CAP treatment, when diagnostic testing is unnecessary, when cultures are indicated, when to use broader regimens, and how to avoid over-testing and over-treating low-risk patients.

Index Case

A 45-year-old woman presents with fever, chills, cough, and mild dyspnea when walking up stairs. She had one week of nasal congestion and sore throat, and her two young children recently had upper respiratory symptoms. She has no medical problems and takes no medications.

Findings

Feature Result
Temperature 37.6°C
Blood pressure 126/75 mm Hg
Pulse 92/min
Respiratory rate 16/min
Oxygen saturation 97% on room air
Lung exam Crackles over left lung base
SARS-CoV-2 test Negative
Chest radiograph Left lower lobe consolidation

Best Next Step

Oral amoxicillin
Otherwise healthy outpatient + CAP on chest x-ray + no comorbidities + no MRSA/Pseudomonas risk factors = treat with oral amoxicillin.

Why This Is Community-Acquired Pneumonia

Community-acquired pneumonia is supported by acute respiratory symptoms plus focal lung findings and a new infiltrate on chest imaging.

Clinical Features Supporting CAP

  • Cough
  • Fever or chills
  • Mild dyspnea
  • Focal crackles
  • New left lower lobe consolidation
  • Recent upper respiratory infection suggesting possible secondary bacterial pneumonia

Common Organisms

Organism Group Examples
Typical bacteria Streptococcus pneumoniae, Haemophilus influenzae
Atypical bacteria Mycoplasma pneumoniae, Chlamydia pneumoniae, Legionella
Viruses Influenza, RSV, SARS-CoV-2, respiratory viruses
Less common severe pathogens Staphylococcus aureus, gram-negative bacilli
A preceding viral upper respiratory illness can increase risk for secondary bacterial pneumonia.

Outpatient Treatment

Treatment is based on site of care and comorbidities.

Healthy Outpatient Without MRSA or Pseudomonas Risk

Preferred Options Comment
Amoxicillin 1 g PO three times daily Preferred high-value option
Doxycycline Alternative outpatient option
Macrolide Only if local pneumococcal macrolide resistance is less than 25%

Outpatient With Comorbidities

Comorbidities include chronic heart, lung, liver, or kidney disease; diabetes mellitus; alcohol use disorder; asplenia; malignancy; and immunosuppression.

Regimen Examples
Respiratory fluoroquinolone monotherapy Levofloxacin or moxifloxacin
Oral beta-lactam plus macrolide or doxycycline Amoxicillin-clavulanate, cefuroxime, or cefpodoxime plus azithromycin or doxycycline
Levofloxacin is not the first choice for an otherwise healthy low-risk outpatient when amoxicillin is appropriate.

Why Amoxicillin?

High-dose amoxicillin targets the most important typical outpatient CAP pathogens, especially Streptococcus pneumoniae and Haemophilus influenzae.

Why Not Routine Azithromycin?

Macrolide resistance in pneumococcus is common in the United States. Therefore, macrolide monotherapy is recommended only when local pneumococcal resistance is known to be less than 25%.

High-dose amoxicillin is narrow, effective, guideline-supported, and avoids unnecessary fluoroquinolone toxicity.

Why the Other Options Are Wrong

Option Why It Is Not Best
Blood cultures Not routinely indicated in low-risk outpatient CAP
Chest CT Not needed when chest x-ray already shows consolidation and patient is low risk
Levofloxacin Reserved for outpatients with comorbidities or selected hospitalized patients
Sputum Gram stain and culture Not routinely indicated in low-risk outpatient CAP
Do not order sputum cultures just because pneumonia is present. In low-risk outpatients, testing usually does not change management.

Diagnostic Testing in CAP

Usually Not Needed in Low-Risk Outpatients

  • Blood cultures
  • Sputum Gram stain and culture
  • Chest CT
  • Broad microbiologic testing

When Blood Cultures Are Indicated

  • Severe pneumonia
  • ICU admission
  • Empiric MRSA coverage being used
  • Empiric Pseudomonas coverage being used
  • Concern for bacteremia or complicated infection

When Sputum Gram Stain and Culture Are Indicated

  • ICU admission
  • Failure of outpatient antibiotics
  • Cavitary lung lesion
  • Underlying structural lung disease
  • Concern for MRSA or Pseudomonas

When Chest CT Is Useful

  • High clinical suspicion but normal or equivocal chest radiograph in high-risk patients
  • Febrile neutropenia
  • ARDS or severe illness with nondiagnostic plain film
  • Concern for abscess, empyema, malignancy, or alternative diagnosis
Chest x-ray is enough for most straightforward outpatient CAP cases.

Site of Care: Outpatient vs Inpatient vs ICU

Site of care is based on severity, comorbidities, vital signs, oxygenation, and clinical judgment.

Outpatient Features

  • Normal or near-normal vital signs
  • No hypoxemia
  • No confusion
  • No hypotension
  • Able to take oral medications
  • Reliable follow-up
  • No major comorbidities or severe CAP features

Severe CAP Major Criteria

Major Criterion Meaning
Need for mechanical ventilation Severe respiratory failure
Septic shock requiring vasopressors Severe circulatory failure

Severe CAP Minor Criteria

  • Respiratory rate ≥30/min
  • Temperature less than 36.8°C
  • Hypotension requiring aggressive fluid resuscitation
  • Confusion or disorientation
  • BUN ≥20 mg/dL
  • Leukocyte count less than 4000/µL
  • Platelets less than 100,000/µL
  • PaO₂/FiO₂ ratio ≤250
  • Multilobar infiltrates
The patient in the index case is stable, oxygenating normally, and has no comorbidities, so outpatient therapy is appropriate.

Empiric Antibiotic Table

Site / Patient Type Likely Organisms Recommended Regimen
Outpatient, healthy, no MRSA/Pseudomonas risk S. pneumoniae, H. influenzae, atypicals, respiratory viruses Amoxicillin OR doxycycline OR macrolide if resistance <25%
Outpatient with comorbidities Same organisms plus broader risk Respiratory fluoroquinolone OR oral beta-lactam plus macrolide/doxycycline
Inpatient, non-ICU S. pneumoniae, atypicals, H. influenzae, Legionella, respiratory viruses IV beta-lactam plus macrolide OR respiratory fluoroquinolone
ICU S. pneumoniae, S. aureus, H. influenzae, Legionella, gram-negative bacilli IV beta-lactam plus macrolide; beta-lactam plus respiratory fluoroquinolone if macrolide contraindicated
Risk for Pseudomonas Pseudomonas aeruginosa Antipseudomonal beta-lactam plus macrolide or antipseudomonal quinolone
Risk for CA-MRSA MRSA Standard therapy plus vancomycin or linezolid

MRSA and Pseudomonas Risk

MRSA Risk Factors

  • Prior respiratory culture growing MRSA
  • Suspicious Gram stain with gram-positive cocci in clusters
  • Failure of conventional therapy
  • Cavitary lung lesions
  • Pleural-based nodules suggesting septic pulmonary emboli
  • Recent influenza
  • Hospitalization or parenteral antibiotics in the prior 90 days
  • Injection drug use

MRSA Treatment Options

  • Vancomycin
  • Linezolid
Daptomycin is ineffective for pneumonia because it binds pulmonary surfactant.

Pseudomonas Risk Factors

  • Prior respiratory culture growing Pseudomonas aeruginosa
  • Structural lung disease
  • Immunocompromised state
  • Repeated antibiotic courses
  • Hospitalization or parenteral antibiotics in the prior 90 days

Antipseudomonal Beta-Lactam Options

  • Piperacillin-tazobactam
  • Cefepime
  • Ceftazidime
  • Aztreonam
  • Imipenem
  • Meropenem

Viral Pneumonia and Bacterial Coinfection

Identification of a viral pathogen does not automatically exclude bacterial coinfection.

Influenza Pearls

  • Treat CAP patients who test positive for influenza with antiviral therapy.
  • Benefit is greatest when started early, but hospitalized patients are generally treated regardless of duration.
  • Postinfluenza necrotizing pneumonia can be caused by S. aureus, S. pneumoniae, or S. pyogenes.

Outpatient Viral CAP Without Comorbidities

If a viral organism is identified and the patient is an outpatient without comorbidities, antibiotics may not be required if bacterial coinfection is unlikely.

Hospitalized Severe Viral CAP

Hospitalized patients with severe disease are generally treated with empiric antibiotics because bacterial coinfection is harder to exclude and the consequences of undertreatment are higher.

Anaerobic Coverage

Anaerobic organisms are uncommon causes of routine CAP.

When to Add Anaerobic Coverage

  • Lung abscess
  • Empyema
  • Clear aspiration syndrome with necrotizing infection

When Not to Add It

  • Routine outpatient CAP
  • Aspiration risk without abscess or empyema
  • Simple lobar pneumonia without necrosis
Do not add anaerobic coverage to routine CAP unless lung abscess or empyema is suspected.

Duration of Therapy

Duration depends on severity and clinical response.

Scenario Duration Concept
Outpatient or nonsevere CAP with clinical stability May be less than 5 days, minimum 3 days in selected patients
Severe inpatient CAP At least 5 days
Cavitary disease or lung abscess Longer course
Empyema Longer course plus drainage/source control
Bacteremia or extrapulmonary infection Longer course depending on pathogen and site
Ongoing instability Continue and reassess diagnosis/source control

Clinical Stability

  • Improving fever
  • Stable blood pressure
  • Improving respiratory rate
  • No hypoxia
  • Clinical improvement
  • Able to take oral therapy if outpatient

Steroids in CAP

Steroids are not used for routine low-risk outpatient CAP.

When Steroids May Be Considered

  • Severe CAP requiring ICU-level care
  • Severe hypoxemia
  • Mechanical ventilation
  • Selected severe inflammatory phenotypes
  • COVID pneumonia requiring oxygen

When to Avoid Steroids

  • Concern for fungal infection
  • Concern for mycobacterial infection
  • Routine mild outpatient CAP
Steroids in CAP are a severe-disease question, not an outpatient low-risk CAP question.

Resident Approach to Outpatient CAP

  1. Confirm pneumonia syndrome: cough, fever/chills, dyspnea, focal exam, infiltrate.
  2. Assess severity and oxygenation.
  3. Decide site of care: outpatient, inpatient, ICU.
  4. Check comorbidities.
  5. Check MRSA and Pseudomonas risk factors.
  6. For healthy outpatient without risk factors, choose amoxicillin or doxycycline.
  7. Avoid routine blood cultures, sputum cultures, and CT in low-risk outpatient CAP.
  8. Give return precautions and reassess if not improving.
In low-risk outpatient CAP, the high-value move is treatment, not broad diagnostic testing.

Common Pitfalls

  • Ordering sputum cultures in low-risk outpatient CAP.
  • Ordering blood cultures in uncomplicated outpatient CAP.
  • Using chest CT when chest x-ray already confirms pneumonia and the patient is stable.
  • Using levofloxacin when narrow therapy is appropriate.
  • Using macrolide monotherapy without knowing local resistance is low.
  • Forgetting that fluoroquinolones and macrolides can prolong QTc.
  • Adding anaerobic coverage without abscess or empyema.
  • Assuming viral detection always excludes bacterial coinfection.
  • Missing MRSA after influenza or in cavitary pneumonia.
  • Forgetting daptomycin does not treat pneumonia.

Clinical Pearls

  • CAP requires compatible symptoms plus a new infiltrate.
  • Streptococcus pneumoniae is the classic outpatient CAP pathogen.
  • Healthy outpatient CAP can be treated with high-dose amoxicillin.
  • Amoxicillin dose for outpatient CAP is commonly 1 g PO three times daily.
  • Doxycycline is an outpatient alternative.
  • Macrolide monotherapy depends on local resistance being less than 25%.
  • Levofloxacin is usually reserved for comorbid outpatients or selected hospitalized patients.
  • Blood cultures are not routinely indicated in low-risk outpatient CAP.
  • Sputum Gram stain and culture are not routinely indicated in low-risk outpatient CAP.
  • Chest CT is not needed when chest x-ray confirms CAP and the patient is stable.
  • MRSA CAP risk increases after influenza, with cavitary lesions, IVDU, or prior MRSA.
  • Pseudomonas risk increases with structural lung disease, prior isolation, and recent IV antibiotics/hospitalization.
  • Daptomycin is ineffective for pneumonia because of surfactant binding.
  • Anaerobic coverage is mainly for lung abscess or empyema, not routine aspiration risk.
  • Steroids are not routine for mild outpatient CAP.

Bottom Line

This patient has uncomplicated outpatient community-acquired pneumonia: acute cough, chills, focal crackles, and a left lower lobe infiltrate, with normal oxygenation, stable vital signs, no comorbidities, and no MRSA or Pseudomonas risk factors. The best next step is oral amoxicillin, typically high-dose 1 g three times daily. Blood cultures, sputum Gram stain and culture, and chest CT are not routinely indicated in low-risk outpatient CAP. Levofloxacin is broader than needed and is generally reserved for patients with comorbidities or selected hospitalized patients.

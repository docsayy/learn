Carbapenem-Resistant Enterobacterales (CRE): Practical Treatment Guide

Added July 1, 2026 • Infectious Disease • ~16 min read

Carbapenem-resistant Enterobacterales are among the most difficult gram-negative infections to treat. This guide uses a pyelonephritis case with ceftriaxone-, cefepime-, imipenem-, and ciprofloxacin-resistant Klebsiella pneumoniae to review how to recognize CRE, why meropenem alone is not effective, when ceftazidime-avibactam is appropriate, why oral fosfomycin is not used for pyelonephritis, and how newer beta-lactam/beta-lactamase inhibitor combinations fit into resistant gram-negative treatment.

Index Case

A 42-year-old woman is hospitalized for pyelonephritis. She was started empirically on ceftriaxone, but by hospital day 3 her symptoms have improved only slightly. She has a history of recurrent left lower extremity cellulitis and deep wound infection after an open tibia/fibula fracture from a motorcycle accident abroad one year ago.

Current Data

Finding Result
Temperature 38.5°C
Blood pressure 106/70 mm Hg
Pulse 98/min
Exam Bilateral CVA tenderness, worse on right
Leukocyte count 15,300/µL
Urine culture Klebsiella pneumoniae
Blood cultures Klebsiella pneumoniae with same susceptibility pattern

Susceptibility Pattern

  • Resistant to ceftriaxone
  • Resistant to cefepime
  • Resistant to imipenem
  • Resistant to ciprofloxacin
A Klebsiella pneumoniae isolate resistant to imipenem should be treated as carbapenem-resistant Enterobacterales pending further testing.

Best Treatment Pending Further Susceptibility Testing

Ceftazidime-avibactam

Core Diagnosis: Carbapenem-Resistant Enterobacterales

This patient has pyelonephritis with bacteremia caused by Klebsiella pneumoniae resistant to imipenem. Imipenem resistance indicates carbapenem-resistant Enterobacterales, or CRE.

Why This Matters

  • CRE organisms are resistant to many beta-lactam antibiotics.
  • They frequently carry additional resistance genes.
  • They may also be resistant to fluoroquinolones and aminoglycosides.
  • Treatment options are limited.
  • Infectious diseases consultation is strongly recommended.
Do not treat CRE bacteremic pyelonephritis with standard cefepime, ceftriaxone, ciprofloxacin, or meropenem monotherapy if the organism is resistant.

Risk Factors for CRE

This patient has several classic risk factors for CRE.

Risk Factor Why It Matters
Health care exposure CRE is often acquired in health care settings
Prior antibiotics Cephalosporins, fluoroquinolones, and carbapenems select for resistant organisms
Medical care abroad Raises concern for uncommon or highly resistant resistance mechanisms
Prior deep wound infections Suggests prior broad-spectrum antibiotic exposure and possible colonization
Current bacteremia Indicates invasive infection, not just colonization
Medical care outside the United States can expose patients to new or regionally different resistance mechanisms, including carbapenemases not commonly seen locally.

Why Ceftazidime-Avibactam?

Ceftazidime-avibactam is a newer beta-lactam/beta-lactamase inhibitor combination with activity against many CRE isolates, especially KPC-producing organisms.

Components

Drug Role
Ceftazidime Antipseudomonal cephalosporin
Avibactam Newer beta-lactamase inhibitor active against several beta-lactamases, including KPC

Why It Fits This Case

  • The infection is complicated UTI/pyelonephritis.
  • The organism is resistant to imipenem, making CRE likely.
  • The infection is bacteremic, so oral cystitis-only agents are inappropriate.
  • Ceftazidime-avibactam has an indication for complicated UTI caused by susceptible CRE.
For CRE pyelonephritis with bacteremia, choose an IV agent active against CRE while awaiting complete susceptibility and carbapenemase testing.

Why the Other Options Are Wrong

Option Why It Is Wrong
Cefepime The isolate is cefepime resistant and cefepime does not reliably treat CRE.
Oral fosfomycin Fosfomycin is for uncomplicated cystitis, not pyelonephritis or bacteremia.
Meropenem Meropenem alone is not effective against carbapenem-resistant Klebsiella.

Meropenem Nuance

Meropenem alone is incorrect here because the organism is carbapenem resistant. However, meropenem combined with vaborbactam is a treatment option for some CRE infections.

The issue is not that every meropenem-containing regimen is useless. The issue is that meropenem monotherapy does not overcome carbapenemase-mediated resistance.

CRE Overview

Carbapenem-resistant Enterobacterales include organisms such as Klebsiella pneumoniae, Escherichia coli, and Enterobacter species that are resistant to carbapenems.

Common CRE Organisms

  • Klebsiella pneumoniae
  • Escherichia coli
  • Enterobacter species
  • Citrobacter species
  • Serratia species

Common Infection Syndromes

  • Complicated UTI
  • Pyelonephritis
  • Bacteremia
  • Intra-abdominal infection
  • Hospital-acquired pneumonia
  • Ventilator-associated pneumonia
  • Wound infection

Carbapenemases

CRE may be resistant because of carbapenemase production or because of other resistance mechanisms such as porin loss plus beta-lactamase activity.

Carbapenemase Important Feature Therapy Implication
KPC Common in the United States Often susceptible to ceftazidime-avibactam or meropenem-vaborbactam
NDM Metallo-beta-lactamase Not inhibited by avibactam or vaborbactam; requires different strategy
OXA-48-like Variable susceptibility pattern Ceftazidime-avibactam may be active depending on isolate
Carbapenemase type matters. KPC and NDM are not treated the same way.

CRE Treatment Options

Final treatment should be guided by susceptibility testing, site of infection, renal function, severity, source control, and carbapenemase mechanism.

Agent Potential Role Important Caveat
Ceftazidime-avibactam CRE complicated UTI, intra-abdominal infection, selected pneumonia/bacteremia depending on susceptibility Not reliable for NDM metallo-beta-lactamase producers
Meropenem-vaborbactam KPC-producing CRE infections Not active against metallo-beta-lactamases like NDM
Imipenem-cilastatin-relebactam Selected CRE complicated UTI and other gram-negative infections Activity depends on resistance mechanism
Cefiderocol Highly resistant gram-negative infections including some CRE Reserve for selected resistant infections; ID guidance important
Tigecycline Some complicated intra-abdominal or soft tissue infections Poor urinary and bloodstream levels; not ideal for UTI or bacteremia
Colistin Older salvage agent High nephrotoxicity; avoid when safer active agents available
For serious CRE infections, choose therapy based on the organism, carbapenemase mechanism, infection site, and susceptibility testing—not just the name of the antibiotic class.

CRE UTI and Pyelonephritis

Syndrome Reasonable Treatment Concept Avoid
Uncomplicated cystitis Oral options may be possible if susceptible, including fosfomycin for selected isolates Overtreating colonization
Pyelonephritis IV active agent such as ceftazidime-avibactam or another active CRE agent Oral fosfomycin
Bacteremic UTI IV active CRE therapy guided by susceptibility Cystitis-only agents

Why Fosfomycin Is Not Enough

Fosfomycin achieves high urinary concentrations and can be useful for uncomplicated cystitis. However, it is not recommended for upper urinary tract infection, pyelonephritis, or bacteremia.

Oral fosfomycin is not treatment for bacteremic pyelonephritis.

ESBL vs CRE

ESBL-producing organisms and CRE are related but not the same.

Feature ESBL CRE
Typical resistance clue Resistance to ceftriaxone, cefotaxime, ceftazidime, or cefepime Resistance to carbapenems such as imipenem or meropenem
Preferred serious infection therapy Carbapenem, often meropenem or ertapenem depending on organism/site Newer CRE-active agents based on mechanism and susceptibility
Meropenem role Often preferred for serious ESBL infection Not effective alone if carbapenem resistant
ESBL serious infection often pushes you toward a carbapenem. CRE means the carbapenem may no longer work.

Other Resistant Organisms: ESKAPE Framework

Problematic resistant organisms are often remembered with the ESKAPE concept.

Organism Resistance Concern
Enterococcus faecium VRE
Staphylococcus aureus MRSA
Klebsiella pneumoniae ESBL, CRE, KPC, NDM
Acinetobacter species Multidrug resistance, carbapenem resistance
Pseudomonas aeruginosa Multidrug resistance, difficult pneumonia/bacteremia
Enterobacter species AmpC, ESBL, CRE

Pseudomonas Pearl

Ceftolozane-tazobactam is an important option for multidrug-resistant Pseudomonas aeruginosa infections.

Acinetobacter and Stenotrophomonas Pearl

Minocycline can retain activity against multidrug-resistant Acinetobacter and may also be useful for Stenotrophomonas maltophilia.

Older Antibiotics That Still Matter

Fosfomycin

  • Oral bactericidal antibiotic.
  • Has gram-negative and some gram-positive activity.
  • Useful for uncomplicated cystitis caused by selected resistant organisms.
  • Not recommended for pyelonephritis, upper tract infection, or bacteremia.

Colistin

  • Polymyxin antibiotic.
  • Can treat multidrug-resistant aerobic gram-negative infections.
  • May be IV or nebulized depending on indication.
  • High nephrotoxicity risk, up to about 50% in some reports.
  • Usually reserved when safer active agents are unavailable.

Organisms Intrinsically Resistant to Colistin

  • Proteus
  • Providencia
  • Burkholderia
  • Morganella
  • Serratia
Newer beta-lactam/beta-lactamase inhibitor agents have reduced reliance on colistin for many CRE infections.

Resident Approach to CRE Infection

  1. Confirm this is infection, not colonization.
  2. Identify the site: cystitis, pyelonephritis, bacteremia, pneumonia, wound, intra-abdominal infection.
  3. Review susceptibility pattern.
  4. Ask whether carbapenem resistance is present.
  5. Request carbapenemase testing when available.
  6. Consult infectious diseases early.
  7. Choose an active IV agent for invasive disease.
  8. Do not use cystitis-only drugs for upper tract infection or bacteremia.
  9. Assess source control: obstruction, abscess, catheter, stone, hardware.
  10. Repeat blood cultures if bacteremic and clinically indicated.
For CRE bacteremic pyelonephritis, the question is not “which common UTI drug?” It is “which active CRE agent reaches the kidney and bloodstream?”

Common Pitfalls

  • Calling ceftriaxone-resistant Klebsiella “just a UTI” without checking for ESBL or CRE.
  • Using meropenem despite documented imipenem resistance.
  • Using oral fosfomycin for pyelonephritis.
  • Assuming all CRE is KPC without checking resistance mechanism.
  • Forgetting medical care abroad as a major risk factor for unusual resistance.
  • Using daptomycin or vancomycin logic for gram-negative resistance problems.
  • Forgetting source control in complicated UTI, such as stone, obstruction, abscess, or hardware.
  • Not involving infectious diseases early for CRE bacteremia.

Clinical Pearls

  • Imipenem-resistant Klebsiella pneumoniae suggests CRE.
  • CRE often carries resistance to other classes such as fluoroquinolones and aminoglycosides.
  • Ceftazidime-avibactam is active against many KPC-producing CRE isolates.
  • Meropenem alone is not effective for carbapenem-resistant Klebsiella.
  • Meropenem-vaborbactam is different from meropenem monotherapy.
  • Fosfomycin is for uncomplicated cystitis, not pyelonephritis or bacteremia.
  • Prior health care exposure and prior antibiotic exposure are major CRE risk factors.
  • Care outside the United States raises concern for unusual resistance mechanisms.
  • ESBL serious infection often requires a carbapenem; CRE requires CRE-active therapy.
  • NDM producers are not reliably treated with avibactam or vaborbactam combinations.
  • Colistin has significant nephrotoxicity and is generally reserved for limited-option cases.
  • Infectious diseases consultation is strongly recommended for CRE infections.

Bottom Line

This patient has bacteremic pyelonephritis due to imipenem-resistant Klebsiella pneumoniae, which should be treated as CRE pending further susceptibility and carbapenemase testing. Ceftazidime-avibactam is the best option listed because it is active against many CRE isolates, including many KPC-producing strains, and is appropriate for complicated UTI/pyelonephritis when susceptible. Meropenem alone is incorrect because the organism is carbapenem resistant, cefepime is inactive because the isolate is cefepime resistant, and oral fosfomycin is inappropriate for pyelonephritis or bacteremia. CRE management requires ID consultation, active IV therapy, source control, and careful interpretation of resistance mechanisms.

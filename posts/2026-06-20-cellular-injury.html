Pathology Foundations: Cellular Injury, Inflammation, Immunodeficiency, Autoimmunity, and Wound Healing

Added June 20, 2026 • Pathology • ~30 min read

A high-yield, organized review of early pathology foundations: growth adaptations, cellular injury, cell death, free radical injury, amyloidosis, acute and chronic inflammation, primary immunodeficiency, autoimmune disease, and wound healing.

Overview

The first major pathology framework begins with how cells adapt to stress, what happens when adaptation fails, how cells die, and how the body responds through inflammation and repair.

Core framework: Cellular stress → adaptation → reversible injury → irreversible injury → cell death → inflammation → repair or regeneration.

Growth Adaptations

Cells respond to stress by changing their size, number, phenotype, or growth pattern. These adaptations are often reversible, but some can predispose to cancer.

Adaptation Definition Reversibility Cancer Risk
Hyperplasia Increase in cell number Reversible Pathologic hyperplasia may progress to dysplasia and cancer
Hypertrophy Increase in cell size Reversible Not directly premalignant
Atrophy Decrease in cell size or number Often reversible Not directly premalignant
Metaplasia Change from one mature cell type to another Reversible Can progress to dysplasia and cancer
Dysplasia Disordered growth of precancerous cells Reversible Can progress to cancer

Hyperplasia and Hypertrophy

Hyperplasia and hypertrophy often occur together. Hyperplasia requires cells capable of division, while hypertrophy can occur in permanent cells that cannot divide.

Hyperplasia

  • Increase in cell number.
  • Can be physiologic, such as pregnancy-related breast or uterine growth.
  • Can be pathologic, such as endometrial hyperplasia.
  • Pathologic hyperplasia can progress to dysplasia and cancer.
Classic exception: Benign prostatic hyperplasia is pathologic hyperplasia but does not increase prostate cancer risk.

Hypertrophy

Permanent cells undergo hypertrophy rather than hyperplasia because they cannot meaningfully divide.

  • Cardiac myocytes
  • Skeletal muscle cells
  • Neurons

Atrophy, Metaplasia, and Dysplasia

Atrophy

Atrophy can occur through decreased cell size or decreased cell number.

  • Decreased cell size occurs through ubiquitin-proteasome degradation of cytoskeleton.
  • Autophagy removes cellular components.
  • Apoptosis can reduce cell number.

Metaplasia

Metaplasia is a change in cell type due to reprogramming of stem cells. It most often affects surface epithelium.

Example Cellular Change Clinical Importance
Barrett esophagus Squamous to columnar epithelium Can progress to dysplasia and adenocarcinoma
Vitamin A deficiency Specialized epithelium becomes squamous-like Keratomalacia
Myositis ossificans Mesenchymal tissue becomes bone Bone formation in injured skeletal muscle
Apocrine metaplasia of breast Benign breast epithelial change Does not increase breast cancer risk

Dysplasia

Dysplasia is disordered proliferation of precancerous cells. It often arises from longstanding pathologic hyperplasia or metaplasia.

Important: Dysplasia is reversible, but persistent dysplasia can progress to carcinoma.

Aplasia and Hypoplasia

Term Definition Example
Aplasia Failure of cell production during embryogenesis Unilateral renal agenesis
Hypoplasia Decreased cell production during embryogenesis Streak ovary in Turner syndrome

Cellular Injury

Cell injury occurs when cellular stress exceeds the cell’s ability to adapt.

Determinants of Injury

  • Type of stress
  • Severity of stress
  • Cell type
Neurons tolerate hypoxia for only minutes, while skeletal muscle can tolerate hypoxia for much longer.

Hypoxia and Oxygen Delivery

Hypoxia means low oxygen delivery to tissue. It can occur through ischemia, hypoxemia, or reduced oxygen-carrying capacity.

Cause Mechanism PaO₂ O₂ Saturation
Ischemia Reduced blood flow Variable Variable
Hypoxemia Low arterial oxygen, PaO₂ <60 mmHg Low Low
Anemia Low hemoglobin Normal Normal
Carbon monoxide poisoning CO binds hemoglobin strongly Normal Low by co-oximetry
Methemoglobinemia Fe²⁺ oxidized to Fe³⁺ Normal Low

Carbon Monoxide Poisoning

  • Early symptom: headache
  • Possible cherry-red skin
  • PaO₂ can remain normal

Methemoglobinemia

  • Oxidized hemoglobin cannot bind oxygen.
  • Causes include sulfa drugs, nitrate drugs, and newborn physiology.
  • Classic finding: cyanosis with chocolate-colored blood.
  • Treatment: IV methylene blue.
Methemoglobinemia = Fe²⁺ → Fe³⁺

Low ATP Injury

Hypoxia and mitochondrial dysfunction reduce ATP production. Low ATP disrupts ion pumps and cellular structure.

Consequences of Low ATP

  • Increased glycolysis → lactic acidosis
  • Na⁺/K⁺ pump failure → sodium and water enter cell
  • Calcium pump failure → calcium accumulates in cytosol
  • Random enzyme activation by calcium

Reversible Injury

Hallmark of reversible injury: Cellular swelling.
  • Loss of microvilli
  • Membrane blebbing
  • Swelling of rough endoplasmic reticulum
  • Ribosomes detach
  • Decreased protein synthesis

Irreversible Injury

Hallmark of irreversible injury: Membrane damage.
  • Cellular enzymes leak into blood
  • Calcium influx worsens injury
  • Mitochondrial cytochrome C leakage triggers apoptosis
  • Lysosomal enzymes leak and digest cellular components

Cell Death

The morphologic hallmark of cell death is loss of the nucleus.

Step Description
Pyknosis Nuclear shrinking
Karyorrhexis Nuclear fragmentation
Karyolysis Nuclear dissolution

Necrosis

Necrosis is pathologic cell death involving inflammation and often affects groups of cells.

Type Description Classic Setting
Coagulative necrosis Architecture preserved by protein coagulation Ischemic infarction of solid organs except brain
Liquefactive necrosis Enzymatic digestion liquefies tissue Brain infarct, abscess, pancreatitis
Gangrenous necrosis Coagulative necrosis resembling mummified tissue Lower limb or GI ischemia
Caseous necrosis Soft, friable, “cottage cheese” appearance TB and fungal granulomas
Fat necrosis Chalky white necrotic fat from saponification Pancreatitis or trauma to fat
Fibrinoid necrosis Bright pink vessel wall injury from protein leakage Malignant hypertension, vasculitis, preeclampsia

Coagulative Necrosis

Coagulative necrosis preserves tissue architecture despite loss of nuclei. Infarcts are often pale and wedge-shaped, with the wedge pointing toward the blocked vessel.

Liquefactive Necrosis

Liquefactive necrosis occurs when enzymes digest cells and proteins, producing liquid debris. It is classic in brain infarcts and abscesses.

Gangrenous Necrosis

Dry gangrene is coagulative necrosis. If bacterial infection supervenes, liquefactive necrosis develops and the process becomes wet gangrene.

Fat Necrosis

Fat necrosis → fatty acids + calcium → saponification → chalky white deposits

Fibrinoid Necrosis

Fibrinoid necrosis is necrotic vascular injury. It is seen in malignant hypertension and vasculitis.

Pathologic Calcification

Type Mechanism Serum Calcium
Dystrophic calcification Calcium deposits in dead or damaged tissue Normal
Metastatic calcification Calcium deposits because serum calcium or phosphate is elevated High calcium or phosphate
Metastatic calcification does not mean metastatic cancer. It refers to calcium deposition due to elevated serum calcium or phosphate.

Apoptosis

Apoptosis is programmed, energy-dependent death of individual cells or small groups of cells. It does not cause inflammation.

Examples

  • Endometrial shedding during menstruation
  • Removal of cells during embryogenesis
  • CD8 T-cell killing of virally infected cells

Mechanism

  • Cell shrinks
  • Cytoplasm becomes more eosinophilic
  • Nucleus fragments
  • Apoptotic bodies form
  • Macrophages remove apoptotic bodies
Key biochemical step in apoptosis = Caspase activation

Pathways to Caspase Activation

Pathway Trigger Mechanism
Intrinsic mitochondrial pathway Cell injury, DNA damage, decreased hormonal stimulation Bcl-2 inhibition → cytochrome C leakage → caspases
Extrinsic receptor-ligand pathway Fas-FasL or TNF-TNF receptor Death receptor activation → caspases
CD8 T-cell pathway Recognition of infected or abnormal cell Perforin creates pores; granzyme activates caspases

Free Radical Injury

Free radicals are atoms or molecules with unpaired electrons. They damage DNA, proteins, and lipids.

Oxygen-Derived Free Radicals

O₂ → Superoxide → Hydrogen Peroxide → Hydroxyl Radical → Water

The hydroxyl radical is the most damaging oxygen-derived free radical.

Causes of Pathologic Free Radical Formation

  • Ionizing radiation
  • Inflammation
  • Iron and copper overload
  • Acetaminophen toxicity
  • Carbon tetrachloride
  • Thyroid hormone excess

Free Radical Removal

Defense Function
Antioxidants Neutralize free radicals
Transferrin and ferritin Bind metals and reduce radical formation
Superoxide dismutase Removes superoxide
Catalase Removes hydrogen peroxide
Glutathione peroxidase Removes hydroxyl radicals and peroxides

Classic Examples

  • Carbon tetrachloride causes fatty liver by free radical injury and decreased apolipoprotein synthesis.
  • Reperfusion injury occurs when oxygen returns to ischemic tissue and generates free radicals.

Amyloidosis

Amyloid is misfolded protein that deposits extracellularly and damages tissue.

Amyloid = Misfolded protein with β-pleated sheet structure

Diagnosis

  • Congo red positive
  • Apple-green birefringence under polarized light
  • Often diagnosed by abdominal fat pad or rectal biopsy

Systemic Amyloidosis

Type Amyloid Protein Association
Primary amyloidosis AL amyloid Plasma cell dyscrasia
Secondary amyloidosis AA amyloid Chronic inflammation, malignancy, familial Mediterranean fever

Classic Findings

  • Kidney involvement with nephrotic syndrome
  • Restrictive cardiomyopathy
  • Arrhythmia
  • Hepatosplenomegaly
  • Malabsorption
  • Macroglossia

Localized Amyloidosis

Condition Amyloid
Senile cardiac amyloidosis Normal transthyretin
Familial amyloid cardiomyopathy Mutated transthyretin
Type 2 diabetes Amylin in pancreatic islets
Alzheimer disease β-amyloid plaques
Dialysis-related amyloidosis β2-microglobulin in joints
Medullary thyroid carcinoma Calcitonin amyloid

Acute Inflammation

Inflammation is the movement of inflammatory cells, plasma proteins, and fluid out of vessels into tissue.

Acute inflammation is defined by neutrophils, not by duration.

Triggers

  • Infection
  • Necrosis

Goal

  • Clear pathogens
  • Remove necrotic debris
  • Begin healing

Phases

Phase Main Feature
Fluid phase Edema and plasma proteins
Neutrophil phase Peaks around 24 hours
Macrophage phase Peaks around 2–3 days

Toll-Like Receptors

Toll-like receptors recognize pathogen-associated molecular patterns and damage-associated molecular patterns. TLR activation upregulates NF-κB, a master inflammatory switch.

TLR4/CD14 recognizes lipopolysaccharide from gram-negative bacteria.

Arachidonic Acid Metabolism

Phospholipase A2 releases arachidonic acid from cell membrane phospholipids.

Pathway Products Functions
Cyclooxygenase Prostaglandins Vasodilation, increased vascular permeability, fever, pain
5-lipoxygenase Leukotrienes Neutrophil chemotaxis, bronchospasm, vascular permeability

Key Mediators

  • PGE2 mediates fever and pain.
  • LTB4 attracts and activates neutrophils.
  • LTC4, LTD4, and LTE4 cause bronchospasm and increased vascular permeability.
Neutrophil chemotaxis = LTB4 + C5a + IL-8 + bacterial products

Mast Cells, Complement, and Cardinal Signs

Mast Cell Activation

  • Tissue trauma
  • C3a and C5a
  • IgE cross-linking by antigen

Mast Cell Response

Response Mediator Effect
Immediate Histamine Vasodilation and increased vascular permeability
Delayed Leukotrienes Bronchospasm and vascular permeability

Complement

Complement Product Function
C3a Mast cell activation
C5a Mast cell activation and neutrophil chemotaxis
C3b Opsonization
C5b-9 Membrane attack complex

Cardinal Signs of Inflammation

Sign Cause
Rubor Vasodilation
Calor Vasodilation
Tumor Increased vascular permeability
Dolor Bradykinin and PGE2
Fever IL-1 and TNF → hypothalamic COX → PGE2

Neutrophil Recruitment

Neutrophils exit blood vessels through a predictable sequence.

Step Key Molecules
Margination Vasodilation brings leukocytes toward vessel wall
Rolling Selectins bind Sialyl-Lewis X
Adhesion Integrins bind cellular adhesion molecules
Transmigration Movement through postcapillary venules
Chemotaxis LTB4, C5a, IL-8, bacterial products

Selectins and Integrins

  • P-selectin is released from Weibel-Palade bodies by histamine.
  • E-selectin is induced by IL-1 and TNF.
  • Cellular adhesion molecules are upregulated by IL-1 and TNF.
  • Neutrophil integrins are activated by C5a and LTB4.

Leukocyte Adhesion Deficiency

Leukocyte adhesion deficiency is an autosomal recessive defect in integrins, classically involving CD18.

Classic triad: Delayed umbilical cord separation, recurrent bacterial infections without pus, and increased circulating neutrophils.

Phagocytosis

Phagocytosis begins with engulfment of material into a phagosome, which then fuses with a lysosome to form a phagolysosome.

Major Opsonins

  • IgG
  • C3b

Oxygen-Dependent Killing

O₂ → Superoxide → H₂O₂ → HOCl
Step Enzyme
O₂ to superoxide NADPH oxidase
Superoxide to hydrogen peroxide Superoxide dismutase
Hydrogen peroxide to bleach Myeloperoxidase

Chronic Granulomatous Disease

Chronic granulomatous disease is caused by defective NADPH oxidase, impairing oxygen-dependent killing.

Catalase-Positive Organisms

  • Staphylococcus aureus
  • Pseudomonas cepacia
  • Serratia marcescens
  • Nocardia
  • Aspergillus
Catalase-positive organisms degrade their own hydrogen peroxide, so patients with CGD cannot use microbial H₂O₂ to make bleach.

Myeloperoxidase Deficiency

  • Usually asymptomatic
  • Increased risk of Candida infection
  • Normal nitroblue tetrazolium test

Chediak-Higashi Syndrome

Chediak-Higashi syndrome is a microtubule trafficking defect.

  • Giant granules in leukocytes
  • Neutropenia
  • Recurrent pyogenic infections
  • Defective primary hemostasis
  • Albinism
  • Peripheral neuropathy

Chronic Inflammation

Chronic inflammation is a delayed, more specific inflammatory response dominated by lymphocytes, plasma cells, and macrophages.

Common Triggers

  • Persistent infection
  • Viral, mycobacterial, fungal, or parasitic infection
  • Autoimmune disease
  • Foreign material
  • Some cancers

T Cells

Cell Recognizes Function
CD4 T cell MHC II Helper cytokine secretion
CD8 T cell MHC I Cytotoxic killing

CD4 Subtypes

Subtype Cytokines Function
Th1 IFN-γ, IL-2 Macrophage activation and CD8 activation
Th2 IL-4, IL-5, IL-10 Eosinophils, class switching, anti-inflammatory effects

B Cells

Naive B cells express IgM and IgD. They can be activated directly by antigen or through T-cell help.

  • IL-4 and IL-5 promote class switching.
  • Somatic hypermutation improves antibody affinity.
  • B cells mature into plasma cells.

Granulomatous Inflammation

Granulomatous inflammation is a type of chronic inflammation defined by epithelioid histiocytes.

Defining feature of granuloma: Epithelioid histiocytes, often with multinucleated giant cells.

Granuloma Formation

Macrophage presents antigen → IL-12 → Th1 differentiation → IFN-γ → epithelioid histiocytes and giant cells

Noncaseating Granulomas

  • Foreign body reaction
  • Sarcoidosis
  • Beryllium exposure
  • Crohn disease
  • Cat scratch disease

Caseating Granulomas

  • Tuberculosis
  • Fungal infection
If a caseating granuloma is seen, use acid-fast staining for TB and GMS silver staining for fungal organisms.

Primary Immunodeficiency

Disorder Defect Classic Findings
DiGeorge syndrome 22q11 deletion with failed 3rd/4th pouch development T-cell deficiency, hypocalcemia, cardiac and facial abnormalities
SCID Defective T and B immunity Severe opportunistic infections, live vaccine danger
X-linked agammaglobulinemia Bruton tyrosine kinase mutation Recurrent bacterial, enterovirus, and Giardia infections after 6 months
CVID Low antibodies due to B or T cell defect Bacterial infections, autoimmune disease, lymphoma risk
IgA deficiency Low serum and mucosal IgA Mucosal infections; associated with celiac disease
Hyper-IgM syndrome CD40 or CD40L defect High IgM, low IgG/IgA/IgE, recurrent pyogenic infections
Wiskott-Aldrich syndrome WASP mutation, X-linked Thrombocytopenia, eczema, recurrent infections

Complement Deficiencies

  • C5-C9 deficiency → recurrent Neisseria infection
  • C1 inhibitor deficiency → hereditary angioedema

Autoimmune Disorders

Autoimmune disease results from loss of self-tolerance or an overactive immune system in genetically susceptible individuals.

Systemic Lupus Erythematosus

SLE can cause type II and type III hypersensitivity reactions.

  • Malar rash with photosensitivity
  • Diffuse proliferative glomerulonephritis
  • Pleuritis, pericarditis, myocarditis
  • Libman-Sacks endocarditis
  • Anemia, thrombocytopenia, leukopenia
  • CNS psychosis
  • Fever, weight loss, arthritis
Test Use
ANA Sensitive screening test
Anti-dsDNA Specific for SLE
Antihistone Drug-induced lupus

Antiphospholipid Syndrome

  • Anticardiolipin antibody → false positive syphilis test
  • Lupus anticoagulant → falsely elevated PTT but hypercoagulable state
  • Can cause DVT, stroke, hepatic vein thrombosis, and pregnancy loss

Sjögren Syndrome

  • Autoimmune destruction of lacrimal and salivary glands
  • Dry eyes and dry mouth
  • Recurrent dental caries
  • Parotid enlargement
  • Anti-SSA and anti-SSB antibodies
  • Associated with rheumatoid arthritis and B-cell lymphoma
Unilateral parotid enlargement late in Sjögren syndrome should raise concern for B-cell lymphoma.

Scleroderma

Type Features Antibody
Diffuse scleroderma Diffuse skin disease and early visceral involvement Anti-Scl-70
Limited scleroderma / CREST Calcinosis, Raynaud, esophageal dysmotility, sclerodactyly, telangiectasia Anti-centromere

Mixed Connective Tissue Disease

Mixed connective tissue disease has features of SLE, scleroderma, and polymyositis.

Mixed connective tissue disease = Anti-U1 ribonucleoprotein antibody

Wound Healing

Term Definition
Regeneration Replacement of damaged tissue with native tissue
Repair Replacement of damaged tissue with fibrous scar

Tissue Regeneration Capacity

Tissue Type Regenerative Capacity Examples
Labile Continuously regenerates Intestine, skin, bone marrow, lung epithelium
Stable Can regenerate when needed Liver, renal proximal tubule
Permanent Cannot regenerate Myocardium, skeletal muscle, neurons

Granulation Tissue

Granulation tissue is early repair tissue and should not be confused with granuloma.

  • Fibroblasts
  • Capillaries
  • Myofibroblasts
  • Type III collagen

Primary vs Secondary Intention

Type Description
Primary intention Wound edges are approximated; minimal scar
Secondary intention Wound edges are far apart; granulation tissue fills gap; more scar and contraction

Collagen and Scar Formation

Collagen Types

Type Location
Type I Bone, skin, tendon, mature scar
Type II Cartilage
Type III Granulation tissue and embryonic tissue
Type IV Basement membrane

Collagen Synthesis

  • Collagen alpha chains have Gly-X-Y structure.
  • Proline and lysine are hydroxylated using vitamin C.
  • Cross-linking occurs outside the cell.
  • Lysyl oxidase performs cross-linking and requires copper.
  • Collagenase replaces type III collagen with type I collagen and requires zinc.
Vitamin C = hydroxylation
Copper = cross-linking
Zinc = collagen remodeling

Delayed Wound Healing

  • Infection, the most common cause
  • Vitamin C deficiency
  • Copper deficiency
  • Zinc deficiency
  • Foreign body
  • Ischemia
  • Diabetes
  • Malnutrition

Abnormal Scars

Scar Type Description Collagen
Hypertrophic scar Large scar limited to wound borders Excess type I collagen
Keloid Scar extends beyond wound borders Excess type III collagen
Keloids are more common in African American patients and often affect earlobes, face, and upper extremities.

High-Yield Pearls

  1. Pathologic hyperplasia can progress to dysplasia and cancer.
  2. Benign prostatic hyperplasia does not increase prostate cancer risk.
  3. Metaplasia occurs through stem cell reprogramming.
  4. Barrett esophagus can progress to dysplasia and adenocarcinoma.
  5. Dysplasia is reversible but can progress to cancer.
  6. Cellular swelling is the hallmark of reversible injury.
  7. Membrane damage is the hallmark of irreversible injury.
  8. Loss of nucleus is the morphologic hallmark of cell death.
  9. Coagulative necrosis occurs in ischemic infarcts of solid organs except brain.
  10. Liquefactive necrosis occurs in brain infarcts and abscesses.
  11. Caseous necrosis is classic for TB and fungal infection.
  12. Fat necrosis causes chalky white deposits by saponification.
  13. Fibrinoid necrosis occurs in malignant hypertension and vasculitis.
  14. Apoptosis is caspase-mediated and does not cause inflammation.
  15. Hydroxyl radical is the most damaging oxygen free radical.
  16. Amyloid is Congo red positive with apple-green birefringence.
  17. Acute inflammation is defined by neutrophils.
  18. PGE2 mediates fever and pain.
  19. LTB4 and C5a attract neutrophils.
  20. C3b is an opsonin.
  21. C5b-9 forms the membrane attack complex.
  22. Leukocyte adhesion deficiency causes delayed cord separation and no pus.
  23. Chronic granulomatous disease causes infections with catalase-positive organisms.
  24. Noncaseating granulomas occur in sarcoidosis and Crohn disease.
  25. Caseating granulomas suggest TB or fungal infection.
  26. DiGeorge syndrome causes T-cell deficiency and hypocalcemia.
  27. X-linked agammaglobulinemia presents after 6 months of life.
  28. IgA deficiency is the most common immunoglobulin deficiency.
  29. Anti-dsDNA is specific for SLE.
  30. Anti-Scl-70 is associated with diffuse scleroderma.
  31. Anti-centromere is associated with limited scleroderma.
  32. Infection is the most common cause of delayed wound healing.
  33. Keloids are caused by excess type III collagen.

Bottom Line

The early pathology framework starts with how cells adapt to stress and what happens when adaptation fails. Reversible injury is marked by cellular swelling, while irreversible injury is marked by membrane damage and eventual cell death. Necrosis is pathologic and inflammatory, while apoptosis is programmed and non-inflammatory. Acute inflammation is neutrophil-driven, chronic inflammation is lymphocyte and macrophage-driven, and granulomas are organized macrophage responses. Immunodeficiency syndromes reflect defects in T cells, B cells, phagocytes, or complement. Autoimmune disease results from loss of self-tolerance. Wound healing depends on tissue regenerative capacity, granulation tissue, collagen remodeling, and adequate vitamin C, copper, zinc, blood supply, and nutrition.

HIV and Opportunistic Infections: The Complete Advanced Resident Guide

Added July 14, 2026 • Infectious Disease • ~55 min read

Modern antiretroviral therapy allows people with HIV to achieve durable viral suppression, immune recovery, near-normal life expectancy, and elimination of sexual HIV transmission while suppression is maintained. Advanced or newly diagnosed HIV, however, may present with life-threatening opportunistic infections, malignancy, severe drug interactions, immune-reconstitution inflammatory syndrome, or treatment failure. This guide reviews HIV diagnosis, staging, initial inpatient evaluation, ART selection and timing, resistance testing, prophylaxis thresholds, major opportunistic infections, IRIS, vaccinations, long-term monitoring, and practical bedside management.

Clinical Overview

Human immunodeficiency virus is a chronic retroviral infection that progressively impairs cell-mediated immunity when untreated. Effective combination antiretroviral therapy suppresses viral replication, allows CD4 recovery, prevents most opportunistic infections, reduces inflammation, and prevents sexual transmission.

Diagnose Early + Start Effective ART + Maintain Viral Suppression

The Major Clinical Questions

  1. Has HIV infection been definitively confirmed?
  2. Is the patient experiencing acute HIV, chronic infection, or advanced disease?
  3. What are the HIV RNA level and CD4 count?
  4. Is an active opportunistic infection or AIDS-defining malignancy present?
  5. Can ART begin immediately, or does a CNS infection justify a short delay?
  6. Which regimen is effective, tolerable, accessible, and compatible with comorbidities?
  7. Does the patient need primary or secondary OI prophylaxis?
  8. Are adherence, access, stigma, housing, mental health, or substance use threatening continuity?

Modern Treatment Goals

  • Durable HIV RNA suppression
  • Immune recovery
  • Prevention and treatment of opportunistic disease
  • Prevention of HIV transmission
  • Reduction of long-term cardiovascular, renal, hepatic, neurologic, and malignant complications
  • Preservation of quality of life
ART is recommended for every person with HIV, regardless of CD4 count, symptoms, age, or transmission route.

HIV Biology

Major Viral Steps

  1. Attachment to CD4 and a coreceptor, usually CCR5 or CXCR4
  2. Fusion with the host-cell membrane
  3. Reverse transcription of viral RNA into DNA
  4. Integration into the host genome
  5. Transcription and translation of viral proteins
  6. Assembly and budding
  7. Protease-mediated maturation

Antiretroviral Drug Classes

Drug Class Target Examples
NRTIs Reverse transcriptase Tenofovir, emtricitabine, lamivudine, abacavir
NNRTIs Reverse transcriptase Doravirine, rilpivirine, efavirenz
INSTIs Integrase Bictegravir, dolutegravir, raltegravir, cabotegravir
Protease inhibitors Viral protease Darunavir, atazanavir
Entry and attachment agents Attachment, fusion, or CCR5 Maraviroc, fostemsavir, enfuvirtide, ibalizumab
Capsid inhibitor Capsid assembly and function Lenacapavir

Viral Reservoir

Integrated proviral DNA persists in long-lived cells despite plasma viral suppression. ART controls HIV but does not eliminate this reservoir.

Stopping ART generally results in viral rebound, even after years of undetectable HIV RNA.

Transmission and U=U

Important Transmission Routes

  • Sexual exposure
  • Shared injection equipment
  • Perinatal transmission
  • Breastfeeding
  • Occupational blood exposure
  • Contaminated blood products in settings without adequate screening

HIV Is Not Transmitted By:

  • Casual contact
  • Sharing food or utensils
  • Toilet seats
  • Hugging
  • Mosquitoes
  • Intact-skin contact

Undetectable Equals Untransmittable

Sustained HIV RNA <200 Copies/mL = No Sexual HIV Transmission

A person taking ART who achieves and maintains viral suppression does not sexually transmit HIV.

Important U=U Nuances

  • Suppression must first be achieved and maintained.
  • Adherence and viral-load monitoring remain essential.
  • U=U refers specifically to sexual transmission.
  • Condoms may still prevent other sexually transmitted infections and pregnancy.
  • Partners may use PrEP until durable suppression is established or whenever additional protection is desired.

Clinical Stages of HIV

Acute HIV Infection

  • Very high HIV RNA
  • Antigen may be detectable before antibody
  • High infectiousness
  • Possible mononucleosis-like syndrome

Chronic HIV Infection

  • May remain asymptomatic for years
  • Persistent viral replication without treatment
  • Gradual CD4 decline
  • Chronic lymphadenopathy may occur

Advanced HIV or AIDS

  • CD4 count below 200 cells/mm³
  • Or an AIDS-defining condition regardless of CD4 count
  • High risk of opportunistic infection and malignancy

CD4 Count as a Risk Map

CD4 Range Important Clinical Risks
>500 Many routine infections occur similarly to the general population
200–500 Bacterial pneumonia, tuberculosis, zoster, oral candidiasis, HPV disease
<200 Pneumocystis pneumonia
<100 Toxoplasma encephalitis, cryptococcosis, chronic protozoal diarrhea
<50 Disseminated MAC, CMV retinitis, primary CNS lymphoma
CD4 thresholds guide probability and prophylaxis but are not absolute. Opportunistic infections may occasionally occur above the typical range.

Acute HIV Infection

When to Suspect It

  • Recent high-risk exposure
  • Fever
  • Diffuse maculopapular rash
  • Pharyngitis
  • Lymphadenopathy
  • Myalgia
  • Headache or aseptic meningitis
  • Oral or genital ulcers
  • Diarrhea
  • Cytopenias or transaminitis

Major Diagnostic Trap

A screening antigen/antibody test may be negative or indeterminate during very early infection. Order HIV-1 RNA when acute HIV is clinically possible.

Laboratory Pattern

  • High or rapidly rising HIV RNA
  • Reactive p24 antigen before full antibody response
  • Negative or indeterminate differentiation assay
  • Temporary reduction in CD4 count

Treatment

Start ART promptly after diagnosis. Do not wait for complete seroconversion.

Additional Evaluation

  • Baseline genotype
  • Hepatitis B and C testing
  • STI testing
  • Pregnancy testing when relevant
  • Review prior oral or injectable PrEP exposure

HIV Diagnostic Testing Algorithm

Step 1

Laboratory HIV-1/HIV-2 Antigen–Antibody Combination Assay

Step 2

If reactive, perform an HIV-1/HIV-2 antibody differentiation immunoassay.

Step 3

If the differentiation assay is negative or indeterminate, perform HIV-1 nucleic-acid testing.

Screen Differentiation HIV-1 RNA Interpretation
Reactive HIV-1 positive Usually not required for confirmation HIV-1 diagnosed
Reactive HIV-2 positive HIV-2-specific evaluation HIV-2 diagnosed or suspected
Reactive Negative or indeterminate Positive Acute HIV-1 infection
Reactive Negative or indeterminate Negative Likely false-positive screen; repeat if recent exposure remains possible

Rapid and Home Tests

Most home tests are antibody based and have longer window periods than laboratory antigen–antibody testing. A reactive result requires confirmatory laboratory testing.

When to Order HIV RNA Despite a Negative Screen

  • Acute retroviral syndrome
  • Very recent high-risk exposure
  • Recent PrEP or PEP use
  • Unexplained aseptic meningitis
  • New STI with compatible systemic symptoms
  • Discordant serologic results

Discordant and Difficult HIV Tests

False-Positive Screening Tests

Possible settings include:

  • Pregnancy
  • Autoimmune disease
  • Recent vaccination or viral infection
  • Malignancy
  • Laboratory cross-reactivity

PrEP or PEP Exposure

Antiretroviral exposure can suppress viral replication and delay antibody development, producing ambiguous results.

Approach

  • Obtain laboratory antigen–antibody testing.
  • Obtain HIV-1 RNA.
  • Repeat testing when needed.
  • Review adherence and timing of PrEP or PEP.
  • Obtain expert input before starting an incomplete regimen.

HIV-2

Consider HIV-2 with:

  • West African epidemiology
  • HIV-2 differentiation result
  • Low or undetectable HIV-1 RNA despite convincing serology
  • Unexpected treatment failure with an NNRTI-based regimen
HIV-2 is intrinsically resistant to NNRTIs and requires an HIV-2-active ART regimen.

AIDS Definition

AIDS is diagnosed by either:

CD4 Count <200 Cells/mm³ or CD4 Percentage <14%

or:

An AIDS-Defining Clinical Condition

Selected AIDS-Defining Conditions

  • Pneumocystis pneumonia
  • Esophageal candidiasis
  • Cryptococcosis outside the lungs
  • Toxoplasmosis of the brain
  • CMV retinitis or selected end-organ disease
  • Disseminated MAC
  • Extrapulmonary tuberculosis
  • Recurrent bacterial pneumonia
  • Recurrent Salmonella septicemia
  • Chronic cryptosporidiosis or cystoisosporiasis
  • Progressive multifocal leukoencephalopathy
  • Kaposi sarcoma
  • Invasive cervical cancer
  • Selected aggressive lymphomas
Once a person has met an AIDS definition, that historical diagnosis remains clinically relevant even if the CD4 count later recovers.

Initial Evaluation After HIV Diagnosis

History

  • Previous HIV tests and estimated acquisition timing
  • Previous ART, PrEP, or PEP exposure
  • Prior resistance results
  • Opportunistic infections
  • Vaccination history
  • Sexual history and pregnancy potential
  • Injection-drug use
  • TB exposure and travel
  • Medication access and adherence barriers
  • Mental health, housing, transportation, and food security
  • Cardiovascular, renal, hepatic, neurologic, and bone disease

Physical Examination

  • Weight and nutritional status
  • Oral cavity
  • Lymph nodes
  • Skin and nails
  • Funduscopic and visual symptoms
  • Pulmonary examination
  • Neurologic examination
  • Anogenital examination when indicated

Do Not Delay ART for Every Pending Test

Rapid ART can usually begin before the genotype, CD4 count, and all screening results return, provided the initial regimen is selected appropriately and active CNS opportunistic infection has been considered.

Baseline Laboratory Evaluation

HIV-Specific Tests

  • Quantitative HIV-1 RNA
  • CD4 count and percentage
  • Genotypic resistance testing
  • Integrase resistance testing when indicated

General Laboratory Tests

  • CBC
  • Electrolytes and renal function
  • Liver tests
  • Urinalysis
  • Fasting or nonfasting lipid profile
  • Glucose or HbA1c
  • Pregnancy testing when relevant

Coinfection Screening

  • Hepatitis A immunity
  • Hepatitis B surface antigen, surface antibody, and core antibody
  • Hepatitis C antibody with RNA confirmation when positive
  • TB testing with IGRA or tuberculin skin testing
  • Syphilis
  • Gonorrhea and chlamydia at all exposed sites

Additional Baseline Tests

  • Toxoplasma IgG
  • G6PD level before dapsone or primaquine
  • HLA-B*5701 before abacavir
  • Cervical and anal cancer screening according to current guidance
  • Cryptococcal antigen in selected patients with advanced immune suppression
  • Strongyloides testing when epidemiologically relevant and steroids may be used

Resistance Testing

Obtain a genotype before treatment, but do not necessarily delay rapid ART while awaiting the result.

Integrase Genotype

Particularly consider integrase resistance testing with:

  • Prior long-acting cabotegravir exposure
  • HIV acquisition while using integrase-based PrEP
  • Suspected transmitted integrase resistance
  • Failure of an integrase-containing regimen

Evaluation of Advanced HIV

Symptoms Requiring Immediate Investigation

  • Headache or confusion
  • Visual change or floaters
  • Dyspnea or hypoxemia
  • Fever and weight loss
  • Odynophagia
  • Persistent diarrhea
  • Focal neurologic deficit
  • Severe cytopenias
  • Lymphadenopathy or organomegaly

Practical CD4-Directed Screening

CD4 Count Important Immediate Considerations
<200 PCP symptoms and prophylaxis eligibility
<100 Toxoplasma IgG, cryptococcal symptoms and antigen screening, chronic diarrhea
<50 CMV visual symptoms, disseminated MAC, CNS lymphoma

Before Rapid ART in Advanced HIV

Actively assess for meningitis before immediate outpatient ART when the patient has:

  • Persistent headache
  • Neck stiffness
  • Visual symptoms
  • Confusion
  • Cranial neuropathy
  • Markedly elevated serum cryptococcal antigen
Unrecognized cryptococcal or tuberculous meningitis is one of the major situations in which immediate ART can cause dangerous CNS IRIS.

Core ART Principles

Use a Fully Suppressive Combination

Most initial regimens contain:

Second-Generation Integrase Inhibitor + Two Active NRTIs

ART Should Be:

  • Potent
  • Durable
  • Simple
  • Tolerable
  • Compatible with comorbidities
  • Accessible after discharge
  • Selected with resistance and interactions in mind

Never Use Incomplete Therapy

Do not prescribe one or two drugs from a standard three-drug regimen because the complete regimen is unavailable. Partial ART rapidly selects resistance.

Do Not Routinely Interrupt ART

Interrupting therapy can cause:

  • Rapid viral rebound
  • CD4 decline
  • Clinical progression
  • Transmission risk
  • Resistance when drugs have mismatched half-lives

Rapid or Same-Day ART

Who Should Start Rapidly?

  • Newly diagnosed patients
  • Acute HIV infection
  • Pregnancy
  • Advanced HIV
  • Patients hospitalized with most opportunistic infections
  • Patients reengaging after treatment interruption

Benefits

  • Earlier viral suppression
  • Improved linkage to care
  • Reduced loss to follow-up
  • Reduced morbidity and transmission

Reasonable Exceptions

  • Cryptococcal meningitis
  • Tuberculous meningitis
  • Selected severe CNS opportunistic infections
  • Unresolved concern for a regimen-compromising resistance pattern after injectable PrEP failure

Rapid ART Does Not Mean Careless ART

Before prescribing, assess:

  • Current medications
  • Renal and hepatic function
  • Hepatitis B status
  • Pregnancy
  • Previous ART or PrEP exposure
  • Ability to obtain the same regimen after discharge

Preferred Initial ART Regimens

For Most Adults

  • Bictegravir/tenofovir alafenamide/emtricitabine
  • Dolutegravir plus tenofovir alafenamide/emtricitabine
  • Dolutegravir plus tenofovir disoproxil fumarate/emtricitabine
  • Dolutegravir plus tenofovir disoproxil fumarate/lamivudine

Selected Patients

  • Dolutegravir/lamivudine

Why Integrase-Based Regimens Are Preferred

  • High potency
  • Rapid viral-load decline
  • High resistance barrier with bictegravir or dolutegravir
  • Generally favorable tolerability
  • Fewer interactions than boosted protease-inhibitor regimens

Initial Regimen Is Not One-Size-Fits-All

Alternative regimens may be appropriate when:

  • Important resistance exists
  • Renal or bone disease limits tenofovir use
  • Rifamycin treatment is required
  • Pregnancy-specific considerations apply
  • Drug interactions prevent a preferred option
  • Previous long-acting PrEP exposure creates integrase-resistance concern

Choosing the Right Regimen

Hepatitis B Coinfection

Use Two Drugs Active Against HBV

The ART backbone should generally include:

  • TAF or TDF
  • Plus emtricitabine or lamivudine
Do not use lamivudine or emtricitabine as the only HBV-active agent. HBV resistance develops rapidly.

Renal Disease

  • Review eGFR and proteinuria.
  • TDF can cause proximal tubular and bone toxicity.
  • TAF usually has less renal and bone toxicity but may have less favorable lipid effects.
  • Individual fixed-dose combinations have renal-function limitations.

Cardiovascular Risk

  • Review metabolic effects and drug interactions.
  • Avoid unnecessary boosted regimens when simpler options are effective.
  • Consider the debated cardiovascular signal associated with abacavir.

Pregnancy Potential

Use current perinatal guidance because preferred medications and available pregnancy-safety data evolve.

Weight and Metabolism

Weight gain may occur after ART initiation, particularly with some integrase inhibitors and TAF-containing regimens. The clinical significance varies, and effective ART should not be withheld solely because of this possibility.

Dolutegravir/Lamivudine Two-Drug ART

Potential Advantages

  • One tablet daily
  • Less cumulative NRTI exposure
  • High efficacy in appropriately selected patients

Do Not Use as Initial Therapy When:

  • HIV RNA exceeds 500,000 copies/mL
  • Active hepatitis B infection is present
  • HBV testing is not yet available during rapid initiation
  • Baseline resistance results are unavailable and transmitted resistance is a concern
  • Lamivudine or integrase resistance is known
  • The clinical situation requires a broader empiric resistance barrier

Not a Universal Rapid-Start Regimen

A three-drug integrase-based regimen is usually safer when rapid ART begins before genotype and hepatitis B results return.

Important ART Drug Interactions

Polyvalent Cations

Aluminum, magnesium, calcium, and iron can bind integrase inhibitors and reduce absorption.

  • Separate antacids according to the specific drug instructions.
  • Some calcium or iron products can be taken with integrase inhibitors when accompanied by food.

Rifamycins

  • Rifampin strongly induces drug metabolism.
  • Bictegravir should not be combined with rifampin.
  • Dolutegravir often requires dose adjustment with rifampin.
  • Rifabutin may be substituted in selected cases but also requires interaction review.

Acid Suppression

  • Proton-pump inhibitors are incompatible with oral rilpivirine.
  • Atazanavir absorption is reduced by acid suppression.

Boosted Regimens

Ritonavir and cobicistat inhibit CYP3A and interact with:

  • Statins
  • Anticoagulants
  • Anticonvulsants
  • Antiarrhythmics
  • Transplant medications
  • Inhaled or injected corticosteroids
Fluticasone combined with ritonavir or cobicistat can cause iatrogenic Cushing syndrome and adrenal suppression.

Metformin

Dolutegravir can increase metformin exposure. Review dose and monitor glycemic effects.

Always Check Interactions With:

  • TB treatment
  • Antiseizure medications
  • Azole antifungals
  • Hepatitis C therapy
  • Transplant immunosuppression
  • Chemotherapy

HIV Drug-Resistance Testing

Baseline Genotype

Obtain genotypic resistance testing at entry to care, ideally before ART.

Testing During Virologic Failure

Perform resistance testing while the patient is taking the failing regimen or within approximately four weeks after discontinuation whenever possible.

Why Timing Matters

After selective drug pressure is removed, resistant variants may fall below the assay detection threshold even though archived resistance remains clinically important.

Integrase Resistance

Include integrase genotyping with:

  • Failure of an integrase-containing regimen
  • HIV acquisition during long-acting cabotegravir PrEP
  • Prior documented integrase resistance

Proviral DNA Genotype

May be considered in selected patients with suppressed or low-level HIV RNA when historical resistance information is missing, but it may fail to detect archived mutations.

Never design a salvage regimen by adding a single active drug to a failing regimen.

Viral Load and CD4 Monitoring

Viral Load

  • At baseline
  • Within approximately 2–4 weeks after ART initiation
  • Every 4–8 weeks until suppressed
  • Every 3–4 months after suppression
  • Every 6 months may be reasonable after prolonged stable suppression in selected patients

Expected Response

Most adherent patients taking a fully active regimen achieve suppression within several months.

Viral Blip

A single low-level detectable viral load followed by resuppression may represent a blip and does not automatically indicate failure.

CD4 Monitoring

  • At baseline
  • More frequently while CD4 is low or prophylaxis decisions are changing
  • Less frequently once sustained suppression and immune stability are established

Discordant Response

Some patients achieve viral suppression without robust CD4 recovery. Evaluate:

  • Older age
  • Very low nadir CD4 count
  • Coinfections
  • Malignancy
  • Medications causing cytopenia
Adding medications to a suppressive regimen solely to increase CD4 count has not shown clinical benefit.

Virologic Failure

Definition

Persistent HIV RNA of at least 200 copies/mL despite ART generally represents virologic failure.

Common Causes

  • Missed doses
  • Loss of insurance or medication access
  • Food requirement not followed
  • Polyvalent-cation interaction
  • Rifamycin or anticonvulsant interaction
  • Vomiting or malabsorption
  • Preexisting resistance
  • Incomplete regimen

Evaluation

  1. Confirm the viral load.
  2. Review every dose and refill.
  3. Review food requirements.
  4. Review prescription, nonprescription, and supplement interactions.
  5. Obtain resistance testing.
  6. Review all historical resistance.
  7. Construct a new regimen with at least two fully active agents when possible.

Low-Level Viremia

Persistent HIV RNA below 200 copies/mL requires adherence and interaction review but does not always require immediate regimen change.

Do not stop all ART reflexively during virologic failure. Treatment interruption can worsen immune function and transmission risk.

Primary Opportunistic-Infection Prophylaxis

Infection When to Start Preferred Agent
Pneumocystis pneumonia Not on ART or starting ART with CD4 <200; on ART with CD4 <100 regardless of RNA, or CD4 100–200 with detectable HIV RNA TMP-SMX
Toxoplasma encephalitis Toxoplasma IgG positive and CD4 <100 TMP-SMX DS daily
Disseminated MAC CD4 <50 and not starting effective ART, remaining viremic, or lacking a suppressive regimen Azithromycin or clarithromycin
Histoplasmosis CD4 <150 with specific hyperendemic or intense exposure risk Itraconazole
Coccidioidomycosis Selected newly seropositive patients with CD4 <250 in endemic settings Fluconazole

Most Important Prevention

Fully Suppressive ART

Before Starting Prophylaxis

Exclude active disease when symptoms are present. Prophylaxis doses are inadequate for active opportunistic infection.

Pneumocystis Prophylaxis

Preferred Regimens

  • TMP-SMX one double-strength tablet daily
  • TMP-SMX one single-strength tablet daily

Alternative Regimens

  • TMP-SMX double-strength tablet three times weekly
  • Atovaquone 1,500 mg daily with food
  • Dapsone after confirming normal G6PD activity
  • Aerosolized pentamidine in Toxoplasma-seronegative patients
  • Dapsone plus pyrimethamine and leucovorin when Toxoplasma coverage is needed

Benefits of TMP-SMX

  • Most effective PCP prevention
  • Toxoplasma protection
  • Protection against selected bacterial respiratory infections

Stopping Primary Prophylaxis

Usually discontinue after CD4 has increased to at least 200 cells/mm³ for at least three months in response to ART.

Discontinuation may be considered in selected patients with CD4 100–200 and sustained undetectable HIV RNA for at least 3–6 months.

Restarting

  • CD4 below 100 regardless of HIV RNA
  • CD4 100–200 with detectable HIV RNA

Toxoplasma Prophylaxis

Indication

Toxoplasma IgG Positive + CD4 <100 Cells/mm³

Preferred

  • TMP-SMX one double-strength tablet daily

Alternatives

  • TMP-SMX double strength three times weekly
  • Dapsone plus pyrimethamine plus leucovorin
  • Atovaquone with or without pyrimethamine and leucovorin

What Does Not Prevent Toxoplasmosis?

  • Aerosolized pentamidine
  • Dapsone alone

Stopping

Usually stop when the CD4 count is above 200 for at least three months with sustained viral suppression.

Exposure Prevention

  • Avoid undercooked meat.
  • Wash produce.
  • Use gloves for soil exposure.
  • Avoid handling cat litter when possible or change it daily with gloves and handwashing.

MAC Prophylaxis

Who Needs It?

CD4 <50 + No Immediate Fully Suppressive ART

Primary prophylaxis is not recommended when effective ART is started immediately.

Preferred Options

  • Azithromycin 1,200 mg orally once weekly
  • Clarithromycin 500 mg orally twice daily
  • Azithromycin 600 mg orally twice weekly

Before Starting

Rule out disseminated MAC before prescribing macrolide monotherapy. Treating occult MAC with one drug can select resistance.

When to Stop

Stop primary prophylaxis when the patient begins a fully suppressive ART regimen.

Pneumocystis jirovecii Pneumonia

Clinical Presentation

  • Subacute fever
  • Progressive exertional dyspnea
  • Dry cough
  • Hypoxemia
  • Diffuse bilateral pulmonary opacities

Imaging

  • Chest radiograph may show bilateral interstitial or ground-glass opacities.
  • Radiograph may be normal early.
  • CT often shows diffuse ground-glass change.
  • Focal consolidation, nodules, cysts, and pneumothorax may occur.

Laboratory Findings

  • Elevated LDH is nonspecific.
  • Serum beta-D-glucan is often elevated and is useful for ruling out disease when low.
  • Hypoxemia may be disproportionate to examination.

Diagnosis

  • Induced sputum
  • Bronchoalveolar lavage
  • Microscopy with special stain
  • PCR interpreted with organism burden and clinical context
Pneumocystis cannot be reliably cultured. A highly sensitive PCR may detect colonization, particularly when organism burden is low.

Preferred Treatment

TMP-SMX for 21 Days

Dose by the trimethoprim component:

  • TMP 15–20 mg/kg/day
  • SMX 75–100 mg/kg/day
  • Divided every 6–8 hours

Adjunctive Corticosteroid Indication

PaO₂ <70 mmHg or A–a Gradient ≥35 mmHg

Typical Steroid Schedule

  • Prednisone 40 mg twice daily on days 1–5
  • Prednisone 40 mg daily on days 6–10
  • Prednisone 20 mg daily on days 11–21

IV methylprednisolone may be used at approximately 75% of the prednisone dose.

Timing of Steroids

Begin as soon as possible and ideally within 72 hours of starting PCP therapy.

Alternatives

  • Clindamycin plus primaquine
  • IV pentamidine
  • Atovaquone for mild-to-moderate disease
  • Dapsone plus trimethoprim for mild-to-moderate disease

ART Timing

Start ART Within 2 Weeks of PCP Diagnosis

Treatment Complications

  • TMP-SMX rash
  • Hyperkalemia
  • Creatinine rise from tubular secretion
  • True kidney injury
  • Cytopenias
  • Transient respiratory worsening after therapy begins

Toxoplasma Encephalitis

Risk Profile

  • CD4 usually below 100
  • Toxoplasma IgG positive
  • No effective prophylaxis
  • Not taking suppressive ART

Clinical Features

  • Headache
  • Fever
  • Confusion
  • Focal neurologic deficit
  • Seizure
  • Movement disorder

Imaging

  • Multiple ring-enhancing lesions
  • Surrounding edema
  • Basal ganglia or corticomedullary-junction involvement
  • Solitary lesions can occur

Differential

  • Primary CNS lymphoma
  • Tuberculoma
  • Pyogenic abscess
  • Fungal abscess
  • Neurocysticercosis

Diagnosis

In a typical patient, presumptive treatment is reasonable. Clinical improvement is usually expected within approximately 10–14 days.

Preferred Acute Therapy

  • Pyrimethamine
  • Sulfadiazine
  • Leucovorin

Common Alternative

  • High-dose TMP-SMX

Duration

At Least 6 Weeks of Acute Therapy

Corticosteroids

Use only for clinically significant mass effect or edema.

Antiseizure Medication

Treat patients with seizures. Routine prophylaxis without seizures is not generally required.

When to Biopsy

  • Toxoplasma IgG negative
  • Atypical imaging
  • Single lesion with high lymphoma concern
  • Clinical or radiographic deterioration
  • No improvement after approximately 10–14 days

Secondary Prophylaxis

Continue chronic maintenance after acute treatment until immune reconstitution criteria are met.

Cryptococcal Meningitis

Clinical Features

  • Subacute headache
  • Fever
  • Confusion
  • Visual symptoms
  • Cranial neuropathy
  • Nausea and vomiting
  • Minimal meningeal signs may occur

Diagnosis

  • Serum cryptococcal antigen
  • Lumbar puncture
  • Opening pressure
  • CSF cryptococcal antigen
  • Fungal culture

Induction Therapy

Liposomal Amphotericin B + Flucytosine

Common U.S. Regimen

  • Liposomal amphotericin B 3–4 mg/kg IV daily
  • Flucytosine 25 mg/kg orally every 6 hours
  • Usually at least 2 weeks

Consolidation

  • Fluconazole at high dose, commonly 800 mg daily
  • Usually for at least 8 weeks

Maintenance

  • Fluconazole 200 mg daily
  • Usually for at least one year and until immune recovery criteria are met

Intracranial Pressure

Opening Pressure ≥25 cm CSF or Pressure Symptoms → Therapeutic LP
  • Reduce pressure toward normal or by approximately 50%.
  • Repeat daily or more frequently when symptoms recur.
  • Consider lumbar drain or ventricular diversion when repeated LP is inadequate.

Do Not Use Routinely for Pressure Control

  • Acetazolamide
  • Mannitol
  • Corticosteroids

Toxicity Monitoring

  • Creatinine
  • Potassium
  • Magnesium
  • CBC
  • Liver tests
  • Flucytosine concentration when available or toxicity is suspected

ART Timing

Delay ART Approximately 4–6 Weeks After Starting Antifungal Therapy
Starting ART immediately during cryptococcal meningitis increases the risk of dangerous CNS IRIS and mortality.

Tuberculosis and HIV

Clinical Presentation Changes With CD4 Count

At higher CD4 counts, pulmonary TB often resembles disease in people without HIV. With advanced immune suppression:

  • Cavitation may be absent.
  • Lower-lobe or diffuse infiltrates may occur.
  • Chest radiograph may be normal.
  • Extrapulmonary and disseminated disease become more common.

Diagnosis

  • Airborne isolation when pulmonary or laryngeal TB is possible
  • Sputum AFB smears
  • Mycobacterial cultures
  • Nucleic-acid amplification testing
  • Drug-resistance testing
  • Sampling of extrapulmonary sites

Treatment

Drug-susceptible disease usually begins with:

  • Rifamycin
  • Isoniazid
  • Pyrazinamide
  • Ethambutol
  • Pyridoxine with isoniazid

ART Timing for Non-CNS TB

CD4 Count ART Timing
<50 Within approximately 2 weeks of starting TB treatment
≥50 Within approximately 2–8 weeks

TB Meningitis

Start ART after TB meningitis is clinically controlled and after at least approximately two weeks of anti-TB therapy, with individualized specialist guidance.

Rifamycin Interactions

  • Rifampin reduces many ARV concentrations.
  • Dolutegravir may require twice-daily dosing.
  • Bictegravir is not used with rifampin.
  • Protease inhibitors generally require rifabutin-based planning.

TB-IRIS

Early ART increases IRIS risk but reduces mortality, particularly with CD4 below 50.

Disseminated Mycobacterium avium Complex

Risk

  • CD4 usually below 50
  • Not taking effective ART
  • No MAC prophylaxis when indicated

Clinical Features

  • Persistent fever
  • Night sweats
  • Weight loss
  • Diarrhea or abdominal pain
  • Lymphadenopathy
  • Hepatosplenomegaly
  • Anemia
  • Elevated alkaline phosphatase

Diagnosis

  • Mycobacterial blood cultures
  • Bone-marrow culture in selected cytopenic patients
  • Lymph-node or tissue culture
  • Species identification and macrolide susceptibility

Treatment

Macrolide + Ethambutol
  • Azithromycin or clarithromycin
  • Plus ethambutol
  • Consider rifabutin for severe disease, high burden, lack of effective ART, or resistance concern

Duration

  • At least 12 months
  • No symptoms or signs of active MAC
  • CD4 above 100 for at least six months on effective ART

ART Timing

Start ART as Soon as Possible, Preferably With MAC Therapy

MAC IRIS

May present with fever and focal lymphadenitis after immune recovery, often without positive blood cultures.

Cytomegalovirus End-Organ Disease

Risk

  • CD4 usually below 50
  • Not taking suppressive ART

CMV Retinitis

Symptoms

  • Floaters
  • Flashes
  • Blurred vision
  • Visual-field defect
  • Loss of vision
New visual symptoms in a patient with CD4 below 50 require urgent dilated ophthalmologic examination.

Treatment

  • Oral valganciclovir for many patients
  • IV ganciclovir when absorption is unreliable or disease is severe

Common Induction

  • Valganciclovir 900 mg orally twice daily for 14–21 days

Maintenance

  • Valganciclovir 900 mg daily
  • Continue until immune recovery and ophthalmologic criteria are met

CMV Esophagitis or Colitis

  • Odynophagia
  • Abdominal pain
  • Diarrhea
  • GI bleeding
  • Diagnosis generally requires endoscopy and tissue histopathology

Blood CMV PCR

CMV DNA in blood supports viral replication but does not independently establish tissue-invasive disease.

ART Timing

  • Usually begin within approximately 1–2 weeks after starting anti-CMV therapy.

Toxicity

  • Neutropenia
  • Anemia
  • Thrombocytopenia
  • Renal dose adjustment

Candidiasis

Oropharyngeal Candidiasis

  • White plaques that can be removed
  • Erythematous mucosa
  • Altered taste
  • Oral discomfort

Treatment

  • Fluconazole for moderate or extensive disease
  • Topical agents for selected mild disease
  • Usually 7–14 days

Esophageal Candidiasis

Clinical Features

  • Odynophagia
  • Dysphagia
  • Retrosternal discomfort
  • Oral thrush may be absent

Treatment

Systemic Fluconazole for 14–21 Days

A clinical treatment trial is reasonable in a typical presentation. Perform endoscopy when:

  • Symptoms fail to improve within several days
  • CMV or HSV esophagitis is possible
  • Diagnosis is uncertain

Fluconazole-Refractory Disease

  • Voriconazole
  • Posaconazole
  • Echinocandin
  • Amphotericin formulation

Candida in Respiratory Secretions

Candida in sputum or BAL usually represents colonization and does not diagnose Candida pneumonia.

HSV and VZV

HSV

HIV may cause more frequent, severe, prolonged, or atypical HSV disease.

Clinical Syndromes

  • Orolabial ulcers
  • Genital or perianal ulcers
  • Esophagitis
  • Proctitis
  • Encephalitis
  • Disseminated disease

Treatment

  • Acyclovir
  • Valacyclovir
  • Famciclovir
  • IV acyclovir for severe or disseminated disease

Acyclovir Resistance

Suspect resistance when lesions do not improve despite adequate therapy. Foscarnet is the usual treatment for confirmed acyclovir-resistant HSV.

Varicella-Zoster Virus

  • Localized dermatomal zoster
  • Multidermatomal disease
  • Disseminated cutaneous disease
  • Retinitis
  • Encephalitis or vasculopathy

Treatment

  • Oral valacyclovir or famciclovir for uncomplicated localized zoster
  • IV acyclovir for extensive, disseminated, visceral, ophthalmic, or neurologic disease

Isolation

Use airborne and contact precautions for disseminated zoster or localized zoster in a severely immunocompromised patient until dissemination is excluded and lesions are crusted.

Bacterial Infections in HIV

Community-Acquired Pneumonia

  • Pneumococcus remains important.
  • Risk rises with smoking, viremia, and low CD4 count.
  • Broaden empiric therapy only when standard clinical risk factors justify it.

Recurrent Pneumonia

Recurrent bacterial pneumonia is an AIDS-defining condition and should prompt evaluation for:

  • Uncontrolled HIV
  • Bronchiectasis
  • Aspiration
  • Smoking
  • Humoral immune deficiency

Salmonella

  • Higher risk of bacteremia and recurrence
  • Obtain blood cultures with systemic illness
  • Use susceptibility-directed therapy

Bartonella

May cause bacillary angiomatosis, peliosis hepatis, endocarditis, or prolonged fever.

Clues

  • Vascular skin lesions
  • Cat exposure
  • Hepatic lesions
  • Culture-negative endocarditis

Syphilis

  • May occur at any CD4 count.
  • Neurologic, ocular, or otic symptoms require syndrome-specific evaluation.
  • ART does not replace appropriate penicillin therapy.

Chronic Diarrhea in Advanced HIV

Important Infectious Causes

  • Cryptosporidium
  • Cystoisospora belli
  • Microsporidia
  • CMV
  • Disseminated MAC
  • Giardia
  • Bacterial enteric infection
  • Clostridioides difficile

Diagnostic Evaluation

  • Medication and laxative review
  • Stool bacterial testing
  • C. difficile testing when appropriate
  • Ova and parasite or targeted molecular testing
  • Modified acid-fast staining when needed
  • Endoscopy with biopsy when CMV, malignancy, or unexplained disease is possible

Cryptosporidiosis

  • Profuse watery diarrhea
  • Volume and electrolyte loss
  • Biliary disease may occur
  • Effective ART and immune restoration are central
  • Nitazoxanide may be considered, but benefit is limited without immune recovery

Cystoisosporiasis

TMP-SMX Is Preferred Treatment

Microsporidiosis

  • ART and immune recovery are essential.
  • Albendazole is active against selected species.
  • Species identification may affect therapy.

Disseminated Histoplasmosis

Epidemiology

  • Ohio and Mississippi River valleys
  • Soil enriched with bird or bat droppings

Clinical Features

  • Fever
  • Weight loss
  • Respiratory symptoms
  • Hepatosplenomegaly
  • Lymphadenopathy
  • GI symptoms
  • Adrenal or CNS disease
  • Cytopenias
  • Very elevated ferritin or alkaline phosphatase

Diagnosis

  • Urine Histoplasma antigen
  • Serum antigen
  • Blood fungal cultures
  • Bone-marrow or tissue biopsy
  • Histopathology

Severe Disseminated Disease

Liposomal Amphotericin B Followed by Itraconazole

Mild-to-Moderate Disease

  • Itraconazole

Monitoring

  • Itraconazole concentration

  • Drug interactions

Coccidioidomycosis

Epidemiology

  • Arizona
  • California’s Central Valley
  • Other arid regions of the southwestern United States
  • Soil and dust exposure

Clinical Syndromes

  • Pneumonia
  • Diffuse pulmonary disease
  • Skin and bone dissemination
  • Meningitis

Treatment

  • Fluconazole or itraconazole for many nonmeningeal syndromes
  • Liposomal amphotericin B for severe disease
  • High-dose fluconazole for meningitis

Coccidioidal Meningitis

Coccidioidal meningitis usually requires lifelong azole therapy because relapse is common after treatment discontinuation.

ART

Begin effective ART with attention to azole interactions and IRIS.

Progressive Multifocal Leukoencephalopathy

Cause

Reactivation of JC virus produces lytic infection of oligodendrocytes and progressive demyelination.

Clinical Features

  • Progressive weakness
  • Ataxia
  • Visual-field deficits
  • Aphasia
  • Cognitive change
  • Symptoms evolve over days to weeks

MRI

  • Multifocal asymmetric white-matter lesions
  • Little mass effect
  • Little or no enhancement in classic disease
  • Enhancement may appear during IRIS

Diagnosis

  • Compatible clinical and MRI syndrome
  • CSF JC-virus PCR
  • Brain biopsy when uncertainty remains

Rapid Initiation or Optimization of Effective ART

No antiviral medication has proven reliable efficacy against JC virus.

PML-IRIS

May cause new enhancement, edema, mass effect, and clinical worsening after ART. Corticosteroids may be used for severe inflammatory mass effect.

HIV-Associated Malignancies

AIDS-Defining Cancers

  • Kaposi sarcoma
  • Selected non-Hodgkin lymphomas
  • Invasive cervical cancer

Other Increased-Risk Malignancies

  • Anal cancer
  • Hodgkin lymphoma
  • Hepatocellular carcinoma
  • Lung cancer
  • HPV-associated head and neck cancer

Kaposi Sarcoma

  • Associated with HHV-8
  • Violaceous skin or oral lesions
  • Pulmonary or GI disease may occur
  • ART is essential
  • Systemic chemotherapy is required for selected visceral or progressive disease

Primary CNS Lymphoma

  • Often associated with EBV in advanced HIV
  • Enhancing cerebral lesions
  • May mimic toxoplasmosis
  • Diagnosis often requires biopsy

Screening

  • Cervical screening
  • Anal cancer screening according to age, risk, and current guidance
  • Routine age-appropriate cancer screening
  • Smoking cessation
  • HBV and HCV treatment

Immune Reconstitution Inflammatory Syndrome

Definition

IRIS is inflammatory clinical worsening after ART-associated immune recovery despite improving HIV control.

Types

Paradoxical IRIS

A known and treated infection worsens after ART begins.

Unmasking IRIS

A previously unrecognized infection becomes clinically apparent after immune recovery.

Risk Factors

  • Very low baseline CD4 count
  • High HIV RNA
  • High pathogen burden
  • Short interval between OI treatment and ART
  • Rapid virologic response

Common Causes

  • Tuberculosis
  • Cryptococcus
  • MAC
  • CMV
  • VZV
  • PML
  • Kaposi sarcoma

Diagnosis of Exclusion

Before diagnosing IRIS, evaluate for:

  • Untreated infection
  • Drug resistance
  • Inadequate adherence
  • Drug toxicity
  • New opportunistic infection
  • Malignancy

Management

  • Continue ART in most cases.
  • Continue effective OI treatment.
  • Drain abscesses or effusions when needed.
  • Use NSAIDs for mild inflammation.
  • Use corticosteroids for selected severe IRIS.

When ART Interruption May Be Considered

Only in exceptional life-threatening CNS or airway-compromising IRIS after multidisciplinary expert assessment.

Clinical worsening after ART is not automatically IRIS. Treatment failure and new infection must be actively excluded.

Immunizations in Adults With HIV

Generally Recommended

  • Annual influenza vaccine
  • Current COVID-19 vaccination
  • Pneumococcal vaccination
  • Hepatitis A when nonimmune and indicated
  • Hepatitis B when nonimmune
  • HPV vaccination according to age and shared decision-making guidance
  • Meningococcal ACWY primary series and boosters
  • Tdap followed by Td or Tdap boosters
  • Recombinant zoster vaccine beginning at age 19

Pneumococcal Options

Current schedules may use:

  • A single higher-valency conjugate vaccine such as PCV20 or PCV21
  • Or PCV15 followed by PPSV23 when indicated

Prior vaccine history determines the exact schedule.

Hepatitis B

  • Confirm post-vaccine surface antibody in people with HIV.
  • Revaccinate nonresponders using an appropriate regimen.
  • Response is better when HIV is suppressed and CD4 count is higher, but vaccination should not always be deferred.

Live Vaccines

Live vaccines such as MMR or varicella may be used only in appropriately selected patients with adequate immune function, generally CD4 at least 200, and no other contraindication.

Do not administer live varicella or MMR vaccine to a patient with severe immune suppression.

Sexual Health

HIV and Pregnancy

Principles

  • Start or continue effective ART as soon as possible.
  • Use the current perinatal guideline for regimen selection.
  • Monitor HIV RNA more frequently.
  • Provide HBV, HCV, STI, and resistance assessment.
  • Coordinate obstetric and HIV specialty care.

Transmission Prevention

  • Maternal viral suppression
  • Appropriate intrapartum management
  • Infant antiretroviral prophylaxis
  • Infant virologic testing
  • Individualized infant-feeding counseling using current U.S. guidance

Opportunistic Infections

Life-threatening OIs require effective maternal treatment. Pregnancy-specific toxicity and fetal considerations should modify—but not prevent—necessary therapy.

Hospitalized Patient With Newly Diagnosed HIV

First-Day Workflow

  1. Confirm the HIV diagnosis.
  2. Obtain HIV RNA and CD4 count.
  3. Send genotype and baseline laboratory testing.
  4. Evaluate symptoms for opportunistic disease.
  5. Begin OI therapy immediately when indicated.
  6. Determine ART timing.
  7. Start prophylaxis when thresholds are met.
  8. Arrange HIV-specialist follow-up before discharge.

Medication Reconciliation

  • Verify the exact outpatient ART regimen.
  • Confirm the last dose and pharmacy fill history.
  • Avoid accidental partial ART.
  • Check enteral-tube compatibility.
  • Check interactions with antacids, rifamycins, anticonvulsants, and azoles.

Do Not Hold ART Solely Because the Patient Is NPO

Determine whether:

  • Tablets can be crushed
  • A liquid formulation exists
  • Temporary alternative therapy is possible
  • The interruption is truly unavoidable

Discharge Planning

  • Medication supply in hand
  • Insurance or assistance-program enrollment
  • Follow-up appointment scheduled
  • OI treatment and prophylaxis duration documented
  • Laboratory monitoring arranged
  • Public-health and partner services offered appropriately
Starting ART in the hospital without ensuring outpatient access can create an avoidable interruption and resistance risk.

Daily HIV and OI Checklist

  • Is the HIV diagnosis confirmed?
  • What are the HIV RNA and CD4 count?
  • Is this acute, chronic, or advanced HIV?
  • Is an opportunistic infection present?
  • Were appropriate microbiologic specimens obtained?
  • Is OI therapy dosed correctly?
  • Is adjunctive corticosteroid therapy indicated?
  • When should ART start?
  • Is the ART regimen fully active?
  • Are hepatitis B and resistance results available?
  • Are there important rifamycin, azole, anticonvulsant, or cation interactions?
  • Does the patient need PCP, Toxoplasma, or MAC prophylaxis?
  • Is secondary prophylaxis required?
  • Are renal function, cytopenias, electrolytes, and liver tests being monitored?
  • Is clinical worsening due to IRIS, treatment failure, or a new infection?
  • Is medication access confirmed after discharge?
  • Is HIV follow-up scheduled?

How to Present on ID Rounds

  • HIV diagnosis date and treatment history
  • Current HIV RNA and CD4 count
  • Previous resistance
  • Current ART and adherence
  • OI syndrome and microbiology
  • OI treatment start date
  • ART timing decision
  • Prophylaxis plan
  • Drug interactions
  • Access and follow-up barriers
Avoid saying only, “The patient has AIDS and is not on medications.” State the CD4 count, viral load, OI syndrome, prior treatment, resistance risk, ART plan, and prophylaxis requirements.

Common Clinical Scenarios

Case 1: Reactive Screen With Negative Differentiation Assay

A patient with fever, rash, and recent sexual exposure has a reactive antigen–antibody screen but negative differentiation assay.

Order HIV-1 RNA immediately. This may represent acute HIV infection.

Case 2: New HIV With CD4 38 and Headache

A newly diagnosed patient has CD4 38, headache, blurred vision, and positive serum cryptococcal antigen.

Perform LP with opening pressure before rapid ART. Treat cryptococcal meningitis and delay ART approximately 4–6 weeks.

Case 3: Dyspnea With Normal Chest Radiograph

A patient with CD4 72 has progressive dyspnea, dry cough, and exertional desaturation despite a nearly normal radiograph.

PCP remains likely. Obtain CT, beta-D-glucan, and induced sputum or BAL while beginning appropriate treatment when suspicion is high.

Case 4: PCP With PaO₂ 62

A patient has confirmed PCP and an arterial PaO₂ of 62 mmHg.

Treat with TMP-SMX for 21 days and add adjunctive corticosteroids promptly.

Case 5: Multiple Ring-Enhancing Lesions

A patient with CD4 28 and positive Toxoplasma IgG has multiple basal-ganglia ring-enhancing lesions.

Begin empiric toxoplasmosis treatment. Expect improvement within approximately 10–14 days; otherwise pursue biopsy.

Case 6: CD4 42 Starting ART Today

An asymptomatic patient with CD4 42 begins a fully suppressive integrase-based regimen immediately.

Routine MAC prophylaxis is not required when effective ART starts immediately. PCP prophylaxis is required, and Toxoplasma prophylaxis depends on IgG status.

Case 7: TB With CD4 24

A patient has pulmonary TB and CD4 24 without CNS involvement.

Begin TB treatment and initiate compatible ART within approximately two weeks despite increased IRIS risk.

Case 8: New Floaters With CD4 18

A patient reports new floaters and blurred peripheral vision.

Arrange same-day dilated ophthalmologic examination for possible CMV retinitis.

Case 9: Rising Viral Load on Bictegravir

HIV RNA rises from undetectable to 8,000 copies/mL. The patient recently began daily magnesium and calcium supplements.

Review integrase–cation timing, adherence, and refill history, then repeat viral load and obtain resistance testing if failure persists.

Case 10: Worsening Lymph Nodes After ART

A patient treated for disseminated MAC develops painful enlarging lymph nodes three weeks after ART initiation, while HIV RNA is falling.

Consider MAC IRIS, but first exclude inadequate MAC therapy, resistance, abscess, and another infection.

Case 11: HBV Coinfection and Proposed DTG/3TC

A patient has positive hepatitis B surface antigen and is offered dolutegravir/lamivudine.

Do not use this two-drug regimen for HBV coinfection. Include tenofovir plus emtricitabine or lamivudine.

Case 12: Inpatient ART Not on Formulary

Only two components of the patient’s outpatient three-drug regimen are immediately available.

Do not administer partial ART. Urgently obtain the complete regimen or construct a fully suppressive alternative with HIV pharmacy or specialist input.

Common Pitfalls

  • Ruling out acute HIV because the antibody test is negative
  • Failing to order HIV RNA with a compatible acute syndrome
  • Delaying ART until CD4 results return in an otherwise appropriate rapid-start patient
  • Starting immediate ART without assessing headache or meningitis in advanced HIV
  • Using dolutegravir/lamivudine before hepatitis B and genotype results are available
  • Using lamivudine alone as HBV treatment
  • Forgetting baseline resistance testing
  • Ignoring prior cabotegravir PrEP exposure
  • Giving partial ART during hospitalization
  • Stopping ART because of a single low-level viral blip
  • Changing ART without reviewing adherence and drug interactions
  • Adding one active drug to a failing regimen
  • Forgetting cation interactions with integrase inhibitors
  • Combining rifampin with bictegravir
  • Combining boosted ART with fluticasone without interaction review
  • Failing to start PCP prophylaxis when indicated
  • Using aerosolized pentamidine in a patient who also needs Toxoplasma prevention
  • Starting MAC prophylaxis without ruling out disseminated MAC
  • Giving routine MAC prophylaxis when fully suppressive ART starts immediately
  • Using standard-dose TMP-SMX rather than treatment dosing for PCP
  • Failing to calculate PaO₂ or A–a gradient for PCP steroid eligibility
  • Missing PCP because the chest radiograph is normal
  • Stopping acyclovir or antifungal therapy without confirming diagnosis and immune-recovery criteria
  • Failing to measure opening pressure in cryptococcal meningitis
  • Starting ART immediately in cryptococcal meningitis
  • Waiting too long to start ART during non-CNS TB
  • Calling every post-ART deterioration IRIS
  • Failing to obtain urgent ophthalmology evaluation for visual symptoms at CD4 below 50
  • Using blood CMV PCR alone to diagnose CMV colitis
  • Treating Candida in sputum as Candida pneumonia
  • Discharging without ensuring ART access

Board and ID Consult Pearls

  • ART is recommended for every person with HIV.
  • Start ART immediately or as soon as possible after diagnosis in most patients.
  • A fourth-generation assay detects HIV p24 antigen and HIV-1/2 antibodies.
  • A reactive screen with negative differentiation assay requires HIV-1 RNA testing.
  • Acute HIV may have a negative antibody test.
  • AIDS is defined by CD4 below 200, CD4 percentage below 14%, or an AIDS-defining illness.
  • Sustained HIV RNA below 200 copies/mL prevents sexual HIV transmission.
  • Bictegravir/TAF/FTC and dolutegravir plus two NRTIs are preferred initial regimens for most adults.
  • Do not use DTG/3TC with active hepatitis B.
  • Do not use DTG/3TC for rapid start before HBV and resistance results are available.
  • Integrase inhibitors interact with polyvalent cations.
  • Rifampin is incompatible with bictegravir.
  • Never give partial ART.
  • Persistent HIV RNA at or above 200 copies/mL represents virologic failure.
  • Obtain resistance testing while the patient is taking the failing regimen whenever possible.
  • PCP prophylaxis is centered on CD4 below 200 and current ART/viral-suppression status.
  • Toxoplasma prophylaxis requires positive IgG and CD4 below 100.
  • MAC prophylaxis is not needed when effective ART starts immediately.
  • TMP-SMX is preferred for both PCP treatment and prophylaxis.
  • PCP treatment lasts 21 days.
  • Add corticosteroids for PCP when PaO₂ is below 70 or A–a gradient is at least 35.
  • Start ART within two weeks of PCP diagnosis.
  • Toxoplasmosis commonly causes multiple ring-enhancing basal-ganglia lesions.
  • Failure to improve after 10–14 days of toxoplasmosis therapy should prompt reconsideration or biopsy.
  • Cryptococcal meningitis requires opening-pressure measurement.
  • Serial therapeutic lumbar punctures are essential for cryptococcal intracranial hypertension.
  • Delay ART for approximately 4–6 weeks during cryptococcal meningitis.
  • Start ART within two weeks of TB treatment when CD4 is below 50 and CNS TB is absent.
  • Disseminated MAC requires at least two active agents, including a macrolide and ethambutol.
  • Start ART promptly during disseminated MAC.
  • Visual symptoms with CD4 below 50 require urgent retinal examination.
  • Blood CMV PCR does not prove CMV end-organ disease.
  • Esophageal candidiasis requires systemic fluconazole.
  • PML treatment is effective ART and immune restoration.
  • IRIS is a diagnosis of exclusion.
  • Continue ART during most IRIS episodes.
  • Recombinant zoster vaccine is recommended beginning at age 19 in adults with HIV.
  • Live vaccines generally require adequate immune function.

Bottom Line

HIV should be diagnosed using the antigen–antibody and differentiation-testing algorithm, with HIV RNA added whenever acute infection or discordant serology is possible. After diagnosis, obtain viral load, CD4 count, resistance testing, hepatitis studies, STI screening, TB testing, and a focused evaluation for opportunistic disease.

ART is recommended for every person with HIV and should begin immediately or as soon as possible in most situations. Preferred initial therapy generally uses bictegravir or dolutegravir with an appropriate NRTI backbone. Regimen selection must account for hepatitis B, renal disease, pregnancy, previous PrEP or ART exposure, resistance, access, and drug interactions.

Opportunistic-infection prevention is driven primarily by effective ART and selected CD4 thresholds. TMP-SMX is preferred for PCP prophylaxis and also protects against toxoplasmosis. Routine MAC prophylaxis is not needed when fully suppressive ART begins immediately.

Major OIs require syndrome-specific therapy and careful ART timing. Start ART within two weeks for PCP and advanced non-CNS tuberculosis, promptly during MAC and most other infections, but delay ART approximately 4–6 weeks during cryptococcal meningitis and briefly during TB meningitis. Clinical worsening after ART requires evaluation for IRIS, treatment failure, resistance, drug toxicity, and new infection.

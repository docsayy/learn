Steroids in Critical Care: Practical ICU Guide

Added June 25, 2026 • Critical Care • ~22 min read

Steroids are used constantly in the ICU, but the real challenge is knowing when they clearly help, when they probably help, when they are controversial, and when they should be avoided. This article reviews practical steroid pharmacology and bedside use in COVID, non-COVID viral pneumonia, influenza, community-acquired pneumonia, septic shock, ARDS, status asthmaticus, anaphylaxis, tapering, and steroid-related ICU complications.

Overview

Steroids are one of the most common ICU medications, but they live in the gray zone of medicine. The same drug class can be clearly indicated in one patient, controversial in another, and potentially unnecessary in a third.

The practical question is not simply “do steroids work?” The better question is:

What disease process am I treating, what steroid effect do I want, what dose is appropriate, and how long should I continue it?

Common ICU Steroid Indications

  • COVID pneumonia requiring oxygen
  • Septic shock requiring vasopressors
  • ARDS with significant hypoxemia
  • Status asthmaticus
  • COPD exacerbation
  • Selected severe community-acquired pneumonia
  • Adrenal insufficiency
  • Airway edema or cerebral edema in selected settings

Major Controversial Areas

  • Influenza pneumonia
  • Non-COVID viral pneumonia without bronchospasm
  • Community-acquired pneumonia without shock or ARDS
  • Fludrocortisone in septic shock
  • Prolonged steroid tapers in ARDS or viral disease
  • Steroids after anaphylaxis discharge

Steroid Types and Mechanisms

Steroids differ in their glucocorticoid and mineralocorticoid activity.

Glucocorticoid Effects

  • Anti-inflammatory effect
  • Immunomodulation
  • Reduced cytokine signaling
  • Reduced leukocyte trafficking
  • Gluconeogenesis
  • Hyperglycemia

Mineralocorticoid Effects

  • Sodium retention
  • Water retention
  • Potassium excretion
  • Potential volume expansion
Steroid Main Activity Practical Use
Dexamethasone Mostly glucocorticoid, minimal mineralocorticoid COVID, ARDS, cerebral edema, airway edema
Methylprednisolone Strong glucocorticoid, some mineralocorticoid Asthma, COPD, some lung inflammation protocols
Prednisone Glucocorticoid with some mineralocorticoid Oral COPD/asthma, outpatient inflammatory disease
Hydrocortisone Glucocorticoid and mineralocorticoid Septic shock, adrenal insufficiency
Fludrocortisone Mostly mineralocorticoid Adrenal insufficiency, debated adjunct in septic shock
Dexamethasone has very little mineralocorticoid activity, which is one reason it is attractive in ARDS where fluid retention is undesirable.

Steroid Duration and Dosing Logic

Steroid half-life matters. It affects how often you dose the medication and how long its biologic effect lasts.

Steroid Approximate Duration Practical Implication
Hydrocortisone Short acting Usually dosed multiple times daily, commonly q6h in shock
Prednisone / Methylprednisolone Intermediate acting Often daily to twice daily depending on indication
Dexamethasone Long acting Often once daily; long half-life creates a built-in taper effect

Practical ICU Dosing Patterns

  • Dexamethasone is often reasonable once daily.
  • Methylprednisolone is commonly divided at least twice daily in ICU lung disease.
  • Hydrocortisone for septic shock is commonly given as 50 mg IV q6h.
  • Hydrocortisone infusion is sometimes used to smooth exposure and may help glycemic control, but q6h dosing is more common in many ICUs.
Do not choose the steroid only by tradition. Choose it based on the disease, desired effect, half-life, mineralocorticoid activity, and practical ICU workflow.

Viral Pneumonia: First Decide What You Are Treating

Viral pneumonia is not one disease. Steroid use differs depending on the virus, disease severity, oxygen requirement, bronchospasm, and whether the patient has developed ARDS.

Clues Suggesting Viral Pneumonia

  • Sick contacts
  • Pharyngitis
  • Conjunctivitis
  • Mucous membrane irritation
  • Fever and chills
  • Lymphocyte-predominant pattern
  • Less dense lobar consolidation
  • Tree-in-bud changes on CT in some viral bronchiolitis patterns

Tree-in-Bud Differential

  • Nontuberculous mycobacterial disease
  • Aspiration
  • Viral bronchiolitis
Do not use procalcitonin alone to decide whether a sick ICU patient with suspected bacterial pneumonia should receive antibiotics.

COVID-19 Pneumonia

COVID changed ICU steroid practice because randomized trial data showed benefit in hospitalized patients requiring oxygen.

Who Gets Steroids?

COVID Scenario Steroids?
Positive COVID test but no respiratory symptoms, no oxygen need No
COVID pneumonia requiring supplemental oxygen Yes
COVID ARDS Yes; often dexamethasone-based strategy

Typical Regimen

Dexamethasone 6 mg daily for up to 10 days
For COVID, steroids are used when the patient has clinically significant respiratory disease requiring oxygen, not merely because the swab is positive.

Non-COVID Viral Infections

For non-COVID viral infections, steroid use is more individualized.

When Steroids Make Sense

  • Underlying asthma exacerbation
  • Underlying COPD exacerbation
  • Clear bronchospasm or wheezing
  • Reactive airway physiology triggered by viral infection
  • ARDS physiology from viral pneumonia

Important Nuance

Adults are often trained to think wheezing requires asthma or COPD, but viral infections such as RSV, human metapneumovirus, and parainfluenza can produce significant bronchospasm even in patients without known obstructive lung disease.

In these cases, steroids may be used for the bronchospasm phenotype rather than for the virus itself.

Duration

  • COPD exacerbation: often prednisone 40 mg daily for 5 days.
  • Asthma or severe bronchospasm: duration should be guided by clinical response and ventilator mechanics, not an automatic stop date.
  • Viral bronchospasm without known obstructive disease: consider continuing until symptoms or mechanics improve.

Influenza: The Controversial Zone

Steroids in influenza remain controversial. Older retrospective data and meta-analyses raised concern for harm, but many studies were confounded by severity, used high steroid doses, or were not randomized.

Common Practice Patterns

Clinical Situation Reasonable Approach
Pure influenza pneumonia, no bronchospasm, no shock, no ARDS Many clinicians avoid steroids
Influenza triggering asthma or COPD exacerbation Use steroids for obstructive exacerbation
Severe influenza with ARDS physiology Many clinicians treat ARDS phenotype with steroids
Severe influenza without ARDS Gray zone; practice varies

The COVID Extrapolation Debate

Some clinicians became more comfortable using steroids in severe influenza after COVID data showed benefit in severe viral pneumonia requiring oxygen. Others still prefer to wait for stronger influenza-specific randomized evidence.

Influenza steroid practice is not settled. The key is to be explicit about what you are treating: influenza itself, bronchospasm, septic shock, or ARDS.

Community-Acquired Pneumonia

Steroids in bacterial community-acquired pneumonia have moved back and forth over time. Some clinicians used methylprednisolone frequently in the past, then guidelines became more cautious, and newer trials reopened the discussion.

When Steroids Are Easier to Justify

  • CAP with septic shock requiring vasopressors
  • CAP with ARDS
  • CAP with COPD or asthma exacerbation
  • Severe inflammatory phenotype in selected ICU patients

CAP-Specific Trial Nuance

The CAPE COD trial supported hydrocortisone in severe community-acquired pneumonia, but some clinicians remain cautious because of trial design concerns, patient selection, screened-but-not-enrolled patients, and uncertainty about which subgroup benefits most.

Common Regimen From Severe CAP Practice

Hydrocortisone 200 mg/day, often divided or infused, with duration/taper depending on response
Steroids in severe CAP are more compelling when the patient is in shock, developing ARDS, or has another steroid-responsive indication.

Septic Shock

Steroids are not used to prevent septic shock. They are used when shock is established and vasopressor requirements suggest refractory physiology.

When to Start

A practical ICU threshold is to start hydrocortisone when the patient is requiring a second vasopressor or norepinephrine has reached a meaningful dose.

Trigger Practical Meaning
Starting vasopressin Common time to start hydrocortisone
Norepinephrine around 10–15 mcg/min Common bedside threshold
Norepinephrine around 0.2 mcg/kg/min Weight-based equivalent used by many clinicians
Refractory shock Hydrocortisone reasonable

Typical Regimen

Hydrocortisone 50 mg IV q6h

Expected Benefit

  • Shorter duration of vasopressor requirement
  • Shorter shock duration
  • Possible mortality benefit depending on trial and regimen
  • May reduce ventilator duration in some studies
The most consistent benefit of steroids in septic shock is faster shock reversal. Mortality benefit is more variable across trials.

Major Septic Shock Trials to Know

Trial Steroid Strategy Practical Takeaway
HYPRESS Hydrocortisone before shock Did not prevent progression to septic shock
CORTICUS Hydrocortisone Faster shock reversal, no clear mortality benefit
ADRENAL Hydrocortisone infusion Faster shock reversal, no clear mortality benefit
APROCCHSS Hydrocortisone + fludrocortisone Showed mortality benefit

Tapering in Septic Shock

There is no single universal taper strategy. The practical approach depends on duration of therapy, vasopressor response, and patient frailty.

Common ICU Approach

  • Continue hydrocortisone while the patient remains on vasopressors.
  • After vasopressors are off, continue for about 24 hours in many patients.
  • If steroids were used only 2–3 days, many clinicians stop without taper.
  • If steroids were used 5–7 days or longer, tapering is more reasonable.
  • Avoid spacing hydrocortisone less frequently than q12h if still using it for shock physiology.

Important Bedside Nuance

Some patients appear to come off vasopressors quickly after vasopressin and steroids are started. Others remain pressor-sensitive and worsen when steroids are stopped too early.

The patient’s trajectory matters. A frail ventilated patient recovering from severe septic shock may deserve a slower steroid de-escalation than a patient who rapidly improves after a short course.

The Fludrocortisone Debate

Fludrocortisone is controversial in septic shock. Some trials showing mortality benefit used hydrocortisone plus fludrocortisone, while other hydrocortisone-only trials showed faster shock reversal without mortality benefit.

Arguments For Fludrocortisone

  • APROCCHSS used hydrocortisone plus fludrocortisone and showed mortality benefit.
  • May provide more complete mineralocorticoid support.
  • Some clinicians use it in severe septic shock, especially clear bacterial shock.

Arguments Against Routine Fludrocortisone

  • Hydrocortisone already has mineralocorticoid activity.
  • Fludrocortisone is oral, which may be unreliable in shock or poor gut absorption.
  • COIITSS did not show clear added benefit of fludrocortisone over hydrocortisone alone.
  • ARDS patients may not benefit from extra mineralocorticoid-related fluid retention.
Fludrocortisone is reasonable in some septic shock practices, but many intensivists do not add it routinely if hydrocortisone is already being used.

ARDS

ARDS is one of the more steroid-responsive ICU syndromes when treated early in the inflammatory phase.

Why Dexamethasone Makes Sense

  • Strong glucocorticoid effect
  • Minimal mineralocorticoid effect
  • Less concern for fluid retention than hydrocortisone
  • Long half-life creates a practical taper effect

DEXA-ARDS Regimen

Dexamethasone 20 mg IV daily for 5 days, then 10 mg IV daily for 5 days

When to Consider

  • ARDS with P/F ratio less than 200
  • Early ARDS rather than late fibroproliferative phase
  • Pulmonary ARDS such as pneumonia
  • Non-pulmonary ARDS such as pancreatitis, trauma, or sepsis

Important Timing Nuance

Steroids are most biologically plausible early, when the goal is to blunt inflammatory lung injury and prevent progression from the exudative phase to fibroproliferation.

Late initiation of steroids in established ARDS, especially after day 14, has been associated with worse outcomes in older trial data.

Septic Shock Plus ARDS

If a patient starts with septic shock on hydrocortisone and later develops clear ARDS, many clinicians switch to a dexamethasone ARDS regimen and consider that moment the start of the ARDS steroid course.

ECMO Nuance

For patients already transitioned to VV-ECMO after prolonged severe ARDS, some clinicians stop steroids if the acute inflammatory phase has passed and focus on lung rest, avoiding complications, and allowing time for recovery.

Status Asthmaticus

Status asthmaticus is one of the scariest obstructive lung disease emergencies in critical care. Steroids are essential, but ventilator mechanics and air trapping guide ongoing management.

Clinical Clues

  • Severe wheezing or silent chest
  • Hypoxemia or hypercapnia
  • Respiratory fatigue
  • High airway pressures
  • Air trapping on ventilator waveform
  • Hemodynamic compromise from dynamic hyperinflation

Typical ICU Steroid Practice

Regimen Comment
Methylprednisolone 40 mg IV q6h Common ICU regimen
Methylprednisolone 60 mg IV q6h Used by some clinicians in severe status asthmaticus

Duration

Unlike routine COPD exacerbation, status asthmaticus should not always be stopped automatically after 5 days. Steroid duration should be guided by clinical improvement, wheezing, ventilator mechanics, and resolution of air trapping.

In intubated status asthmaticus, taper decisions should be based on obstructive physiology and ventilator mechanics, not just the calendar.

Ventilator Waveform Pearl

Expiratory flow that fails to return to baseline before the next breath indicates air trapping and dynamic hyperinflation.

Anaphylaxis

Epinephrine is the life-saving treatment for anaphylaxis. Steroids are not the primary therapy.

Initial Treatment Priorities

  • IM epinephrine
  • Airway assessment
  • Oxygen if needed
  • IV fluids for hypotension
  • Antihistamines for cutaneous symptoms
  • Bronchodilators for bronchospasm

Steroid Nuance

Steroids have historically been used to prevent biphasic reactions, but evidence that discharge steroids prevent rebound anaphylaxis is weak. Some guidelines have moved away from routine steroid discharge prescriptions after anaphylaxis.

Do not let steroids delay epinephrine in anaphylaxis.

Practical Takeaway

Scenario Approach
Acute anaphylaxis Epinephrine first; steroids may be adjunctive
Bronchospasm/asthma component Steroids are more reasonable
Discharge after resolved anaphylaxis Routine steroids are increasingly questioned

Downsides of Steroids

Many historic steroid fears came from older trials using very high doses for prolonged periods. Modern ICU steroid regimens are generally lower dose and shorter duration.

Most Common Bedside Concern

Hyperglycemia is the most common practical steroid complication in the ICU.

Other Potential Harms

  • Hyperglycemia
  • Delirium or mood changes
  • Fluid retention depending on steroid choice
  • Myopathy with prolonged exposure
  • Secondary infection risk with prolonged or high-dose courses
  • GI bleeding risk in selected high-risk patients
  • Impaired wound healing with prolonged courses

Nuance

Short 5–7 day courses are less likely to cause the feared complications associated with older high-dose prolonged protocols. The risk rises with higher doses, longer duration, frailty, malnutrition, immunocompromise, and repeated courses.

Glycemic Control

Steroid-induced hyperglycemia is common, especially in septic shock, ARDS, pancreatitis, and patients with diabetes.

When to Consider Insulin Drip

  • Severe hyperglycemia
  • Rapidly changing steroid dose
  • Critically ill patient on vasopressors
  • Pancreatitis
  • DKA or HHS physiology
  • Need for tight titration in unstable ICU patient
Hydrocortisone infusion may theoretically smooth glucose excursions compared with intermittent boluses, but nursing workload and local ICU practice often determine feasibility.

Rapid Decision Framework

Condition Steroid Choice Typical Approach
COVID requiring oxygen Dexamethasone 6 mg daily up to 10 days
Septic shock on significant vasopressors Hydrocortisone 50 mg IV q6h
ARDS P/F <200 Dexamethasone 20 mg daily x5 days, then 10 mg daily x5 days
Status asthmaticus Methylprednisolone 40–60 mg IV q6h, guided by mechanics
COPD exacerbation Prednisone or methylprednisolone Often prednisone 40 mg daily x5 days
Severe CAP Hydrocortisone Consider in severe ICU CAP, especially shock/ARDS phenotype
Influenza Controversial Use if ARDS, shock, asthma/COPD, or selected severe cases depending on practice
Anaphylaxis Adjunct only Epinephrine first; routine discharge steroids questioned

Clinical Pearls

  • Dexamethasone is mostly glucocorticoid with minimal mineralocorticoid effect.
  • Hydrocortisone provides both glucocorticoid and mineralocorticoid activity.
  • Hyperglycemia is the most common practical steroid problem in the ICU.
  • COVID patients need steroids when they require oxygen, not just because the swab is positive.
  • Non-COVID viral infections may still need steroids if they trigger bronchospasm.
  • Influenza steroids remain controversial; be clear whether you are treating influenza, ARDS, shock, or bronchospasm.
  • Severe CAP steroids are an evolving area, especially after CAPE COD.
  • Start septic shock steroids when vasopressor requirements suggest refractory shock.
  • Hydrocortisone 50 mg IV q6h is a common septic shock regimen.
  • Do not use steroids to prevent septic shock in patients who are septic but not in shock.
  • Fludrocortisone in septic shock is debated and not universally used.
  • ARDS steroid timing matters; early inflammatory ARDS is different from late fibroproliferative ARDS.
  • Dexamethasone is attractive in ARDS because it avoids mineralocorticoid fluid retention.
  • Status asthmaticus steroid duration should follow mechanics and air trapping, not an automatic 5-day rule.
  • Do not let steroids delay epinephrine in anaphylaxis.
  • After anaphylaxis, routine discharge steroids are increasingly questioned.
  • If stopping steroids causes vasopressor needs to rise again, reconsider adrenal physiology and shock trajectory.
  • Do not oversimplify steroid decisions: the disease phenotype matters.

Bottom Line

Steroids in critical care are best understood by matching the steroid to the physiology. Use dexamethasone when you want strong glucocorticoid effect with minimal mineralocorticoid activity, such as COVID pneumonia and ARDS. Use hydrocortisone when treating vasopressor-dependent septic shock, where shock reversal is the most consistent benefit. Use methylprednisolone or prednisone for obstructive airway disease, especially asthma, COPD, and status asthmaticus. Influenza, severe CAP, fludrocortisone, and anaphylaxis discharge steroids remain gray zones where practice varies. The most practical bedside complication is hyperglycemia, and tapering should be individualized based on duration, vasopressor trajectory, frailty, and disease response.

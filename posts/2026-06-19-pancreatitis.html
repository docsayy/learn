Acute Pancreatitis: Diagnosis, Management, and Complications

Added June 19, 2026 • Gastroenterology • ~12 min read

A practical approach to acute pancreatitis including diagnosis, common causes, initial management, fluid resuscitation, pain control, nutrition, severity assessment, and complications such as necrosis, pseudocysts, organ failure, and hemorrhagic pancreatitis.

Diagnosis

The diagnosis of acute pancreatitis requires at least two of three diagnostic criteria.

Diagnostic Criteria Description
Typical symptoms Acute epigastric pain radiating to the back
Elevated enzymes Lipase or amylase >3× upper limit of normal
Imaging findings Characteristic CT, MRI, or ultrasound findings
Key point: Diagnosis requires any 2 of the 3 criteria above.

Classic Symptoms

  • Acute severe epigastric pain
  • Pain radiating to the back
  • Nausea and vomiting
  • Pain sometimes relieved by leaning forward

Causes of Pancreatitis

The two most common causes in the United States are gallstones and alcohol.

Resident pearl: Always check triglycerides in unexplained pancreatitis. Triglycerides >1000 mg/dL can cause pancreatitis.

Initial Treatment

Initial management is built on two major pillars: fluid resuscitation and adequate pain control.

Fluid Resuscitation

Acute pancreatitis causes capillary leak and third spacing, which can lead to hypovolemia, hemoconcentration, acute kidney injury, and organ dysfunction.

Preferred fluid: Lactated Ringer’s is generally preferred over normal saline.

Example Fluid Strategy

  • 1 L Lactated Ringer’s bolus initially
  • Then 250 mL/hr for approximately 12 hours
  • Then adjust based on clinical response

Fluid requirements should be individualized based on volume status, renal function, and cardiac function.

Pain Control

Pancreatitis is frequently very painful and often requires opioid therapy.

  • Hydromorphone
  • Morphine
  • Oxycodone
  • Patient-controlled analgesia when appropriate
Meperidine: Historically thought to be preferred because of less sphincter of Oddi spasm, but current evidence does not clearly show superiority over other opioids.

Nutrition & Antibiotics

Nutrition

Patients were historically kept NPO for prolonged periods, but modern management supports early enteral feeding.

  • Advance diet as tolerated
  • Use clear liquids if severe symptoms
  • Advance to low-fat diet or regular diet as tolerated
  • Avoid prolonged bowel rest
Key point: Early enteral feeding is preferred over prolonged NPO status.

Antibiotics

Routine prophylactic antibiotics are not recommended in uncomplicated acute pancreatitis.

  • Do not treat uncomplicated pancreatitis with antibiotics.
  • Use antibiotics only when infection is suspected or confirmed.

Common Admission Findings

  • Elevated BUN
  • Elevated hemoglobin
  • Hypocalcemia
Hypocalcemia in pancreatitis = saponification

Pancreatic enzymes bind calcium within areas of fat necrosis, causing hypocalcemia.

Major Complications

Systemic Complications

  • ARDS
  • Shock
  • Acute kidney injury
  • Multiorgan failure

Chronic Pancreatitis

  • Exocrine insufficiency
  • Malabsorption
  • Diabetes mellitus
Important: Patients with chronic pancreatitis may have normal lipase levels despite severe symptoms.

Splenic Vein Thrombosis

Inflammation around the pancreas can extend to the adjacent splenic vein. Treatment is usually directed toward the underlying pancreatitis rather than routine anticoagulation unless clot extension occurs.

Pancreatic Fluid Collections

Type Timing Setting
Acute Peripancreatic Fluid Collection <4 weeks Interstitial pancreatitis
Acute Necrotic Collection <4 weeks Necrotizing pancreatitis
Pancreatic Pseudocyst >4 weeks Interstitial pancreatitis
Walled-Off Necrosis >4 weeks Necrotizing pancreatitis
Key point: Pseudocysts and walled-off necrosis usually develop after four weeks and have a mature wall.

Management

  • Asymptomatic collections: observation, repeat imaging, supportive care
  • Symptomatic collections: endoscopic ultrasound-guided drainage or percutaneous drainage in selected cases
  • Persistent symptoms or infected necrosis: consider necrosectomy

When to Re-image

Patients who initially improve but then worsen after 5–7 days should undergo repeat CT imaging to evaluate for infected necrosis, walled-off necrosis, or other pancreatic complications.

Hemorrhagic Pancreatitis

Severe necrotizing pancreatitis can cause retroperitoneal bleeding.

Sign Finding
Cullen sign Periumbilical ecchymosis
Grey Turner sign Flank ecchymosis
Clinical warning: Cullen sign and Grey Turner sign suggest severe hemorrhagic pancreatitis.

Clinical Pearls

  1. Diagnosis requires 2 of 3 criteria: symptoms, enzymes, or imaging.
  2. Gallstones and alcohol are the most common causes.
  3. Always check triglycerides in unexplained pancreatitis.
  4. Lactated Ringer’s is generally preferred for resuscitation.
  5. Early fluid resuscitation helps prevent organ dysfunction.
  6. Early enteral feeding is preferred over prolonged NPO status.
  7. Routine prophylactic antibiotics are not recommended.
  8. Hypocalcemia occurs from saponification.
  9. ARDS is a major systemic complication.
  10. Pseudocysts and walled-off necrosis generally occur after four weeks.
  11. Worsening after initial improvement should prompt repeat CT imaging.
  12. Cullen sign and Grey Turner sign suggest hemorrhagic pancreatitis.

Bottom Line

Acute pancreatitis is diagnosed using two of three criteria: characteristic pain, elevated pancreatic enzymes, and imaging findings. The cornerstone of management is early fluid resuscitation with Lactated Ringer’s, adequate pain control, early enteral nutrition, and avoidance of unnecessary antibiotics. Monitor closely for necrosis, pseudocysts, organ failure, splenic vein thrombosis, and hemorrhagic complications.

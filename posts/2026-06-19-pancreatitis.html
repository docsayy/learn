Acute Pancreatitis: Diagnosis, Management, and Complications

June 19, 2026 Gastroenterology

Acute pancreatitis is one of the most common gastrointestinal causes of hospitalization. Effective management depends on early diagnosis, aggressive fluid resuscitation, adequate pain control, identification of the underlying cause, and recognition of complications such as necrosis, pseudocysts, and organ failure.

Diagnosis

The diagnosis of acute pancreatitis requires at least two of three diagnostic criteria.

Diagnostic Criteria Description
Typical symptoms Acute epigastric pain radiating to the back
Elevated enzymes Lipase or amylase >3× upper limit of normal
Imaging findings Characteristic CT, MRI, or ultrasound findings
Diagnosis requires any 2 of the 3 criteria above.

Classic Symptoms

  • Acute severe epigastric pain
  • Pain radiating to the back
  • Nausea and vomiting (present in >90%)
  • Pain sometimes relieved by leaning forward

Causes of Pancreatitis

The two most common causes in the United States are:

  1. Gallstones
  2. Alcohol

Important Laboratory Causes

  • Triglycerides >1000 mg/dL
  • Calcium >15 mg/dL
Always check triglycerides in unexplained pancreatitis.

Initial Treatment

Management is built on two major pillars:

  1. Aggressive fluid resuscitation
  2. Adequate pain control

Fluid Resuscitation

Acute pancreatitis causes massive third spacing due to capillary leak from inflammatory pancreatic enzymes.

This results in:

  • Hypovolemia
  • Hemoconcentration
  • Acute kidney injury
  • Organ dysfunction

Preferred Fluid

Lactated Ringer's is generally preferred over normal saline.

Example Fluid Strategy

  • 1 L LR bolus initially
  • 250 mL/hr for approximately 12 hours
  • Then decrease based on clinical response

Fluid requirements should be individualized based on volume status, renal function, and cardiac function.

Pain Control

Pancreatitis is frequently extremely painful and often requires opioid therapy.

Common options:

  • Hydromorphone
  • Morphine
  • Oxycodone
  • Patient-controlled analgesia (PCA) when appropriate

Meperidine (Demerol)

Historically thought to be preferred because it may cause less sphincter of Oddi spasm.

Current evidence does not clearly support superiority over other opioids.

Nutrition & Antibiotics

Nutrition

Historically patients were kept NPO for prolonged periods.

Modern evidence supports:

  • Early enteral feeding
  • Advance diet as tolerated
  • Avoid prolonged bowel rest

Suggested Progression

  • Clear liquids if severe symptoms
  • Low-fat diet
  • Regular diet as tolerated
Early feeding shortens recovery and reduces complications.

Antibiotic Prophylaxis

Routine prophylactic antibiotics are NOT recommended.

  • Do not treat uncomplicated pancreatitis with antibiotics.
  • Use antibiotics only when infection is suspected or confirmed.

Severity Assessment

BISAP Score

The BISAP score is commonly used to predict mortality risk.

Common Admission Findings

  • Elevated BUN
  • Elevated hemoglobin
  • Hypocalcemia

Why Is Calcium Low?

Pancreatic enzymes bind calcium within areas of fat necrosis.

This process is called:

Saponification

Major Complications

Systemic Complications

  • ARDS
  • Shock
  • Acute kidney injury
  • Multiorgan failure

Chronic Pancreatitis

  • Exocrine insufficiency
  • Malabsorption
  • Diabetes mellitus
Patients with chronic pancreatitis may have normal lipase levels despite severe symptoms.

Splenic Vein Thrombosis

Inflammation around the pancreas can extend to the adjacent splenic vein.

Treatment is usually directed toward the underlying pancreatitis rather than routine anticoagulation unless clot extension occurs.

Pancreatic Fluid Collections

Type Timing Setting
Acute Peripancreatic Fluid Collection <4 weeks Interstitial pancreatitis
Acute Necrotic Collection <4 weeks Necrotizing pancreatitis
Pancreatic Pseudocyst >4 weeks Interstitial pancreatitis
Walled-Off Necrosis >4 weeks Necrotizing pancreatitis
Pseudocysts and walled-off necrosis have a mature, defined wall and usually develop after four weeks.

Management

Asymptomatic

  • Observation
  • Repeat imaging
  • Supportive care

Symptomatic

  • Endoscopic ultrasound-guided drainage
  • Percutaneous drainage in selected cases

Persistent Symptoms

  • Necrosectomy

When to Re-image

Patients who initially improve but worsen after 5–7 days should undergo repeat CT imaging to evaluate for:

  • Infected necrosis
  • Walled-off necrosis
  • Other pancreatic complications

Hemorrhagic Pancreatitis

Severe necrotizing pancreatitis can cause retroperitoneal bleeding.

Cullen Sign

  • Periumbilical ecchymosis

Grey Turner Sign

  • Flank ecchymosis
These findings suggest severe hemorrhagic pancreatitis and are classic examination findings frequently tested on exams.

Clinical Pearls

  • Diagnosis requires 2 of 3 criteria: symptoms, enzymes, or imaging.
  • Gallstones and alcohol account for most cases.
  • Always check triglycerides.
  • Lactated Ringer's is generally preferred for resuscitation.
  • Aggressive early fluid resuscitation improves outcomes.
  • Early enteral feeding is preferred over prolonged NPO status.
  • Routine prophylactic antibiotics are not recommended.
  • Hypocalcemia occurs from saponification.
  • ARDS is a major systemic complication.
  • Pseudocysts and walled-off necrosis generally occur after four weeks.
  • Worsening after initial improvement should prompt repeat CT imaging.
  • Cullen sign and Grey Turner sign suggest hemorrhagic pancreatitis.

Bottom Line

Acute pancreatitis is diagnosed using two of three criteria: characteristic pain, elevated pancreatic enzymes, and imaging findings. The cornerstone of management is aggressive early fluid resuscitation with Lactated Ringer's, adequate pain control, early enteral nutrition, and avoidance of unnecessary antibiotics. Monitor carefully for necrosis, pseudocysts, organ failure, and hemorrhagic complications.

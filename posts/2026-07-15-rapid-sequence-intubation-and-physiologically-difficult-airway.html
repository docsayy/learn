Rapid Sequence Intubation and the Physiologically Difficult Airway

Added July 15, 2026 • Pulmonary and Critical Care • ~30 min read

A critical-care approach to airway decision-making, preparation, preoxygenation, hemodynamic optimization, medication selection, first-pass success, rescue planning, and immediate post-intubation management in patients at risk of peri-intubation hypoxemia, hypotension, acidosis, or right-ventricular collapse.

Clinical Overview

A technically easy laryngoscopy can still be physiologically lethal. Severe hypoxemia, shock, metabolic acidosis, pulmonary hypertension, and right-ventricular failure make the transition from spontaneous breathing to induction and positive-pressure ventilation dangerous.

Anatomic Difficulty + Physiologic Difficulty + Operational Difficulty

When to Intubate

  • Failure of oxygenation despite optimized noninvasive support
  • Failure of ventilation with worsening acidosis or fatigue
  • Inability to protect the airway
  • Impending obstruction
  • Severe encephalopathy
  • Shock requiring controlled ventilation or procedures
Intubate for trajectory, not for a single oxygen saturation or blood-gas value.

Airway Preparation

  • Assign airway operator, medication nurse, monitor, and backup operator.
  • Prepare suction, video laryngoscope, bougie, supraglottic airway, and surgical-airway equipment.
  • Position head and torso appropriately; use ramped positioning in obesity.
  • Apply full monitoring and place reliable IV access.
  • State Plan A, Plan B, oxygenation rescue, and front-of-neck access plan aloud.

Preoxygenation

  • Use a well-sealed nonrebreather, HFNC, NIV, or bag-mask with PEEP according to physiology.
  • NIV is often superior for severe shunt physiology or obesity when tolerated.
  • Continue nasal oxygen for apneic oxygenation when appropriate.
  • Optimize position and recruit lung before induction.

The goal is not merely a high SpO₂; it is the largest possible oxygen reservoir and a plan to maintain oxygenation during apnea.

Hemodynamic Optimization

  • Identify distributive, hypovolemic, cardiogenic, or obstructive shock.
  • Start norepinephrine before induction when hypotension is likely.
  • Use fluid only when the patient is likely to respond.
  • Reduce unnecessary PEEP in profound preload-dependent shock.
  • Prepare push-dose vasopressor only within a safe local protocol.
“Peri-intubation cardiac arrest” is often predictable. Hypotension before induction, severe hypoxemia, and high shock index demand active preparation.

Severe Metabolic Acidosis

Patients with profound metabolic acidosis may require extraordinary minute ventilation to maintain pH. Apnea and post-intubation hypoventilation can precipitate arrest.

  • Delay intubation only long enough to optimize and assemble the team.
  • Consider awake or minimally apneic techniques in selected expert settings.
  • Match or approach the pre-intubation minute ventilation immediately after tube placement.
  • Treat the cause and consider bicarbonate only for appropriate indications.

Pulmonary Hypertension and RV Failure

  • Avoid hypoxemia, hypercapnia, acidosis, and excessive mean airway pressure.
  • Maintain systemic pressure to perfuse the right coronary artery.
  • Have norepinephrine or vasopressin ready.
  • Use cautious PEEP and tidal volume.
  • Consider inhaled pulmonary vasodilator rescue in severe decompensation.

Tube Confirmation and Immediate Care

  • Continuous waveform capnography
  • Bilateral chest assessment and tube-depth check
  • Immediate ventilator settings matched to physiology
  • Post-intubation analgesia and sedation before awareness occurs
  • Blood pressure reassessment and vasopressor titration
  • Chest imaging when appropriate but not as a substitute for capnography

Failed First Attempt

  1. Stop and oxygenate.
  2. Use two-person bag-mask ventilation with adjuncts and PEEP.
  3. Insert a supraglottic airway if needed.
  4. Change technique, operator, blade, or adjunct.
  5. Proceed to emergency front-of-neck access when oxygenation cannot be restored.
Repeated laryngoscopy without oxygenation is not an airway plan.

Common Pitfalls

  • Inducing an unresuscitated shock patient
  • Using propofol doses appropriate for a stable elective case
  • Forgetting post-intubation sedation after rocuronium
  • Allowing severe acidosis to become hypoventilation
  • Failing to use capnography
  • No explicit rescue plan

Bottom Line

Successful critical-care intubation means survival through induction and the first 15 minutes afterward. Optimize oxygen reserve, blood pressure, acid-base physiology, and RV function; prioritize first-pass success; and prepare a rescue oxygenation strategy.

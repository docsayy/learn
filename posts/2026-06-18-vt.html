Ventricular Tachycardia Management: A Practical Guide for Residents

Added June 18, 2026 • Cardiology • ~7 min read

A practical approach to ventricular tachycardia management, including how to distinguish non-sustained from sustained VT, when to observe, when to use beta blockers or antiarrhythmics, when to shock, and how to recognize VT storm.

Overview

Ventricular tachycardia is a wide-complex rhythm that can range from a brief asymptomatic telemetry finding to a life-threatening unstable arrhythmia requiring immediate shock.

The key to management is first deciding whether the rhythm is non-sustained or sustained, and then determining whether the patient is stable or unstable.

The first management branch point is: non-sustained VT vs sustained VT.

First Step: Define the Duration

Type Definition Clinical Meaning
Non-sustained VT Less than 30 seconds Common telemetry finding; often lower urgency if asymptomatic
Sustained VT Greater than or equal to 30 seconds Requires urgent assessment and treatment based on stability

This distinction matters because many pages on the wards are for brief 5-beat or 10-beat runs of VT. These are usually non-sustained VT and are managed very differently from sustained monomorphic VT.

Non-Sustained Ventricular Tachycardia

Non-sustained VT is VT lasting less than 30 seconds. It is commonly detected on telemetry and often prompts nursing pages overnight.

Initial Response

  • Assess the patient.
  • Determine whether they are symptomatic.
  • Check electrolytes.
  • Correct potassium and magnesium abnormalities.
  • Review medications and ischemic symptoms.

Asymptomatic Non-Sustained VT

If the patient is asymptomatic, hemodynamically stable, and the run was brief, there may be no immediate antiarrhythmic treatment required.

For asymptomatic short runs of NSVT, the most common immediate step is to check and replete electrolytes.

Symptomatic Non-Sustained VT

If the patient is symptomatic, treatment may include rate-suppressing therapy.

  • Beta blocker
  • Non-dihydropyridine calcium channel blocker in selected cases
  • Amiodarone if refractory or higher-risk
  • Catheter ablation if recurrent and refractory
Always interpret NSVT in clinical context. NSVT in severe cardiomyopathy, ischemia, syncope, or electrolyte derangement deserves more attention than an isolated brief run in an otherwise stable patient.

PVCs and VT Risk

When Do PVCs Become VT?

Three or more consecutive premature ventricular complexes are considered ventricular tachycardia.

3 or more consecutive PVCs = VT.

PVC Burden

A high PVC burden can cause or worsen cardiac remodeling.

  • Greater than 10% PVC burden is often considered clinically significant.
  • High burden may contribute to PVC-induced cardiomyopathy.
  • This may push clinicians toward treatment even if symptoms are minimal.

Rules of Malignancy for PVCs

Certain PVC patterns are more concerning because they have a higher risk of triggering VT.

  • Frequent PVCs
  • Multiform PVCs
  • R-on-T phenomenon
R-on-T occurs when a PVC lands on the T wave of the preceding beat and can precipitate dangerous ventricular arrhythmias.

Sustained Monomorphic VT

Sustained VT lasts at least 30 seconds. The next step is to determine whether the patient is stable or unstable.

Question Next Step
Unstable? Immediate electrical cardioversion/defibrillation
Stable? Antiarrhythmic therapy first

Unstable Sustained VT

Unstable VT requires immediate electrical therapy.

Signs of Instability

  • Hypotension
  • Altered mental status
  • Signs of shock
  • Ischemic chest pain
  • Acute heart failure or pulmonary edema
  • Loss of pulse

Electrical Treatment

Scenario Treatment
Awake with pulse and unstable VT Synchronized cardioversion
Pulseless VT Immediate defibrillation and ACLS
Severely unstable or pulseless Start with high-energy shock per ACLS protocol
Do not delay shock for medications in unstable sustained VT.

Stable Sustained VT

If the patient is in sustained monomorphic VT but remains hemodynamically stable, treatment usually begins with an IV antiarrhythmic.

Common Initial Options

  • Amiodarone
  • Lidocaine
  • Procainamide
Amiodarone is commonly used because many clinicians are familiar with it and it reduces VT recurrence.

Antiarrhythmic Choices

Medication Typical Use / Dose Mentioned Pros Limitations
Amiodarone 150 mg IV bolus over 10 minutes, then 1 mg/min infusion for 6 hours Common default; reduces recurrence; good chance of termination Slower onset; can cause hypotension and long-term toxicities
Lidocaine 1–1.5 mg/kg IV Less hypotension; useful in ischemia/MI-associated VT Lower rate of VT termination
Procainamide 20–50 mg/min IV infusion Can terminate VT or slow the rate Can cause hypotension; avoid in certain structural heart disease or prolonged QT contexts

How to Choose

  • Amiodarone: common default choice and familiar to most teams.
  • Lidocaine: consider when hypotension is a major concern or in ischemia-associated VT.
  • Procainamide: effective option for stable monomorphic VT but requires attention to blood pressure and contraindications.
Antiarrhythmic selection should be guided by patient stability, blood pressure, QT interval, ischemia, structural heart disease, and local protocols.

Refractory VT

If sustained VT persists despite antiarrhythmic infusion, electrical cardioversion is the next step.

  • Continue close monitoring.
  • Escalate to cardiology or electrophysiology.
  • Prepare for synchronized cardioversion if stable but refractory.
  • Shock immediately if instability develops.
If VT terminates during the antiarrhythmic infusion, the infusion may sometimes be stopped or reassessed depending on recurrence risk and cardiology guidance.

VT Storm and Incessant VT

VT Storm

VT storm refers to recurrent episodes of VT over a short period.

Term Definition
VT storm Three or more recurrent VT episodes within 24 hours
VT recurrence within minutes VT recurring within about 5 minutes of termination can also be treated as storm physiology
Incessant VT VT lasting for hours despite hemodynamic stability

Management Concepts

  • Antiarrhythmic therapy
  • Add beta blocker if not already on one
  • Consider propranolol in selected storm cases with specialist input
  • Correct electrolytes aggressively
  • Treat ischemia or triggers
  • Involve cardiology/electrophysiology early
  • Consider catheter ablation for refractory recurrent VT
VT storm is high risk. Involve cardiology, electrophysiology, and ICU early.

Take-Home Pearls

  • Less than 30 seconds is non-sustained VT.
  • Greater than or equal to 30 seconds is sustained VT.
  • Three or more PVCs in a row is VT.
  • For asymptomatic NSVT, assess the patient and replete electrolytes.
  • Symptomatic NSVT may require beta blocker or calcium channel blocker therapy.
  • Unstable sustained VT requires immediate electrical cardioversion or defibrillation.
  • Stable sustained monomorphic VT is usually treated first with IV antiarrhythmic therapy.
  • Amiodarone is a common default antiarrhythmic option.
  • Lidocaine causes less hypotension and may be useful in ischemia-associated VT.
  • Procainamide can terminate VT or slow the rate but requires careful monitoring.
  • VT storm is three or more VT episodes in 24 hours and needs urgent escalation.

The Sexual History: A High-Yield Framework Beyond the 5 P's

Added July 19, 2026 • Clinical Skills • ~10 min read

A sexual history is more than "are you sexually active?" — it is a structured tool for determining exposure risk, transmission risk, disease risk, and public health implications, using the CDC-based 5 P's plus attending-level additions.

Core Concept

A sexual history is not just "are you sexually active?" You are trying to determine:

  • Exposure risk (who + how)
  • Transmission risk (protection)
  • Disease risk (past + symptoms)
  • Public health implications (partners, HIV risk)
Core principle: Sexual history guides testing site and treatment decisions — it is not just a screening formality.

The 5 P's Framework

This CDC-based framework is high-yield for structuring a sexual history.

P What to Ask Why It Matters
Partners Sexually active? Number and gender of partners, new/multiple partners, partner with STI? More partners increase risk of gonorrhea, chlamydia, and HIV; MSM require rectal/pharyngeal screening; partner history predicts exposure.
Practices Vaginal, anal (receptive vs. insertive), or oral sex Determines testing site — oral exposure warrants pharyngeal testing, anal exposure warrants rectal GC/CT, and symptoms differ by site (proctitis vs. urethritis).
Protection Condom use (always/sometimes/never), type of protection, partner testing discussions Inconsistent condom use signals high STI risk and informs screening, counseling, and PrEP eligibility.
Past STIs Prior STI diagnosis (which, when, treatment), HIV testing history, partner STI history Prior STI is a strong predictor of future STI and a marker for HIV risk.
Pregnancy intention Pregnancy plans, contraception use Guides pregnancy testing before treatment, counseling, and awareness of STI-related pregnancy complications.

Beyond the 5 P's

These additions are often missed but clinically critical at the attending level.

Symptoms

Ask about discharge (urethral/vaginal), dysuria, genital ulcers, rash on palms/soles (suggesting syphilis), pelvic pain, rectal pain/discharge, and pharyngitis from oral exposure. Symptom pattern guides empiric treatment versus screening — gonorrhea/chlamydia typically present with discharge, syphilis with a painless ulcer, and HSV with painful vesicles.

Timing of Exposure

Ask about the last sexual contact and incubation timing. This determines the window period for HIV and syphilis testing and whether HIV PEP is indicated (within 72 hours).

High-Risk Behaviors

Ask about IV drug use, sex work, multiple/concurrent partners, and substance use during sex. These are strong predictors of HIV and hepatitis B/C and change screening and prophylaxis decisions.

HIV-Specific Risk

Ask about known HIV-positive partners, PrEP use, and recent STI diagnosis. This determines the need for HIV testing and PrEP eligibility.

Safety and trauma are often overlooked. Ask about non-consensual sex and intimate partner violence — findings here change management (prophylaxis) and may carry legal or forensic implications.

How to Actually Ask

Normalize the questions: "I ask all patients these questions…" Be neutral and avoid assumptions. Be specific — don't say "sex," define the type.

Bad Good
"Do you practice safe sex?" "How often do you use condoms — always, sometimes, or never?"

How History Changes Management

History Finding What It Changes
Receptive anal sex Rectal GC/CT testing
Oral sex Pharyngeal testing
Multiple partners Full STI panel
Prior STI Repeat screening + counseling
HIV risk PrEP / HIV testing
Recent exposure (<72h) HIV PEP

Quick ICU/ED Shortcut

If time is short, ask:

  • Are you sexually active?
  • Men, women, or both?
  • How many partners recently?
  • Condom use?
  • Any discharge, ulcers, or pain?
Clinical pearl: This shortcut alone captures most actionable risk when time is limited.

Attending-Level Pearls

Questions worth being able to answer on rounds:

  • Why is asking about sexual practices more important than sexual orientation?
  • How does sexual history determine anatomical site testing?
  • Why does prior STI increase risk of HIV acquisition?
  • When should you offer HIV PEP based on history?
  • Why is "condom use" an inadequate question unless quantified?
Board-style vignette: A 24-year-old man presents for STI screening. He reports sex with men and women, inconsistent condom use, and receptive anal intercourse, with no symptoms. Next best step: test urine, rectal, and pharyngeal GC/CT, plus HIV and syphilis testing.
Rapid recall: Use the 5 P's framework; always ask the type of sex for site-specific testing; prior STI predicts future risk; condom use must be quantified; sexual history guides testing, not symptoms alone.

Bottom Line

Use the 5 P's — Partners, Practices, Protection, Past STIs, Pregnancy — and add Symptoms, Timing, and High-Risk Behaviors. The goal is to determine exposure, risk, testing sites, and the need for treatment or prevention.

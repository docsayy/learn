Cough: Practical Inpatient Approach

Added June 19, 2026 • Hospital Medicine / Pulmonary • ~8 min read

Cough is one of the most common symptoms encountered in hospitalized patients. Although treating the underlying cause is always the priority, symptomatic treatment can improve comfort, sleep, and recovery. This guide reviews practical inpatient approaches to acute and chronic cough management.

Overview

Cough is a frequent inpatient complaint and can be frustrating for both patients and clinicians. While many therapies are commonly prescribed, relatively few medications have strong evidence supporting nonspecific cough suppression.

The most important intervention is identifying and treating the underlying cause. Symptomatic medications should be viewed as adjunctive therapies while definitive treatment is pursued.

Practical approach: Start with Tessalon Perles plus guaifenesin. If symptoms persist, consider dextromethorphan and additional targeted therapies.

Practical Treatment Algorithm

  1. Treat the underlying cause whenever possible.
  2. Start with benzonatate plus guaifenesin.
  3. If symptoms persist, add dextromethorphan.
  4. Provide supportive therapies for sore throat and airway irritation.
  5. Evaluate for GERD, asthma, chronic bronchitis, or post-viral cough.
  6. Avoid routine opioid cough suppressants.
For most hospitalized patients, Tessalon Perles plus guaifenesin is a reasonable first-line symptomatic regimen.

Benzonatate (Tessalon Perles)

Benzonatate is the most commonly used peripheral antitussive.

Medication Mechanism Role
Benzonatate Anesthetizes stretch receptors in lungs and pleura First-line symptomatic cough suppression

Clinical Pearls

  • Acts peripherally rather than centrally.
  • Generally well tolerated.
  • Common first medication prescribed for inpatient cough.
  • Often paired with guaifenesin.
Many clinicians start with Tessalon Perles plus guaifenesin before escalating to stronger antitussive therapies.

Guaifenesin (Mucinex)

Guaifenesin is an expectorant that helps loosen respiratory secretions and may improve mucus clearance.

Medication Class Primary Use
Guaifenesin Expectorant Loosening secretions and improving mucus clearance

When It Is Helpful

  • Productive cough
  • Bronchitis
  • Upper respiratory infections
  • Patients with significant chest congestion
If a patient sounds congested or has significant secretions, guaifenesin is usually a reasonable addition.

Dextromethorphan (Robitussin)

Dextromethorphan is the most commonly used non-opioid centrally acting antitussive.

Unlike codeine, it suppresses cough without opioid-related adverse effects such as constipation, respiratory depression, and sedation.

Medication Mechanism Comments
Dextromethorphan Central cough suppression Often preferred over codeine
Robitussin-DM Dextromethorphan + Guaifenesin Provides antitussive and expectorant effects
Avoid automatically reaching for codeine. Dextromethorphan often works as well or better without opioid-related side effects.

Supportive Therapies

Many patients benefit from simple supportive measures.

Therapy Potential Benefit
Honey Best evidence among non-pharmacologic options
Warm tea Soothes irritated throat
Warm fluids Symptomatic relief
Soft foods Reduce throat irritation
Throat lozenges Temporary symptom relief
Phenol spray Useful for sore throat symptoms
Benzocaine spray Local anesthetic effect

Throat Lozenges

  • Menthol lozenges
  • Lidocaine-containing lozenges
  • Standard cough drops

These therapies often help patients whose cough is worsened by throat irritation.

Ipratropium Bromide

Ipratropium is the only inhaled anticholinergic with guideline support for some forms of cough related to upper respiratory infections and chronic bronchitis.

Potential Uses

  • Post-viral cough
  • Upper respiratory infections
  • Chronic bronchitis
Although anticholinergics generally dry secretions, inhaled ipratropium remains a reasonable therapeutic trial in selected patients.

Consider GERD

Gastroesophageal reflux disease is a common cause of chronic cough.

Clues Suggesting GERD

  • Heartburn
  • Worse symptoms when lying flat
  • Chronic throat clearing
  • Hoarseness
  • Nighttime cough

Treatment Considerations

  • Pantoprazole
  • Omeprazole
  • Lifestyle modification
  • Head-of-bed elevation
A chronic cough without obvious pulmonary findings should prompt consideration of reflux disease.

Consider Asthma

Cough-variant asthma may present primarily as chronic cough.

Clinical Clues

  • Wheezing
  • Nocturnal cough
  • Exercise-induced symptoms
  • History of asthma or allergies

Treatment Trial

  • Albuterol inhaler
  • Nebulized bronchodilators
  • Inhaled corticosteroids when appropriate

Chronic Cough

Patients with chronic refractory cough may require therapies beyond standard antitussives.

Medication Potential Role
Gabapentin Neuromodulation of chronic cough reflex
Pregabalin Alternative neuromodulator

These medications are typically reserved for chronic cough after common etiologies have been evaluated.

Gabapentin and pregabalin are not routine therapies for acute inpatient cough.

Bonus: Hiccups

Persistent hiccups can be surprisingly difficult to manage.

Medication Use
Chlorpromazine (Thorazine) Persistent hiccups
Baclofen Persistent hiccups

These therapies are occasionally effective in patients with refractory hiccups.

Clinical Pearls

  • Treat the underlying cause whenever possible.
  • Tessalon Perles plus guaifenesin is a reasonable first-line regimen.
  • Dextromethorphan is generally preferred over codeine.
  • Honey has surprisingly good evidence for symptomatic relief.
  • Consider GERD in chronic cough without obvious pulmonary findings.
  • Consider asthma in patients with nocturnal cough or wheezing.
  • Ipratropium may help selected patients with URI-related or chronic bronchitis-associated cough.
  • Gabapentin and pregabalin may help chronic refractory cough.
  • Avoid unnecessary opioid cough suppressants.
  • Review medications that may contribute to cough, including ACE inhibitors.

Bottom Line

For most hospitalized patients with uncomplicated cough, start with benzonatate and guaifenesin. If symptoms persist, add dextromethorphan and supportive therapies such as lozenges, honey, and warm fluids. Always evaluate for underlying causes such as infection, GERD, asthma, medication effects, and chronic pulmonary disease rather than relying solely on symptomatic suppression.

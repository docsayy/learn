What Is Acute Coronary Syndrome?

Acute Coronary Syndrome (ACS) refers to a spectrum of clinical conditions caused by sudden reduction of blood flow through a coronary artery. Most cases occur when an atherosclerotic plaque ruptures or erodes, exposing thrombogenic material to circulating blood and triggering platelet activation, thrombus formation, and myocardial ischemia.

ACS is not a single diagnosis. Instead, it represents a group of related clinical syndromes that differ primarily by the degree of coronary occlusion, the presence of myocardial necrosis, ECG findings, and biomarker elevation.

Clinical Pearl

Think of ACS as a continuum. The underlying mechanism is often similar—a ruptured plaque with superimposed thrombus—but the extent of coronary obstruction determines whether the patient develops unstable angina, NSTEMI, or STEMI.

The Acute Coronary Syndrome Spectrum

The ACS spectrum consists of three primary entities:

Condition Troponin Myocardial Necrosis Typical ECG Findings
Unstable Angina Negative No Normal, ST depression, or T-wave inversion
NSTEMI Positive Yes Normal, ST depression, or T-wave inversion
STEMI Positive Yes ST elevation or STEMI equivalent

Historically, unstable angina was commonly diagnosed. With the widespread use of high-sensitivity troponin assays, many patients who would previously have been labeled unstable angina are now identified as NSTEMI because even very small amounts of myocardial injury can be detected.

Unstable Angina vs NSTEMI vs STEMI

Unstable Angina

Unstable angina occurs when plaque rupture and thrombosis produce myocardial ischemia without detectable myocardial necrosis. Troponin levels remain normal because cardiomyocyte death has not occurred.

  • Chest pain at rest or minimal exertion
  • May have ST depression or T-wave inversion
  • No troponin elevation
  • No myocardial infarction

NSTEMI

NSTEMI occurs when coronary thrombosis produces myocardial necrosis, but the infarction is generally limited to the subendocardial region rather than the full myocardial thickness.

  • Positive troponin elevation
  • Subendocardial infarction
  • ST depression or T-wave inversion common
  • No diagnostic ST elevation

STEMI

STEMI represents complete or near-complete coronary artery occlusion causing transmural myocardial infarction. These patients require immediate reperfusion therapy because ongoing myocardial death occurs minute by minute.

  • Positive troponins
  • Transmural infarction
  • Diagnostic ST elevation
  • Emergent PCI indicated

Pathophysiology of ACS

The classic sequence begins with development of an atherosclerotic plaque inside a coronary artery.

  1. Endothelial injury develops
  2. Lipid accumulation occurs
  3. Inflammatory cells infiltrate plaque
  4. Plaque becomes vulnerable
  5. Plaque ruptures or erodes
  6. Platelets activate
  7. Thrombus forms
  8. Coronary blood flow decreases
  9. Myocardial ischemia develops
  10. Prolonged ischemia causes infarction

Resident Takeaway

The primary therapeutic targets in ACS are:

  • Prevent platelet aggregation
  • Prevent thrombus propagation
  • Restore coronary blood flow
  • Stabilize vulnerable plaque
  • Prevent recurrent cardiovascular events

ECG Diagnosis of Acute Coronary Syndrome

The ECG remains one of the most important diagnostic tools in ACS evaluation. Every patient presenting with possible ACS should receive a 12-lead ECG within 10 minutes of arrival.

Common ECG Findings in ACS

Finding Implication
ST Elevation Acute transmural ischemia
ST Depression Subendocardial ischemia
T-Wave Inversion Myocardial ischemia
Hyperacute T Waves Early STEMI
New LBBB Possible STEMI equivalent

Diagnostic STEMI Criteria

STEMI requires ST elevation in at least two contiguous leads.

The traditional threshold is:

  • ≥ 1 mm ST elevation in most leads

However, leads V2 and V3 have special thresholds:

Patient Group Required ST Elevation
Women > 1.5 mm
Men ≥40 years > 2.0 mm
Men <40 years > 2.5 mm

Board Pearl

ST elevation must occur in at least two contiguous leads. A single isolated lead with ST elevation is not diagnostic for STEMI.

Sgarbossa Criteria and Left Bundle Branch Block

Diagnosing acute MI in the presence of a left bundle branch block (LBBB) can be challenging because normal LBBB already causes secondary ST-T changes.

The Sgarbossa Criteria help identify STEMI in patients with LBBB or ventricular paced rhythms.

Modified Sgarbossa Criteria improve diagnostic sensitivity and are increasingly favored in modern clinical practice.

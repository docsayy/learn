Cirrhosis: A Practical Guide for Internal Medicine Residents

Added June 18, 2026 • Gastroenterology / Hepatology • ~18 min read

A practical resident-level review of cirrhosis management using the VIBES framework: volume, infection, bleeding, encephalopathy, and screening, plus high-yield complications including hepatorenal syndrome, hepatopulmonary syndrome, portopulmonary hypertension, hepatic hydrothorax, and coagulopathy.

Overview

Cirrhosis has many complications, so it is helpful to use a checklist-based approach every time a patient with cirrhosis is admitted.

A useful framework is the mnemonic VIBES: Volume, Infection, Bleeding, Encephalopathy, and Screening.

The VIBES Mnemonic

Letter Meaning Common Issue
V Volume Ascites
I Infection Spontaneous bacterial peritonitis
B Bleeding Esophageal varices
E Encephalopathy Hepatic encephalopathy
S Screening HCC, varices, vaccines, transplant referral

Volume / Ascites

Ascites is one of the most common complications of cirrhosis. Management includes sodium restriction, diuretics, diagnostic paracentesis, therapeutic paracentesis, and evaluation for refractory disease.

Diuretics

The classic diuretic ratio in cirrhotic ascites is spironolactone to furosemide in a 5:2 ratio.

Spironolactone : Furosemide = 100 mg : 40 mg
  • Spironolactone is the main driver of diuresis in cirrhotic ascites.
  • Furosemide helps balance potassium and improves fluid removal.
  • Common starting combinations include 100/40 mg or 50/20 mg.

Diagnostic Paracentesis

Every hospitalized patient with cirrhosis and ascites should undergo diagnostic paracentesis, even without fever or abdominal pain.

Cirrhotic patients may not show classic signs of infection. They may be hypothermic, may not mount a fever, and may not have abdominal pain even with SBP.

Therapeutic Paracentesis

Therapeutic or large-volume paracentesis is performed for symptom relief.

  • Painful abdominal distension
  • Shortness of breath from tense ascites
  • Large symptomatic fluid burden

Albumin After Large-Volume Paracentesis

Albumin is used after large-volume paracentesis to reduce complications from fluid shifts.

Albumin 6–8 g per liter removed after approximately 4–5 L

A common practical regimen is:

25 g of 25% albumin for every 4 L removed
Use 25% albumin, not 5% albumin, because 5% albumin gives much more volume back.

When to Consider TIPS

TIPS, or transjugular intrahepatic portosystemic shunt, may be considered for refractory ascites.

TIPS reduces portal hypertension by creating a shunt between the portal and hepatic venous systems.

TIPS can worsen hepatic encephalopathy.

SAAG: Serum Ascites Albumin Gradient

SAAG helps determine whether ascites is due to portal hypertension.

SAAG = Serum Albumin − Ascites Albumin
SAAG Interpretation Examples
≥ 1.1 Portal hypertensive ascites Cirrhosis, cardiac ascites
< 1.1 Non-portal hypertensive ascites Peritoneal carcinomatosis, TB, nephrotic syndrome

Total Protein Helps Further Classify Ascites

SAAG Total Protein Likely Cause
≥ 1.1 < 2.5 Cirrhosis
≥ 1.1 ≥ 2.5 Cardiac ascites
< 1.1 Variable Malignancy, TB, nephrotic syndrome

Example

Serum albumin 2.5 − Ascites albumin 1.0 = SAAG 1.5

A SAAG of 1.5 suggests portal hypertensive ascites.

Infection / Spontaneous Bacterial Peritonitis

Spontaneous bacterial peritonitis, or SBP, is infection of ascitic fluid.

Diagnosis

Ascites PMN ≥ 250 cells/mm³ = SBP
Positive cultures are not required to diagnose SBP if PMNs are ≥250.

Treatment

  • Ceftriaxone for 5–7 days
  • Albumin to reduce risk of hepatorenal syndrome

Albumin Dosing in SBP

Day Dose
Day 1 Albumin 1.5 g/kg
Day 3 Albumin 1 g/kg

SBP Secondary Prophylaxis

Any patient who has had SBP should receive lifelong secondary prophylaxis.

  • TMP-SMX
  • Ciprofloxacin

SBP Primary Prophylaxis

Primary prophylaxis may be indicated in high-risk patients with very low ascites protein and renal or hepatic dysfunction.

Ascites Protein Additional Risk Factor
< 1.5 Creatinine > 1.2
< 1.5 BUN > 25
< 1.5 Sodium < 130
< 1.5 Child-Pugh > 9
< 1.5 Total bilirubin > 3

PPI Pearl

Stop unnecessary PPIs after SBP because PPIs can increase risk of SBP recurrence.

Bleeding / Esophageal Varices

Portal hypertension can cause esophageal varices. Acute variceal bleeding is a high-mortality emergency.

Acute Variceal Bleeding Management

  • Two large-bore IVs
  • GI consult
  • IV PPI
  • Ceftriaxone for SBP prophylaxis
  • Octreotide
  • Endoscopy for control of bleeding and possible band ligation
Octreotide causes splanchnic vasoconstriction and helps reduce portal pressure.

Severe Bleeding

If bleeding is severe or airway protection is needed, consider:

  • Intubation
  • Sengstaken-Blakemore tube as a bridge
  • Urgent endoscopic therapy
  • ICU-level care

Chronic Variceal Bleeding Prevention

Non-selective beta blockers reduce risk of variceal bleeding.

  • Propranolol
  • Nadolol
  • Carvedilol
Goal heart rate: 55–60 bpm
Do not start beta blockers during an acute variceal bleed. They are for chronic prevention after stabilization.

If beta blockers are not tolerated, patients should be evaluated by GI for possible variceal band ligation.

Hepatic Encephalopathy

Hepatic encephalopathy is one of the most common decompensating events in cirrhosis.

Treatment

Medication Role
Lactulose First-line therapy
Rifaximin Add for recurrent or refractory encephalopathy
Zinc sulfate May help in selected patients
Lactulose goal = 2–3 bowel movements/day

Precipitating Factors

  • Missed lactulose
  • Infection
  • Over-diuresis
  • Dehydration
  • Hypokalemia
  • GI bleeding
Do not anchor only on lactulose non-adherence. Always search for other triggers.

Asterixis and Milkmaid's Grip

Asterixis is a negative myoclonus often seen in hepatic encephalopathy.

If the patient cannot cooperate with testing for asterixis, check for milkmaid's grip by asking the patient to squeeze your fingers and watching for rhythmic loss of tone.

How Lactulose Works

Lactulose is a non-absorbable sugar metabolized by gut bacteria. This acidifies the colon.

NH₃ + H⁺ → NH₄⁺

Ammonia is converted into ammonium, which is less absorbable and is excreted in stool.

Screening and Prevention

Hepatocellular Carcinoma Screening

Right upper quadrant ultrasound every 6 months

Variceal Screening

EGD every 3 years, or more often if varices are present

Vaccinations

  • Hepatitis A vaccination
  • Hepatitis B vaccination

Transplant Referral

Refer for liver transplant evaluation when MELD score starts rising above approximately 10.

Transplant evaluation can take a long time because it includes medical, psychosocial, and socioeconomic assessment.

Hepatorenal Syndrome

Hepatorenal syndrome should be considered in a patient with cirrhosis, ascites, AKI, and low blood pressure.

Ascites is essentially required for hepatorenal syndrome.

Pathophysiology

Portal hypertension causes splanchnic vasodilation and blood pooling in the splanchnic circulation. The kidneys perceive low effective arterial volume and become hypoperfused.

Diagnostic Clues

  • Cirrhosis
  • Ascites
  • AKI
  • Low blood pressure
  • Very low urine sodium
  • No response to albumin challenge

Albumin Challenge

Albumin 1 g/kg/day × 2 days

If kidney function improves, the patient likely had prerenal AKI. If not, hepatorenal syndrome becomes more likely.

Treatment

  • Stop diuretics
  • Stop beta blockers
  • Stop ACE inhibitors and ARBs
  • Terlipressin plus albumin when available
  • Norepinephrine plus albumin in ICU settings
  • Midodrine plus octreotide plus albumin if other options are unavailable
Low MAP in cirrhosis is dangerous. Stop unnecessary antihypertensives, beta blockers, ACE inhibitors, and ARBs when hypotension or renal dysfunction develops.

Hepatopulmonary Syndrome

Hepatopulmonary syndrome occurs when cirrhosis leads to intrapulmonary vascular dilations and right-to-left shunting.

Classic Findings

Term Meaning
Platypnea Shortness of breath worse when upright
Orthodeoxia Hypoxemia worse when upright
Patients often feel better lying down and worse sitting or standing upright.

Diagnosis

  • Contrast bubble echocardiogram
  • Bubbles appear in the left heart after 3–8 cardiac cycles
  • Delayed appearance suggests intrapulmonary shunt

Treatment

  • Supportive oxygen
  • Liver transplant evaluation
Definitive treatment for hepatopulmonary syndrome is liver transplantation.

Portopulmonary Hypertension

Portopulmonary hypertension is pulmonary arterial hypertension occurring in the setting of portal hypertension and liver disease.

It is categorized similarly to WHO Group 1 pulmonary hypertension.

Treatment Options

  • Endothelin receptor antagonists
  • Phosphodiesterase-5 inhibitors
  • Specialist pulmonary hypertension management
Severe portopulmonary hypertension can complicate or contraindicate liver transplantation because the transplanted liver can become congested from high pulmonary pressures.

Hyponatremia in Cirrhosis

Hyponatremia is common in cirrhosis because low effective arterial volume stimulates ADH release.

In cirrhotic hyponatremia, 25% albumin is often used rather than normal saline when intravascular support is needed.

Hepatic Hydrothorax

Hepatic hydrothorax is a pleural effusion caused by ascitic fluid moving across the diaphragm into the pleural space.

Classic Pattern

  • Usually right-sided pleural effusion
  • Occurs in cirrhosis and portal hypertension
  • May occur even without massive visible ascites

Treatment

  • Sodium restriction
  • Diuretics
  • Therapeutic thoracentesis if symptomatic
  • Consider TIPS in selected refractory cases
Avoid routine chest tube placement for hepatic hydrothorax because it can continuously drain protein, electrolytes, and fluid.

DIC vs Cirrhosis Coagulopathy

Both DIC and cirrhosis can cause elevated INR and coagulopathy.

Feature DIC Cirrhosis
INR Elevated Elevated
Schistocytes Present Usually absent
Factor VIII Low Normal or elevated
Factor VIII is produced by endothelium, so it is preserved in cirrhosis but consumed in DIC.

High-Yield Cirrhosis Pearls

  • Use VIBES: Volume, Infection, Bleeding, Encephalopathy, Screening.
  • Spironolactone to furosemide ratio is 100:40.
  • Every hospitalized cirrhotic patient with ascites needs diagnostic paracentesis.
  • Large-volume paracentesis requires 25% albumin replacement.
  • SAAG ≥1.1 suggests portal hypertensive ascites.
  • SBP is diagnosed when ascites PMNs are ≥250.
  • SBP treatment is ceftriaxone plus albumin.
  • Any prior SBP episode requires lifelong prophylaxis.
  • Stop unnecessary PPIs after SBP.
  • Acute variceal bleeding requires ceftriaxone, octreotide, PPI, GI consult, and large-bore IVs.
  • Do not start beta blockers during an acute variceal bleed.
  • Lactulose goal is 2–3 bowel movements per day.
  • Always look for encephalopathy triggers.
  • HRS = cirrhosis + ascites + AKI + very low urine sodium.
  • Platypnea and orthodeoxia suggest hepatopulmonary syndrome.
  • Right-sided pleural effusion in cirrhosis suggests hepatic hydrothorax.
  • DIC lowers factor VIII; cirrhosis usually preserves factor VIII.

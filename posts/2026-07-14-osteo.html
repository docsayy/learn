Bone, Joint, and Spinal Infections: The Complete Advanced Resident Guide

Added July 14, 2026 • Infectious Disease • ~42 min read

Bone, joint, and spinal infections require careful integration of anatomy, microbiology, imaging, diagnostic aspiration or biopsy, antimicrobial penetration, hardware management, and surgical source control. This guide reviews native osteomyelitis, vertebral osteomyelitis and discitis, spinal epidural abscess, native-joint septic arthritis, prosthetic-joint infection, diabetic-foot osteomyelitis, pyomyositis, unusual pathogens, treatment duration, and high-yield inpatient and board-review principles.

Clinical Overview

Musculoskeletal infection may involve:

  • Bone: osteomyelitis
  • Intervertebral disc and adjacent vertebral endplates: spondylodiscitis
  • Spinal epidural space: epidural abscess
  • Native synovial joint: septic arthritis
  • Prosthetic joint or orthopedic hardware: implant-associated infection
  • Skeletal muscle: pyomyositis
  • Adjacent soft tissue, tendon sheath, or bursa

The key management challenge is that infected bone, necrotic tissue, avascular sequestra, abscesses, and biofilm-covered implants are difficult to sterilize with antibiotics alone.

Microbiologic Diagnosis + Active Antibiotic + Source Control = Best Chance of Cure

The Major Questions

  1. Which anatomic structure is infected?
  2. Is the infection hematogenous, contiguous, postoperative, or implant associated?
  3. Is the patient neurologically or hemodynamically unstable?
  4. Can blood, synovial fluid, bone, disc, or deep tissue be sampled before antibiotics?
  5. Is an abscess, necrotic bone, unstable spine, infected joint, or implant present?
  6. Can the infection be cured with antibiotics alone?
  7. What is the appropriate route and duration of treatment?
Do not allow a prolonged empiric-antibiotic course to replace diagnostic aspiration, biopsy, or surgical source control when those procedures are needed.

Musculoskeletal Infection Emergencies

  • Spinal epidural abscess with neurologic deficit
  • Spinal infection with cord or cauda-equina compression
  • Septic arthritis of a major native joint
  • Necrotizing soft-tissue infection extending to muscle or bone
  • Infected prosthetic joint with sepsis
  • Diabetic-foot infection with deep abscess, compartment syndrome, gangrene, or severe ischemia
  • Open fracture or postoperative infection with unstable hardware

Classification

Osteomyelitis by Route of Infection

Type Mechanism Common Setting
Hematogenous Bloodstream seeding of bone Vertebral osteomyelitis, bacteremia, injection drug use
Contiguous spread Extension from adjacent soft-tissue infection or ulcer Diabetic-foot ulcer, pressure injury
Direct inoculation Trauma, surgery, open fracture, injection, or procedure Postoperative or post-traumatic osteomyelitis
Implant associated Microbial adherence and biofilm on prosthetic material Prosthetic joints, plates, screws, rods, spinal hardware

Acute vs Chronic Osteomyelitis

Feature Acute Chronic
Tempo Days to weeks Weeks to months or relapsing over years
Pathology Acute inflammation and marrow edema Necrotic bone, sequestrum, involucrum, sinus tract
Source control May respond to antibiotics if diagnosed early Frequently requires debridement
Relapse risk Lower with early effective therapy Substantial if devitalized bone or hardware remains

Native vs Implant-Associated Infection

Native infection occurs without foreign material. Implant-associated infection is more difficult to eradicate because organisms form biofilm on the prosthesis or hardware.

Foreign Material + Biofilm = Reduced Antibiotic Susceptibility and Greater Source-Control Need

Pathogenesis

Hematogenous Seeding

Transient or sustained bacteremia may seed areas of bone with favorable vascular anatomy. In adults, hematogenous osteomyelitis frequently affects the vertebral column.

Contiguous Spread

An ulcer or deep soft-tissue infection may extend through the cortex into bone. This is common in diabetes-related foot disease and pressure injuries.

Direct Inoculation

  • Open fracture
  • Orthopedic surgery
  • Penetrating trauma
  • Injection drug use
  • Joint injection
  • Animal or human bite involving bone or joint

Biofilm

Within biofilm, microorganisms attach to implant surfaces, produce an extracellular matrix, alter their metabolic state, and become less vulnerable to host immunity and antimicrobial therapy.

Sequestrum and Involucrum

  • Sequestrum: devitalized infected bone separated from viable bone
  • Involucrum: new bone formation around a sequestrum
Antibiotics cannot reliably sterilize a large avascular sequestrum. Chronic osteomyelitis with necrotic bone generally requires surgical debridement for cure.

Staphylococcus aureus

S. aureus is the single most important organism across hematogenous osteomyelitis, vertebral infection, septic arthritis, epidural abscess, and implant-associated infection.

Musculoskeletal infection caused by S. aureus should prompt careful evaluation for bacteremia, endocarditis, metastatic infection, and an uncontrolled intravascular source.

Risk Factors

General Risk Factors

  • Diabetes mellitus
  • Peripheral arterial disease
  • Chronic kidney disease or hemodialysis
  • Injection drug use
  • Recent bacteremia
  • Recent surgery or trauma
  • Prosthetic joint or orthopedic hardware
  • Chronic skin ulcer or pressure injury
  • Immunosuppression
  • Advanced age
  • Malignancy
  • Poor dentition or invasive infection elsewhere

Risk Factors for Vertebral Infection

  • S. aureus bacteremia
  • Injection drug use
  • Hemodialysis
  • Long-term vascular catheter
  • Recent spinal procedure
  • Endocarditis
  • Urinary or gastrointestinal bacteremia
  • Older age

Risk Factors for Septic Arthritis

  • Older age
  • Preexisting joint disease
  • Rheumatoid arthritis
  • Prosthetic joint
  • Recent joint surgery or injection
  • Immunosuppression
  • Diabetes
  • Bacteremia
  • Skin infection
  • Injection drug use

Risk Factors for Prosthetic-Joint Infection

  • Previous PJI
  • Recent arthroplasty
  • Wound drainage or dehiscence
  • Obesity
  • Diabetes
  • Immunosuppression
  • Hematogenous bacteremia
  • Revision surgery
  • Severe peripheral vascular disease

Initial Approach

Step 1: Identify Instability

  • Septic shock
  • Progressive neurologic deficit
  • Bowel or bladder dysfunction
  • Cauda-equina syndrome
  • Spinal instability
  • Acute joint destruction
  • Necrotizing infection
Neurologic deficit with suspected spinal infection requires emergency MRI and immediate spine-surgery consultation.

Step 2: Define the Anatomy

  • Focal bone pain
  • Back or neck pain
  • Pain with passive joint movement
  • Joint effusion
  • Overlying ulcer or sinus tract
  • Orthopedic hardware
  • Neurologic deficits
  • Deep muscular pain

Step 3: Obtain Microbiology Before Antibiotics When Safe

  • Blood cultures
  • Synovial-fluid aspiration
  • Deep tissue or bone culture
  • Image-guided vertebral or disc biopsy
  • Operative cultures

Step 4: Start Immediate Antibiotics When Necessary

Do not delay antimicrobial therapy for biopsy when the patient has:

  • Septic shock
  • Hemodynamic instability
  • Progressive neurologic compromise
  • Impending spinal-cord compression
  • Rapidly destructive septic arthritis

Step 5: Obtain Early Surgical Input

  • Orthopedic surgery
  • Spine surgery or neurosurgery
  • Podiatry
  • Interventional radiology
  • Plastic surgery or vascular surgery when appropriate

Test Clinical Role

Normal WBC Does Not Exclude Infection

Patients with vertebral osteomyelitis, chronic osteomyelitis, prosthetic-joint infection, or immune compromise may have a normal peripheral WBC count.

ESR and CRP

  • Useful screening tests in vertebral osteomyelitis
  • Frequently elevated in septic arthritis and osteomyelitis
  • Nonspecific and cannot identify the organism
  • May remain elevated despite clinical improvement
  • Should not be used alone to determine cure

Cultures, Aspiration, and Biopsy

Blood Cultures

Obtain at least two sets before antibiotics in suspected:

  • Vertebral osteomyelitis
  • Spinal epidural abscess
  • Hematogenous osteomyelitis
  • Septic arthritis with systemic illness
  • Pyomyositis
  • Prosthetic-joint infection with fever or acute symptoms

Bone Biopsy

Bone biopsy is the microbiologic gold standard for many forms of osteomyelitis.

Send Bone for:

  • Aerobic and anaerobic culture
  • Histopathology
  • Fungal culture when indicated
  • Mycobacterial culture when indicated
  • Molecular testing in selected culture-negative cases

Superficial Wound Cultures

A superficial swab from a chronic ulcer or draining wound frequently reflects colonizing organisms and may not identify the pathogen within bone.

Sinus-Tract Cultures

Sinus-tract cultures are generally unreliable for defining chronic osteomyelitis microbiology, although repeated isolation of S. aureus may sometimes correlate with the deep pathogen.

When Vertebral Biopsy May Be Unnecessary

Image-guided biopsy may not be required when:

  • Blood cultures grow S. aureus and MRI is compatible with vertebral osteomyelitis.
  • A typical bloodstream organism clearly explains the spinal infection.
  • Urgent surgery will provide operative specimens.

Culture-Negative Infection

If initial biopsy is nondiagnostic:

  • Review prior antibiotics.
  • Confirm that the sampled target was appropriate.
  • Send histopathology.
  • Consider repeat image-guided biopsy or open biopsy.
  • Consider fungal, mycobacterial, Brucella, or molecular testing.
  • Reconsider malignancy and inflammatory disease.

Imaging

Plain Radiography

Plain films are useful for:

  • Baseline anatomy
  • Fracture
  • Foreign body
  • Hardware position
  • Chronic cortical destruction
  • Periosteal reaction

Early osteomyelitis may have a normal radiograph because structural changes take time to develop.

MRI

MRI With Contrast = Preferred Test for Vertebral Osteomyelitis, Epidural Abscess, and Many Deep Bone Infections

MRI Can Demonstrate:

  • Marrow edema
  • Disc and endplate involvement
  • Epidural abscess
  • Paraspinal abscess
  • Joint infection
  • Pyomyositis
  • Sinus tracts
  • Osteomyelitis extent

CT

  • Better definition of cortical bone
  • Useful for surgical planning
  • Guides percutaneous biopsy
  • Detects gas, sequestra, and complex anatomy
  • May be used when MRI is contraindicated

Nuclear Imaging

Potential options include:

  • Technetium bone scan
  • Gallium scan
  • Tagged leukocyte scan
  • FDG PET/CT

These modalities may help when MRI cannot be performed or when multifocal or implant-associated infection is suspected.

Ultrasound

  • Detects joint effusion
  • Guides arthrocentesis
  • Identifies superficial abscess
  • Cannot exclude osteomyelitis
Imaging abnormalities may persist after microbiologic cure. Repeat imaging should be driven by clinical failure, neurologic deterioration, or source-control concerns rather than performed routinely.

General Osteomyelitis

Clinical Presentation

  • Focal bone pain
  • Localized tenderness
  • Swelling
  • Reduced function
  • Fever may be absent
  • Chronic draining sinus
  • Nonhealing ulcer with exposed bone
  • Relapsing infection at a previous surgical site

Diagnostic Framework

  1. Establish compatible clinical findings.
  2. Determine route and chronicity.
  3. Obtain inflammatory markers and blood cultures when appropriate.
  4. Use MRI or another suitable imaging modality.
  5. Obtain bone or deep tissue for microbiology and histopathology.
  6. Determine whether devitalized bone or hardware must be removed.

Antibiotic Principles

  • Use bactericidal therapy when possible.
  • Select an agent with reliable bone penetration and activity.
  • Narrow based on deep cultures.
  • Adjust for renal function and drug toxicity.
  • Do not assume IV therapy is always superior to a highly bioavailable oral agent.
  • Coordinate duration with surgical findings and source control.

Hematogenous Osteomyelitis

Hematogenous osteomyelitis results from bloodstream seeding. In adults, vertebral involvement is particularly common.

Sources

  • Intravascular catheter
  • Endocarditis
  • Skin and soft-tissue infection
  • Urinary tract bacteremia
  • Injection drug use
  • Hemodialysis access
  • Pneumonia or other systemic infection

Management

  • Obtain blood cultures.
  • Search for the bloodstream source.
  • Evaluate for endocarditis when indicated.
  • Image the symptomatic bone or spine.
  • Drain associated abscesses.
  • Use organism-directed therapy.
Do not treat hematogenous osteomyelitis as an isolated bone problem. Identify and control the source of bacteremia.

Contiguous and Post-Traumatic Osteomyelitis

Common Settings

  • Chronic diabetic ulcer
  • Pressure injury
  • Open fracture
  • Penetrating trauma
  • Postoperative wound
  • Radiation-damaged tissue
  • Peripheral arterial disease

Microbiology

Acute untreated contiguous infection may be caused by streptococci or S. aureus. Chronic, ischemic, previously treated, or healthcare-associated infection may be polymicrobial.

Management Priorities

  • Assess perfusion.
  • Debride necrotic tissue and bone.
  • Obtain deep cultures.
  • Relieve pressure.
  • Remove foreign material when necessary.
  • Restore soft-tissue coverage.
  • Optimize glucose and nutrition.

Open Fracture Infection

Open-fracture infection requires coordinated orthopedic and infectious disease management. The plan may include serial debridement, stabilization, dead-space management, local antimicrobial delivery, and delayed definitive closure.

Chronic Osteomyelitis

Features

  • Persistent or recurrent pain
  • Draining sinus tract
  • Necrotic bone
  • Sequestrum
  • Hardware exposure
  • Relapsing infection with the same organism
  • Failure after multiple antibiotic courses

Management

Debridement + Deep Culture + Dead-Space Management + Targeted Antibiotics + Soft-Tissue Coverage

When Cure May Be Difficult

  • Critical limb ischemia
  • Extensive necrotic bone
  • Hardware that cannot be removed
  • Severe medical comorbidity
  • Inability to obtain wound coverage
  • Persistent pressure or trauma

Suppressive Therapy

Long-term suppressive antibiotics may be considered when infected hardware or devitalized bone cannot be removed and definitive cure is not feasible.

Suppressive therapy controls infection but usually does not eradicate biofilm or necrotic bone. The indication, monitoring plan, expected duration, and signs of failure should be documented.

Native Vertebral Osteomyelitis and Discitis

Native vertebral osteomyelitis usually involves adjacent vertebral endplates and the intervening disc.

Clinical Presentation

  • Persistent focal back or neck pain
  • Pain worse with movement
  • Localized spinal tenderness
  • Fever may be absent
  • Radicular pain
  • Elevated ESR or CRP
  • History of bacteremia

When to Suspect It

  • New back pain with S. aureus bacteremia
  • Back pain plus fever
  • Back pain plus elevated inflammatory markers
  • Back pain after endocarditis or bloodstream infection
  • Back pain in a patient on hemodialysis
  • Back pain in a person who injects drugs
  • Persistent severe back pain without mechanical explanation

Initial Evaluation

  1. Perform a complete neurologic examination.
  2. Obtain ESR and CRP.
  3. Obtain at least two blood-culture sets.
  4. Obtain MRI of the affected spinal region with contrast when possible.
  5. Evaluate for endocarditis and another bacteremic source.
  6. Obtain image-guided biopsy if cultures do not establish the organism.

Holding Antibiotics

In a stable patient without neurologic compromise, antibiotics may be withheld temporarily until blood cultures and biopsy are obtained.

Do not withhold antibiotics in septic shock, hemodynamic instability, progressive neurologic deficit, or impending spinal-cord compression.

When Surgery Is Considered

  • Progressive neurologic deficit
  • Spinal instability
  • Significant vertebral destruction
  • Epidural or paraspinal abscess requiring drainage
  • Persistent bacteremia
  • Failure of appropriate medical therapy
  • Intractable pain
  • Need for diagnostic tissue when percutaneous biopsy fails

Duration

Most bacterial native vertebral osteomyelitis is treated for approximately six weeks with parenteral or highly bioavailable oral therapy.

Follow-Up MRI

Do not routinely repeat MRI when the patient is clinically improving and inflammatory markers are decreasing. Bone and disc changes may appear unchanged or worse despite successful treatment.

Spinal Epidural Abscess

Classic Triad

Back Pain + Fever + Neurologic Deficit
The full classic triad is present in only a minority of patients. Do not wait for all three findings.

Risk Factors

  • S. aureus bacteremia
  • Injection drug use
  • Diabetes
  • Hemodialysis
  • Spinal procedure
  • Epidural catheter
  • Endocarditis
  • Immunosuppression

Neurologic Warning Signs

  • Weakness
  • Sensory loss
  • Gait dysfunction
  • Saddle anesthesia
  • Urinary retention
  • Fecal incontinence
  • Hyperreflexia or flaccidity depending on level

Diagnosis

Urgent Contrast-Enhanced MRI

When symptoms are extensive or the level is uncertain, imaging of the entire spine may be necessary because noncontiguous infection can occur.

Management

  • Immediate spine-surgery or neurosurgical consultation
  • Blood cultures
  • Prompt MRSA and gram-negative antimicrobial coverage
  • Urgent decompression and drainage when neurologic deficits, compression, instability, or extensive abscess exists
  • Frequent documented neurologic examinations

Medical Management Alone

Selected patients without neurologic deficit, instability, uncontrolled sepsis, or major compression may be managed nonoperatively with extremely close monitoring.

Neurologic deterioration can become irreversible. Any worsening during medical management requires emergency reassessment for surgery.

Native-Joint Septic Arthritis

Septic arthritis is an infection of the synovial joint that can rapidly destroy cartilage and cause sepsis.

Clinical Presentation

  • Acute monoarticular pain
  • Joint swelling
  • Warmth
  • Effusion
  • Restricted active and passive range of motion
  • Fever may be absent

Commonly Affected Joints

  • Knee
  • Hip
  • Shoulder
  • Wrist
  • Ankle
  • Sternoclavicular joint in injection drug use
  • Sacroiliac joint

Diagnostic Principle

Suspected Septic Arthritis → Urgent Arthrocentesis

Synovial Fluid Tests

  • WBC count and differential
  • Gram stain
  • Aerobic and anaerobic culture
  • Crystal analysis
  • Additional fungal, mycobacterial, or molecular testing when indicated

Blood Cultures

Obtain blood cultures in febrile, systemically ill, immunocompromised, or hematogenous cases.

Antibiotics Before Aspiration

In a stable patient, aspirate before antibiotics. In shock or rapidly progressive sepsis, obtain cultures promptly and do not delay therapy.

Drainage

A septic joint requires removal of infected synovial fluid through:

  • Repeated needle aspiration
  • Arthroscopic washout
  • Open surgical drainage

Large joints, deep joints, loculated infection, delayed presentation, or inadequate response commonly require operative management.

Synovial Fluid Interpretation

Finding Interpretation
Purulent appearance Strong concern for septic arthritis but not organism specific
WBC >50,000 cells/µL Raises probability of bacterial septic arthritis
WBC >100,000 cells/µL Very high probability in the appropriate setting
WBC <50,000 cells/µL Does not exclude septic arthritis
High neutrophil percentage Supports acute bacterial inflammation
Positive Gram stain Useful when positive but insensitive
Positive culture Establishes microbiologic diagnosis
Monosodium urate or CPP crystals Diagnoses crystal deposition but does not exclude simultaneous infection
Crystals do not rule out septic arthritis. Gout and bacterial infection can coexist in the same joint.

Low Synovial WBC Can Occur With:

  • Early infection
  • Prior antibiotics
  • Leukopenia
  • Immunosuppression
  • Prosthetic-joint infection
  • Disseminated gonococcal infection

Serum Tests Cannot Replace Arthrocentesis

ESR, CRP, peripheral WBC, procalcitonin, and imaging may support the diagnosis but cannot safely exclude septic arthritis without synovial sampling when suspicion is meaningful.

Disseminated Gonococcal Infection

Clinical Syndromes

Arthritis-Dermatitis Syndrome

  • Migratory polyarthralgia
  • Tenosynovitis
  • Few painless or mildly painful pustular skin lesions
  • Fever

Purulent Monoarthritis

  • Acute swollen joint
  • Frequently knee, wrist, ankle, or elbow
  • Genital symptoms may be absent

Diagnostic Testing

  • NAAT from all exposed mucosal sites
  • Synovial-fluid culture
  • Blood cultures
  • Culture for susceptibility when possible
  • Testing for chlamydia, HIV, and syphilis

Treatment Principles

  • Ceftriaxone-based therapy
  • Treat possible chlamydia if it has not been excluded
  • Drain purulent joints
  • Notify and treat partners according to public-health guidance
Synovial and blood cultures are often negative in the arthritis-dermatitis syndrome. Positive mucosal NAAT supports the diagnosis.

Prosthetic-Joint Infection

Clinical Presentations

Pattern Typical Features
Early postoperative Wound drainage, erythema, fever, acute pain, implant instability
Delayed indolent Chronic pain, loosening, subtle dysfunction, low-grade inflammation
Acute hematogenous Sudden pain in a previously well-functioning prosthesis during bacteremia

Clinical Clues

  • New prosthetic-joint pain
  • Persistent wound drainage
  • Sinus tract communicating with the prosthesis
  • Joint effusion
  • Loosening
  • Acute dysfunction during bacteremia
  • Unexpected positive operative cultures
A sinus tract communicating with a prosthesis is definitive evidence of prosthetic-joint infection.

Diagnostic Evaluation

  • ESR and CRP
  • Plain radiograph
  • Joint aspiration for cell count, differential, and culture
  • Blood cultures in acute or febrile disease
  • Multiple intraoperative tissue cultures
  • Histopathology
  • Implant sonication in selected centers

Aspiration

Synovial WBC thresholds are lower in prosthetic joints than in native-joint septic arthritis and vary by:

  • Time since implantation
  • Joint involved
  • Acute vs chronic presentation
  • Laboratory assay

Culture Collection

  • Withhold antibiotics before aspiration or surgery when clinically safe.
  • Obtain several separate deep tissue specimens.
  • Avoid a single superficial swab.
  • Use prolonged incubation when low-virulence organisms such as Cutibacterium are suspected.

Prosthetic-Joint Surgical Strategies

Debridement, Antibiotics, and Implant Retention

DAIR may be considered when:

  • The implant is stable.
  • Symptoms are acute.
  • There is no sinus tract.
  • Soft tissue is suitable.
  • The organism is susceptible to an effective biofilm-active regimen.
  • Thorough debridement and modular-component exchange are feasible.
DAIR Works Best With Short Symptom Duration and a Stable Implant

One-Stage Exchange

The infected implant is removed and a new implant is placed during the same operation. This may be appropriate in selected patients with:

  • Known susceptible organism
  • Good soft tissue
  • Limited bone loss
  • Adequate surgical expertise
  • No major sinus or uncontrolled infection

Two-Stage Exchange

The prosthesis is removed, the joint is debrided, an antibiotic spacer may be placed, and reimplantation occurs later.

This remains a common strategy for chronic or complex PJI.

Resection Arthroplasty

Permanent removal without reimplantation may be considered when reconstruction is not feasible or infection risk is prohibitive.

Amputation

Reserved for severe uncontrollable infection, nonreconstructable tissue loss, critical ischemia, or life-threatening disease.

Chronic Suppression

Suppressive antibiotics may be used when:

  • Curative surgery is not possible.
  • The implant must remain.
  • The organism is susceptible to a tolerable oral agent.
  • The patient understands that relapse may occur.

Diabetes-Related Foot Osteomyelitis

When to Suspect Bone Infection

  • Chronic ulcer over a bony prominence
  • Visible or palpable bone
  • Positive probe-to-bone test
  • “Sausage toe”
  • Persistent drainage
  • Failure of a soft-tissue infection to resolve
  • Cortical destruction on imaging
  • Elevated ESR in the appropriate clinical setting

Initial Diagnostic Strategy

Probe-to-Bone + Plain Radiograph + ESR/CRP

When uncertainty remains, obtain MRI.

Bone Culture

When microbiologic diagnosis is needed, obtain bone rather than superficial wound culture.

Medical Treatment Without Surgery

Antibiotic treatment without bone resection may be considered in selected forefoot osteomyelitis when:

  • There is no immediate need for incision and drainage.
  • Peripheral arterial disease is absent or manageable.
  • There is no exposed bone requiring removal.
  • The infection is clinically stable.
  • Close follow-up is feasible.

Surgical Management

  • Drain abscesses.
  • Remove necrotic tissue.
  • Resect infected bone when indicated.
  • Restore perfusion.
  • Provide off-loading and wound care.
  • Obtain clean-margin bone specimens when possible.

Treatment Duration

Situation Typical Framework
Osteomyelitis treated without bone resection Approximately 6 weeks
Minor amputation with positive bone margin Up to approximately 3 weeks
Complete resection of infected bone with clean margins Short postoperative course may be sufficient
Antibiotics alone cannot overcome critical ischemia, persistent pressure, an undrained abscess, or extensive necrotic bone.

Fracture-Related and Orthopedic Hardware Infection

Clinical Clues

  • Persistent wound drainage
  • Sinus tract
  • Hardware exposure
  • Nonunion
  • New pain after initial improvement
  • Hardware loosening
  • Recurrent cellulitis over an implant

Management Questions

  1. Has the fracture healed?
  2. Is the hardware mechanically stable?
  3. Can hardware be removed without losing stability?
  4. Is the infection acute or chronic?
  5. Is there necrotic bone or a soft-tissue defect?

Possible Strategies

  • Debridement with implant retention
  • Hardware exchange
  • Temporary external fixation
  • Removal after fracture union
  • Local antibiotic beads or spacer
  • Soft-tissue flap coverage

Biofilm-Active Therapy

Rifampin combinations may be useful for susceptible staphylococcal infection when hardware is retained.

Never use rifampin alone. Begin it only as part of an active combination after adequate debridement and control of ongoing bacteremia or drainage.

Sternal and Postoperative Bone Infection

Risk Factors

  • Recent sternotomy
  • Diabetes
  • Obesity
  • Prolonged surgery
  • Reoperation
  • Wound dehiscence

Clinical Features

  • Sternal pain
  • Wound drainage
  • Instability or clicking
  • Fever
  • Deep collection
  • Mediastinitis

Management

  • Cardiothoracic surgical evaluation
  • CT chest when deep infection is suspected
  • Deep operative cultures
  • Debridement
  • Hardware evaluation
  • Negative-pressure therapy or flap coverage
  • Prolonged organism-directed antibiotics

Sacroiliac, Pelvic, and Sternoclavicular Infection

Sacroiliac Septic Arthritis

  • Buttock, hip, groin, or low-back pain
  • Pain with walking
  • Fever may be absent
  • MRI is often required
  • Blood cultures may identify the organism

Sternoclavicular Septic Arthritis

Risk Factors

  • Injection drug use
  • Diabetes
  • Central venous catheter
  • Bacteremia

Complications

  • Mediastinitis
  • Chest-wall abscess
  • Osteomyelitis
  • Great-vessel involvement

CT or MRI is often needed, and surgical debridement may be required.

Special and Indolent Organisms

Tuberculous Osteoarticular Infection

  • Chronic pain and swelling
  • Cold abscess
  • Minimal systemic inflammation
  • Spinal disease may cause vertebral collapse and gibbus deformity
  • Requires tissue for histopathology, culture, and molecular testing

Brucellar Spondylitis

Clues

  • Unpasteurized dairy
  • Livestock exposure
  • Endemic travel
  • Fever, sweats, and back pain

Obtain Brucella serology and cultures using appropriate laboratory precautions.

Fungal Osteomyelitis

  • Candida after candidemia, injection drug use, surgery, or immunosuppression
  • Aspergillus in severe immunocompromise
  • Endemic fungi based on geography
  • Often requires prolonged therapy and selective surgery

Cutibacterium acnes

  • Shoulder prosthetic-joint infection
  • Spinal hardware infection
  • Indolent pain and loosening
  • Requires multiple deep cultures and prolonged incubation

Kingella and Pediatric Organisms

Although this guide focuses on adults, remember that organism distribution and synovial-fluid findings differ in children.

Empiric Antibiotic Therapy

When to Withhold Empiric Therapy

In a stable patient with chronic osteomyelitis or vertebral infection, consider waiting for diagnostic specimens.

When to Start Immediately

  • Septic shock
  • Neurologic compromise
  • Spinal-cord compression
  • Rapidly destructive septic arthritis
  • Severe diabetic-foot infection
  • Necrotizing infection

Native Vertebral Osteomyelitis

Empiric treatment usually needs:

  • MRSA coverage
  • Streptococcal coverage
  • Aerobic gram-negative coverage

A common structure is vancomycin plus ceftriaxone or cefepime, depending on resistant gram-negative and Pseudomonas risk.

Native-Joint Septic Arthritis

  • Vancomycin when MRSA is possible
  • Add a third- or fourth-generation cephalosporin when gram-negative infection is possible
  • Use ceftriaxone for suspected gonococcal infection
  • Broaden for bites, injection exposure, or healthcare-associated infection

Prosthetic-Joint or Hardware Infection

Empiric therapy may require:

  • Vancomycin
  • Plus cefepime, ceftriaxone, or another gram-negative agent
  • Broader anaerobic coverage only when the clinical setting supports it

Diabetic-Foot Osteomyelitis

Empiric spectrum depends on:

  • Severity
  • Previous antibiotics
  • Previous cultures
  • Ischemia and necrosis
  • Healthcare exposure
  • Local resistance
Do not automatically use vancomycin plus piperacillin-tazobactam for every bone or joint infection. Match empiric breadth to severity, source, previous cultures, and exposure.

Organism-Directed Therapy

Organism Common Preferred Therapy Important Considerations
MSSA Cefazolin, nafcillin, or oxacillin Beta-lactams are preferred over vancomycin
MRSA Vancomycin or high-dose daptomycin Monitor exposure, renal function, CK, and source control
Streptococci Penicillin, ceftriaxone, or cefazolin depending on species Narrow therapy when susceptible
Enterococcus faecalis Ampicillin when susceptible Evaluate for bacteremia and endocarditis
Enterobacterales Ceftriaxone, cefepime, fluoroquinolone, or susceptibility-guided agent Consider ESBL and AmpC mechanisms
Pseudomonas Cefepime, ceftazidime, piperacillin-tazobactam, or another active agent Use susceptibility and source-control data
Staphylococcal retained implant Active companion drug plus rifampin in selected cases Never rifampin monotherapy
Cutibacterium Penicillin, ceftriaxone, or another susceptible agent Confirm significance with multiple deep cultures

MSSA Pearl

Do not continue vancomycin as definitive therapy for MSSA osteomyelitis or septic arthritis when an antistaphylococcal beta-lactam can be safely used.

MRSA Vancomycin Monitoring

For serious MRSA infection, use AUC-guided vancomycin dosing when feasible, generally targeting an AUC/MIC of 400–600.

Rifampin

Rifampin is valuable because of activity against staphylococci in biofilm, but:

  • Resistance emerges rapidly with monotherapy.
  • Drug interactions are substantial.
  • Hepatotoxicity can occur.
  • It should be paired with another active agent.
  • It should not substitute for surgical debridement.

Source Control

Source-Control Procedures

  • Joint washout
  • Abscess drainage
  • Spinal decompression
  • Debridement of necrotic bone
  • Removal or exchange of infected implant
  • Resection of infected diabetic-foot bone
  • Restoration of blood flow
  • Soft-tissue flap or wound coverage

Indicators of Inadequate Source Control

  • Persistent bacteremia
  • Continued fever or shock
  • Worsening pain
  • New neurologic deficit
  • Persistent wound drainage
  • Enlarging abscess
  • Relapse after treatment
  • Implant loosening
Persistent Infection Despite Active Antibiotics → Reassess Source Control

Treatment Duration

Native-joint septic arthritis Often 2–4 weeks Depends on organism, joint, drainage, osteomyelitis, and response
Gonococcal arthritis Usually shorter than nongonococcal septic arthritis Transition according to response and susceptibility

Factors Requiring Individualization

  • Inadequate debridement
  • Retained hardware
  • Extensive necrotic bone
  • Persistent bacteremia
  • Immunosuppression
  • Poor vascular supply
  • Fungal, mycobacterial, or resistant organism
  • Slow clinical response
Do not extend therapy solely because MRI remains abnormal. Clinical response, inflammatory-marker trend, microbiology, and source-control status are more important.

Oral Therapy and OPAT

IV Therapy Is Not Always Required for the Entire Course

Highly bioavailable oral therapy may be appropriate when:

  • The organism and susceptibilities are known.
  • The oral drug reaches adequate serum and bone concentrations.
  • The patient is clinically stable.
  • Source control is adequate.
  • GI absorption is reliable.
  • Adherence and follow-up are reliable.

Potential Oral Agents

  • Fluoroquinolones for susceptible gram-negative infection
  • Linezolid for selected resistant gram-positive infection
  • TMP-SMX in selected susceptible infections
  • Clindamycin when susceptible
  • Doxycycline in selected infections
  • Rifampin-containing combinations for retained staphylococcal implants
  • High-dose oral beta-lactams in selected susceptible infections

OPAT Requirements

  • Stable access plan
  • Drug-toxicity laboratory monitoring
  • Defined treatment stop date
  • Wound and surgical follow-up
  • Assessment for line complications
  • Clear plan for new fever, pain, drainage, or neurologic symptoms
Do not discharge a patient with spinal infection to OPAT without a documented neurologic baseline, source-control plan, follow-up appointment, laboratory schedule, and clear return precautions.

Monitoring Treatment Response

Clinical Monitoring

  • Pain and function
  • Fever curve
  • Neurologic examination
  • Joint range of motion
  • Wound drainage
  • Hardware stability
  • Ability to bear weight

Laboratory Monitoring

  • CRP trend
  • ESR trend when useful
  • CBC
  • Creatinine
  • Liver enzymes
  • Vancomycin AUC
  • CK with daptomycin
  • Cytopenias with linezolid
  • Drug interactions with rifampin

Repeat Blood Cultures

Repeat cultures are important with:

  • S. aureus bacteremia
  • Candidemia
  • Persistent fever or instability
  • Endovascular-source concern
  • Inadequate source control

Repeat Imaging

Repeat imaging when:

  • Neurologic status worsens
  • Pain or fever is worsening
  • Inflammatory markers rise with clinical concern
  • Persistent bacteremia occurs
  • New abscess or implant failure is suspected

Suspected Treatment Failure

Common Causes

  • Incorrect organism
  • Inactive antimicrobial
  • Inadequate dose
  • Poor adherence or absorption
  • Undrained abscess
  • Necrotic bone
  • Retained infected implant
  • Spinal instability
  • Critical limb ischemia
  • Incorrect diagnosis

Evaluation

  1. Reassess the original diagnosis.
  2. Review all microbiology and pathology.
  3. Confirm dosing and drug exposure.
  4. Repeat blood cultures when appropriate.
  5. Reassess source control.
  6. Obtain targeted repeat imaging.
  7. Consider repeat biopsy or surgery.
  8. Evaluate for resistant, fungal, or mycobacterial infection.
Do not respond to persistent symptoms by indefinitely broadening antibiotics without reexamining anatomy, microbiology, source control, and alternative diagnoses.

Daily Bone, Joint, and Spine Infection Checklist

  • What exact structure is infected?
  • Is the infection native or implant associated?
  • What is the route of infection?
  • What organism has been identified?
  • Are blood cultures cleared?
  • Was a deep specimen obtained?
  • Is an abscess present?
  • Is there necrotic bone?
  • Is hardware stable and can it remain?
  • Has the joint been adequately drained?
  • Is the spine stable?
  • Is the neurologic examination changing?
  • Is endocarditis evaluation needed?
  • Can empiric therapy be narrowed?
  • Can treatment transition to oral therapy?
  • What is the first day of effective therapy?
  • What is the planned duration and stop date?
  • What surgical, wound, vascular, and rehabilitation follow-up is required?

How to Present on ID Rounds

  • Exact anatomic diagnosis
  • Native versus prosthetic or hardware associated
  • Route and likely source
  • Blood-culture dates and clearance
  • Deep culture and pathology results
  • Imaging findings
  • Neurologic examination when spinal disease exists
  • Operative procedures and source-control status
  • Current antibiotic, dose, and start date
  • Oral options
  • Planned duration
  • Follow-up strategy
Avoid saying only, “The patient has osteomyelitis and is on vancomycin.” State the bone involved, mechanism, culture source, organism, surgical status, implant status, and planned duration.

Common Clinical Scenarios

Case 1: S. aureus Bacteremia and New Back Pain

A patient with MSSA bacteremia develops focal lumbar pain.

Assume vertebral osteomyelitis or epidural infection is possible. Obtain a detailed neurologic examination and urgent spinal MRI.

Case 2: Stable Vertebral Osteomyelitis With Negative Cultures

A stable patient has MRI-compatible vertebral osteomyelitis but negative blood cultures and no neurologic deficit.

Obtain image-guided biopsy before starting empiric antibiotics when clinically safe.

Case 3: Back Pain and Urinary Retention

A patient with fever and severe back pain develops urinary retention and leg weakness.

This is spinal-cord or cauda-equina compression until proven otherwise. Obtain emergency MRI and immediate spine-surgery consultation.

Case 4: Hot Swollen Knee With Urate Crystals

Arthrocentesis reveals monosodium urate crystals and 70,000 WBC/µL with 92% neutrophils.

Crystals do not exclude infection. Continue septic-arthritis evaluation and culture-directed management.

Case 5: Acute Pain in a Previously Well-Functioning Prosthetic Knee

A patient with S. aureus bacteremia develops sudden prosthetic-knee pain.

Suspect acute hematogenous PJI. Obtain orthopedic consultation and aspiration when feasible.

Case 6: Chronic Prosthetic-Joint Pain With a Draining Sinus

A patient has a sinus tract over a total hip arthroplasty.

A communicating sinus tract establishes prosthetic-joint infection. Curative management generally requires an implant-directed surgical strategy.

Case 7: Diabetic Foot Ulcer With Positive Probe-to-Bone

A chronic plantar ulcer probes to bone and radiographs are equivocal.

Osteomyelitis is likely. Obtain MRI if needed and pursue bone culture rather than relying on a superficial swab.

Case 8: Persistent Fever After Septic-Knee Washout

A patient remains febrile with ongoing knee pain after arthroscopic drainage.

Reassess for inadequate joint drainage, adjacent osteomyelitis, resistant organism, bacteremia, or metastatic infection.

Case 9: Shoulder Prosthesis With Indolent Pain

A patient has chronic shoulder-prosthesis pain without fever and low inflammatory markers.

Consider Cutibacterium acnes. Obtain multiple deep specimens and ensure prolonged culture incubation.

Case 10: Sickle-Cell Disease With Focal Bone Pain and Fever

A patient with sickle-cell disease has focal tibial pain and fever.

Consider both S. aureus and Salmonella osteomyelitis while distinguishing infection from vaso-occlusive pain.

Case 11: Sternoclavicular Pain in Injection Drug Use

A patient who injects drugs develops unilateral sternoclavicular swelling and fever.

Evaluate for sternoclavicular septic arthritis, osteomyelitis, chest-wall abscess, and mediastinal extension.

Case 12: Persistent MRI Abnormality After Treatment

A patient with vertebral osteomyelitis has improved clinically with falling CRP, but follow-up MRI still shows vertebral inflammation.

Persistent MRI abnormalities do not independently prove treatment failure. Correlate with symptoms, examination, markers, bacteremia, and soft-tissue findings.

Common Pitfalls

  • Ruling out osteomyelitis because the WBC count is normal
  • Ruling out spinal infection because fever is absent
  • Failing to ask about new back pain during S. aureus bacteremia
  • Delaying MRI in a patient with neurologic deficits
  • Waiting for the complete epidural-abscess triad
  • Giving antibiotics before biopsy in a stable culture-negative vertebral infection
  • Delaying antibiotics in shock or neurologic compromise
  • Using a superficial ulcer swab to define bone microbiology
  • Failing to send histopathology with bone biopsy
  • Calling septic arthritis unlikely because synovial WBC is below 50,000
  • Calling a joint noninfected because crystals are present
  • Treating septic arthritis without adequate drainage
  • Continuing vancomycin for MSSA infection
  • Using rifampin alone
  • Starting rifampin while bacteremia or uncontrolled drainage persists
  • Ignoring a painful prosthetic joint during bacteremia
  • Missing PJI because ESR and CRP are low
  • Treating chronic osteomyelitis indefinitely without debridement assessment
  • Using antibiotics alone despite critical ischemia or necrotic bone
  • Repeating MRI routinely despite clinical improvement
  • Extending therapy solely because imaging remains abnormal
  • Discharging spinal infection without a documented neurologic baseline
  • Failing to evaluate for endocarditis in hematogenous musculoskeletal infection

Board and ID Consult Pearls

  • S. aureus is the most important cause of adult hematogenous bone and joint infection.
  • Adult hematogenous osteomyelitis commonly involves the spine.
  • Persistent focal back pain plus elevated ESR or CRP should raise concern for vertebral osteomyelitis.
  • MRI is the preferred test for vertebral osteomyelitis and spinal epidural abscess.
  • Fever is frequently absent in vertebral osteomyelitis.
  • The full epidural-abscess triad is uncommon.
  • Neurologic deficit requires emergency MRI and surgical consultation.
  • Blood cultures should be obtained in suspected vertebral infection.
  • A stable culture-negative vertebral infection should generally be biopsied before empiric antibiotics.
  • S. aureus bacteremia plus compatible spinal MRI may eliminate the need for percutaneous biopsy.
  • Most bacterial native vertebral osteomyelitis is treated for approximately six weeks.
  • Routine follow-up MRI is unnecessary in clinically improving vertebral osteomyelitis.
  • Septic arthritis is a drainage disease as well as an antibiotic disease.
  • Pain with passive range of motion strongly supports intra-articular pathology.
  • Synovial WBC below 50,000 does not exclude septic arthritis.
  • Crystals do not exclude concomitant bacterial arthritis.
  • Obtain mucosal NAAT in suspected disseminated gonococcal infection.
  • A sinus tract communicating with a prosthesis establishes PJI.
  • DAIR works best with short symptom duration, a stable implant, and no sinus tract.
  • Multiple deep operative cultures are required for PJI diagnosis.
  • Cutibacterium acnes is an important cause of indolent shoulder-prosthesis infection.
  • Rifampin should never be used alone.
  • MSSA bone and joint infection should receive a beta-lactam when possible.
  • Bone culture is preferred over superficial wound culture in diabetic-foot osteomyelitis.
  • A positive probe-to-bone test raises the probability of diabetic-foot osteomyelitis.
  • Diabetic-foot osteomyelitis without bone resection is commonly treated for approximately six weeks.
  • Peripheral arterial disease, pressure, and wound care are as important as antibiotic selection in diabetic-foot infection.
  • Chronic osteomyelitis with a sequestrum usually requires debridement.
  • Spontaneous relapse suggests retained necrotic bone, implant biofilm, or inadequate source control.
  • Highly bioavailable oral antibiotics can complete treatment in selected stable patients.

Bottom Line

Bone, joint, and spinal infections require precise anatomic diagnosis, microbiologic sampling, appropriate imaging, active antimicrobial therapy, and early assessment for source control. Blood cultures are essential in hematogenous and spinal infection, while synovial-fluid aspiration is essential in suspected septic arthritis. Bone, deep tissue, or operative specimens are superior to superficial wound cultures.

Persistent back pain with bacteremia or elevated inflammatory markers should prompt evaluation for vertebral osteomyelitis. Neurologic deficit, urinary retention, or cord-compression symptoms require emergency MRI and immediate spine-surgery consultation. Stable culture-negative vertebral infection should generally undergo biopsy before antibiotics, whereas unstable patients require immediate treatment.

A septic native joint must be drained. Synovial WBC thresholds are supportive rather than absolute, and crystals do not exclude infection. Prosthetic-joint infection requires an implant-specific surgical plan, and a communicating sinus tract establishes the diagnosis.

Chronic osteomyelitis, diabetic-foot osteomyelitis, and implant-associated infection cannot be managed by antibiotics alone when necrotic bone, abscess, ischemia, pressure, unstable hardware, or biofilm remains. Treatment duration depends on the anatomy, organism, operation, hardware status, and adequacy of source control.

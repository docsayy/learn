Point-of-Care Ultrasound in Shock and Respiratory Failure

Added July 15, 2026 • Pulmonary and Critical Care • ~30 min read

A focused critical-care ultrasound framework integrating cardiac, lung, pleural, vascular, and abdominal findings while avoiding overinterpretation of isolated images or IVC measurements.

Clinical Overview

POCUS answers focused bedside questions and should be integrated with the history, examination, ECG, laboratory data, and formal imaging. It is strongest when serial findings change management.

Question → Image → Interpretation → Clinical Action → Reassessment

Focused Shock Examination

  • LV systolic function and gross chamber size
  • RV dilation, pressure overload, and septal flattening
  • Pericardial effusion and tamponade physiology
  • Lung B-lines, consolidation, and pleural sliding
  • Aorta and abdominal free fluid when relevant
  • Venous congestion and response to intervention

Lung Ultrasound Patterns

FindingSuggestsCautions
Diffuse bilateral B-linesInterstitial edema or diffuse parenchymal processNot specific for cardiogenic edema
Focal B-lines/consolidationPneumonia, contusion, infarctionCorrelate with clinical context
Absent sliding with lung pointPneumothoraxAbsent sliding has other causes
Anechoic pleural spacePleural effusionCharacterize complexity and safe window

Focused Cardiac Views

  • Parasternal long and short axis
  • Apical four chamber
  • Subcostal four chamber and IVC
  • Additional views based on the question

Estimate rather than over-quantify when image quality is limited. Formal echocardiography is needed for valve severity, detailed diastology, complex congenital disease, and many regional-wall-motion questions.

Right-Ventricular Assessment

  • RV/LV size ratio
  • Septal flattening
  • Tricuspid annular motion
  • IVC and venous congestion
  • Pulmonary acceleration or Doppler clues when trained
RV dilation is not synonymous with acute PE. Chronic pulmonary hypertension, ventilation, and volume status matter.

Pericardial Effusion and Tamponade

  • Right-atrial systolic collapse
  • Right-ventricular diastolic collapse
  • Plethoric venous system
  • Marked respiratory variation in Doppler flow
  • Clinical hypotension, tachycardia, or obstructive physiology

Tamponade is a clinical-hemodynamic diagnosis. Positive-pressure ventilation can worsen collapse.

IVC Interpretation

IVC findings can support an overall picture but are unreliable as a stand-alone fluid-responsiveness test. Spontaneous effort, PEEP, abdominal pressure, tricuspid disease, and RV function change its behavior.

Serial Ultrasound

  • VTI response after passive leg raise or fluid
  • B-line change during diuresis
  • RV response after PE therapy
  • Resolution of pneumothorax or effusion
  • Cardiac function after vasopressor or inotrope change

Procedure Guidance

  • Ultrasound-guided thoracentesis
  • Central venous access
  • Arterial access
  • Paracentesis
  • Pericardiocentesis in selected emergencies

Common Pitfalls

  • Diagnosing volume depletion from a small IVC alone
  • Calling all B-lines heart failure
  • Diagnosing PE from RV dilation alone
  • Ignoring image quality
  • Failing to save images
  • Allowing POCUS to delay definitive CT, surgery, or formal echo

Bottom Line

POCUS is most useful as a serial extension of the examination. Ask a narrow question, obtain adequate views, integrate the findings, and confirm complex or high-stakes diagnoses when needed.

Blood Gas Interpretation: The Complete ICU Resident Guide

Added July 5, 2026 • Critical Care • ~55 min read

Blood gases are not just numbers. They are a bedside physiology test showing ventilation, oxygenation, acid-base status, compensation, and whether the patient is failing noninvasive support or needs ventilator changes. This guide covers ABG/VBG basics, chemistry bicarbonate, compensation formulas, anion gap, corrected anion gap, delta gap, HAGMA, NAGMA, metabolic alkalosis, ventilator adjustment, BiPAP, HFNC, intubation, and ICU attending-style questions.

Why Blood Gases Matter

A blood gas answers four ICU questions quickly:

  • Ventilation: Is the patient clearing CO₂?
  • Oxygenation: Is oxygen transfer adequate?
  • Acid-base: Is the pH dangerous, and what is driving it?
  • Compensation: Is the body responding appropriately, or is there a mixed disorder?
The ABG does not diagnose the disease by itself. It tells you the physiology. The diagnosis comes from matching that physiology to the clinical situation.

When to Order an ABG

  • Hypoxemia or escalating oxygen requirement
  • Respiratory distress or suspected ventilatory failure
  • Altered mental status where CO₂ retention is possible
  • Shock, severe sepsis, or high lactate concern
  • DKA, severe metabolic acidosis, poisoning, renal failure
  • Mechanical ventilation initiation or major ventilator changes
  • Monitoring severe COPD/asthma exacerbation
  • Assessing P/F ratio in ARDS

When a VBG May Be Enough

  • Screening pH and CO₂ trend in many stable patients
  • DKA monitoring when oxygenation is not the question
  • Serial acid-base trend when arterial access is not needed
A VBG is not reliable for PaO₂ or oxygenation. If the question is oxygenation, shunt, P/F ratio, or ARDS severity, use an ABG.

ABG vs VBG

Value ABG VBG Practical Meaning
pH Gold standard Usually ~0.03 lower than arterial VBG is usually acceptable for acidemia screening
PaCO₂ / PvCO₂ Best for ventilation Usually higher than arterial, less predictable in shock Useful trend, but ABG better if exact CO₂ matters
PaO₂ Gold standard Not useful Do not use VBG oxygen for oxygenation decisions
HCO₃⁻ Calculated Calculated Usually close to chemistry bicarbonate but not identical
Lactate Can measure Often acceptable Interpret in clinical context

Memory Aid: “VBG for pH, ABG for O₂.”

A VBG can often answer “is the patient acidemic?” but it cannot answer “is the patient oxygenating?”

Every Blood Gas Number Explained

Value Normal What It Means High-Yield ICU Interpretation
pH 7.35–7.45 Net acid-base status Acidemia below 7.35, alkalemia above 7.45
PaCO₂ 35–45 mm Hg Respiratory acid / ventilation High CO₂ = hypoventilation; low CO₂ = hyperventilation
HCO₃⁻ 22–26 mEq/L Metabolic base / kidney side Low in metabolic acidosis, high in metabolic alkalosis or chronic CO₂ retention
PaO₂ ~80–100 mm Hg on room air, age dependent Arterial oxygen tension Use with FiO₂ to calculate P/F ratio
SaO₂ ~95–100% Hemoglobin oxygen saturation Can be misleading in CO poisoning, methemoglobinemia, poor waveform
Base excess −2 to +2 Metabolic acid-base burden Negative = metabolic acidosis; positive = metabolic alkalosis
Lactate Usually <2 mmol/L Perfusion/metabolic stress marker High lactate is not always shock, but in shock it matters
CO₂ is the respiratory acid. Bicarbonate is the metabolic base. pH is the final scoreboard.

The Carbonic Acid Equation

CO₂ + H₂O ⇌ H₂CO₃ ⇌ H⁺ + HCO₃⁻

The lungs control CO₂. The kidneys control bicarbonate over time. That is the entire logic of acid-base compensation.

ABG vs Chemistry CO₂ / Bicarbonate

On a BMP or CMP, the “CO₂” is not the same thing as PaCO₂. Serum chemistry CO₂ is mostly bicarbonate.

Test What It Reports Clinical Meaning
ABG PaCO₂ Dissolved arterial carbon dioxide gas Ventilation
ABG HCO₃⁻ Calculated bicarbonate using pH and PaCO₂ Metabolic component
BMP/CMP CO₂ Total CO₂ content, mostly bicarbonate Serum bicarbonate estimate

Why They May Differ

  • ABG bicarbonate is calculated; chemistry bicarbonate is measured as total CO₂.
  • Different sampling times.
  • Air exposure and processing delays.
  • Rapid clinical changes.
  • Lab variation.

Attending: “The BMP CO₂ is 30 but ABG bicarbonate is 27. Which is right?”

Answer: They measure related but not identical things. BMP CO₂ is mostly measured serum bicarbonate/total CO₂. ABG HCO₃⁻ is calculated from pH and PaCO₂. If close, interpret the trend clinically. If very different, repeat or check timing/sample issues.

Memory Aid: “BMP CO₂ = bicarbonate-ish.”

Do not confuse chemistry CO₂ with PaCO₂. Chemistry CO₂ lives on the metabolic side; PaCO₂ lives on the respiratory side.

Stepwise Blood Gas Interpretation

  1. Look at pH: acidemia, alkalemia, or normal?
  2. Look at PaCO₂: is CO₂ moving in the direction that explains pH?
  3. Look at HCO₃⁻: is bicarbonate moving in the direction that explains pH?
  4. Identify the primary disorder.
  5. Check expected compensation.
  6. Calculate anion gap.
  7. Correct anion gap for albumin.
  8. If anion gap is high, calculate delta gap.
  9. Assess oxygenation: PaO₂, SpO₂, FiO₂, P/F ratio, A-a gradient.
  10. Match the physiology to the patient.

Memory Aid: ROME

  • Respiratory = Opposite: pH and PaCO₂ move opposite directions.
  • Metabolic = Equal: pH and HCO₃⁻ move same direction.

Expected Compensation Formulas

Primary Disorder Expected Compensation
Metabolic acidosis Expected PaCO₂ = 1.5 × HCO₃⁻ + 8 ± 2
Metabolic alkalosis Expected PaCO₂ ≈ 0.7 × (HCO₃⁻ − 24) + 40 ± 5
Acute respiratory acidosis HCO₃⁻ rises ~1 mEq/L for every 10 mm Hg PaCO₂ above 40
Chronic respiratory acidosis HCO₃⁻ rises ~3.5–4 mEq/L for every 10 mm Hg PaCO₂ above 40
Acute respiratory alkalosis HCO₃⁻ falls ~2 mEq/L for every 10 mm Hg PaCO₂ below 40
Chronic respiratory alkalosis HCO₃⁻ falls ~4–5 mEq/L for every 10 mm Hg PaCO₂ below 40
Compensation never overshoots. If pH crosses fully to the opposite side, there is another primary disorder.

Shortcut: The 1-2-3-4 Rule

  • Acute respiratory acidosis: HCO₃ rises 1 per 10 CO₂.
  • Chronic respiratory acidosis: HCO₃ rises 4 per 10 CO₂.
  • Acute respiratory alkalosis: HCO₃ falls 2 per 10 CO₂.
  • Chronic respiratory alkalosis: HCO₃ falls 4–5 per 10 CO₂.

Attending: “PaCO₂ is 60 and HCO₃ is 26. Acute or chronic respiratory acidosis?”

Answer: CO₂ is 20 above normal. Acute compensation should raise HCO₃ by about 2, from 24 to 26. That fits acute respiratory acidosis.

Attending: “PaCO₂ is 60 and HCO₃ is 32. Acute or chronic?”

Answer: CO₂ is 20 above normal. Chronic compensation raises HCO₃ by about 8, from 24 to 32. That fits chronic respiratory acidosis, like COPD or obesity hypoventilation.

Anion Gap

Anion gap = Na⁺ − (Cl⁻ + HCO₃⁻)

Normal anion gap is often around 8–12 mEq/L, but depends on the lab. Albumin is the major unmeasured anion, so low albumin can hide a high anion gap acidosis.

Albumin-Corrected Anion Gap

Corrected AG = AG + 2.5 × (4 − albumin)

Example: Na 140, Cl 110, HCO₃ 18, albumin 2.0.

  • AG = 140 − (110 + 18) = 12
  • Correction = 2.5 × (4 − 2) = 5
  • Corrected AG = 17
A “normal” anion gap in a hypoalbuminemic ICU patient may actually be elevated after correction.

High Anion Gap Metabolic Acidosis

High anion gap metabolic acidosis means bicarbonate is low because unmeasured acids have accumulated.

Mnemonic: GOLD MARK

  • Glycols: ethylene glycol, propylene glycol
  • Oxoproline: chronic acetaminophen → 5-oxoproline
  • L-lactate
  • D-lactate
  • Methanol
  • Aspirin / salicylates
  • Renal failure / uremia
  • Ketoacidosis: diabetic, alcoholic, starvation

Common ICU HAGMA Causes

Cause Clues Management Direction
Lactic acidosis Shock, sepsis, hypoxemia, seizures, beta-agonists, liver failure Fix perfusion/oxygen delivery/source control; trend lactate
DKA Hyperglycemia, ketones, low bicarbonate, high AG Fluids, insulin, potassium, close gap
Alcoholic ketoacidosis Alcohol use, poor intake, vomiting, normal/low glucose Dextrose, thiamine, fluids, electrolytes
Uremia Renal failure, high BUN/Cr Dialysis if indicated, treat AKI/CKD cause
Salicylates Mixed respiratory alkalosis + metabolic acidosis, tinnitus Alkalinize serum/urine, dialysis if severe
Methanol Visual symptoms, high osm gap, severe acidosis Fomepizole, dialysis, folate
Ethylene glycol AKI, calcium oxalate crystals, high osm gap Fomepizole, dialysis, thiamine/pyridoxine

Attending: “What acid-base pattern do salicylates cause?”

Answer: Early respiratory alkalosis from medullary stimulation, plus high anion gap metabolic acidosis from organic acids. A normal pH can hide a dangerous mixed disorder.

Normal Anion Gap Metabolic Acidosis

NAGMA usually means bicarbonate is lost or acid is retained with chloride rising to maintain electroneutrality. This is also called hyperchloremic metabolic acidosis.

Mnemonic: HARDUP

  • Hyperalimentation / TPN
  • Acetazolamide / adrenal insufficiency
  • Renal tubular acidosis
  • Diarrhea
  • Ureterosigmoidostomy / urinary diversion
  • Pancreatic fistula

GI vs Renal Bicarbonate Loss

Cause Mechanism Clues
Diarrhea GI bicarbonate loss Low K often, volume depletion, history obvious
Pancreatic fistula Loss of bicarbonate-rich pancreatic fluid Post-op drains, pancreatitis
RTA type 1 Distal acid secretion defect Urine pH high, stones, hypokalemia
RTA type 2 Proximal bicarbonate wasting Fanconi features, hypokalemia
RTA type 4 Hypoaldosterone / aldosterone resistance Hyperkalemia, diabetes, CKD, ACEi/ARB, spironolactone
Normal saline Chloride load lowers strong ion difference Rising chloride after large-volume saline
If chloride is high and bicarbonate is low, think NAGMA.

Delta Gap / Delta-Delta

When an anion gap acidosis is present, the delta gap helps identify an additional metabolic disorder.

Delta ratio = (AG − 12) / (24 − HCO₃⁻)
Delta Ratio Interpretation
<0.4 Mostly NAGMA
0.4–0.8 Mixed HAGMA + NAGMA likely
0.8–2.0 Typical pure HAGMA
>2.0 HAGMA plus metabolic alkalosis or chronic respiratory acidosis with high bicarbonate

Worked Example

Na 140, Cl 100, HCO₃ 10.

  • AG = 140 − (100 + 10) = 30
  • Delta AG = 30 − 12 = 18
  • Delta HCO₃ = 24 − 10 = 14
  • Delta ratio = 18 / 14 = 1.3 → typical HAGMA

Mixed Disorder Example

Na 140, Cl 112, HCO₃ 10.

  • AG = 18
  • Delta AG = 6
  • Delta HCO₃ = 14
  • Delta ratio = 0.43 → HAGMA + NAGMA

Attending: “DKA patient has an anion gap, but delta ratio is 0.4. What does that mean?”

Answer: DKA explains HAGMA, but the low delta ratio suggests an additional NAGMA, often diarrhea, renal tubular acidosis, or hyperchloremic acidosis from large-volume normal saline.

Metabolic Alkalosis

Metabolic alkalosis is high bicarbonate with alkalemia. The key bedside question is whether it is chloride responsive or chloride resistant.

Type Urine Chloride Common Causes Treatment Direction
Chloride responsive <10–20 mEq/L Vomiting, NG suction, remote diuretics, volume depletion Normal saline, potassium repletion, stop losses
Chloride resistant >20 mEq/L Mineralocorticoid excess, active diuretics, Bartter/Gitelman Treat aldosterone state, stop diuretics, K/Mg repletion

ICU Causes

  • Loop diuretics
  • Vomiting or NG suction
  • Post-hypercapnic alkalosis after ventilating chronic CO₂ retainers
  • Hypokalemia
  • Steroids or mineralocorticoid excess
  • Massive transfusion citrate metabolism
Hypokalemia maintains metabolic alkalosis. You often cannot fix the alkalosis without fixing potassium and chloride.

Oxygenation: PaO₂, A-a Gradient, and P/F Ratio

P/F Ratio

P/F ratio = PaO₂ / FiO₂

FiO₂ must be written as a decimal. Example: PaO₂ 80 on FiO₂ 0.40 → P/F = 200.

P/F Ratio Interpretation
400–500 Normal-ish at sea level
200–300 Mild ARDS range if ARDS criteria met
100–200 Moderate ARDS range
≤100 Severe ARDS range

A-a Gradient

The A-a gradient compares oxygen in the alveolus to oxygen in the artery. It helps distinguish hypoventilation from problems with gas exchange.

PAO₂ ≈ FiO₂ × (Patm − PH₂O) − PaCO₂/R

At sea level on room air, a shortcut is:

PAO₂ ≈ 150 − PaCO₂ / 0.8

A-a Gradient Interpretation

  • Normal A-a gradient + hypoxemia: hypoventilation or high altitude.
  • High A-a gradient: V/Q mismatch, shunt, diffusion limitation.

Memory Aid: “Normal A-a = pump problem; high A-a = lung problem.”

Not perfect, but useful: if the gradient is normal, the lungs can exchange oxygen and ventilation is the issue. If high, gas exchange is impaired.

Ventilator Adjustment Based on Blood Gas

Core Rule

Problem Ventilator Lever Why
High PaCO₂ / respiratory acidosis Increase minute ventilation More alveolar ventilation clears CO₂
Low PaCO₂ / respiratory alkalosis Decrease minute ventilation Less ventilation retains CO₂
Low PaO₂ / hypoxemia Increase FiO₂ and/or PEEP Improves oxygenation
High PaO₂ Decrease FiO₂ first Avoid oxygen toxicity
Minute ventilation = respiratory rate × tidal volume

Adjusting CO₂

  • To lower PaCO₂: increase RR or tidal volume.
  • In lung-protective ventilation, prefer increasing RR before increasing tidal volume above safe limits.
  • Watch plateau pressure, driving pressure, auto-PEEP, and pH.

Adjusting Oxygen

  • Increase FiO₂ for rapid short-term oxygenation.
  • Increase PEEP to recruit alveoli and reduce shunt.
  • If FiO₂ is high for a prolonged time, try to improve oxygenation with PEEP, positioning, diuresis, recruitment strategy, or treating cause.

Example: Respiratory Acidosis on Vent

ABG: 7.18 / 65 / 80 / 24 on volume control, RR 16, TV 420, FiO₂ 40%, PEEP 5.

Interpretation: Primary respiratory acidosis. Oxygenation acceptable.

Vent move: Increase minute ventilation. If plateau pressure is safe and no severe air trapping, increase RR, for example 16 → 20. Recheck ABG or VBG trend.

Example: Hypoxemia on Vent

ABG: 7.38 / 40 / 55 / 24 on FiO₂ 60%, PEEP 5.

Interpretation: Oxygenation failure. P/F ratio 55/0.60 = 92, severe oxygenation defect if ARDS criteria met.

Vent move: Increase PEEP and FiO₂, assess ARDS, consider proning if severe ARDS, treat cause.

Asthma/COPD Vent Nuance

  • Do not chase normal CO₂ immediately if it worsens air trapping.
  • Use lower RR, longer expiratory time, permissive hypercapnia if needed.
  • Watch expiratory flow returning to baseline.
  • High plateau pressure and auto-PEEP may cause hypotension.
In severe asthma, raising RR to fix CO₂ can worsen breath stacking and kill the patient. Sometimes the correct move is permissive hypercapnia.

BiPAP / Noninvasive Ventilation

BiPAP provides inspiratory pressure support and expiratory pressure. It improves ventilation and can reduce work of breathing.

Best Indications

  • COPD exacerbation with acute hypercapnic respiratory failure
  • Obesity hypoventilation syndrome exacerbation
  • Cardiogenic pulmonary edema
  • Selected neuromuscular weakness if airway protection is intact

Common NIV Threshold

  • pH <7.35 with elevated PaCO₂ despite optimal medical therapy in acute hypercapnic respiratory failure
  • Increased work of breathing
  • Awake, cooperative, able to protect airway

Contraindications / Failure Signs

  • Unable to protect airway
  • Active vomiting or high aspiration risk
  • Severe agitation or inability to tolerate mask
  • Facial trauma or poor mask seal
  • Severe shock or arrest
  • Worsening mental status
  • Persistent or worsening acidosis after trial

BiPAP Settings Logic

Problem Adjustment
High CO₂ / low pH Increase IPAP to increase tidal volume and ventilation
Hypoxemia Increase EPAP and/or FiO₂
Poor comfort/synchrony Adjust mask, rise time, trigger, pressure, coaching
IPAP − EPAP = pressure support. Pressure support is what helps clear CO₂.

High-Flow Nasal Cannula

HFNC provides heated humidified high-flow oxygen. It improves oxygenation, washes out nasopharyngeal dead space, reduces work of breathing, and provides small amounts of PEEP-like effect.

Best Uses

  • Hypoxemic respiratory failure
  • Pneumonia
  • Early ARDS physiology
  • Post-extubation support in selected patients
  • Patients who need high FiO₂ but do not need ventilatory support

HFNC Is Not Ideal When

  • Primary problem is severe hypercapnia needing ventilatory assistance
  • Patient cannot protect airway
  • Severe work of breathing is worsening
  • Shock, exhaustion, or impending arrest
Tool Best For Main Physiologic Help
HFNC Hypoxemia High FiO₂, flow, dead-space washout
BiPAP Hypercapnia/COPD, cardiogenic pulmonary edema Ventilation and pressure support
Intubation Failure of oxygenation, ventilation, airway protection, or work of breathing Full control/support

When to Intubate

Do not intubate a number. Intubate a failing patient.

Major Indications

  • Unable to protect airway
  • Severe or worsening hypoxemia despite noninvasive support
  • Severe or worsening hypercapnia with acidemia and fatigue
  • Shock with respiratory failure
  • Severe work of breathing or exhaustion
  • Altered mental status from CO₂ narcosis or other cause
  • Need for procedure, transport, or airway control
A patient with pH 7.20 who is awake, improving on BiPAP, and protecting airway may avoid intubation. A patient with pH 7.28 who is tiring, altered, and diaphoretic may need intubation now.

ICU Cases

Case 1: COPD Exacerbation

ABG: 7.26 / 70 / 62 / 31.

Interpretation: Acute on chronic respiratory acidosis. High bicarbonate suggests chronic CO₂ retention, but pH is low because CO₂ rose acutely.

Management: BiPAP if awake/protecting airway, bronchodilators, steroids, controlled oxygen target, treat trigger. Repeat gas after NIV.

Case 2: DKA

ABG: 7.12 / 22 / 95 / 7. Na 132, Cl 96, glucose 550.

AG: 132 − (96 + 7) = 29.

Winter: expected CO₂ = 1.5 × 7 + 8 ±2 = 18.5 ±2. Actual 22 is slightly high, suggesting inadequate respiratory compensation or early fatigue.

Management: Fluids, insulin, potassium protocol, close monitoring. Do not miss impending respiratory failure if compensation fails.

Case 3: Salicylate Poisoning

ABG: 7.44 / 20 / 13. AG elevated.

Interpretation: Mixed respiratory alkalosis and high anion gap metabolic acidosis.

Management: Serum salicylate level, bicarbonate infusion for alkalinization, potassium repletion, poison control, dialysis if severe.

Case 4: Septic Shock

ABG: 7.29 / 28 / 75 / 13. Lactate 6.

Interpretation: HAGMA from lactic acidosis with respiratory compensation.

Management: Source control, antibiotics, fluids/pressors as appropriate, trend lactate in context, oxygen delivery, perfusion.

Case 5: Vomiting

ABG: 7.52 / 48 / 80 / 38. Urine chloride low.

Interpretation: Metabolic alkalosis with respiratory compensation, likely chloride responsive from vomiting/volume depletion.

Management: Normal saline, potassium chloride, antiemetics, stop gastric losses.

ICU Attending Pimp Questions

Q: What is the first thing you look at on an ABG?

A: pH. It tells you whether the final state is acidemia, alkalemia, or near-normal with possible mixed disorders.

Q: Can a normal pH be dangerous?

A: Yes. A normal pH can hide mixed disorders, such as salicylate poisoning with respiratory alkalosis plus metabolic acidosis.

Q: Why does CO₂ cause acidosis?

A: CO₂ combines with water to form carbonic acid, which dissociates into H⁺ and bicarbonate.

Q: Why does increasing respiratory rate lower PaCO₂?

A: It increases minute ventilation and alveolar ventilation, allowing more CO₂ elimination.

Q: Why might increasing RR be dangerous in asthma?

A: It shortens expiratory time, worsening air trapping, auto-PEEP, hypotension, and barotrauma.

Q: Why doesn't oxygen fix hypercapnia?

A: Oxygen improves oxygenation, not ventilation. CO₂ clearance requires alveolar ventilation.

Q: What does Winter’s formula tell you?

A: Expected PaCO₂ in metabolic acidosis. If actual CO₂ is higher, there is additional respiratory acidosis. If lower, additional respiratory alkalosis.

Q: What does an elevated delta ratio above 2 mean?

A: High anion gap acidosis plus metabolic alkalosis or chronic respiratory acidosis with elevated baseline bicarbonate.

Q: Why correct the anion gap for albumin?

A: Albumin is a major unmeasured anion. Low albumin lowers the measured gap and can hide HAGMA.

Q: What ABG suggests acute-on-chronic hypercapnic respiratory failure?

A: High PaCO₂, high bicarbonate, and low pH. The high bicarbonate shows chronic compensation; the low pH shows acute worsening.

Q: When is BiPAP better than HFNC?

A: When the main problem is ventilation and CO₂ clearance, such as COPD exacerbation with hypercapnic acidemia.

Q: When is HFNC better than BiPAP?

A: Often in hypoxemic respiratory failure where oxygenation and high flow are needed but ventilatory assistance is not the main problem.

Q: What is permissive hypercapnia?

A: Accepting elevated CO₂ to avoid ventilator-induced lung injury or dynamic hyperinflation, as long as pH and clinical status are acceptable.

Q: What is the most important treatment for lactic acidosis?

A: Treat the cause: shock, sepsis, hypoxemia, seizures, ischemia, or impaired clearance. Bicarbonate does not fix the underlying problem.

Clinical Pearls

  • Do not interpret a blood gas without the chemistry panel.
  • Always calculate the anion gap in metabolic acidosis.
  • Always correct the anion gap for albumin in ICU patients.
  • A normal pH does not exclude severe mixed acid-base disease.
  • Winter’s formula is mandatory in metabolic acidosis.
  • If PaCO₂ is higher than expected in metabolic acidosis, the patient is not ventilating enough.
  • In DKA, a rising CO₂ or “normalizing” PaCO₂ can be ominous if bicarbonate remains low.
  • Do not use VBG oxygen values for oxygenation decisions.
  • HFNC helps oxygenation more than ventilation.
  • BiPAP helps ventilation and CO₂ clearance.
  • Oxygen does not treat hypercapnia.
  • In severe asthma/COPD, avoid creating auto-PEEP by excessive RR.
  • Metabolic compensation takes time; respiratory compensation is fast.
  • Compensation does not overshoot.
  • Large-volume normal saline can cause hyperchloremic metabolic acidosis.
  • Hypokalemia maintains metabolic alkalosis.
  • Salicylate toxicity is a classic mixed disorder.
  • Post-hypercapnic metabolic alkalosis occurs when chronic CO₂ retainers are ventilated quickly.
  • Trend gases, but treat patients—not numbers.

Bottom Line

Blood gas interpretation starts with pH, PaCO₂, and bicarbonate, but ICU-level interpretation requires compensation, anion gap, albumin correction, delta gap, oxygenation assessment, and clinical context. PaCO₂ reflects ventilation; PaO₂ reflects oxygenation; chemistry CO₂ mostly reflects bicarbonate. Metabolic acidosis requires Winter’s formula and anion gap analysis. High anion gap acidosis uses GOLD MARK; normal gap acidosis uses HARDUP. Ventilator changes are physiology-based: change minute ventilation for CO₂, and change FiO₂/PEEP for oxygenation. BiPAP is best for selected hypercapnic ventilatory failure; HFNC is best for many hypoxemic patients; intubation is for airway failure, oxygenation failure, ventilation failure, exhaustion, or shock. The best ICU resident does not just label the ABG—they explains what the body is doing and what needs to change next.

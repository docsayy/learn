What Is Acute Coronary Syndrome?

Acute Coronary Syndrome (ACS) refers to a spectrum of clinical conditions caused by sudden reduction of blood flow through a coronary artery. Most cases occur when an atherosclerotic plaque ruptures or erodes, exposing thrombogenic material to circulating blood and triggering platelet activation, thrombus formation, and myocardial ischemia.

ACS is not a single diagnosis. Instead, it represents a group of related clinical syndromes that differ primarily by the degree of coronary occlusion, the presence of myocardial necrosis, ECG findings, and biomarker elevation.

Clinical Pearl

Think of ACS as a continuum. The underlying mechanism is often similar—a ruptured plaque with superimposed thrombus—but the extent of coronary obstruction determines whether the patient develops unstable angina, NSTEMI, or STEMI.

The Acute Coronary Syndrome Spectrum

The ACS spectrum consists of three primary entities:

Condition Troponin Myocardial Necrosis Typical ECG Findings
Unstable Angina Negative No Normal, ST depression, or T-wave inversion
NSTEMI Positive Yes Normal, ST depression, or T-wave inversion
STEMI Positive Yes ST elevation or STEMI equivalent

Historically, unstable angina was commonly diagnosed. With the widespread use of high-sensitivity troponin assays, many patients who would previously have been labeled unstable angina are now identified as NSTEMI because even very small amounts of myocardial injury can be detected.

Unstable Angina vs NSTEMI vs STEMI

Unstable Angina

Unstable angina occurs when plaque rupture and thrombosis produce myocardial ischemia without detectable myocardial necrosis. Troponin levels remain normal because cardiomyocyte death has not occurred.

  • Chest pain at rest or minimal exertion
  • May have ST depression or T-wave inversion
  • No troponin elevation
  • No myocardial infarction

NSTEMI

NSTEMI occurs when coronary thrombosis produces myocardial necrosis, but the infarction is generally limited to the subendocardial region rather than the full myocardial thickness.

  • Positive troponin elevation
  • Subendocardial infarction
  • ST depression or T-wave inversion common
  • No diagnostic ST elevation

STEMI

STEMI represents complete or near-complete coronary artery occlusion causing transmural myocardial infarction. These patients require immediate reperfusion therapy because ongoing myocardial death occurs minute by minute.

  • Positive troponins
  • Transmural infarction
  • Diagnostic ST elevation
  • Emergent PCI indicated

Pathophysiology of ACS

The classic sequence begins with development of an atherosclerotic plaque inside a coronary artery.

  1. Endothelial injury develops
  2. Lipid accumulation occurs
  3. Inflammatory cells infiltrate plaque
  4. Plaque becomes vulnerable
  5. Plaque ruptures or erodes
  6. Platelets activate
  7. Thrombus forms
  8. Coronary blood flow decreases
  9. Myocardial ischemia develops
  10. Prolonged ischemia causes infarction

Resident Takeaway

The primary therapeutic targets in ACS are:

  • Prevent platelet aggregation
  • Prevent thrombus propagation
  • Restore coronary blood flow
  • Stabilize vulnerable plaque
  • Prevent recurrent cardiovascular events

ECG Diagnosis of Acute Coronary Syndrome

The ECG remains one of the most important diagnostic tools in ACS evaluation. Every patient presenting with possible ACS should receive a 12-lead ECG within 10 minutes of arrival.

Common ECG Findings in ACS

Finding Implication
ST Elevation Acute transmural ischemia
ST Depression Subendocardial ischemia
T-Wave Inversion Myocardial ischemia
Hyperacute T Waves Early STEMI
New LBBB Possible STEMI equivalent

Diagnostic STEMI Criteria

STEMI requires ST elevation in at least two contiguous leads.

The traditional threshold is:

  • ≥ 1 mm ST elevation in most leads

However, leads V2 and V3 have special thresholds:

Patient Group Required ST Elevation
Women > 1.5 mm
Men ≥40 years > 2.0 mm
Men <40 years > 2.5 mm

Board Pearl

ST elevation must occur in at least two contiguous leads. A single isolated lead with ST elevation is not diagnostic for STEMI.

Sgarbossa Criteria and Left Bundle Branch Block

Diagnosing acute MI in the presence of a left bundle branch block (LBBB) can be challenging because normal LBBB already causes secondary ST-T changes.

The Sgarbossa Criteria help identify STEMI in patients with LBBB or ventricular paced rhythms.

Modified Sgarbossa Criteria improve diagnostic sensitivity and are increasingly favored in modern clinical practice.

Posterior Myocardial Infarction and Right Ventricular Infarction

Not all STEMIs present with obvious ST elevation on the standard 12-lead ECG. Posterior infarctions and right ventricular infarctions are commonly missed if clinicians do not actively look for them.

Posterior STEMI

A posterior myocardial infarction typically results from occlusion of the right coronary artery (RCA) or left circumflex artery (LCx). Because the posterior wall is not directly visualized by standard ECG leads, the infarction often appears as reciprocal changes rather than classic ST elevation.

Key ECG findings include:

  • Horizontal ST depression in V1-V3
  • Tall dominant R waves in V1-V3
  • Upright T waves in anterior leads
  • High suspicion in patients with inferior STEMI

Board Pearl

ST depression in V1-V3 can represent a posterior STEMI until proven otherwise. Consider obtaining posterior leads V7-V9.

Right Ventricular Myocardial Infarction

Right ventricular infarction most commonly occurs with inferior STEMI due to proximal RCA occlusion.

Because the right ventricle is preload dependent, treatments that reduce preload can cause profound hypotension.

Clinical clues include:

  • Hypotension
  • Elevated jugular venous pressure
  • Clear lungs
  • Inferior STEMI

The diagnosis is confirmed using right-sided ECG leads, particularly V4R.

Critical Management Point

Avoid nitrates in patients with suspected right ventricular infarction. These patients depend on adequate preload, and nitrates may precipitate severe hypotension.

Initial Emergency Management of Acute Coronary Syndrome

The initial treatment approach is remarkably similar for unstable angina, NSTEMI, and STEMI. Management focuses on:

  1. Preventing further platelet aggregation
  2. Preventing thrombus propagation
  3. Stabilizing vulnerable plaque
  4. Reducing myocardial oxygen demand
  5. Preventing major adverse cardiovascular events (MACE)

A useful framework is:

ACS Initial Management

  • Aspirin immediately
  • P2Y12 inhibitor
  • Anticoagulation
  • High-intensity statin
  • Beta blocker (if appropriate)
  • ACE inhibitor or ARB
  • Nitroglycerin for symptom relief
  • Revascularization when indicated

Dual Antiplatelet Therapy (DAPT)

Dual antiplatelet therapy is a cornerstone of ACS treatment. Platelet activation is central to coronary thrombosis, making rapid platelet inhibition essential.

Aspirin

All ACS patients should receive:

  • Aspirin 325 mg loading dose
  • Then aspirin 81 mg daily indefinitely

P2Y12 Inhibitors

Medication Loading Dose Maintenance
Ticagrelor 180 mg 90 mg twice daily
Clopidogrel 600 mg 75 mg daily
Prasugrel 60 mg 10 mg daily

Current evidence generally favors ticagrelor over clopidogrel because of stronger platelet inhibition and superior cardiovascular outcomes.

Clinical Pearl

Clopidogrel is a prodrug requiring hepatic activation. Ticagrelor is already active, leading to faster and more predictable platelet inhibition.

When Might You Delay the P2Y12 Inhibitor?

If cardiology suspects severe multivessel disease or left main disease requiring CABG, some clinicians defer P2Y12 loading until coronary anatomy is known.

Giving a P2Y12 inhibitor too early may delay urgent bypass surgery because the medication must be held before CABG.

Medication Recommended Hold Before CABG
Clopidogrel 5 days
Ticagrelor 3-5 days
Prasugrel 7 days

Anticoagulation

Anticoagulation prevents ongoing thrombus propagation while definitive management is arranged.

Common options include:

Agent Common Use
Unfractionated Heparin Most common inpatient option
Enoxaparin Alternative strategy
Fondaparinux Selected NSTEMI patients
Bivalirudin Certain PCI situations

Most institutions use either a heparin infusion or therapeutic-dose enoxaparin during initial ACS management.

Plaque Stabilization and Secondary Prevention

High-Intensity Statins

All ACS patients should receive high-intensity statin therapy unless contraindicated.

Common regimens:

  • Atorvastatin 80 mg daily
  • Rosuvastatin 20-40 mg daily

Benefits include:

  • Plaque stabilization
  • Reduced inflammation
  • Reduced recurrent MI risk
  • Reduced mortality

Beta Blockers

Beta blockers reduce myocardial oxygen demand by lowering heart rate, blood pressure, and contractility.

Typical inpatient regimen:

  • Metoprolol tartrate 12.5-25 mg
  • Titrate as tolerated

Avoid beta blockers in:

  • Cardiogenic shock
  • Severe bradycardia
  • High-grade AV block
  • Acute decompensated heart failure

ACE Inhibitors

ACE inhibitors reduce ventricular remodeling and improve long-term cardiovascular outcomes.

Consider initiation within 24 hours in:

  • Anterior MI
  • Reduced ejection fraction
  • Diabetes
  • Hypertension

The MONA-BASH Framework: Useful but Imperfect

Many trainees learn ACS treatment using the MONA-BASH mnemonic:

Letter Medication
M Morphine
O Oxygen
N Nitroglycerin
A Aspirin
B Beta Blocker
A ACE Inhibitor
S Statin
H Heparin

Although still useful as a memory aid, modern ACS treatment has evolved significantly.

Important Updates

  • Morphine is no longer routinely recommended.
  • Oxygen should only be used if hypoxic.
  • DAPT is now standard of care.
  • Rapid revascularization is central to management.

Oxygen

Supplemental oxygen should not be given routinely.

Current recommendations:

  • Give oxygen if saturation <90%
  • Give oxygen for respiratory distress
  • Avoid unnecessary oxygen in normoxic patients

Morphine

Morphine may be considered for persistent severe chest pain despite nitrates, but it is no longer routinely administered because observational studies suggest possible adverse cardiovascular outcomes.

Nitroglycerin

Nitroglycerin remains highly effective for symptom relief.

Typical approach:

  • Sublingual nitroglycerin every 5 minutes
  • Maximum 3 doses
  • If persistent pain, consider IV nitroglycerin infusion

Contraindications to Nitroglycerin

  • Hypotension
  • Bradycardia
  • Right ventricular infarction
  • Recent sildenafil use
  • Recent tadalafil use

STEMI Reperfusion Strategy

STEMI is fundamentally a reperfusion emergency. Every minute of delay results in additional myocardial necrosis.

Time Is Myocardium

The primary objective in STEMI management is restoration of coronary blood flow as rapidly as possible.

Primary PCI

Primary PCI is the preferred reperfusion strategy whenever available.

Target Goal Time
Door-to-Balloon Time < 90 minutes
First Medical Contact to Device < 120 minutes

Patients presenting to PCI-capable centers should proceed directly to the catheterization laboratory unless contraindications exist.

Transfer for PCI

Patients presenting to non-PCI hospitals should be transferred if PCI can be performed within 120 minutes of first medical contact.

Fibrinolysis

If timely PCI is not available, fibrinolytic therapy may be considered.

Target Goal Time
Door-to-Needle Time < 30 minutes

Common fibrinolytics include:

  • Tenecteplase (TNK)
  • Alteplase (tPA)
  • Reteplase

Fibrinolysis is most effective within the first 12 hours after symptom onset.

NSTEMI and Unstable Angina Risk Stratification

Unlike STEMI, which generally proceeds directly to urgent reperfusion therapy, patients with NSTEMI and unstable angina require risk stratification to determine the urgency of invasive evaluation.

The primary goal is to identify which patients will benefit from immediate cardiac catheterization versus those who can initially be managed medically.

Current practice primarily relies on:

  • TIMI Score
  • GRACE Score
  • Clinical judgment
  • Hemodynamic stability
  • Dynamic ECG changes
  • Troponin trends

Clinical Pearl

GRACE generally performs better than TIMI for mortality prediction and is favored in contemporary guidelines, although TIMI remains widely used because it is simple to calculate at the bedside.

TIMI Risk Score

The TIMI score helps estimate the risk of death, recurrent MI, or urgent revascularization in patients with unstable angina and NSTEMI.

Each criterion receives one point:

TIMI Variable Points
Age ≥ 65 years 1
At least 3 CAD risk factors 1
Known coronary stenosis ≥50% 1
Aspirin use in previous 7 days 1
Severe angina episodes within 24 hours 1
ST-segment deviation 1
Positive cardiac biomarkers 1

Total scores range from 0 to 7.

  • 0-1 = Low Risk
  • 2-4 = Intermediate Risk
  • 5-7 = High Risk

GRACE Risk Score

The GRACE score is more comprehensive and predicts both in-hospital and long-term mortality.

Variables include:

  • Age
  • Heart rate
  • Systolic blood pressure
  • Serum creatinine
  • Cardiac arrest at presentation
  • ST deviation
  • Elevated biomarkers
  • Killip class
GRACE Score Risk Category
<109 Low Risk
109-140 Intermediate Risk
>140 High Risk

Exam Pearl

A GRACE score greater than 140 generally supports an early invasive strategy within 24 hours.

Choosing an Invasive Strategy

After risk stratification, patients generally fall into one of four treatment pathways:

  1. Ischemia-guided strategy
  2. Delayed invasive strategy
  3. Early invasive strategy
  4. Immediate invasive strategy

Ischemia-Guided Strategy

This is typically reserved for very low-risk patients.

Typical Candidates

  • GRACE score <109
  • TIMI score 0-1
  • Negative troponins
  • No high-risk ECG changes
  • Hemodynamically stable

Management usually includes:

  • Medical therapy
  • Observation
  • Stress testing
  • Outpatient cardiology follow-up

Failure of medical therapy generally prompts catheterization.

Delayed Invasive Strategy (<72 Hours)

Intermediate-risk patients frequently undergo delayed invasive evaluation.

Typical Features

  • TIMI ≥2
  • GRACE 109-140
  • Multiple CAD risk factors
  • Recurrent symptoms

Coronary angiography is typically performed within 72 hours.

Early Invasive Strategy (<24 Hours)

Higher-risk NSTEMI patients benefit from earlier catheterization.

Common Indications

  • GRACE >140
  • Dynamic troponin rise
  • New ST depressions
  • Large ischemic burden
  • High-risk clinical features

These patients should generally undergo angiography within 24 hours.

Immediate Invasive Strategy (<2 Hours)

Certain NSTEMI and unstable angina patients require urgent catheterization despite not meeting classic STEMI criteria.

Indications

Finding Reason
Refractory angina Ongoing ischemia
Hemodynamic instability Potential cardiogenic shock
Acute heart failure Large ischemic territory
Sustained VT/VF Electrical instability
Worsening mitral regurgitation Mechanical complication
Recurrent ischemia despite therapy Failed medical management

When to Call the Cath Lab

Persistent chest pain despite nitrates and aggressive medical therapy should immediately raise concern for ongoing coronary occlusion and prompt urgent cardiology involvement.

Cardiac Catheterization Explained

Cardiac catheterization remains the gold standard for evaluating coronary anatomy.

A catheter is advanced through either:

  • Radial artery (preferred)
  • Femoral artery

Contrast dye is injected into the coronary arteries, allowing visualization of:

  • Coronary stenosis
  • Plaque burden
  • Coronary occlusions
  • Blood flow patterns
  • Coronary anatomy

PCI Versus CABG

Once significant coronary disease is identified, the next decision is whether revascularization should occur with PCI or CABG.

Percutaneous Coronary Intervention (PCI)

PCI involves balloon angioplasty followed by stent placement to restore blood flow.

Advantages

  • Minimally invasive
  • Rapid recovery
  • Short hospital stay
  • Preferred for most STEMI patients

Coronary Artery Bypass Grafting (CABG)

CABG involves using another vessel to bypass obstructed coronary arteries.

Common grafts include:

  • Left internal mammary artery (LIMA)
  • Right internal mammary artery
  • Saphenous vein graft
  • Radial artery graft

When CABG Is Preferred

Several coronary anatomy patterns consistently favor surgical revascularization.

Finding Preferred Strategy
Left Main Disease CABG
Three Vessel Disease CABG
Two Vessel Disease + LAD + LV Dysfunction CABG
Complex Multivessel Disease CABG

Left Main Coronary Artery Disease

The left main coronary artery supplies blood flow to both the LAD and circumflex arteries. Significant stenosis places a massive amount of myocardium at risk.

Classic ECG Clue

  • ST elevation in aVR
  • Diffuse ST depression elsewhere

Board Pearl

ST elevation in aVR with widespread ST depression should immediately raise suspicion for left main or severe proximal LAD disease.

Complications of Coronary Stents

Every resident should understand the distinction between stent thrombosis and restenosis.

Complication Timing Mechanism
Stent Thrombosis Early Acute clot formation
In-Stent Restenosis Late Neointimal hyperplasia

Stent Thrombosis

Stent thrombosis is a potentially catastrophic complication characterized by acute thrombotic occlusion of a recently placed stent.

Major Risk Factor

  • Premature discontinuation of DAPT

Presentation

  • Sudden chest pain
  • STEMI
  • Cardiac arrest
  • Cardiogenic shock

Resident Pearl

The primary purpose of DAPT after PCI is prevention of stent thrombosis.

In-Stent Restenosis

Restenosis develops gradually due to neointimal hyperplasia and progressive narrowing within the stent.

Typical Presentation

  • Progressive exertional angina
  • Months to years after PCI
  • Usually less dramatic than thrombosis

Unlike thrombosis, restenosis is generally not caused by stopping antiplatelet therapy.

Drug-Eluting Versus Bare-Metal Stents

Feature Bare Metal Stent Drug-Eluting Stent
Restenosis Higher Lower
DAPT Duration Shorter Longer
Current Use Rare Standard of Care

Modern drug-eluting stents dramatically reduce restenosis by releasing antiproliferative medications that inhibit neointimal growth.

Key Concept

Drug-eluting stents prevent restenosis. Dual antiplatelet therapy prevents thrombosis.

Beyond ACS: Understanding Elevated Troponins

One of the most common mistakes made by trainees is assuming that every elevated troponin represents an acute coronary syndrome. In reality, many hospitalized patients have elevated troponins without plaque rupture, coronary thrombosis, or a need for cardiac catheterization.

Distinguishing between Type 1 MI, Type 2 MI, and non-ischemic myocardial injury is one of the most important skills in inpatient medicine and cardiology.

Clinical Pearl: Troponin elevation does not automatically equal ACS. The clinical context matters more than the number itself.

The Universal Definition of Myocardial Infarction

Modern cardiology classifies myocardial infarctions into five major categories.

MI Type Definition
Type 1 MI Plaque rupture with acute coronary thrombosis
Type 2 MI Supply-demand mismatch causing myocardial ischemia
Type 3 MI Sudden cardiac death presumed due to MI
Type 4 MI PCI-related myocardial infarction
Type 5 MI CABG-related myocardial infarction

Board Pearl

STEMI and NSTEMI are both Type 1 myocardial infarctions because they result from plaque rupture and coronary thrombosis.

Type 2 Myocardial Infarction

Type 2 MI occurs when myocardial oxygen demand exceeds oxygen supply, resulting in ischemia and infarction without acute plaque rupture.

The coronary arteries may contain significant CAD, mild CAD, or even no CAD. The infarction occurs because of physiologic stress rather than acute thrombosis.

Common Causes of Type 2 MI

  • Septic shock
  • Severe hypotension
  • Acute blood loss anemia
  • Massive pulmonary embolism
  • Tachyarrhythmias
  • Hypertensive emergency
  • Severe hypoxemia
  • Respiratory failure
  • Cardiogenic shock

Characteristics of Type 2 MI

Feature Type 2 MI
Troponin Elevation Yes
Dynamic Troponin Rise Yes
Myocardial Infarction Yes
Coronary Thrombus No
EKG Changes May be present
DAPT Required No
Routine Heparin No
Routine Cath No
Important: Type 2 MI is not treated with the ACS algorithm. Treatment focuses on correcting the underlying physiologic stressor.

Treatment of Type 2 MI

  • Treat sepsis
  • Correct hypotension
  • Transfuse severe anemia when appropriate
  • Treat pulmonary embolism
  • Control arrhythmias
  • Improve oxygenation

Once the underlying condition improves, troponins typically stabilize and fall.

Non-MI Troponin Elevation (Myocardial Injury)

Many patients have elevated troponins without infarction. This condition is commonly called myocardial injury or chronic myocardial injury.

The key distinction is that myocardial injury does not represent active ischemic infarction.

Common Causes

  • Chronic kidney disease
  • End-stage renal disease
  • Advanced age
  • Chronic heart failure
  • Pulmonary hypertension
  • Structural heart disease
  • Chronic inflammatory states

Characteristics of Myocardial Injury

Feature Myocardial Injury
Troponin Positive Yes
Dynamic Rise/Fall No
Infarction No
Acute Plaque Rupture No
ACS No
Cardiac Catheterization Not Indicated
Resident Pearl: CKD patients frequently have chronically elevated troponins. The trend is often more important than the absolute value.

Type 2 MI vs Myocardial Injury

Feature Type 2 MI Myocardial Injury
Troponin Positive Yes Yes
Dynamic Troponin Rise Yes No
Myocardial Infarction Yes No
Supply-Demand Mismatch Yes No
ACS Treatment No No

The Simplest Way to Remember

  • Dynamic troponins + infarction = Type 2 MI
  • Stable troponins without infarction = Myocardial Injury

Typical vs Atypical Chest Pain

Correct classification of chest pain helps determine pretest probability for coronary artery disease.

Three Features of Typical Angina

  1. Substernal chest discomfort
  2. Provoked by exertion or emotional stress
  3. Relieved by rest or nitroglycerin
Criteria Met Classification
3 / 3 Typical Angina
2 / 3 Atypical Angina
0–1 / 3 Non-Cardiac Chest Pain

Example of Atypical Chest Pain

  • Diffuse chest discomfort
  • Partially exertional symptoms
  • Chest wall tenderness
  • Normal troponins
  • Normal ECG

These patients often require risk stratification and may undergo outpatient stress testing depending on overall risk profile.

Atypical ACS Presentations

Not all ACS patients present with crushing substernal chest pain.

Patients Who Frequently Present Atypically

  • Elderly patients
  • Women
  • Patients with diabetes mellitus

Common Symptoms

  • Shortness of breath
  • Nausea
  • Vomiting
  • Fatigue
  • Malaise
  • Weakness
  • Diaphoresis
Clinical Warning: Never dismiss ACS simply because chest pain is absent. Elderly, diabetic, and female patients often present atypically.

Highest Yield Historical Finding for MI

One of the most interesting findings from diagnostic studies is that pain radiating to the right arm or right shoulder carries one of the strongest positive likelihood ratios for myocardial infarction.

Board Pearl: Right arm radiation is more specific for MI than left arm radiation, although left arm radiation is more common.

Major CAD Risk Factors

  • Type 2 diabetes mellitus
  • Hypertension
  • Hyperlipidemia
  • Smoking history
  • Obesity
  • Chronic kidney disease
  • Male age > 45 years
  • Female age > 55 years

Family History of Premature MI

Relative Definition
Male First-Degree Relative MI before age 55
Female First-Degree Relative MI before age 65

Key Takeaway

Elevated troponin ≠ ACS. Always determine whether the patient has:

  1. Type 1 MI (ACS)
  2. Type 2 MI (supply-demand mismatch)
  3. Myocardial injury (non-ischemic troponin elevation)
  4. Non-cardiac pathology

Correct classification prevents unnecessary heparin drips, unnecessary catheterizations, and inappropriate antiplatelet therapy.

Atypical Chest Pain

Chest pain evaluation is one of the most common and challenging tasks encountered in emergency medicine, hospital medicine, and cardiology.

Understanding the distinction between typical angina, atypical chest pain, and non-cardiac chest pain is crucial because it guides subsequent testing and determines whether further ischemic evaluation is required.

Typical Angina Criteria

Three classic criteria define typical anginal chest pain:

  1. Substernal chest discomfort
  2. Provoked by exertion or emotional stress
  3. Relieved by rest or nitroglycerin
Criteria Met Classification
3 / 3 Typical Angina
2 / 3 Atypical Chest Pain
0–1 / 3 Non-Cardiac Chest Pain
Two of the three criteria = atypical chest pain.

Most Specific Historical Finding for MI

Although most clinicians associate myocardial infarction with radiation down the left arm, studies have demonstrated that pain radiating to the right arm or shoulder has one of the highest positive likelihood ratios for acute MI.

Right-arm radiation is more specific for MI than left-arm radiation.

Who Presents Atypically?

Certain patient populations frequently present without classic chest pain.

  • Elderly patients
  • Women
  • Patients with diabetes mellitus

These patients may instead present with:

  • Shortness of breath
  • Nausea
  • Vomiting
  • Fatigue
  • Generalized weakness
  • Malaise
  • Syncope
Absence of chest pain does NOT exclude ACS, particularly in elderly, diabetic, and female patients.

Major CAD Risk Factors

  • Diabetes mellitus
  • Hypertension
  • Hyperlipidemia
  • Smoking
  • Obesity
  • CKD
  • Family history of premature MI

Definition of Premature MI

Relative Age Threshold
Male first-degree relative <55 years
Female first-degree relative <65 years

Approach to Atypical Chest Pain

Risk Level Management
Low Risk Risk-factor modification
Intermediate Risk Stress testing
High Risk Consider invasive evaluation
High-risk atypical chest pain is often managed similarly to unstable angina.

Stress Testing in Acute Coronary Syndrome and Chest Pain Evaluation

Stress testing is a cornerstone of ischemic heart disease evaluation and is most commonly used when the diagnosis of coronary artery disease (CAD) remains uncertain after the initial history, ECG, biomarkers, and risk stratification.

The primary purpose of stress testing is to determine whether myocardial ischemia occurs under stress conditions and whether additional invasive evaluation such as coronary angiography is warranted.

Stress tests are generally used for:
  • Intermediate-risk chest pain
  • Atypical chest pain
  • Low-risk unstable angina
  • Low-risk NSTEMI undergoing ischemia-guided management

When Is Stress Testing Appropriate?

Clinical Scenario Stress Test Appropriate?
STEMI No – proceed directly to reperfusion
High-risk NSTEMI No – early invasive strategy
Low-risk NSTEMI Yes
Unstable Angina (low risk) Yes
Atypical Chest Pain Often appropriate
Clearly Non-Cardiac Pain Usually unnecessary
The decision to stress test depends heavily on pretest probability. Testing very low-risk patients increases false positives, while testing very high-risk patients may delay necessary intervention.

Two Major Categories of Stress Testing

Functional Tests Anatomic Tests
Assess ischemia Assess coronary anatomy
Evaluate physiologic significance Detect coronary lesions
Determine blood-flow limitations Show plaque burden

Examples

Functional Tests Anatomic Tests
Exercise ECG Coronary CT Angiography (CCTA)
Stress Echocardiography Invasive Coronary Angiography
Nuclear Perfusion Imaging
PET Myocardial Perfusion
Functional tests answer: "Does this lesion cause ischemia?"

Anatomic tests answer: "Is there a lesion present?"

Exercise ECG Stress Test (Treadmill Test)

The exercise treadmill ECG is the oldest, simplest, and least expensive stress-testing modality. Patients walk on a treadmill while ECG monitoring evaluates for exercise-induced ischemic changes.

What It Detects

  • Exercise-induced ST-segment depression
  • Exercise-induced arrhythmias
  • Exercise tolerance
  • Chronotropic response

Advantages

  • Widely available
  • Low cost
  • No radiation exposure
  • Rapid testing

Limitations

  • Lower sensitivity (~70%)
  • Lower specificity than imaging studies
  • Cannot localize ischemia

Contraindications

  • Inability to exercise
  • Baseline Left Bundle Branch Block (LBBB)
  • Ventricular paced rhythm
  • Baseline ST abnormalities
  • LVH with strain pattern
If baseline ECG abnormalities already exist, ischemic ST changes cannot be interpreted reliably.

Positive ECG Stress Test Findings

  • ≥1 mm horizontal ST depression
  • Down-sloping ST depression
  • Exercise-induced angina
  • Hemodynamic instability

Exercise Stress Echocardiography

Stress echocardiography combines exercise with cardiac ultrasound imaging. Echocardiograms are obtained before and immediately after exercise.

New wall-motion abnormalities that appear during stress indicate ischemia.

Advantages

  • Higher sensitivity than treadmill ECG
  • No radiation exposure
  • Can identify ischemic territory
  • Provides structural information

What Is Evaluated?

  • Wall-motion abnormalities
  • Left ventricular function
  • Valvular disease
  • Regional myocardial contractility

Contraindications

  • Pre-existing wall-motion abnormalities
  • Markedly reduced EF
  • Significant bundle branch block

Pharmacologic Stress Testing

Many hospitalized patients cannot exercise adequately due to age, frailty, orthopedic limitations, neurologic disease, or critical illness.

Pharmacologic stress testing reproduces physiologic stress through medications rather than exercise.

If the patient cannot walk, think pharmacologic stress testing.

Dobutamine Stress Echocardiography

Dobutamine is a beta-adrenergic agonist that increases:

  • Heart rate
  • Myocardial contractility
  • Myocardial oxygen demand

This simulates exercise and may reveal ischemia-induced wall-motion abnormalities on echocardiography.

Advantages

  • No radiation
  • Useful when exercise impossible
  • Excellent functional assessment

Contraindications

  • Recent myocardial infarction
  • Unstable arrhythmias
  • Severe hypertension
  • Significant LVOT obstruction

Nuclear Myocardial Perfusion Imaging

Nuclear stress testing evaluates myocardial blood flow by comparing perfusion during stress and rest.

Areas supplied by stenotic coronary arteries receive less tracer uptake during stress and appear as perfusion defects.

Common Modalities

  • SPECT
  • PET Myocardial Perfusion Imaging
PET myocardial perfusion imaging is increasingly favored because of superior image quality, improved sensitivity, and shorter scan times.

PET Myocardial Perfusion Imaging

PET perfusion imaging is one of the most commonly utilized stress tests in modern hospitals, particularly for patients unable to exercise.

How It Works

  1. Coronary vasodilator administered
  2. Coronary blood flow increases
  3. Radiotracer injected
  4. Perfusion defects become visible

Common Pharmacologic Agents

  • Regadenoson
  • Adenosine
  • Dipyridamole

Mechanism of Ischemia Detection

Healthy coronary arteries dilate substantially when exposed to vasodilators. Severely stenotic arteries cannot increase flow proportionally, producing relative hypoperfusion and a visible perfusion defect.

Contraindications

  • Hypotension
  • Severe asthma
  • Active bronchospasm
  • Severe COPD with wheezing

Preparing for PET Myocardial Perfusion Imaging

NPO Status

  • No food prior to study
  • Institution-specific fasting period

Medications and Substances to Hold

Hold Before Test Reason
Caffeine Interferes with vasodilator response
Nitrates May affect perfusion assessment

Beta Blockers

Beta blockers are generally NOT required to be held before PET myocardial perfusion imaging.

One of the most common test-day issues: patients accidentally consume caffeine before PET imaging, resulting in study cancellation or reduced accuracy.

Coronary CT Angiography (CCTA)

Coronary CT angiography is a non-invasive anatomical test that directly visualizes coronary arteries and coronary plaque burden.

Advantages

  • Rapid evaluation
  • Excellent negative predictive value
  • Direct visualization of plaque
  • Non-invasive

Ideal Candidate

  • Younger patient
  • Low-to-intermediate risk
  • Baseline bradycardia
  • No prior coronary stents

Limitations

  • Tachycardia causes motion artifact
  • Heavy calcification decreases accuracy
  • Prior stents create artifact
CCTA excels at ruling OUT significant CAD.

Duke Treadmill Score

The Duke Treadmill Score is used to estimate prognosis following an exercise treadmill test.

Duke Score = Exercise Time − (5 × ST Deviation) − (4 × Angina Index)
Score Risk Category
≥ +5 Low Risk
-10 to +4 Intermediate Risk
< -10 High Risk
Higher Duke scores indicate better prognosis.

Clinical Scenarios and Diagnostic Reasoning

Understanding ACS requires more than memorizing definitions. The true challenge is applying these principles to real patients who often present with overlapping symptoms, multiple comorbidities, and imperfect clinical pictures.

The following cases illustrate common inpatient and emergency department scenarios and demonstrate how experienced clinicians differentiate between ACS, Type 2 MI, chronic myocardial injury, and non-cardiac chest pain.

Always ask yourself:
  1. Is there evidence of infarction?
  2. Is there evidence of acute coronary thrombosis?
  3. Could another condition explain the troponin elevation?
  4. Would the patient benefit from catheterization?

Case 1: Intermediate-Risk Atypical Chest Pain

Presentation

  • 63-year-old woman
  • Type 2 diabetes
  • Hypertension
  • Hyperlipidemia
  • Exertional chest pain
  • Relieved by rest
  • Chest wall tenderness on palpation
  • Normal ECG
  • Negative troponins

Reasoning

She meets two of the three criteria for typical angina:

  • Exertional symptoms ✔
  • Relieved by rest ✔
  • Substernal classic discomfort ✖

Chest wall tenderness suggests a musculoskeletal component, but significant CAD risk factors prevent immediate dismissal.

Diagnosis

Atypical Chest Pain

Management

  • No immediate catheterization
  • Stress testing appropriate
  • Risk-factor modification
  • Further outpatient CAD evaluation

Case 2: Young Patient with Positive Troponins

Presentation

  • 39-year-old male
  • Hypertension
  • Substernal chest pain
  • Exertional
  • Improved with rest
  • Normal ECG
  • Troponins positive but down-trending

Reasoning

Despite relatively few risk factors, the history is strongly suggestive of myocardial ischemia.

Troponin positivity confirms myocardial injury. There is no alternative explanation such as sepsis, anemia, pulmonary embolism, or shock.

Diagnosis

Type 1 NSTEMI

Management

  • DAPT
  • Anticoagulation
  • High-intensity statin
  • Risk stratification
  • Delayed invasive strategy reasonable

Case 3: Incidental Troponin Elevation

Presentation

  • 67-year-old man
  • Chronic kidney disease
  • Presented after stubbing his toe
  • No ischemic symptoms
  • Normal ECG
  • Mildly elevated troponins

Reasoning

Troponins were obtained incidentally. There is no ischemic history, no ECG changes, and no dynamic rise and fall pattern.

Diagnosis

Chronic Myocardial Injury

Management

  • No ACS treatment
  • No anticoagulation
  • No catheterization
  • Treat underlying conditions only

Case 4: Classic Unstable Angina

Presentation

  • 52-year-old male
  • Diabetes
  • Hypertension
  • Hyperlipidemia
  • Family history of premature MI
  • Progressive exertional chest pain
  • ST depressions on ECG
  • Negative troponins

Reasoning

This patient has multiple CAD risk factors and dynamic ischemic ECG changes but no biochemical evidence of infarction.

Diagnosis

Unstable Angina

Management

  • DAPT
  • Anticoagulation
  • High-intensity statin
  • Early invasive strategy

Case 5: Septic Shock with Dynamic Troponins

Presentation

  • 74-year-old man
  • Known CAD
  • Septic shock from UTI
  • Hypotension
  • ST depressions
  • Troponins 100 → 500 → 1200

Reasoning

The dynamic troponin rise confirms infarction. However, septic shock provides a clear explanation for supply-demand mismatch.

Diagnosis

Type 2 MI

Management

  • Treat sepsis aggressively
  • Restore perfusion pressure
  • Support oxygen delivery
  • No routine DAPT
  • No routine cath
Dynamic troponins do NOT automatically equal NSTEMI.

Case 6: STEMI with Severe Thrombocytopenia

Presentation

  • 65-year-old lymphoma patient
  • Chemotherapy-induced pancytopenia
  • Platelets 12,000
  • Hemoglobin 6.8 g/dL
  • ST elevations in V2-V3
  • Dynamic troponin rise

Diagnosis

STEMI

Clinical Dilemma

STEMI normally requires emergent PCI. However, profound thrombocytopenia makes DAPT and PCI extremely dangerous.

Management Considerations

  • Individualized cardiology consultation
  • Balance bleeding risk versus ischemic risk
  • May not be PCI candidate
Not every STEMI receives a stent. Procedural risks occasionally outweigh potential benefits.

Case 7: Elderly Woman with Atypical ACS Presentation

Presentation

  • 70-year-old woman
  • Known CAD
  • Prior PCI
  • Heavy smoking history
  • Shortness of breath
  • Nausea
  • T-wave inversions
  • Positive troponins

Reasoning

Women frequently present without chest pain. Multiple CAD risk factors combined with ischemic ECG changes and positive troponins strongly suggest ACS.

Diagnosis

Type 1 NSTEMI

Management

  • DAPT
  • Anticoagulation
  • Statin therapy
  • Early invasive evaluation

Case 8: Nonadherent NSTEMI Patient

Presentation

  • 65-year-old homeless patient
  • IV drug use
  • Chronic medication nonadherence
  • T-wave inversions
  • Positive troponins

Diagnosis

Type 1 NSTEMI

Clinical Challenge

A stent requires prolonged DAPT. If DAPT is not taken, acute stent thrombosis can occur, potentially resulting in catastrophic reinfarction.

Management Considerations

  • Assess adherence likelihood
  • Shared decision-making
  • May favor conservative management

Case 9: Trauma Patient with Elevated Troponins

Presentation

  • 32-year-old after major MVC
  • Cardiac arrest
  • Pneumothorax
  • Rhabdomyolysis
  • Acute blood loss anemia
  • Troponin 215 → 257

Diagnosis

Type 2 MI

Reasoning

Multiple severe physiologic stressors are present. ACS is substantially less likely than demand ischemia.

Management

  • Treat trauma
  • Correct shock
  • Correct anemia
  • No ACS-directed therapy

Case 10: Pulmonary Embolism with Troponin Elevation

Presentation

  • Recent surgery
  • Submassive pulmonary embolism
  • Chest pain
  • Troponins 200 → 1600
  • No ischemic ECG changes

Diagnosis

Type 2 MI Secondary to PE

Reasoning

Right ventricular strain from PE causes myocardial oxygen supply-demand mismatch and dynamic troponin elevation.

Management

  • Treat PE
  • Risk-stratify PE severity
  • No routine coronary angiography

10. High-Yield Summary, Resident Pearls & Final Review

This final section consolidates Acute Coronary Syndrome (ACS) into rapid-review frameworks, clinical pearls, landmark trials, and guideline-level takeaways for real-world decision making.

🔥 High-Yield ACS Summary (One-Look Framework)

  • UA: Ischemia only → No troponin rise
  • NSTEMI: Subendocardial infarction → Troponin positive
  • STEMI: Transmural infarction → ST elevation + troponin positive

Core principle: ACS is defined by plaque rupture with downstream myocardial ischemia/injury — differentiated by ECG and troponin dynamics.

⚡ Rapid Comparison Table

Feature UA NSTEMI STEMI
Troponin Negative Positive Positive
ECG Normal / ST↓ / T inversion Same as UA ST elevation / new LBBB
Infarction No Subendocardial Transmural
Management urgency Risk-based Early invasive if high risk Immediate PCI

🧠 Resident Pearls (What Attendings Love to Ask)

  • ST elevation definition: ≥2 contiguous leads; V2–V3 have higher cutoffs
  • New LBBB + ischemia symptoms = consider STEMI equivalent
  • Troponin rise alone ≠ ACS (think Type 2 MI or chronic injury)
  • DAPT is for stent thrombosis prevention, not restenosis
  • Unstable angina is now rare due to high-sensitivity troponin
  • Radial access PCI reduces bleeding complications

⚠️ Common Pitfalls

  • Confusing Type 2 MI with NSTEMI
  • Over-calling ACS in CKD patients with mild troponin elevation
  • Missing atypical presentations in women, elderly, diabetics
  • Forgetting contraindications to fibrinolysis
  • Ignoring medication adherence before stenting

📊 Rapid Decision Algorithm (Simplified)

  • STEMI → Immediate PCI (or fibrinolysis if no PCI)
  • NSTEMI/UA → GRACE/TIMI risk stratification
  • Low risk → Medical management ± stress test
  • Intermediate → Cath within 72h
  • High risk → Cath within 24h
  • Very high risk → Immediate cath

📚 Landmark Trials in ACS

  • CURE Trial: Showed benefit of dual antiplatelet therapy (aspirin + clopidogrel) in ACS
  • PLATO Trial: Ticagrelor superior to clopidogrel in reducing cardiovascular death and MI
  • TRITON–TIMI 38: Prasugrel superior to clopidogrel in ACS patients undergoing PCI
  • MIRACL Trial: Early high-intensity statin therapy reduces ischemic events post-ACS
  • PROVE-IT TIMI 22: High-intensity statin (atorvastatin 80 mg) superior to moderate statin therapy
  • AVERT Trial: Early statin therapy reduces ischemic events even before revascularization

  • 2014 AHA/ACC NSTEMI Guidelines

  • ESC Guidelines on ACS Management (latest update)

🏁 Final Clinical Conclusion

Acute Coronary Syndrome is not a single disease but a spectrum of coronary ischemia driven by plaque instability and thrombosis.

The clinician’s role is to rapidly:

  • Recognize ischemia vs infarction
  • Interpret ECG and troponin trends correctly
  • Stratify risk using TIMI and GRACE scores
  • Select appropriate reperfusion or invasive strategy
  • Balance benefits of PCI against bleeding and adherence risks

Ultimately, ACS management is a time-sensitive decision system where physiology, risk stratification, and procedural eligibility intersect.

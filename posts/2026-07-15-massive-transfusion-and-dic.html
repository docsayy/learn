Massive Transfusion, Hemorrhagic Shock, and Disseminated Intravascular Coagulation

Added July 15, 2026 • Pulmonary and Critical Care • ~30 min read

A critical-care guide to activating massive transfusion, balanced blood-component resuscitation, fibrinogen and calcium replacement, warming, viscoelastic testing, anticoagulant reversal, hemorrhage control, DIC recognition, and avoiding dilutional or thrombotic complications.

Clinical Overview

Hemorrhagic shock requires simultaneous bleeding control and restoration of oxygen-carrying capacity, coagulation, temperature, and perfusion. DIC is systemic pathologic coagulation activation caused by an underlying disease and may produce both thrombosis and bleeding.

Activate Massive Transfusion Early

  • Use institutional criteria and prediction tools.
  • Notify blood bank, surgery, interventional radiology, anesthesia, and ICU.
  • Use rapid infuser and blood warmer.
  • Send CBC, coagulation studies, fibrinogen, ionized calcium, blood gas, lactate, and type/cross.
  • Move immediately toward hemorrhage control.

Balanced Resuscitation

  • Use an institutional balanced RBC, plasma, and platelet strategy during uncontrolled massive bleeding.
  • Transition from fixed ratios to laboratory or viscoelastic-guided therapy as data become available.
  • Minimize crystalloid dilution.
  • Consider whole blood where established and appropriate.

Fibrinogen and Calcium

  • Fibrinogen often falls early and should be replaced with cryoprecipitate or fibrinogen concentrate according to protocol.
  • Monitor ionized calcium frequently because citrate in blood products can cause hypocalcemia.
  • Correct hypothermia and severe acidosis, which impair coagulation.

Tranexamic Acid

TXA benefits selected trauma and postpartum hemorrhage patients when given early according to syndrome-specific protocols. It is not a universal treatment for every GI bleed or all-cause hemorrhage.

Anticoagulant Reversal

  • Warfarin: vitamin K plus four-factor PCC for major bleeding.
  • Unfractionated heparin: protamine.
  • Dabigatran: idarucizumab where indicated.
  • Factor Xa inhibitors: andexanet alfa or PCC according to indication, timing, availability, and protocol.
  • Do not delay mechanical hemorrhage control while waiting for reversal.

Recognizing DIC

  • Falling platelets
  • Prolonged PT and often aPTT
  • Elevated D-dimer/fibrin degradation products
  • Falling fibrinogen in severe consumptive disease
  • Schistocytes may occur but are not required
  • Clinical bleeding, purpura, ischemia, organ failure, or thrombosis

Treating DIC

  • Treat the cause: sepsis, trauma, malignancy, obstetric catastrophe, or vascular disease.
  • Give platelets, plasma, or fibrinogen for active bleeding or high-risk procedures—not laboratory abnormalities alone.
  • Consider anticoagulation when thrombosis predominates or in selected chronic DIC.
  • Reassess serially because the phenotype changes.

Complications of Massive Transfusion

  • Hypocalcemia
  • Hypothermia
  • Hyperkalemia
  • Dilutional coagulopathy
  • TACO and TRALI
  • Acid-base shifts
  • Abdominal compartment syndrome

Common Pitfalls

  • Delaying activation
  • Large-volume crystalloid before blood
  • Ignoring calcium and temperature
  • No fibrinogen plan
  • Treating DIC labs without treating the cause
  • Giving plasma to normalize INR without bleeding

Bottom Line

Massive hemorrhage care is coordinated damage-control resuscitation plus immediate source control. Replace blood components, fibrinogen, calcium, and warmth while minimizing crystalloid; manage DIC by treating its cause and supporting clinically significant bleeding.

Pain Regimen: Practical Inpatient Approach

June 19, 2026 Hospital Medicine

Pain is one of the most common inpatient complaints. A good pain regimen uses multimodal therapy, matches the medication to the type of pain, avoids unnecessary opioid escalation, and always includes safety measures such as bowel regimens and overdose precautions when opioids are used.

Overview

A practical inpatient pain regimen should start with non-opioid and topical options whenever possible, then escalate thoughtfully based on pain severity, renal function, hepatic function, age, opioid tolerance, and the expected duration of pain.

Key principle: Match the medication to the pain type. Nociceptive pain may respond to acetaminophen, NSAIDs, topical agents, and opioids. Neuropathic pain often responds poorly to opioids and usually requires different medications.

Acetaminophen

Acetaminophen is one of the safest and most commonly used inpatient analgesics.

Situation Dose
Typical PRN dosing 650 mg every 4 hours PRN
Alternative dosing 1000 mg every 6 hours PRN
Maximum daily dose 4 g/day in typical patients
Cirrhosis or liver dysfunction Usually max 2 g/day

Acetaminophen is often ordered PRN on admission for most hospitalized patients.

Practical Notes

  • Consider scheduled dosing for uncontrolled pain.
  • Oral and rectal routes are both useful.
  • IV acetaminophen is effective but expensive and often restricted.
  • Avoid confusing abbreviations such as APAP when writing patient-facing instructions.
Always account for acetaminophen contained in combination opioid products such as hydrocodone-acetaminophen or oxycodone-acetaminophen.

NSAIDs

NSAIDs can be very effective for inflammatory pain, musculoskeletal pain, renal colic, and gout, but they are used cautiously in hospitalized patients.

Avoid or Use Caution In

  • AKI or advanced CKD
  • High GI bleed risk
  • Active peptic ulcer disease
  • Therapeutic anticoagulation
  • Severe heart failure or volume-sensitive states
Medication Typical Dose Notes
Ibuprofen 400–600 mg PO every 6–8 hours 400 mg often provides similar analgesia with fewer side effects.
Naproxen Usually every 12 hours Convenient dosing but higher GI bleed concern.
Ketorolac 10–15 mg IV/IM every 6 hours Useful for acute pain, renal colic, or nausea limiting PO intake.
Indomethacin 50 mg TID for gout flare Less commonly used inpatient for general pain.
Celecoxib Often 200 mg BID May have lower GI bleeding risk than nonselective NSAIDs.
For ketorolac, lower doses such as 10–15 mg often provide similar pain relief to 30 mg with potentially less toxicity.

Topical Agents

Lidocaine Patch

Lidocaine patches are useful for localized musculoskeletal pain.

  • Can use up to 3 patches at a time.
  • Typically placed for 12 hours and removed for 12 hours.
  • Prescription 5% patches may be difficult to obtain outpatient.
  • OTC 4% patches are available.

Capsaicin Cream

Capsaicin can help some patients with osteoarthritis, neuropathic pain, and cancer-related pain, although many patients dislike the burning sensation.

  • 0.025% cream: commonly used for osteoarthritis
  • 0.075% cream: often used for neuropathic pain
  • Works through nociceptor stimulation followed by desensitization

Diclofenac Gel

Diclofenac gel is a topical NSAID commonly used for osteoarthritis and localized musculoskeletal pain.

  • 1% gel can be used up to four times daily.
  • 3% gel is for actinic keratosis, not pain.
  • Low systemic absorption helps reduce systemic NSAID toxicity.

Opioids

Opioids are appropriate for moderate to severe acute pain when non-opioid strategies are insufficient, but they require careful monitoring and safety planning.

Medication Typical Starting Dose Important Notes
Tramadol 50 mg every 4–6 hours PRN Variable response; lowers seizure threshold; falling out of favor.
Oxycodone 5 mg PO PRN; consider 2.5 mg in elderly Onset 10–30 minutes; lasts 3–6 hours.
Hydromorphone Use cautiously Rapid onset may cause more euphoria; avoid or reduce in liver dysfunction.
Morphine Use cautiously Useful for air hunger; avoid or reduce in kidney dysfunction.
Meperidine Rarely used Sometimes used for rigors; generally avoided for routine pain.

Combination Products

Common outpatient medications include:

  • Hydrocodone-acetaminophen
  • Oxycodone-acetaminophen

In the hospital, it is often better to separate the opioid and acetaminophen so each can be adjusted independently.

Opioid Safety Checklist

  • Use an opioid conversion chart when switching agents.
  • Consider dose reduction when rotating opioids due to incomplete cross-tolerance.
  • Order naloxone or an opioid overdose reversal plan when appropriate.
  • Start a bowel regimen: usually polyethylene glycol plus senna.
  • Monitor sedation, respiratory rate, renal function, and hepatic function.
Avoid long-acting opioids for routine acute inpatient pain. They are generally reserved for cancer pain, palliative care, or carefully selected chronic pain situations.

Neuropathic Pain

Neuropathic pain often responds poorly to opioids.

Common Options

  • Duloxetine
  • Venlafaxine
  • Gabapentin
  • Pregabalin
  • Tricyclic antidepressants

Tricyclic antidepressants can be effective but are less commonly started inpatient because of anticholinergic effects, sedation, orthostasis, QT effects, and overdose risk.

Burning, shooting, electric, or tingling pain should prompt consideration of neuropathic pain therapy rather than simply escalating opioids.

Methadone

Methadone is a long-acting opioid used in opioid use disorder, chronic pain, and palliative care settings.

Important Features

  • Long half-life
  • Analgesic effect often lasts only 6–8 hours
  • QT prolongation risk
  • High oral bioavailability
  • May have some benefit for neuropathic pain

Patients taking once-daily methadone for opioid use disorder may need divided dosing for acute pain because analgesia does not last the full 24 hours.

Involve pain management, addiction medicine, or pharmacy when starting or adjusting methadone.

Ketamine

Ketamine is increasingly used as an opioid-sparing medication for acute pain, especially in patients with severe pain or opioid tolerance.

Potential Benefits

  • Opioid-sparing effect
  • Useful in opioid-tolerant patients
  • May help with central sensitization

Side Effects

  • Dissociation
  • Emergence reactions
  • Hallucinations
  • Hypertension
  • Tachycardia
Consult pain pharmacy or a pain service before starting ketamine infusions.

Buprenorphine

Buprenorphine is a partial mu-opioid receptor agonist increasingly used for opioid use disorder and chronic pain.

Important Features

  • Partial mu agonist
  • High receptor affinity
  • Lower respiratory depression risk than full agonists
  • Can complicate acute pain management because it occupies opioid receptors strongly

Patients taking buprenorphine can still have acute pain treated, but the plan often requires careful coordination with pain management, addiction medicine, or pharmacy.

Miscellaneous Options

Option Use
Lidocaine swish and swallow Mouth pain or oral ulcers
Lidocaine gel / Uro-Jet Painful Foley placement or urethral discomfort
Warm compress / K-pad Localized swelling or musculoskeletal discomfort
Muscle relaxants Selected spasm-related pain

Wound care consult Pain related to wounds, ulcers, or dressing changes

Muscle Relaxants

Common options include baclofen, cyclobenzaprine, and tizanidine. These often cause sedation and may not substantially improve pain in many patients.

Do not abruptly stop chronic baclofen. Baclofen withdrawal can resemble alcohol withdrawal and may cause seizures.

Post-Operative Pain Control

Post-operative pain should be treated with scheduled non-opioid therapy plus carefully selected opioid rescue medication when needed.

General Approach

  • Use scheduled acetaminophen unless contraindicated.
  • Use NSAIDs such as ketorolac when renal function and bleeding risk allow.
  • Prefer oral medications when the patient can tolerate PO intake.
  • Consider regional nerve blocks.
  • Use short-acting opioids for breakthrough pain.
  • Avoid unnecessary long-acting opioids.

Major Abdominal Incisions

For midline, subcostal, Chevron, or Mercedes incisions, thoracic epidural analgesia may be considered when appropriate.

Oral medications generally last longer and work better for sustained pain control than repeated IV pushes once the patient can tolerate PO intake.

Clinical Pearls

  • Put most patients on PRN acetaminophen unless contraindicated.
  • Schedule acetaminophen when pain is uncontrolled.
  • Avoid NSAIDs in AKI, high GI bleed risk, and severe renal disease.
  • Topical agents are underused and can reduce opioid requirements.
  • Separate acetaminophen from opioid combination products when inpatient.
  • Always order a bowel regimen with opioids.
  • Use opioid conversion charts when rotating opioids.
  • Reduce opioid dose when switching agents to account for incomplete cross-tolerance.
  • Neuropathic pain usually does not respond well to opioids.
  • Consult pain pharmacy for ketamine, methadone, buprenorphine, or complex opioid conversions.

Bottom Line

Start with multimodal non-opioid therapy whenever possible: acetaminophen, topical agents, NSAIDs when safe, and neuropathic agents when appropriate. Use opioids carefully for moderate to severe acute pain, avoid long-acting opioids in routine acute pain, and always pair opioids with safety orders including a bowel regimen and overdose reversal planning.

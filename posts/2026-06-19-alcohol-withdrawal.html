Alcohol Withdrawal Syndrome: Recognition, Treatment, and Relapse Prevention

Added June 19, 2026 • Hospital Medicine • ~12 min read

A practical guide to alcohol withdrawal syndrome including recognition, timing of symptoms, CIWA scoring, benzodiazepine therapy, phenobarbital, delirium tremens, supportive care, and alcohol cessation medications.

Withdrawal Timeline

Recognizing the timing of symptoms is one of the highest-yield aspects of alcohol withdrawal management.

Time Since Last Drink Expected Symptoms
6–36 Hours Tremor, diaphoresis, anxiety, GI upset, tongue fasciculations
12–48 Hours Withdrawal seizures, alcoholic hallucinosis
48–96 Hours Delirium tremens (DTs)

Alcoholic Hallucinosis

  • Usually visual or tactile hallucinations
  • Orientation remains intact
  • Patient is generally aware of surroundings

Delirium Tremens

  • Severe autonomic instability
  • Tachycardia
  • Hypertension
  • Hyperthermia
  • Agitation
  • Profound confusion
  • Disorientation with hallucinations
Important distinction: Hallucinations with preserved orientation suggest alcoholic hallucinosis, whereas hallucinations with severe confusion suggest delirium tremens.
Medical emergency: Delirium tremens carries significant mortality even with treatment and should be treated aggressively.

Withdrawal Seizures

Repeated episodes of alcohol withdrawal increase future seizure risk through the kindling effect. Patients with multiple prior withdrawal episodes are more likely to develop withdrawal seizures.

Who Is At Risk?

Alcohol withdrawal generally occurs in patients with prolonged, heavy alcohol use rather than occasional binge drinkers.

  • Daily alcohol use for weeks to months
  • Near-continuous alcohol intake
  • History of withdrawal symptoms
  • History of withdrawal seizures
  • History of delirium tremens
Brief binge drinking or drinking only a few times per week is much less likely to produce significant alcohol withdrawal.

Pathophysiology

Alcohol withdrawal results from chronic neuroadaptation of the brain.

Normal Neurotransmitter Balance

  • GABA = inhibitory neurotransmitter
  • Glutamate = excitatory neurotransmitter

Effects of Chronic Alcohol Use

  • Alcohol enhances GABA activity
  • Alcohol suppresses glutamate activity
  • The brain compensates by decreasing GABA receptor activity
  • The brain increases glutamate receptor activity

When Alcohol Is Suddenly Stopped

  • Insufficient inhibitory signaling
  • Excess excitatory signaling
  • Tremor
  • Anxiety
  • Diaphoresis
  • Seizures
  • Hallucinations
  • Delirium tremens
Alcohol withdrawal = ↓ GABA activity + ↑ glutamate activity
Alcohol withdrawal is essentially a state of uncontrolled glutamate-mediated excitation.

CIWA & Severity Assessment

The most commonly used withdrawal assessment tool is the CIWA-Ar (Clinical Institute Withdrawal Assessment for Alcohol).

CIWA Evaluates

  • Nausea and vomiting
  • Tremor
  • Sweating
  • Anxiety
  • Agitation
  • Hallucinations
  • Orientation
CIWA Score Severity
<10 Very mild withdrawal
10–15 Mild withdrawal
>15 Moderate withdrawal
>20 Severe withdrawal

Who Requires Admission?

  • History of delirium tremens
  • History of withdrawal seizures
  • Pregnancy
  • Major medical comorbidities
  • Psychosocial instability

Treatment

Benzodiazepines Are First-Line

Benzodiazepines replace deficient GABA activity and remain the cornerstone of treatment.

Medication Comments
Diazepam (Valium) Long acting, preferred in many patients
Chlordiazepoxide (Librium) Long acting, commonly used
Lorazepam (Ativan) Preferred in liver dysfunction
Oxazepam Preferred in liver dysfunction
Temazepam May be used in liver dysfunction

Why Long-Acting Benzodiazepines?

Diazepam and chlordiazepoxide have a self-tapering effect because of their long half-lives, making withdrawal management smoother.

Symptom-Triggered Therapy

Most hospitalized patients are managed using CIWA-driven protocols. Nurses calculate CIWA scores at scheduled intervals and administer benzodiazepines when thresholds are reached.

Phenobarbital

Phenobarbital is commonly used when benzodiazepines alone are insufficient.

Advantages

  • Very long half-life (50–150 hours)
  • Provides prolonged baseline control
  • Useful in severe withdrawal

Typical Dosing

  • 130–260 mg IV
  • May repeat every 15–20 minutes
Phenobarbital can cause significant respiratory depression. Patients requiring repeated doses frequently require ICU-level monitoring.

ICU Therapies

  • Phenobarbital
  • Dexmedetomidine (Precedex)
  • Propofol

Thiamine & Supportive Care

Thiamine

Hospitalized patients with alcohol withdrawal should generally receive:

  • Thiamine
  • Multivitamin
  • Electrolyte replacement as needed

The goal is prevention of Wernicke encephalopathy and nutritional complications.

Physical Therapy

Severe withdrawal can leave patients weak, unsteady, and deconditioned. Consider physical therapy consultation when mobility becomes impaired.

Patients can be in alcohol withdrawal despite having a positive blood alcohol level. Withdrawal occurs relative to their baseline alcohol exposure, not necessarily at a level of zero.

Alcohol Cessation Medications

Naltrexone

Feature Details
Typical Dose 50 mg daily
Mechanism Opioid receptor antagonist
Main Benefit Reduces alcohol cravings

Avoid or Use Caution In

  • Chronic opioid therapy
  • Advanced liver disease

Naltrexone is generally started after acute withdrawal has resolved.

Acamprosate

  • 666 mg three times daily
  • Modulates NMDA and GABA pathways
  • Avoid in significant renal dysfunction

Disulfiram (Antabuse)

  • Creates an unpleasant reaction when alcohol is consumed
  • Does not reduce cravings
  • Requires highly motivated patients

Topiramate

  • May reduce alcohol use
  • Useful when seizure disorder coexists
  • Can cause cognitive slowing
  • May increase kidney stone risk

Recovery Resources

  • Substance use navigators
  • Outpatient treatment programs
  • Partial hospitalization programs
  • Inpatient rehabilitation programs
  • Support groups
  • Alcoholics Anonymous

Clinical Pearls

  1. 6–36 hours: tremor, diaphoresis, anxiety.
  2. 12–48 hours: seizures and alcoholic hallucinosis.
  3. 48–96 hours: delirium tremens.
  4. Withdrawal seizures become more likely with repeated withdrawal episodes (kindling effect).
  5. CIWA is the most commonly used scoring system.
  6. Long-acting benzodiazepines are preferred in most patients.
  7. Use lorazepam or oxazepam when significant liver disease is present.
  8. Phenobarbital is useful in severe refractory withdrawal.
  9. Always administer thiamine.
  10. A positive ethanol level does not exclude alcohol withdrawal.
  11. Naltrexone is generally the preferred pharmacologic relapse-prevention medication.

Bottom Line

Alcohol withdrawal is a predictable syndrome driven by loss of GABA-mediated inhibition and excess glutamate-mediated excitation. Recognizing the timeline of symptoms, using CIWA-guided benzodiazepine therapy, identifying patients at risk for delirium tremens, and connecting patients with long-term cessation resources are the most important components of care.

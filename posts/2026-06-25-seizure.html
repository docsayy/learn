Seizures and Epilepsy: The Complete Resident Guide

Added June 25, 2026 • Neurology • ~24 min read

Seizures are transient episodes of abnormal excessive neuronal activity, while epilepsy is a chronic tendency toward recurrent unprovoked seizures. This guide reviews seizure definitions, ILAE classification, status epilepticus, non-convulsive status, PNES, seizure mimics, diagnostic workup, when to start antiseizure medications, medication selection, side effects, special populations, and high-yield clinical pearls.

Fundamental Definitions

Term Definition Key Point
Seizure Transient signs or symptoms due to abnormal excessive neuronal activity Single event
Epilepsy Two or more unprovoked seizures more than 24 hours apart, or one seizure with high recurrence risk Chronic condition
Acute symptomatic seizure Seizure occurring close to an acute insult such as stroke, trauma, infection, or metabolic derangement Treat the cause; long-term AED may not be needed

Provoked vs Unprovoked

Type Examples Clinical Meaning
Provoked Alcohol withdrawal, hypoglycemia, hyponatremia, fever, acute stroke, infection Address the trigger first
Unprovoked No immediate precipitant Suggests epilepsy risk
One seizure is not automatically epilepsy. Epilepsy implies a tendency toward recurrent unprovoked seizures.

ILAE Seizure Classification

Modern seizure classification begins with seizure onset: focal onset, generalized onset, or unknown onset.

Focal Onset Seizures

Focal onset seizures start in one hemisphere. They may remain focal or spread to become bilateral tonic-clonic seizures.

Awareness Motor Features Non-Motor Features
Focal aware Clonic, tonic, myoclonic, automatisms Sensory, emotional, cognitive, autonomic
Focal impaired awareness Lip smacking, picking, automatisms Behavioral arrest, staring, unresponsiveness
Focal impaired awareness seizure is the modern term for what used to be called complex partial seizure.

Generalized Onset Seizures

Generalized onset seizures involve both hemispheres from onset and usually impair awareness.

Type Description
Tonic-clonic Stiffening followed by rhythmic jerking and post-ictal confusion
Absence Brief staring spell without post-ictal confusion; classic EEG shows 3-Hz spike-wave
Myoclonic Brief shock-like jerks
Atonic Sudden loss of tone, causing drop attacks
Tonic Sustained stiffening

Unknown Onset

Unknown onset seizures are witnessed events where the beginning is unclear. Many present as tonic-clonic seizures.

Status Epilepticus

Status epilepticus is continuous seizure activity or recurrent seizures without return to baseline.

Convulsive status epilepticus = seizure lasting ≥5 minutes or recurrent seizures without recovery

Types

Type Clinical Pattern EEG Requirement
Convulsive status epilepticus Continuous generalized tonic-clonic activity Clinical diagnosis; do not wait for EEG
Non-convulsive status epilepticus Altered mental status without convulsions EEG required
Focal status with impaired awareness Focal motor activity or altered awareness Helpful and often needed

Phase 1: 0–5 Minutes

  • Airway, breathing, circulation
  • Place patient on side if possible
  • Check fingerstick glucose immediately
  • Give thiamine if high risk for deficiency, but do not delay glucose
  • Establish IV access
  • Prepare benzodiazepine

First-Line Benzodiazepines

Medication Typical Dose When Used
Lorazepam 4 mg IV or 0.1 mg/kg IV Preferred when IV access is available
Midazolam 10 mg IM Useful when no IV access
Diazepam Rectal formulation often used in pediatrics/home rescue Home or pediatric rescue option

Phase 2: 5–20 Minutes

If seizures continue after benzodiazepines, load a second-line antiseizure medication.

Medication Typical Loading Dose Clinical Notes
Levetiracetam 60 mg/kg IV, max 4.5 g Commonly preferred; fewer interactions
Fosphenytoin 20 mg PE/kg IV Watch hypotension and arrhythmias
Valproic acid 40 mg/kg IV Avoid in severe liver disease, pregnancy when possible

Phase 3: Refractory Status

  • Intubate if needed.
  • Start continuous EEG.
  • Use anesthetic infusion such as propofol, midazolam, or pentobarbital.
  • Target seizure suppression or burst suppression depending on neurology/ICU plan.
After benzodiazepines and one appropriate second-line load fail, do not keep cycling weak AED doses. Escalate to ICU-level therapy and continuous EEG.

Non-Convulsive Status Epilepticus

Non-convulsive status epilepticus is ongoing seizure activity without obvious convulsions. It is easy to miss because the presentation may be only altered mental status.

When to Suspect NCSE

  • Comatose patient after generalized convulsive status
  • Persistent altered mental status without explanation
  • Fluctuating mental status
  • Critically ill patient with sepsis, trauma, stroke, or hypoxic injury
  • Patient labeled “psychiatric” but not improving

Risk Factors

  • Acute cortical lesion
  • Prior clinical seizure
  • Fluctuating mental status
  • Recent convulsive status epilepticus

EEG Patterns

  • Rhythmic sharp waves or spikes
  • Periodic lateralized epileptiform discharges
  • Electrographic seizures
  • Triphasic waves can mimic NCSE in metabolic encephalopathy
NCSE requires EEG. If the patient remains altered after a convulsive seizure, do not assume they are simply post-ictal forever.

Psychogenic Non-Epileptic Seizures

Psychogenic non-epileptic seizures are paroxysmal episodes that resemble seizures but are not caused by abnormal epileptic neuronal activity.

Feature Epileptic Seizure PNES
Onset Sudden Often gradual or waxing/waning
Eyes Often open Often closed during event
Movements Synchronous and stereotyped Asynchronous, variable, side-to-side
Duration Usually less than 2 minutes Often more than 5 minutes
Injury Lateral tongue biting, falls, injuries possible Serious injury less common
Post-ictal state Confusion and amnesia common May recover quickly or recall event
EEG Ictal epileptiform activity No ictal epileptiform activity

Management

  • Confirm diagnosis with video-EEG when possible.
  • Do not tell the patient they are “faking.”
  • Explain that the events are real and involuntary.
  • Screen for depression, anxiety, PTSD, and trauma history.
  • Refer for cognitive behavioral therapy or appropriate psychotherapy.
  • Avoid unnecessary long-term antiseizure medications if epilepsy is excluded.
PNES is not voluntary behavior. The way the diagnosis is explained can strongly affect whether the patient engages with treatment.

Seizure Mimics

Condition Key Differentiator Pearl
Syncope Presyncope, pallor, brief jerks, rapid recovery Convulsive syncope can look like seizure
Migraine with aura Symptoms spread over minutes Seizure symptoms often evolve over seconds
TIA Negative symptoms such as weakness or numbness Seizures often cause positive symptoms such as jerking or paresthesias
Hypoglycemia Sweating, tremor, confusion, rapid response to glucose Check fingerstick glucose immediately
Cataplexy Emotion-triggered weakness with preserved awareness Associated with narcolepsy
Movement disorder Dystonia, chorea, preserved awareness Video can be very helpful
Panic attack Hyperventilation, palpitations, paresthesias Gradual build favors panic
Do not forget glucose. Hypoglycemia is one of the fastest reversible seizure mimics and seizure triggers.

Diagnostic Workup

Imaging

Modality When to Use What It Finds
CT head without contrast Emergency first-line, trauma, acute bleed concern Hemorrhage, mass effect, large stroke, calcifications
MRI brain Gold standard for new-onset unprovoked seizure when stable Cortical dysplasia, mesial temporal sclerosis, small tumors, cavernomas, stroke
CT with contrast If mass, infection, abscess, or malignancy suspected Enhancing lesions

MRI Protocol Pearls

  • T2/FLAIR for edema, gliosis, and structural lesions
  • DWI for acute ischemia
  • GRE/SWI for blood products or cavernoma
  • Coronal oblique temporal lobe views for hippocampal sclerosis

EEG Modalities

EEG Type Use Key Finding
Routine EEG First-line outpatient study Interictal spikes, slowing, background abnormalities
Video-EEG Spell classification, PNES diagnosis, presurgical evaluation Clinical event plus EEG correlation
Continuous EEG ICU, altered mental status, NCSE detection Real-time seizure detection
Ambulatory EEG Outpatient frequent events Captures habitual events over 24–72 hours
A normal routine EEG does not rule out epilepsy. Sensitivity improves with sleep deprivation and repeat studies.

When to Start Antiepileptic Drugs

After a First Unprovoked Seizure

Do not automatically start long-term medication after every first seizure. Start treatment when recurrence risk is high.

High Recurrence Risk Features

  • Structural brain lesion such as tumor, stroke, cortical dysplasia, or scar
  • Epileptiform discharges on EEG
  • Nocturnal seizure
  • Todd paralysis
  • Prior brain insult
  • Abnormal neurologic exam

Start AEDs When

  • Two unprovoked seizures occur more than 24 hours apart.
  • One seizure occurs with high recurrence risk features.
  • The patient meets criteria for epilepsy.
After a first seizure with normal MRI and normal EEG, observation may be reasonable depending on patient context and shared decision-making.

AED Selection Framework

By Seizure Type

Seizure Type First-Line Alternatives Avoid
Focal onset Lamotrigine Levetiracetam, oxcarbazepine, carbamazepine, lacosamide Depends on comorbidity
Generalized tonic-clonic Valproate in men, levetiracetam or lamotrigine broadly Topiramate Carbamazepine or oxcarbazepine if absence/myoclonus present
Absence Ethosuximide if absence only Valproate, lamotrigine Carbamazepine, phenytoin
Myoclonic Valproate Levetiracetam, clonazepam Carbamazepine, phenytoin
Tonic / atonic Lamotrigine, rufinamide, topiramate Felbamate in selected refractory cases Carbamazepine often avoided

By Patient Population

Patient Type Preferred Avoid / Use Caution
Childbearing potential Lamotrigine, levetiracetam Valproate
Elderly Lamotrigine, levetiracetam Phenytoin, phenobarbital
Liver disease Levetiracetam, lamotrigine Valproate, phenytoin
Renal failure Dose-adjust renally cleared drugs Levetiracetam and gabapentin need renal adjustment
Depression Lamotrigine Levetiracetam may worsen mood
Obesity Topiramate, zonisamide Valproate, gabapentin, pregabalin
Migraine comorbidity Topiramate, valproate Choose based on pregnancy risk and side effects
Valproate is broad-spectrum and effective, but avoid it when possible in patients who could become pregnant because of teratogenicity and neurodevelopmental risk.

Specific AED Profiles

First-Line Agents

Drug Mechanism Key Side Effects Pearls
Levetiracetam SV2A binding Irritability, mood changes, somnolence Few interactions; renally cleared
Lamotrigine Sodium channel blockade Stevens-Johnson syndrome risk, headache, insomnia Titrate slowly; good for focal seizures and mood
Valproic acid Multiple mechanisms including GABA effects Weight gain, tremor, hair loss, hepatotoxicity, teratogenicity Broad-spectrum; strong for generalized seizures
Carbamazepine Sodium channel blockade Hyponatremia, rash, dizziness, drug interactions Useful for focal seizures; enzyme inducer

Second-Line and Adjunctive Agents

Drug Use Case Key Side Effects
Lacosamide Focal seizures; IV option PR prolongation; caution with AV block
Topiramate Focal/generalized seizures, migraine prevention Cognitive slowing, renal stones, metabolic acidosis, weight loss
Zonisamide Focal seizures Renal stones, metabolic acidosis, weight loss, oligohidrosis
Phenytoin Status epilepticus second-line option Zero-order kinetics, gum hyperplasia, hirsutism, osteoporosis, ataxia
Phenobarbital Selected refractory seizures Sedation, cognitive slowing, respiratory depression

Rescue Therapies

  • Lorazepam IV
  • Midazolam IM or intranasal
  • Diazepam rectal gel
  • Clonazepam in selected seizure clusters

AED Escalation and Tapering

Escalation Strategy

  1. Start low and go slow, except during emergencies.
  2. Use monotherapy first when possible.
  3. If the first agent fails, switch to another first-line agent or add a second medication.
  4. Choose combination therapy with different mechanisms.
  5. Avoid duplicating side effects.

When to Consider Tapering

  • Seizure-free for 2–5 years
  • Single seizure type
  • Seizures easily controlled
  • Normal EEG
  • Normal MRI
  • No high-risk epilepsy syndrome

Do Not Taper Lightly When

  • Juvenile myoclonic epilepsy is present
  • Multiple seizure types exist
  • EEG remains epileptiform
  • Structural lesion is present
  • Prior status epilepticus occurred
AEDs should usually be tapered slowly over months. Rapid withdrawal can provoke seizures.

Common Side Effect Management

Side Effect Common Drugs Management
Rash / SJS risk Lamotrigine, phenytoin, carbamazepine Stop immediately if concerning; do not rechallenge severe rash
Hyponatremia Carbamazepine, oxcarbazepine Monitor sodium; switch agent if severe
Weight gain Valproate, gabapentin, pregabalin Diet, exercise, consider alternative
Cognitive slowing Topiramate, phenobarbital, benzodiazepines Lower dose or switch medication
Osteoporosis Phenytoin, phenobarbital, carbamazepine Calcium, vitamin D, bone density monitoring
Mood changes Levetiracetam Lower dose, pyridoxine trial in selected cases, or switch to lamotrigine

Special Populations

Pregnancy

  • Pre-conception counseling is essential.
  • Avoid valproate when possible.
  • Use monotherapy at the lowest effective dose.
  • Folic acid supplementation is recommended.
  • Lamotrigine levels can fall during pregnancy due to increased clearance.
  • Monitor drug levels and seizure control closely.

Elderly Patients

  • Higher risk of drug interactions
  • Higher fall risk
  • Greater sensitivity to cognitive side effects
  • Lamotrigine or levetiracetam often preferred
  • Avoid phenobarbital and chronic phenytoin when possible

Pediatrics

  • Febrile seizures are common and often benign.
  • Infantile spasms require urgent recognition and specific therapy.
  • Many pediatric epilepsy syndromes require syndrome-specific treatment.
  • Dosing is weight-based.

Common Clinical Scenarios

Scenario 1: First Seizure in a Young Adult

A 25-year-old has one generalized tonic-clonic seizure and returns to baseline.

Workup: glucose, electrolytes, CBC/CMP, toxic/metabolic evaluation if indicated, MRI brain, and EEG. If MRI and EEG are normal, observation may be reasonable.

Scenario 2: Breakthrough Seizure on Lamotrigine

  • Check adherence.
  • Ask about sleep deprivation, alcohol, infection, and missed doses.
  • Check drug level if useful.
  • Increase dose or add another agent if truly uncontrolled.

Scenario 3: NCSE in the ICU

A patient remains comatose after convulsive status epilepticus.

Obtain continuous EEG. Persistent altered mental status after seizures may be non-convulsive status.

Scenario 4: PNES

A patient has prolonged shaking episodes with eyes closed, variable movements, and rapid recovery.

Use video-EEG to confirm. Explain the diagnosis respectfully and refer for CBT or psychiatric care.

Landmark Trials and Evidence

Trial / Evidence Key Finding
SANAD II Lamotrigine performed better than levetiracetam and zonisamide for focal epilepsy in treatment failure outcomes
ESETT Levetiracetam, fosphenytoin, and valproate had similar efficacy for benzodiazepine-refractory status epilepticus
Monotherapy studies Most patients who achieve seizure freedom do so with one appropriately selected medication
Older AED comparisons Phenobarbital and phenytoin have more cognitive and long-term toxicity concerns, especially in older adults

Rapid Reference: Seizure Management Algorithm

  1. Confirm the event is likely a seizure.
  2. Check fingerstick glucose immediately.
  3. If seizure lasts more than 5 minutes, treat as status epilepticus.
  4. Give benzodiazepine: lorazepam IV or midazolam IM.
  5. Load AED: levetiracetam, fosphenytoin, or valproate.
  6. If refractory, intubate as needed and start ICU anesthetic infusion with continuous EEG.
  7. For a single recovered seizure, perform MRI and EEG when appropriate.
  8. Start AED if epilepsy criteria or high recurrence risk is present.
  9. Match AED to seizure type, comorbidities, pregnancy potential, renal/liver function, and side effect profile.

Clinical Pearls

  • Epilepsy requires recurrent unprovoked seizures or high recurrence risk after one seizure.
  • Provoked seizures require treatment of the underlying trigger.
  • Focal impaired awareness seizure is the modern term for complex partial seizure.
  • Absence seizures are brief staring spells without post-ictal confusion.
  • Convulsive seizure lasting 5 minutes is status epilepticus.
  • Do not delay benzodiazepines in convulsive status epilepticus.
  • Levetiracetam, fosphenytoin, and valproate are common second-line loads after benzodiazepines.
  • Persistent altered mental status after a seizure should raise concern for NCSE.
  • Video-EEG is the gold standard for PNES diagnosis.
  • Lateral tongue biting favors epileptic seizure; tip-of-tongue biting is less specific.
  • Incontinence can occur in syncope and is not specific for seizure.
  • Todd paralysis localizes to the seizure focus but can mimic stroke.
  • Normal routine EEG does not rule out epilepsy.
  • MRI is preferred for new unprovoked seizure evaluation when stable.
  • Lamotrigine is often favored for focal epilepsy and mood comorbidity.
  • Levetiracetam has few interactions but can worsen irritability or mood.
  • Valproate is broad-spectrum but should be avoided when possible in pregnancy potential.
  • Carbamazepine and oxcarbazepine can worsen absence or myoclonic seizures.
  • Topiramate can cause cognitive slowing, kidney stones, metabolic acidosis, and weight loss.
  • Do not rapidly stop AEDs unless medically necessary.

Bottom Line

Seizure management starts with determining whether the event was provoked, unprovoked, epileptic, or a mimic. Status epilepticus is a time-critical emergency: check glucose, give benzodiazepines early, load an antiseizure medication, and escalate to ICU anesthetic therapy with continuous EEG if refractory. After a first unprovoked seizure, MRI and EEG help estimate recurrence risk and guide whether to start medication. AED choice depends on seizure type, comorbidities, pregnancy potential, renal and liver function, and side effect profile. Always consider NCSE in persistent altered mental status and PNES when events resemble seizures but video-EEG shows no epileptic activity.

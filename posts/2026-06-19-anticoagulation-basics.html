Anticoagulation Basics: Practical Guide to DVT Prophylaxis and Therapeutic Anticoagulation

Added June 19, 2026 • Hematology / Hospital Medicine • ~15 min read

A practical resident-level guide to anticoagulation including DVT prophylaxis, treatment of DVT/PE, atrial fibrillation anticoagulation, DOAC dosing, heparin drips, obesity adjustments, renal considerations, and choosing between apixaban and rivaroxaban.

Overview

Hospital anticoagulation generally falls into two categories:

Type Goal
Prophylactic Anticoagulation Prevent blood clots
Therapeutic Anticoagulation Treat an existing clot or prevent embolic events from high-risk conditions
Most hospitalized medical patients require DVT prophylaxis unless there is a contraindication such as active bleeding or very high bleeding risk.

DVT Prophylaxis

Hospitalization itself is a major risk factor for venous thromboembolism due to immobility, acute illness, and inflammation.

For most medicine patients, DVT prophylaxis should be the default unless a specific reason exists to avoid it.

Who May Not Need Prophylaxis?

  • Fully ambulatory patients
  • Short admissions with minimal risk factors
  • Patients with major bleeding risk

The Padua Risk Score can be used when uncertainty exists.

Common Prophylactic Dosing

Medication Dose Comments
Enoxaparin (Lovenox) 40 mg daily Preferred for most patients
Enoxaparin (renal adjustment) 30 mg daily AKI or severe CKD
Heparin 5000 units q8h Common alternative
Heparin 5000 units q12h Higher bleeding risk

Obesity Adjustments

Medication Dose
Enoxaparin 0.5 mg/kg daily
Heparin 7500 units q8h
Many clinicians prefer enoxaparin because it is once daily rather than requiring multiple injections per day.

Mechanical Prophylaxis

Sequential compression devices (SCDs) or intermittent pneumatic compression devices provide mechanical DVT prophylaxis.

  • Useful when pharmacologic prophylaxis cannot be given
  • Often used in addition to anticoagulation
  • Evidence is modest but generally better than no prophylaxis

Therapeutic Anticoagulation

Therapeutic anticoagulation is used for:

  • DVT
  • Pulmonary embolism
  • Atrial fibrillation
  • Other high-risk thrombotic conditions
Medication Typical Dose
Apixaban 5 mg BID
Rivaroxaban 20 mg daily
Dabigatran 150 mg BID
Enoxaparin 1 mg/kg BID
Heparin drip Titrated infusion

DOAC Dosing Pearls

Apixaban Dose Reduction

Reduce to 2.5 mg BID when the patient meets at least two of the following:

  • Age ≥80 years
  • Weight ≤60 kg
  • Creatinine ≥1.5 mg/dL

New DVT or PE

Apixaban 10 mg BID × 7 days
Then 5 mg BID

Rivaroxaban Loading Dose

Rivaroxaban 15 mg BID × 21 days
Then 20 mg daily
Rivaroxaban should be taken with food to ensure adequate absorption.

Dabigatran

Requires approximately 5 days of parenteral anticoagulation before initiation for acute VTE treatment.

Why Use a Heparin Drip?

Heparin is fast on and fast off.

Advantages:

  • Rapid anticoagulation
  • Quick reversal when stopped
  • Useful before procedures
  • Preferred when bleeding risk is uncertain
  • Useful in severe renal dysfunction

Warfarin

Warfarin remains useful in selected situations but is generally less convenient than DOACs.

Issue Clinical Impact
Frequent INR monitoring Requires regular lab checks
Food interactions Vitamin K intake affects INR
Drug interactions Many significant interactions
Slow onset May require bridging
When a DOAC is appropriate, it is often preferred over warfarin because of easier administration and fewer monitoring requirements.

Apixaban vs Rivaroxaban

Why Many Clinicians Prefer Apixaban

  • Twice-daily dosing produces steadier anticoagulation
  • Lower peak levels
  • Potentially lower bleeding risk
  • Potentially lower thrombotic risk

Advantages of Rivaroxaban

  • Once-daily maintenance dosing
  • Simpler regimen
  • May improve adherence in selected patients
Feature Apixaban Rivaroxaban
Dosing BID Daily
Steady anticoagulation Better Less steady
Adherence convenience Lower Higher
Common clinical preference Often favored Useful when adherence is a concern

Clinical Pearls

  • Most hospitalized medicine patients need DVT prophylaxis.
  • Enoxaparin 40 mg daily is the most common prophylactic regimen.
  • Use renal-adjusted dosing when kidney function is impaired.
  • Therapeutic anticoagulation treats existing clots or high-risk embolic conditions.
  • Apixaban requires a 7-day loading phase for acute DVT/PE.
  • Rivaroxaban requires a 21-day loading phase for acute DVT/PE.
  • Heparin drips are useful because they are rapidly reversible.
  • Warfarin has numerous food and drug interactions.
  • Apixaban is often preferred because of more stable anticoagulation.
  • Rivaroxaban may be better for patients with adherence challenges.

Bottom Line

Think of anticoagulation in two categories: prophylaxis and treatment. Most hospitalized patients require DVT prophylaxis, usually with enoxaparin. For therapeutic anticoagulation, DOACs such as apixaban and rivaroxaban are commonly preferred, while heparin drips remain useful when rapid reversal or procedural flexibility is needed. Apixaban is often favored for its steady anticoagulation profile, whereas rivaroxaban may improve adherence because of once-daily dosing.

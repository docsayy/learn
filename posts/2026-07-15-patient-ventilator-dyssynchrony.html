Patient–Ventilator Dyssynchrony: Waveforms, Causes, and Bedside Correction

Added July 15, 2026 • Pulmonary and Critical Care • ~30 min read

A waveform-based approach to trigger, flow, cycling, and mode-related dyssynchrony, including double triggering, reverse triggering, ineffective efforts, auto-triggering, flow starvation, premature and delayed cycling, excessive respiratory drive, and safer correction before escalating sedation or paralysis.

Clinical Overview

Dyssynchrony occurs when ventilator delivery does not match the patient’s neural timing or demand. It can increase work of breathing, discomfort, sedation exposure, tidal volume, and lung injury. The waveform is a bedside physiologic monitor.

Identify the Type → Identify the Cause → Correct the Cause

Four Questions

  1. Did the patient trigger the ventilator appropriately?
  2. Was inspiratory flow sufficient?
  3. Did the ventilator cycle to expiration at the right time?
  4. Was the delivered tidal volume and pressure safe?

Trigger Dyssynchrony

  • Ineffective effort: patient effort fails to trigger; common with auto-PEEP, weak muscles, insensitive trigger, or over-assistance.
  • Auto-triggering: ventilator breath without true effort; caused by leak, water in circuit, cardiac oscillation, or overly sensitive trigger.
  • Double triggering: a second breath begins before exhalation is complete, often from high respiratory drive or short inspiratory time.

Flow Dyssynchrony

  • Flow starvation occurs when inspiratory demand exceeds delivered flow.
  • Pressure-time waveform may show scooping or concavity.
  • Patient appears air hungry with accessory-muscle use.
  • Correct by increasing inspiratory flow, changing flow pattern, increasing pressure support, or treating excessive drive—while preserving safe tidal volume.

Cycling Dyssynchrony

  • Premature cycling: ventilator ends inspiration before neural inspiration ends; may cause double triggering.
  • Delayed cycling: ventilator continues inspiration after patient wants to exhale; common in obstruction with long machine inspiratory time.
  • Adjust inspiratory time, cycling threshold, flow, or mode according to the pattern.

Reverse Triggering

A ventilator-delivered breath induces a delayed diaphragmatic contraction. If the contraction occurs late, breath stacking and excessive tidal volume can result. It is often seen during controlled ventilation and deep sedation.

  • Confirm with pressure/flow waveforms and, when available, esophageal pressure or diaphragm signal.
  • Reduce unnecessary deep sedation.
  • Adjust respiratory rate or inspiratory timing.
  • Use neuromuscular blockade only when clinically necessary.

Excessive Respiratory Drive

  • Pain or anxiety
  • Fever
  • Metabolic acidosis
  • Hypoxemia
  • Pulmonary edema
  • Sepsis
  • Inadequate support
  • Delirium
Do not treat every rapid respiratory pattern with sedation. The ventilator may be revealing untreated physiology.

Obstructive Disease and Auto-PEEP

Intrinsic PEEP creates a threshold load that the patient must overcome to trigger. Reduce respiratory rate, shorten inspiratory time, increase expiratory time, treat bronchospasm, and consider modest external PEEP to assist triggering in spontaneously breathing patients.

Stepwise Correction

  1. Examine the patient and circuit.
  2. Measure plateau pressure and total PEEP when appropriate.
  3. Identify trigger, flow, or cycle problem.
  4. Treat pain, acidosis, hypoxemia, fever, bronchospasm, or secretions.
  5. Adjust one ventilator variable at a time.
  6. Reassess waveform, effort, tidal volume, and gas exchange.

Sedation and Paralysis

Analgesia and light sedation may improve tolerance, but deep sedation can worsen delirium, prolong ventilation, and produce reverse triggering. Neuromuscular blockade is reserved for severe ARDS, dangerous breath stacking, procedures, or other defined indications—not for an uninterpreted waveform.

Common Pitfalls

  • Calling agitation a sedation deficiency without examining waveforms
  • Increasing pressure support until tidal volume becomes injurious
  • Ignoring auto-PEEP
  • Confusing cardiac oscillation with patient triggering
  • Treating reverse triggering with deeper sedation automatically
  • Changing multiple settings simultaneously

Bottom Line

Dyssynchrony is usually solvable when classified as trigger, flow, or cycling mismatch. Correct the patient’s physiologic problem and the ventilator timing before escalating sedation or paralysis.

Insulin Regimen: Practical Inpatient Approach

Added June 19, 2026 • Endocrinology / Hospital Medicine • ~15 min read

A practical inpatient guide to insulin management including glucose targets, outpatient diabetes medications, basal-bolus dosing, correction insulin, titration, NPO adjustments, steroid-induced hyperglycemia, hypoglycemia safety, and the rule of 1800.

Overview

Inpatient insulin management is common and can feel intimidating early in training. The key is to use a structured approach: review outpatient medications, set an appropriate glucose goal, estimate total daily insulin needs, split insulin into basal and nutritional components, and adjust based on fasting and premeal glucose trends.

Core principle: Basal-bolus insulin is preferred for most hospitalized patients who are eating. Sliding scale alone is usually inadequate.

Inpatient Glucose Targets

Target Inpatient Glucose = 140–180 mg/dL

This target is based largely on ICU data from the NICE-SUGAR trial and has been broadly extrapolated to many inpatient settings.

Practical point: Avoid both severe hyperglycemia and overly aggressive glucose control. Hypoglycemia is dangerous and should be prevented.

Outpatient Diabetes Medications

Traditional inpatient practice has been to discontinue most outpatient antihyperglycemic medications on admission. This is changing, and stable patients who are eating may be candidates for continuation or restarting selected oral agents.

Medication Class Common Concern Inpatient
Metformin Lactic acidosis concern, renal dysfunction, hypoxia, contrast exposure, heart failure exacerbation concerns
Sulfonylureas Hypoglycemia
Thiazolidinediones Fluid retention and heart failure exacerbation
GLP-1 receptor agonists GI symptoms, nausea, vomiting, pancreatitis concern
SGLT2 inhibitors Euglycemic DKA, genitourinary infections, hypovolemia
SGLT2 inhibitor caution: These can cause euglycemic DKA, especially with acute illness, fasting, surgery, dehydration, or reduced insulin. They may be restarted once the patient is clinically improving and eating reliably, depending on institutional practice.

Common Insulin Types

Type Examples Typical Role
Rapid-acting Aspart, Lispro, Glulisine Mealtime and correction insulin
Short-acting Regular insulin Meals, correction, IV insulin protocols
Intermediate-acting NPH Basal coverage, steroid-induced hyperglycemia
Long-acting basal Glargine, Detemir Basal insulin coverage
Ultra-long acting Degludec Long basal coverage, mostly outpatient

Reviewing Glucose Data

Most electronic medical records have a glycemic management, endocrine, or glucose summary tab. This is often the fastest way to review:

  • Fasting morning glucose
  • Premeal glucose
  • Bedtime glucose
  • Total insulin given per day
  • Correctional insulin requirements
  • Hypoglycemia episodes
Pattern recognition: If fasting AM glucose is high, increase basal insulin. If premeal and bedtime glucose are high, increase mealtime insulin.

If the Patient Is Not on Insulin Outpatient

For insulin-naive patients, estimate the total daily dose based on body weight, age, renal function, glucose levels, and hypoglycemia risk.

Clinical Situation Starting Total Daily Dose
Age >70, eGFR <60, or high hypoglycemia risk 0.2–0.3 units/kg/day
Blood glucose 140–200 mg/dL 0.4 units/kg/day
Blood glucose 201–400 mg/dL 0.5 units/kg/day
Insulin resistance or steroid-induced hyperglycemia 0.5–0.6 units/kg/day
Use caution: In AKI, poor oral intake, frailty, or high hypoglycemia risk, start lower and titrate carefully.

Basal-Bolus Strategy

Total Daily Dose = 50% basal + 50% nutritional insulin

Example

80 kg patient × 0.5 units/kg/day = 40 units/day

Basal: 20 units glargine qHS
Prandial: 7 units aspart TID AC

Basal-bolus insulin better matches physiology than sliding scale alone and is preferred for most hospitalized patients who are eating.

If Already on Insulin Outpatient

For patients already using insulin at home, a common starting strategy is to use a reduced percentage of their home total daily dose.

Initial inpatient dose ≈ 75–80% of home total daily insulin dose

Dose reduction accounts for changes in diet, acute illness, renal function, and reduced caloric intake during hospitalization.

Do not blindly continue home insulin: Patients may eat less in the hospital, develop AKI, or have rapidly changing insulin requirements.

Sliding Scale Insulin

Sliding scale insulin alone is inadequate for most patients because it reacts to hyperglycemia after it happens rather than preventing it.

Better Starting Options

  • Basal-bolus insulin plus correctional insulin
  • Basal insulin plus correctional insulin if intake is uncertain
  • Correctional insulin alone only for selected low-risk patients or first 24 hours when insulin needs are unclear
Practical compromise: If you are worried about hypoglycemia, basal insulin at 0.2–0.3 units/kg plus correctional insulin is often safer than full basal-bolus right away.

Correctional Insulin

  • Sensitive scale
  • Moderate scale
  • Resistant scale

Titrating Insulin While Inpatient

If glucose values are consistently above goal, adjust insulin based on the pattern.

Daily adjustment algorithm:
Morning glucose high → increase basal insulin.
Premeal glucose high → increase nutritional insulin.
Everything high → increase total daily dose by 10–20%.

Fasting Morning Glucose Elevated

Fasting Glucose Basal Insulin Adjustment
180–200 mg/dL Increase basal dose by 10%
200–300 mg/dL Increase basal dose by 20%
>300 mg/dL Increase basal dose by 30%

Premeal Glucose Elevated

Premeal Glucose Nutritional Insulin Adjustment
180–200 mg/dL Increase nutritional dose by 10%
200–300 mg/dL Increase nutritional dose by 20%
>300 mg/dL Increase nutritional dose by 30%

All Glucose Values Elevated

New TDD = Prior 24-hour insulin use + 10–20%

Then redistribute the new total daily dose into basal and nutritional insulin.

Steroid-Induced Hyperglycemia

Steroids commonly cause inpatient hyperglycemia and often require proactive insulin adjustment.

Steroid Pattern Typical Glucose Pattern Insulin Pearl
Prednisone Daytime and afternoon hyperglycemia NPH often matches prednisone kinetics
Dexamethasone More prolonged hyperglycemia May need longer basal and prandial coverage
Steroid taper or stop Rapidly falling insulin requirement Reduce insulin quickly to avoid hypoglycemia
Important: Insulin needs can drop dramatically when steroids are reduced or discontinued.

When the Patient Is NPO

NPO Strategy

  • Stop scheduled mealtime rapid-acting insulin.
  • Continue basal insulin, often at a reduced dose depending on risk.
  • Continue correctional insulin.
  • Check blood glucose every 4–6 hours.
Never stop basal insulin in Type 1 diabetes. Even when NPO, patients require basal insulin to prevent DKA.

Hypoglycemia Management

Situation Action
Glucose <70 mg/dL Treat hypoglycemia
Glucose <54 mg/dL Severe hypoglycemia
Awake and able to take PO Give oral carbohydrate such as juice or glucose tabs
Unable to take PO with IV access Give IV dextrose
No IV access Give glucagon
Always order a hypoglycemia protocol so nurses can treat low glucose immediately.

Rule of 1800

The rule of 1800 estimates how much 1 unit of rapid-acting insulin will lower glucose.

Insulin Sensitivity Factor = 1800 ÷ Total Daily Insulin Dose

Example

Patient takes 60 units/day
1800 ÷ 60 = 30

1 unit of rapid-acting insulin lowers glucose by about 30 mg/dL

Landmark Trials

Trial Clinical Pearl
NICE-SUGAR Supports inpatient glucose target around 140–180 mg/dL rather than intensive control
RABBIT-2 Basal-bolus insulin is superior to sliding scale alone in many hospitalized patients
Basal Plus Trial Basal insulin plus correctional insulin is a reasonable alternative in selected patients

Clinical Pearls

  1. Goal inpatient glucose is usually 140–180 mg/dL.
  2. Review fasting, premeal, bedtime, and total insulin use every day.
  3. High fasting glucose means basal insulin is usually too low.
  4. High premeal glucose means nutritional insulin is usually too low.
  5. Basal-bolus insulin is preferred for most patients who are eating.
  6. Sliding scale alone is usually inadequate.
  7. Use lower starting doses in elderly patients, AKI, CKD, frailty, or poor intake.
  8. Continue basal insulin when NPO, especially in Type 1 diabetes.
  9. Stop scheduled mealtime insulin when NPO.
  10. Insulin needs can fall quickly when steroids are stopped.
  11. Always order a hypoglycemia protocol.
  12. Use the rule of 1800 to estimate insulin sensitivity.

Bottom Line

For most hospitalized patients who are eating, basal-bolus insulin plus correctional insulin is the preferred regimen. Start with weight-based dosing if the patient is insulin-naive, or 75–80% of the home insulin dose if already using insulin. Adjust basal insulin for fasting hyperglycemia and nutritional insulin for premeal hyperglycemia. Use correctional insulin as an add-on, not as the only strategy, and always include a hypoglycemia protocol.

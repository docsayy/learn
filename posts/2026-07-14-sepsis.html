Sepsis and Septic Shock: The Complete Infectious Diseases Approach

Added July 14, 2026 • Infectious Disease • ~55 min read

Sepsis is life-threatening organ dysfunction caused by a dysregulated response to infection. Successful treatment requires more than ordering broad-spectrum antibiotics. The clinician must recognize organ dysfunction, obtain meaningful cultures, identify the most likely anatomic source, select empiric therapy according to patient-specific resistance risk, optimize antimicrobial exposure, obtain urgent source control, interpret microbiologic results correctly, narrow or stop therapy promptly, and investigate persistent shock or bacteremia for an uncontrolled source, resistant pathogen, incorrect diagnosis, or noninfectious mimic.

Clinical Overview

Sepsis develops when infection produces life-threatening organ dysfunction through a dysregulated host response. The clinical syndrome may arise from bacterial, fungal, viral, or parasitic infection and may be either culture positive or culture negative.

The infectious-diseases approach asks several questions simultaneously:

  1. Is infection actually present?
  2. What is the most likely anatomic source?
  3. Which organisms are likely?
  4. Which resistance mechanisms are plausible?
  5. Which specimens should be obtained immediately?
  6. Does the patient need urgent drainage, debridement, extraction, or surgery?
  7. Are current antimicrobial doses adequate?
  8. When can therapy be narrowed, changed to oral treatment, or stopped?
Recognize Organ Dysfunction + Treat the Likely Pathogen + Control the Source + Reassess Continuously

Time-Critical Sepsis Syndromes

  • Bacterial meningitis
  • Necrotizing soft-tissue infection
  • Ascending cholangitis with obstruction
  • Perforated viscus
  • Obstructed infected urinary tract
  • Infected intravascular catheter with shock
  • Endocarditis with hemodynamic or embolic complications
  • Neutropenic septic shock
  • Fulminant Clostridioides difficile infection
  • Severe malaria or another life-threatening imported infection
The most powerful sepsis intervention may be an operation, drainage procedure, device removal, or biliary or urinary decompression—not another antibiotic.

Definitions

Infection

Infection is invasion of normally sterile tissue, fluid, or blood by a pathogenic organism, or clinically significant disease arising from a mucosal or surface site.

Sepsis

Sepsis = Life-Threatening Organ Dysfunction Caused by a Dysregulated Host Response to Infection

Clinically, organ dysfunction is represented by an acute increase in Sequential Organ Failure Assessment score of at least two points in the setting of suspected or documented infection.

Septic Shock

Septic shock is a subset of sepsis with profound circulatory, cellular, and metabolic abnormalities.

Vasopressors Required to Maintain MAP ≥65 mm Hg
+ Lactate >2 mmol/L
Despite Adequate Volume Resuscitation

Terms No Longer Preferred

  • Severe sepsis
  • Septicemia
  • Blood poisoning

Bacteremia and sepsis are not interchangeable. A patient may have:

  • Bacteremia without sepsis
  • Sepsis without bacteremia
  • Septic shock with negative cultures

Infection vs Sepsis

Infection Without Sepsis

Examples include:

  • Uncomplicated cellulitis
  • Stable cystitis
  • Mild community-acquired pneumonia without organ dysfunction
  • Localized abscess without systemic illness

Sepsis Requires Organ Dysfunction

Examples include:

  • Pneumonia with acute hypoxemic respiratory failure
  • Pyelonephritis with acute kidney injury and encephalopathy
  • Cholangitis with hypotension and coagulopathy
  • Peritonitis with elevated lactate and oliguria

Organ Dysfunction May Be Subtle

  • New confusion
  • New oxygen requirement
  • Platelet decline
  • Bilirubin elevation
  • Acute kidney injury
  • Hypotension relative to baseline
  • Reduced urine output
Do not diagnose sepsis from fever, tachycardia, or leukocytosis alone. Conversely, do not exclude sepsis because fever or leukocytosis is absent.

Septic Shock

Clinical Features

  • Hypotension
  • Altered mental status
  • Mottled or cool extremities
  • Delayed capillary refill
  • Oliguria
  • Elevated or rising lactate
  • Need for vasopressor support

Not All Septic Shock Is Warm Shock

Patients may have:

  • Warm extremities and bounding pulses
  • Cool extremities and intense vasoconstriction
  • Concurrent septic cardiomyopathy
  • Concurrent hypovolemia
  • Concurrent obstructive or hemorrhagic shock

Shock Without Hypotension

Tissue hypoperfusion may occur before overt hypotension. Warning signs include:

  • Rising lactate
  • Altered mentation
  • Oliguria
  • Mottled skin
  • Prolonged capillary refill
  • Increasing metabolic acidosis

Occult Shock

A normal blood pressure does not guarantee adequate perfusion, particularly in a previously hypertensive patient or one with compensatory vasoconstriction.

Recognition and Screening

Sepsis Is a Clinical Diagnosis

No single:

  • Vital sign
  • Laboratory test
  • Biomarker
  • Screening score
  • Culture result

can independently diagnose or exclude sepsis.

Recognition Requires Two Parallel Assessments

  1. Is infection plausible?
  2. Is acute organ dysfunction present?

Common Presentations

  • Fever or hypothermia
  • Tachypnea
  • New confusion
  • Hypotension
  • New oxygen requirement
  • Acute kidney injury
  • Thrombocytopenia
  • Hyperglycemia
  • Functional decline in an older adult

Sepsis Without Fever

Absence of fever is common in:

  • Older adults
  • Neutropenia
  • Corticosteroid exposure
  • Advanced cirrhosis
  • Profound shock
  • Severe malnutrition

Recognizing Organ Dysfunction

Baseline Matters

Interpret abnormalities relative to the patient’s baseline:

  • Chronic kidney disease
  • Chronic thrombocytopenia
  • Cirrhosis
  • Chronic hypoxemia
  • Baseline cognitive impairment

qSOFA, SIRS, NEWS, and Other Screening Tools

qSOFA Variables

  • Respiratory rate at least 22/min
  • Altered mentation
  • Systolic blood pressure 100 mm Hg or lower

qSOFA Limitation

qSOFA should not be used as the sole screening tool or as a rule-out test for sepsis. A low score does not exclude serious infection.

SIRS Criteria

  • Temperature above 38°C or below 36°C
  • Heart rate above 90/min
  • Respiratory rate above 20/min or low PaCO₂
  • Abnormal WBC count or bandemia

SIRS is sensitive but nonspecific and occurs with:

  • Trauma
  • Pancreatitis
  • Surgery
  • Hemorrhage
  • Drug reactions

Current Screening Approach

NEWS, NEWS2, MEWS, or SIRS may be used for screening, but any positive screen requires clinical evaluation rather than automatic diagnosis.

Immediate Approach

First 10–15 Minutes

  • Assess airway, breathing, circulation, and mental status.
  • Confirm vital signs.
  • Establish IV access.
  • Obtain blood cultures and targeted specimens.
  • Measure lactate when hypoperfusion is possible.
  • Begin fluid and vasopressor management when needed.
  • Choose empiric antimicrobials.
  • Call the procedural or surgical team when source control is likely.

At the Bedside

Immediately examine:

  • Skin and wounds
  • Central and peripheral IV sites
  • Urinary catheter
  • Lungs
  • Abdomen and perineum
  • Joints
  • Spine
  • Implanted-device pockets

Do These in Parallel

Resuscitation + Cultures + Antibiotics + Imaging + Source-Control Planning

Timing of Antimicrobial Therapy

Septic Shock

Administer Antimicrobials Immediately, Ideally Within 1 Hour

Probable or Definite Sepsis Without Shock

Administer antimicrobial therapy immediately, ideally within one hour of recognition.

Possible Sepsis Without Shock

Conduct a rapid, time-limited evaluation. If infection remains a meaningful concern, administer antimicrobials within approximately three hours from the time sepsis was first suspected.

Low Likelihood of Infection Without Shock

It is reasonable to defer antibiotics while:

  • Continuing close observation
  • Repeating the examination
  • Reviewing laboratory trends
  • Pursuing targeted testing

Why This Distinction Matters

Automatic antibiotics for every possible sepsis alert cause:

  • Drug toxicity
  • C. difficile infection
  • Resistance
  • Diagnostic confusion
  • Treatment of noninfectious illnesses
Do not delay antibiotics in septic shock while waiting for CT, lumbar puncture, central-line placement, or a complete diagnostic workup.

Cultures Before Antibiotics

General Rule

Obtain Meaningful Cultures Before Antibiotics When This Does Not Cause Harmful Delay

Why It Matters

  • Identifies the organism
  • Provides susceptibility results
  • Allows de-escalation
  • Clarifies contamination vs infection
  • Supports public-health investigation

Do Not Delay Therapy For:

  • Difficult venous access
  • Waiting for a central line
  • Waiting for every possible specimen
  • Delayed procedure scheduling

After Antibiotics Have Started

Cultures may still be useful, particularly with:

  • Persistent bacteremia
  • Endocarditis
  • Deep abscess
  • Device infection
  • Resistant-organism concern

Blood-Culture Strategy

When to Obtain Blood Cultures

  • Sepsis or septic shock
  • Rigors
  • Endocarditis concern
  • Central-line infection
  • Severe pneumonia
  • Cholangitis
  • Severe intra-abdominal infection
  • Neutropenic fever
  • Immunocompromised host with systemic illness

Number of Sets

Collect at Least 2 Blood-Culture Sets

Two to four sets may be appropriate during evaluation of a septic episode, depending on:

  • Endocarditis probability
  • Catheter evaluation
  • Laboratory and institutional protocol
  • Previous antibiotic exposure

Timing Between Sets

A prolonged waiting interval is unnecessary. Sets may be obtained sequentially from separate anatomic sites.

Peripheral Collection

Peripheral venipuncture is generally preferred because cultures drawn through existing lines have higher contamination risk.

Blood Volume and Collection Technique

Volume Is Critical

An adult blood-culture set should generally contain approximately 20–30 mL of blood, distributed according to the blood-culture system.

At Least 2 Sets = Approximately 40–60 mL Total

Best Practices

  • Perform hand hygiene.
  • Disinfect the venipuncture site properly.
  • Disinfect bottle tops.
  • Allow antiseptic to dry.
  • Fill bottles to the recommended volume.
  • Label the collection site and time.

Common Failure

A single underfilled blood-culture set is not an adequate evaluation for bloodstream infection.

Aerobic and Anaerobic Bottles

Both are generally appropriate because:

  • Some facultative organisms recover better in one bottle.
  • Anaerobic bloodstream infection may occur.
  • Bottle pattern assists interpretation.

Blood Cultures When a Central Line May Be the Source

Obtain:

  • At least one peripheral blood-culture set
  • A simultaneous set from the central catheter
  • Samples from relevant lumens when indicated

Differential Time to Positivity

Catheter Culture Positive ≥2 Hours Before Peripheral Culture → Supports Catheter Source

Limitations

  • Blood volumes must be similar.
  • Collection times must be documented accurately.
  • Antibiotics may reduce reliability.
  • A catheter can still be infected without this finding.

Do Not Draw Every Culture Through the Line

Without peripheral cultures, it becomes difficult to distinguish:

  • Catheter colonization
  • Contamination
  • True systemic bacteremia
  • Catheter-related bloodstream infection

Interpreting Positive Blood Cultures

Organisms Usually Clinically Significant

  • Staphylococcus aureus
  • Enterobacterales
  • Pseudomonas aeruginosa
  • Streptococcus pneumoniae
  • Beta-hemolytic streptococci
  • Candida species
  • Anaerobic gram-negative bacilli

Organisms That May Be Contaminants

  • Coagulase-negative staphylococci
  • Corynebacterium species
  • Cutibacterium acnes
  • Bacillus species other than B. anthracis
  • Micrococcus

Factors Supporting True Infection

  • Multiple positive sets
  • Rapid time to positivity
  • Same organism from repeated cultures
  • Intravascular or prosthetic device
  • Compatible syndrome
  • Immunocompromise
  • Persistent positivity

Polymicrobial Bacteremia

Consider:

  • Intra-abdominal infection
  • Necrotic wound
  • Catheter manipulation
  • Injection of contaminated material
  • Factitious infection
  • Severe mucosal disruption

Blood-Culture Contamination

Consequences

  • Unnecessary vancomycin
  • Unnecessary echocardiography
  • Unnecessary line removal
  • Longer hospitalization
  • Repeat testing
  • Incorrect sepsis diagnosis

One Positive Bottle

One bottle with a common skin commensal in a clinically improving patient may represent contamination, especially when:

  • Other sets remain negative.
  • Time to positivity is prolonged.
  • No device is present.
  • No compatible source exists.

Do Not Dismiss Automatically

Coagulase-negative staphylococci may cause:

  • Prosthetic-valve endocarditis
  • Central-line infection
  • Cardiac-device infection
  • Prosthetic-joint infection
S. aureus, Candida, and enteric gram-negative bacilli should not be dismissed as routine contaminants.

Repeat Blood Cultures

Repeat Until Clearance With:

  • S. aureus bacteremia
  • Candidemia
  • Endocarditis
  • Persistent fever or instability
  • Retained infected intravascular device
  • Suspected endovascular infection
  • Selected multidrug-resistant bloodstream infections

Often Unnecessary With:

  • Improving uncomplicated gram-negative bacteremia
  • Source-controlled urinary bacteremia
  • Source-controlled biliary bacteremia
  • Rapidly improving pneumonia without endovascular concern

When to Obtain

For S. aureus or candidemia, obtain repeat cultures approximately every 24–48 hours until negative.

Persistent Positivity Means:

  • Uncontrolled source
  • Endocarditis
  • Infected thrombosis
  • Retained device
  • Inactive antibiotic
  • Inadequate drug exposure
  • Metastatic infection

Other Microbiologic Specimens

Respiratory

  • Sputum
  • Endotracheal aspirate
  • BAL in selected patients
  • Respiratory viral NAAT

Urine

  • Obtain with a compatible urinary syndrome.
  • Replace an old catheter when possible.
  • Collect from the new catheter or sampling port.
  • Never collect from the drainage bag.

CSF

  • Cell count and differential
  • Glucose and protein
  • Gram stain and culture
  • Targeted molecular testing

Abdominal and Deep-Space Specimens

  • Aspirated pus
  • Fresh operative fluid
  • Deep tissue
  • Aerobic and anaerobic culture
  • Fungal culture when indicated

Chronic Drain Cultures

Cultures from a long-standing drain often reflect colonization and should not automatically determine treatment.

Rapid Diagnostic Testing

Potential Tools

  • Multiplex blood-culture identification panels
  • Rapid resistance-gene detection
  • MALDI-TOF identification
  • Respiratory viral NAAT
  • MRSA nasal PCR
  • Urinary antigens
  • Targeted fungal biomarkers
  • Pathogen-specific molecular testing

Blood-Culture Panels

Rapid panels may identify:

  • Organism
  • mecA or mecC
  • vanA or vanB
  • Selected carbapenemase genes
  • Selected Candida species

Rapid Result Requires Rapid Action

Rapid Test + Stewardship Interpretation → Earlier Effective Therapy or Earlier De-escalation

Limitations

  • Only included targets are detected.
  • Resistance genes do not predict every susceptibility result.
  • Mixed cultures may be difficult to interpret.
  • Conventional cultures remain necessary.

MRSA Nasal PCR

A negative result may support stopping empiric MRSA therapy in many pneumonia patients when:

  • Local negative predictive value is high.
  • No recent MRSA lower-respiratory isolate exists.
  • There is no concurrent MRSA bacteremia or extrapulmonary source.

Identifying the Anatomic Source

Common Sources

  • Pulmonary
  • Urinary
  • Intra-abdominal
  • Biliary
  • Skin and soft tissue
  • Intravascular catheter
  • Endocardial
  • Central nervous system
  • Bone and joint

Bedside Localization

  • New oxygen requirement or infiltrate
  • Flank pain or urinary obstruction
  • Abdominal tenderness or ileus
  • Jaundice or cholestatic liver tests
  • Skin pain, bullae, or crepitus
  • Line-site inflammation or rigors during infusion
  • New murmur or embolic findings
  • Headache, meningismus, or focal neurologic deficit

Imaging

  • Chest radiography or CT
  • Renal ultrasound or CT
  • CT abdomen and pelvis
  • Right-upper-quadrant ultrasound
  • MRI spine
  • Echocardiography

Do Not Anchor on the First Abnormal Test

Bacteriuria, chronic infiltrates, pressure wounds, and colonized drains are common and may not explain the sepsis syndrome.

Source Control

Examples

  • Drain an intra-abdominal abscess.
  • Decompress an obstructed infected urinary tract.
  • Perform ERCP for obstructed cholangitis.
  • Remove an infected vascular catheter.
  • Debride necrotizing soft tissue.
  • Repair a bowel perforation.
  • Wash out a septic joint.
  • Remove infected cardiac hardware.
  • Drain empyema.

Source Control Changes Antibiotic Success

Without source control:

  • Bacterial burden remains high.
  • Antibiotic penetration may be poor.
  • Bacteremia may persist.
  • Resistance may emerge.
  • Organ dysfunction continues.
Antibiotics Cannot Reliably Sterilize Obstructed, Necrotic, or Undrained Anatomy

Timing of Source Control

Current Framework

When sepsis or septic shock has a specific source requiring intervention, source control should occur early—ideally within approximately six hours of diagnosis when feasible.

Immediate or Emergent Intervention

  • Necrotizing fasciitis
  • Perforated viscus
  • Mesenteric ischemia
  • Obstructed infected urinary system with shock
  • Ascending cholangitis with organ dysfunction
  • Abdominal compartment syndrome
  • Infected retained device causing ongoing shock

Balance Stabilization and Procedure

Resuscitation and source control should proceed in parallel. Do not require complete normalization of:

  • Blood pressure
  • Lactate
  • Coagulation
  • Renal function

before a life-saving procedure when the uncontrolled source is driving the instability.

Recognizing Failure of Source Control

Clinical Clues

  • Continued vasopressor requirement
  • Persistent bacteremia
  • Rising lactate
  • Persistent ileus
  • Increasing focal pain
  • Continued purulent or enteric drainage
  • Failure to improve after several days
  • Recurrent fever after initial improvement

Potential Problems

  • Undrained loculation
  • Drain obstruction or malposition
  • Ongoing anastomotic leak
  • Retained infected catheter or hardware
  • Necrotic tissue
  • Unrecognized empyema
  • Persistent biliary or urinary obstruction
Escalating from piperacillin-tazobactam to meropenem will not cure an undrained abscess or leaking anastomosis.

Principles of Empiric Antimicrobial Selection

The Regimen Must Match:

  • Likely source
  • Severity
  • Community vs healthcare acquisition
  • Previous organisms
  • Recent antimicrobial exposure
  • Local resistance patterns
  • Immune status
  • Drug allergy
  • Renal and hepatic function

Adequate Initial Therapy

The initial regimen should include at least one agent likely to be active against each major plausible pathogen.

Avoid Unnecessary Components

  • Routine MRSA coverage
  • Routine double Pseudomonas coverage
  • Routine anaerobic coverage
  • Routine antifungal therapy
  • Routine atypical coverage outside a compatible syndrome

Broad Enough Now, Narrow Enough Later

Empiric Breadth Is Temporary

Use the Patient’s Microbiologic History

Review:

  • Previous blood cultures
  • Previous urine cultures
  • Previous respiratory cultures
  • MRSA screening
  • Previous ESBL, CRE, or VRE
  • Antibiotics received during the last several months
  • Recent hospitalization or overseas healthcare

The Patient-Specific Antibiogram

The patient’s own previous resistant isolate may predict the current pathogen better than the hospital-wide antibiogram.

Recency and Site Matter

A recent ESBL urinary isolate is more relevant to current urosepsis than:

  • A remote superficial wound isolate
  • A single screening swab
  • An organism recovered many years earlier

When to Cover MRSA

Risk Factors

  • Previous MRSA infection or colonization
  • Recent IV antibiotics
  • Healthcare-associated pneumonia
  • Post-influenza necrotizing pneumonia
  • Infected vascular catheter
  • Prosthetic device infection
  • Severe purulent skin infection
  • High local MRSA prevalence

Potential Agents

  • Vancomycin
  • Linezolid for selected pneumonia
  • Daptomycin for bloodstream infection without pneumonia

Daptomycin Limitation

Daptomycin should not be used to treat pneumonia because pulmonary surfactant inactivates it.

Stop MRSA Coverage When:

  • Cultures identify a susceptible non-MRSA pathogen.
  • MRSA testing is negative and clinically reliable.
  • The source does not require MRSA coverage.
  • Clinical reassessment makes MRSA unlikely.

When to Cover Pseudomonas

Risk Factors

  • Previous Pseudomonas infection or colonization
  • Recent broad-spectrum antibiotics
  • Prolonged hospitalization
  • Structural lung disease
  • Neutropenia
  • Severe burns
  • Recurrent urinary instrumentation
  • Healthcare-associated infection

Potential Agents

  • Cefepime
  • Piperacillin-tazobactam
  • Meropenem
  • Imipenem-cilastatin
  • Ceftazidime

Double Coverage

Two antipseudomonal agents from different classes may be considered with:

  • Septic shock
  • High local resistance
  • Previous resistant Pseudomonas
  • Profound neutropenia
  • High risk that no single agent will be active

Definitive Treatment

Once susceptibility is available, use one active beta-lactam in most patients rather than continuing combination therapy.

ESBL and AmpC-Producing Enterobacterales

ESBL Risk Factors

  • Previous ESBL isolate
  • Recent third-generation cephalosporin or fluoroquinolone exposure
  • Recurrent healthcare exposure
  • Recurrent urinary or biliary infection
  • Residence in a region with high ESBL prevalence

Severe ESBL Infection Outside the Urinary Tract

Preferred therapy generally includes:

  • Meropenem
  • Imipenem-cilastatin
  • Ertapenem in selected stable patients without critical illness or hypoalbuminemia

Critically Ill Patient

Meropenem or imipenem-cilastatin is generally preferred over ertapenem because:

  • Critical illness changes drug distribution.
  • Hypoalbuminemia may increase ertapenem clearance.
  • Broader antipseudomonal activity may be temporarily needed.

AmpC-Risk Organisms

  • Enterobacter cloacae complex
  • Klebsiella aerogenes
  • Citrobacter freundii

Definitive Therapy

Cefepime is frequently appropriate when susceptibility and infection severity support it. Avoid ceftriaxone for serious invasive infection caused by organisms at meaningful inducible AmpC risk.

CRE and Difficult-to-Treat Pseudomonas

Risk Factors

  • Previous CRE or carbapenemase-producing organism
  • Overseas hospitalization
  • Prolonged ICU stay
  • Multiple recent broad-spectrum antibiotics
  • Long-term acute-care facility exposure
  • Recurrent device-associated infection

Carbapenemase Testing Matters

The preferred agent depends on the resistance mechanism:

  • KPC
  • NDM or another metallo-beta-lactamase
  • OXA-48-like enzyme

Potential Modern Agents

  • Meropenem-vaborbactam
  • Ceftazidime-avibactam
  • Imipenem-cilastatin-relebactam
  • Cefiderocol
  • Aztreonam combined with ceftazidime-avibactam for selected metallo-beta-lactamase producers
  • Ceftolozane-tazobactam for selected resistant Pseudomonas

Avoid Routine Polymyxins

Polymyxin-based regimens have substantial nephrotoxicity and are generally inferior to active newer beta-lactam agents when those agents are available.

Specialist Input

Involve infectious diseases and antimicrobial-stewardship pharmacy early when treating:

  • CRE
  • Difficult-to-treat Pseudomonas
  • Carbapenem-resistant Acinetobacter
  • Stenotrophomonas

Enterococcus and VRE Coverage

When Enterococcal Coverage May Be Appropriate

  • Healthcare-associated intra-abdominal infection
  • Postoperative abdominal infection
  • Transplantation
  • Previous Enterococcus infection
  • Biliary infection with instrumentation
  • Endocarditis concern
  • Persistent infection despite appropriate gram-negative and anaerobic therapy

Potential Agents

  • Ampicillin for susceptible E. faecalis
  • Piperacillin-tazobactam for selected E. faecalis coverage
  • Vancomycin
  • Linezolid for selected VRE infection
  • High-dose daptomycin for selected VRE bloodstream infection

VRE Risk Factors

  • Previous VRE colonization
  • Hematologic malignancy
  • Transplantation
  • Prolonged hospitalization
  • Extensive vancomycin or broad-spectrum exposure

Anaerobic Coverage

Use With:

  • Intra-abdominal infection
  • Deep pelvic or gynecologic infection
  • Necrotizing soft-tissue infection
  • Brain abscess
  • Head and neck deep-space infection
  • Empyema or lung abscess

Routine Anaerobic Coverage Is Not Needed For:

  • Most isolated urinary sepsis
  • Routine uncomplicated pneumonia
  • Central-line infection
  • Most primary bloodstream infections

Agents With Anaerobic Activity

  • Piperacillin-tazobactam
  • Meropenem
  • Imipenem-cilastatin
  • Ertapenem
  • Metronidazole combined with an appropriate gram-negative agent
Do not routinely add metronidazole to piperacillin-tazobactam or a carbapenem. This is usually duplicate anaerobic therapy.

Empiric Antifungal Therapy

Not Routine

Empiric antifungal therapy is not recommended for every patient with septic shock.

Consider With Strong Candida Risk

  • Recent abdominal surgery
  • Anastomotic leak
  • Recurrent gastrointestinal perforation
  • Necrotizing pancreatitis
  • Parenteral nutrition
  • Prolonged broad-spectrum antibiotics
  • Central venous catheter
  • Neutropenia
  • Multiple-site Candida colonization

Preferred Initial Therapy

  • An echinocandin in critically ill patients

Fluconazole May Be Appropriate When:

  • The patient is stable.
  • No recent azole exposure exists.
  • Azole-resistant species are unlikely.
  • A susceptible organism is identified.

Biomarkers

Beta-D-glucan and other Candida biomarkers should not independently determine whether empiric therapy is started or stopped.

Source Control

Antifungal Therapy + Catheter Removal or Abdominal Source Control

Sepsis From a Pulmonary Source

Severe Community-Acquired Pneumonia

A common regimen is:

  • Ceftriaxone plus azithromycin

Alternatives depend on:

  • Drug allergy
  • Local resistance
  • Aspiration complications
  • Risk of Legionella
  • Immune status

Add MRSA Coverage With:

  • Previous MRSA respiratory isolate
  • Recent influenza with necrotizing pneumonia
  • Cavitation
  • Recent hospitalization and IV antibiotics
  • Severe compatible healthcare-associated syndrome

Add Pseudomonas Coverage With:

  • Previous respiratory Pseudomonas
  • Structural lung disease
  • Recent hospitalization with IV antibiotics
  • Other validated local risk

Hospital-Acquired or Ventilator-Associated Pneumonia

  • Cefepime
  • Piperacillin-tazobactam
  • Meropenem
  • Plus vancomycin or linezolid when MRSA risk exists

Duration

Approximately 7 Days for Most Improving HAP/VAP

Antimicrobial Dosing in Sepsis

Why Standard Dosing May Fail

Critical illness changes:

  • Volume of distribution
  • Protein binding
  • Renal clearance
  • Hepatic metabolism
  • Capillary permeability

Consequences

  • Early underexposure
  • Subtherapeutic tissue concentration
  • Toxic accumulation after renal function worsens
  • Treatment failure

Key Principle

Give an Adequate Initial Dose, Then Adjust Maintenance Dosing

Loading Doses

Why a Loading Dose Is Important

The initial dose is determined largely by volume of distribution rather than current renal clearance.

Practical Implication

Do not reduce the first dose of many life-saving antimicrobials solely because the patient has acute kidney injury. Give an adequate loading dose, then adjust subsequent doses.

Examples Requiring Thoughtful Loading

  • Vancomycin
  • Beta-lactams before prolonged infusion
  • Echinocandins
  • Selected antivirals

Exceptions

Individualize for:

  • Extreme body size
  • Severe toxicity risk
  • Drug-specific contraindications
  • Prior recent dosing

Prolonged Beta-Lactam Infusion

Pharmacodynamic Principle

Beta-lactam effectiveness relates to the time free-drug concentration remains above the organism’s MIC.

Current Recommendation

Initial Loading Dose → Prolonged or Extended Maintenance Infusion

Common Examples

  • Cefepime infused over approximately three to four hours
  • Piperacillin-tazobactam infused over approximately four hours
  • Meropenem infused over approximately three hours

Potential Benefits

  • Improved pharmacodynamic target attainment
  • Improved treatment of higher-MIC organisms
  • Particular value in critical illness

Operational Requirements

  • Compatible IV access
  • Pharmacy preparation
  • Renal-dose adjustment
  • Adequate initial loading dose

Common Clinical Scenarios

Case 1: Possible Sepsis Without Shock

A stable patient has fever, tachycardia, and mild leukocytosis but no organ dysfunction or source.

Perform a rapid focused evaluation. Do not automatically label the patient septic or administer vancomycin and meropenem solely because SIRS criteria are present.

Case 2: Septic Shock

A patient has hypotension, confusion, mottling, and lactate 5 mmol/L.

Obtain cultures immediately, start appropriate antibiotics ideally within one hour, begin fluid and vasopressor resuscitation, and identify a source requiring urgent control.

Case 3: One Underfilled Blood-Culture Set

Only one blood-culture set containing a small volume was collected before antibiotics.

This is an inadequate bloodstream-infection evaluation. Obtain additional properly filled peripheral sets as soon as possible.

Case 4: Previous ESBL E. coli

A patient with septic shock from suspected pyelonephritis had ESBL E. coli in urine two months earlier.

Use the patient-specific microbiology. A carbapenem is appropriate initial therapy while cultures and source-control evaluation proceed.

Case 5: Vancomycin and Meropenem With Negative Cultures

A patient improves rapidly, cultures remain negative, imaging shows no infection, and pulmonary embolism is confirmed.

Stop empiric antimicrobial therapy. Clinical improvement during antibiotics does not prove infection.

Case 6: Cholangitis With Shock

A patient has jaundice, hypotension, fever, and an obstructed common bile duct.

Start antibiotics, but arrange urgent biliary decompression. Antibiotics alone do not treat the obstruction.

Case 7: Persistent Gram-Negative Bacteremia

E. coli blood cultures remain positive despite susceptibility to ceftriaxone.

Search for an obstructed urinary tract, undrained abdominal collection, infected stone, endovascular focus, or retained device rather than merely changing ceftriaxone.

Case 8: MSSA Bacteremia on Vancomycin

Blood cultures identify methicillin-susceptible S. aureus.

Change to cefazolin, nafcillin, or oxacillin when possible. Repeat cultures and evaluate for endocarditis and metastatic infection.

Case 9: Negative MRSA Nasal PCR

A patient with suspected pneumonia is improving, respiratory cultures show no MRSA, and the nasal PCR is negative.

Stop vancomycin when local test performance and the clinical syndrome support de-escalation.

Case 10: High Lactate After Epinephrine

Blood pressure and capillary refill improve, but lactate rises after epinephrine is started.

Reassess perfusion, but recognize that epinephrine may increase lactate through adrenergic metabolism. Do not give endless fluid solely to normalize the number.

Case 11: Persistent Shock After Source Control

The abscess is drained and antibiotics are active, but vasopressor requirements increase.

Evaluate for mixed shock, hemorrhage, pulmonary embolism, myocardial dysfunction, adrenal insufficiency, drain failure, or another missed source.

Case 12: Candidemia With TPN Catheter

Candida grows from blood in a patient receiving parenteral nutrition through a central line.

Start an echinocandin, remove the catheter when feasible, repeat blood cultures until negative, and search for metastatic or deep infection.

Case 13: No Anaerobic Source

A patient with urosepsis is receiving cefepime plus metronidazole.

Metronidazole is unnecessary unless another anaerobic source is suspected.

Case 14: Suspected Necrotizing Infection

A patient has rapidly progressive leg pain, bullae, hypotension, and crepitus.

Administer broad therapy and obtain immediate surgical exploration. Do not wait for MRI or improvement after antibiotics.

Case 15: Seven Days of Therapy Completed

A stable patient has source-controlled E. coli urinary bacteremia, negative follow-up examination, and rapid clinical improvement.

A seven-day total course is commonly sufficient. Sepsis at presentation does not automatically require 14 days.

Common Pitfalls

  • Diagnosing sepsis from fever and leukocytosis alone
  • Using qSOFA as a rule-out test
  • Failing to recognize sepsis without fever
  • Delaying antibiotics in septic shock for extensive testing
  • Administering immediate broad antibiotics to every stable possible-sepsis alert
  • Obtaining only one underfilled blood-culture set
  • Drawing all cultures through one central catheter
  • Failing to label blood-culture sites
  • Dismissing S. aureus or Candida as contamination
  • Treating a single skin-commensal culture without assessing contamination
  • Obtaining urine from the catheter drainage bag
  • Treating pyuria or bacteriuria as the sepsis source without compatible findings
  • Treating chronic airway or drain colonization
  • Ignoring the patient’s previous resistant isolates
  • Using vancomycin and meropenem for every septic patient
  • Continuing double antipseudomonal therapy after susceptibility results
  • Giving routine metronidazole without an anaerobic source
  • Giving empiric antifungal therapy without meaningful risk factors
  • Reducing the initial loading dose solely because of acute kidney injury
  • Failing to use prolonged beta-lactam infusion after loading
  • Failing to adjust maintenance dosing as renal function changes
  • Continuing vancomycin for MSSA bacteremia
  • Using daptomycin for pneumonia
  • Continuing broad therapy because the patient improved
  • Treating the word “sepsis” rather than the infection syndrome
  • Extending therapy until WBC and CRP normalize
  • Failing to document a stop date
  • Broadening antibiotics without reassessing source control
  • Failing to repeat cultures in S. aureus bacteremia or candidemia
  • Giving repeated fluid boluses solely to normalize lactate
  • Failing to consider mixed or noninfectious shock
  • Using procalcitonin alone to start or stop therapy
  • Failing to remove an unnecessary infected device

Board and ID Consult Pearls

  • Sepsis is life-threatening organ dysfunction caused by a dysregulated response to infection.
  • An acute SOFA increase of at least two supports organ dysfunction.
  • Septic shock requires vasopressors to maintain MAP at least 65 mm Hg and lactate above 2 mmol/L despite adequate volume resuscitation.
  • “Severe sepsis” is no longer a preferred Sepsis-3 category.
  • qSOFA should not be used as the only sepsis screening or rule-out tool.
  • Sepsis is a clinical diagnosis and cannot be confirmed by one biomarker.
  • Probable sepsis or septic shock requires immediate antimicrobials, ideally within one hour.
  • Possible sepsis without shock permits a rapid diagnostic assessment, with therapy within about three hours if concern persists.
  • Low infection probability without shock may justify close observation without immediate antibiotics.
  • Obtain blood cultures before antibiotics when this does not create harmful delay.
  • Collect at least two properly filled blood-culture sets.
  • Blood volume is one of the most important determinants of culture sensitivity.
  • Obtain simultaneous peripheral and catheter cultures when line infection is possible.
  • S. aureus and Candida in blood require full evaluation and should not be dismissed.
  • Repeat blood cultures until clearance for S. aureus bacteremia and candidemia.
  • Routine repeat cultures are unnecessary for many improving uncomplicated gram-negative bacteremias.
  • Previous patient cultures are critical for empiric antibiotic selection.
  • Cover an MDR pathogen empirically only when the patient has meaningful risk.
  • Do not provide routine anaerobic coverage without an anaerobic source.
  • Do not provide routine empiric antifungal therapy to every patient with septic shock.
  • Source control should occur early, ideally within approximately six hours when required.
  • Obstructed pyelonephritis requires urinary decompression.
  • Cholangitis requires biliary decompression.
  • Necrotizing infection requires immediate surgical exploration.
  • Give adequate antimicrobial loading doses during initial sepsis treatment.
  • Use prolonged-infusion beta-lactam maintenance therapy after a loading dose.
  • Norepinephrine is the first-line vasopressor.
  • The usual initial MAP target is approximately 65 mm Hg.
  • Use dynamic measures to guide additional fluid.
  • Lactate elevation is not specific for hypovolemia or anaerobic metabolism.
  • Add vasopressin when norepinephrine requirements escalate.
  • Consider IV hydrocortisone for ongoing vasopressor-dependent septic shock.
  • De-escalate when susceptibility results are available.
  • De-escalation is appropriate even when cultures remain negative.
  • Sepsis does not independently determine antibiotic duration.
  • Many source-controlled gram-negative bacteremias require approximately seven days.
  • Many source-controlled intra-abdominal infections require approximately four days.
  • Most improving HAP and VAP require approximately seven days.
  • MSSA bacteremia should receive an antistaphylococcal beta-lactam rather than vancomycin when possible.
  • Uncomplicated S. aureus bacteremia requires at least 14 days after clearance.
  • Candidemia requires at least 14 days after clearance and symptom resolution.
  • Persistent bacteremia requires renewed source investigation.
  • Persistent shock requires evaluation for inadequate source control, resistant infection, incorrect dosing, or mixed shock.
  • Procalcitonin should not override clinical judgment.

Bottom Line

Sepsis is infection accompanied by life-threatening organ dysfunction. Septic shock is the severe subset characterized by vasopressor-dependent hypotension and elevated lactate despite adequate volume resuscitation. Recognition requires clinical evaluation of infection probability and acute organ dysfunction rather than dependence on one score, biomarker, or culture result.

Probable sepsis and septic shock require immediate antimicrobial treatment, ideally within one hour. Possible sepsis without shock allows a rapid, time-limited diagnostic assessment, while stable patients with a low probability of infection may be monitored without immediate antibiotics. Obtain at least two adequately filled blood-culture sets before treatment whenever this does not create harmful delay.

Empiric antibiotics should be selected according to the anatomic source, severity, the patient’s previous microbiology, recent antibiotic exposure, local resistance, immune status, and device history. MRSA, Pseudomonas, ESBL, CRE, Enterococcus, anaerobic, and fungal coverage should be added selectively—not automatically.

Source control is a central component of sepsis care and should occur early, ideally within approximately six hours when a controllable source exists. Antibiotics cannot reliably cure an obstructed biliary or urinary system, perforated viscus, undrained abscess, necrotic tissue, infected prosthetic device, or infected vascular catheter.

Give adequate loading doses, use prolonged-infusion beta-lactams for maintenance, and reassess dosing as renal function and critical illness evolve. At 48–72 hours, review cultures, rapid diagnostics, source control, drug exposure, route, and duration. Narrow or stop treatment whenever possible, including in culture-negative sepsis.

Persistent shock or bacteremia requires investigation for inadequate source control, resistant pathogens, incorrect antimicrobial dosing, infected thrombus, endocarditis, metastatic infection, retained hardware, or a noninfectious or mixed shock state. The goal is not the broadest or longest therapy—it is prompt effective therapy, definitive source control, and the shortest reliable pathogen-directed course.

Diarrhea: Practical Approach to Acute and Chronic Diarrhea

Added June 20, 2026 • Gastroenterology / Hospital Medicine • ~14 min read

A practical framework for evaluating diarrhea by first separating acute from chronic diarrhea, then deciding who needs stool testing, when to treat empirically, when to avoid antimotility agents, and how to organize chronic diarrhea into functional, osmotic, inflammatory, secretory, and malabsorptive causes.

Overview

Diarrhea is common in both outpatient and inpatient medicine. The first step is determining whether the diarrhea is acute or chronic, because the diagnostic framework changes completely.

Core approach: For acute diarrhea, decide who needs testing. For chronic diarrhea, characterize the stool pattern and classify the diarrhea subtype.

Acute vs Chronic Diarrhea

Category Duration Approach
Acute diarrhea <2 weeks Usually infectious or self-limited
Persistent gray zone 2–4 weeks Can behave like acute or chronic diarrhea
Chronic diarrhea >4 weeks Classify by stool type and mechanism

Acute Diarrhea: Who Does Not Need Testing?

Most patients with acute diarrhea do not need stool testing. The majority have self-limited viral illness and improve with supportive care.

Common Viral Causes

  • Norovirus
  • Rotavirus
  • COVID-associated diarrhea
  • Other viral gastroenteritides
Practical point: In uncomplicated acute diarrhea without red flags, stool cultures are low yield and symptomatic treatment is usually enough.

Food Poisoning Timing

Timing after food exposure can help identify likely causes.

Time After Eating Likely Cause Classic Examples
<6 hours Preformed toxin Staphylococcus aureus, Bacillus cereus
6–18 hours Toxin-mediated illness Clostridium perfringens
>18 hours Often infectious gastroenteritis Viral or bacterial causes
Reheated rice with rapid vomiting or diarrhea is a classic Bacillus cereus association.

Acute Diarrhea: Who Needs Testing?

Testing is indicated when the patient has risk factors for severe disease, inflammatory diarrhea, public health concerns, or C. difficile risk.

Indications for Testing

  • Recent hospitalization
  • Antibiotic exposure within the last 3 months
  • Severe illness
  • Severe hypovolemia
  • More than 6 bowel movements per day
  • Severe abdominal pain
  • Temperature >101.3°F
  • Bloody or mucoid stool
  • Age >70
  • Significant comorbidities such as heart failure or pulmonary hypertension
  • Known inflammatory bowel disease
  • Pregnancy
  • Immunocompromised state
  • Symptoms lasting more than 1 week
  • Public health concern or known outbreak
Red flag: Bloody diarrhea, high fever, severe dehydration, or severe abdominal pain should not be treated as routine viral gastroenteritis.

Acute Diarrhea Workup

Test When to Order
C. difficile testing Recent antibiotics or hospitalization
Stool culture / stool PCR Severe illness, inflammatory diarrhea, high-risk host
Ova and parasites Symptoms >1 week, known outbreak, travel, HIV with CD4 <200
Basic labs Dehydration, AKI concern, severe illness
Ova and parasites: Stool O&P is usually low yield unless symptoms are prolonged, there is a known outbreak, or the patient is significantly immunocompromised.

Acute Diarrhea Treatment

Hydration

Oral hydration is preferred whenever the patient can tolerate it. IV fluids are reserved for patients with severe dehydration, inability to tolerate oral intake, or hemodynamic concerns.

Loperamide

Medication Dose
Loperamide 4 mg once, then 2 mg after each loose stool
Maximum dose 16 mg/day
Avoid loperamide when there is bloody diarrhea, high fever, suspected C. difficile, or suspected inflammatory diarrhea.

Empiric Antibiotics

Empiric antibiotics can be considered when the patient has severe illness, high-risk host features, or inflammatory diarrhea.

Medication When Useful
Azithromycin Preferred in many travel-associated infections and areas with fluoroquinolone resistance
Fluoroquinolone Alternative option depending on local resistance and travel history
Avoid empiric antibiotics if there is a known outbreak or strong suspicion of Shiga toxin-producing E. coli or EHEC because of hemolytic uremic syndrome risk.

Chronic Diarrhea Overview

Chronic diarrhea is best organized by mechanism rather than by listing every possible cause.

Subtype Typical Clues
Functional IBS pattern, abdominal pain, no alarm features
Osmotic Improves with fasting, high stool osmotic gap
Inflammatory Blood, mucus, fever, pain, systemic symptoms
Secretory Nocturnal diarrhea, persists with fasting, low stool osmotic gap
Malabsorptive Greasy, fatty, floating stools; weight loss or nutrient deficiency

Functional Diarrhea / IBS

Functional diarrhea and irritable bowel syndrome are among the most common causes of chronic diarrhea.

Rome Criteria for IBS

IBS is suggested by recurrent abdominal pain at least once weekly plus at least two of the following:

  • Pain related to defecation
  • Change in stool frequency
  • Change in stool consistency

Treatment Options for IBS-D

  • Low FODMAP diet
  • Antispasmodics such as hyoscyamine or dicyclomine
  • Bile acid sequestrants in selected patients
  • Rifaximin in selected patients
  • Tricyclic antidepressants
IBS-D pearl: TCAs often have the best pharmacologic evidence among commonly used options.

Osmotic Diarrhea

Osmotic diarrhea occurs when poorly absorbed solutes remain in the intestinal lumen and pull water into the gut.

Common Causes

  • Lactose intolerance
  • Excess sugar alcohols
  • Artificial sweeteners
  • Dietary triggers
  • Laxatives

Diagnostic Clues

Stool Osmotic Gap >125 = Osmotic Diarrhea
  • Improves with fasting
  • Usually no nocturnal diarrhea
  • Often linked to specific foods or medications

Workup and Treatment

  • Diet journal
  • Avoid suspected triggers
  • Lactose hydrogen breath test if diagnosis is unclear
  • Symptomatic treatment

Inflammatory Diarrhea

Inflammatory diarrhea is caused by mucosal inflammation or invasion.

Common Causes

  • Crohn disease
  • Ulcerative colitis
  • Radiation colitis
  • Immune checkpoint inhibitor colitis
  • Chronic infections such as CMV, TB, MAC, and parasites

Clinical Clues

  • Bloody stool
  • Mucoid stool
  • Abdominal pain
  • Fever
  • Weight loss
  • Arthralgias
  • Skin or eye manifestations
Inflammatory diarrhea should prompt evaluation for IBD, infection, ischemia, medication toxicity, and malignancy depending on context.

Secretory Diarrhea

Secretory diarrhea occurs when the bowel actively secretes fluid and electrolytes.

Common Causes

  • Microscopic colitis
  • Medications
  • Metformin
  • NSAIDs
  • Hyperthyroidism
  • Neuroendocrine tumors

Microscopic Colitis Associations

  • NSAIDs
  • PPIs
  • SSRIs

Diagnostic Clues

Stool Osmotic Gap <50 = Secretory Diarrhea
  • Persists with fasting
  • Nocturnal diarrhea is common
  • Can cause high-volume watery diarrhea

Malabsorptive Diarrhea

Malabsorptive diarrhea occurs when nutrients are not absorbed properly.

Common Causes

  • Celiac disease
  • Tropical sprue
  • Pancreatic insufficiency
  • Chronic pancreatitis
  • Cystic fibrosis
  • Giardiasis
  • Small intestinal bacterial overgrowth
  • Protein-losing enteropathy
  • Bile acid malabsorption
  • Medication-induced enteropathy

Classic Stool Description

  • Greasy stools
  • Fatty stools
  • Floating stools
  • Foul-smelling stools
  • Weight loss or nutrient deficiencies
Olmesartan can cause a sprue-like enteropathy that mimics celiac disease.

Bile Acid Malabsorption

Consider bile acid malabsorption after:

  • Cholecystectomy
  • Ileal resection
  • Ileal disease such as Crohn disease

Chronic Diarrhea Workup

First-Pass Workup

Test Purpose
Stool sodium and potassium Calculate stool osmotic gap
Celiac panel Evaluate for celiac disease
TSH Evaluate for hyperthyroidism
Fecal calprotectin or lactoferrin Evaluate for intestinal inflammation
CRP Supportive marker for inflammation
Fecal elastase Evaluate pancreatic insufficiency
Stool culture / O&P If infection risk or exposure history

When to Image or Scope

  • Significant abdominal pain → CT abdomen/pelvis
  • Onset after age 50 → GI referral and colonoscopy consideration
  • Severe progressive symptoms → Endoscopy consideration
  • Concern for IBD → Endoscopy consideration
  • Alarm features → Endoscopy consideration

Second-Pass Workup

If initial evaluation is unrevealing, assess for malabsorption and nutrient deficiencies.

  • 25-hydroxy vitamin D
  • Folate
  • Vitamin B12
  • Iron studies
  • Calcium
  • Magnesium
  • Albumin
  • Fat-soluble nutrient deficiencies

Third-Pass Workup: Rare Causes

If symptoms remain unexplained, consider rare secretory causes such as neuroendocrine tumors.

  • VIP level
  • Somatostatin level
  • 5-HIAA
  • Calcitonin
  • Gastrin level

Clinical Pearls

  1. Acute diarrhea is less than 2 weeks; chronic diarrhea is more than 4 weeks.
  2. Most acute diarrhea is viral and does not need testing.
  3. Test acute diarrhea when there is severe illness, inflammatory features, high-risk host status, or recent antibiotics/hospitalization.
  4. Check C. difficile testing if antibiotics or hospitalization occurred in the last 3 months.
  5. Stool O&P is low yield unless symptoms are prolonged, outbreak is suspected, or the patient is immunocompromised.
  6. Loperamide should be avoided in bloody diarrhea, high fever, suspected C. difficile, or suspected inflammatory diarrhea.
  7. Azithromycin is often preferred for traveler’s diarrhea, especially where fluoroquinolone resistance is common.
  8. Functional diarrhea and IBS are common causes of chronic diarrhea.
  9. Osmotic diarrhea improves with fasting and has a high stool osmotic gap.
  10. Secretory diarrhea persists with fasting and often occurs at night.
  11. Inflammatory diarrhea causes blood, mucus, abdominal pain, fever, or systemic symptoms.
  12. Malabsorptive diarrhea often causes greasy or fatty stools.
  13. Microscopic colitis is associated with NSAIDs, PPIs, and SSRIs.
  14. Olmesartan can cause sprue-like enteropathy.
  15. Fecal elastase helps evaluate pancreatic insufficiency.
  16. Fecal calprotectin or lactoferrin helps evaluate inflammatory bowel disease.

Bottom Line

For acute diarrhea, the key decision is whether the patient needs testing. Most patients have self-limited viral illness and only require hydration and symptom control. Testing is reserved for severe illness, inflammatory diarrhea, high-risk hosts, recent antibiotics or hospitalization, prolonged symptoms, or outbreak concerns. For chronic diarrhea, classify the stool pattern into functional, osmotic, inflammatory, secretory, or malabsorptive diarrhea, then direct the workup using stool osmotic gap, inflammatory markers, celiac testing, TSH, fecal elastase, and endoscopy when alarm features are present.

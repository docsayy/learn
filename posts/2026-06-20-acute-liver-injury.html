Acute Liver Injury: Practical Approach to Undifferentiated Liver Test Abnormalities

Added June 19, 2026 • Gastroenterology / Hepatology • ~14 min read

A practical framework for approaching undifferentiated liver injury, including recognition of acute liver failure, hepatic encephalopathy staging, hepatocellular versus cholestatic patterns, R factor, major diagnostic categories, and high-yield board review pearls.

Overview

Undifferentiated liver injury has a broad differential, so the most important first step is to use a structured framework. Rather than memorizing a long list of causes, start by determining whether the patient has acute liver failure and then classify the pattern of liver injury.

Core approach: First ask whether this is acute liver failure. Then determine whether the pattern is hepatocellular, cholestatic, or mixed.

Acute Liver Failure

Acute liver failure is a medical emergency and should be recognized early.

Acute Liver Failure = Altered Mental Status + INR > 1.5

This definition matters because acute liver failure should prompt urgent specialty involvement and consideration of transfer to a liver transplant center.

Do not miss this: Altered mental status plus INR greater than 1.5 in a patient with acute liver injury should raise concern for acute liver failure.

Hepatic Encephalopathy Stages

Hepatic encephalopathy ranges from subtle sleep-wake disturbance to coma.

Stage Clinical Features
Stage 1 Sleep-wake disturbance, mild attention changes, subtle behavioral changes
Stage 2 Lethargy, disorientation, personality change, asterixis
Stage 3 Marked confusion, somnolence, severe asterixis
Stage 4 Coma
Early clue: Sleep-wake reversal can be one of the earliest signs of hepatic encephalopathy.

Liver Injury Patterns

Classifying the pattern of liver injury narrows the differential diagnosis dramatically.

Pattern Main Lab Abnormality Typical Differential
Hepatocellular Predominant AST/ALT elevation DILI, viral hepatitis, autoimmune hepatitis, ischemic hepatitis, alcohol-associated hepatitis
Cholestatic / Obstructive Predominant alkaline phosphatase and bilirubin elevation Gallstones, biliary obstruction, PSC, PBC, malignancy, infiltrative disease
Mixed Both AST/ALT and alkaline phosphatase elevated Drug injury, biliary disease, systemic illness, overlap syndromes

R Factor

When the pattern is unclear, calculate the R factor.

R Factor = (ALT ÷ ALT Upper Limit of Normal) ÷ (Alk Phos ÷ Alk Phos Upper Limit of Normal)
R Factor Pattern
>5 Hepatocellular
2–5 Mixed
<2 Cholestatic
Practical point: The R factor is especially useful when AST/ALT, alkaline phosphatase, and bilirubin are all abnormal.

Hepatocellular Injury

A hepatocellular pattern is dominated by AST and ALT elevation.

Major Causes

  • Drug-induced liver injury
  • Viral hepatitis
  • Autoimmune hepatitis
  • Ischemic hepatitis or shock liver
  • Alcohol-associated hepatitis
  • Wilson disease
  • Hereditary hemochromatosis
  • NASH / metabolic dysfunction-associated steatohepatitis
  • Malignancy

Drug-Induced Liver Injury

Drug-induced liver injury is one of the most common causes of acute liver injury. Always review medications, supplements, herbals, acetaminophen exposure, antibiotics, antiepileptics, and recent medication changes.

High-Yield Medication Review

  • Acetaminophen
  • Antibiotics
  • Antiepileptics
  • Statins
  • Herbal supplements
  • Recent new medications
Practical resource: LiverTox is a useful reference when assessing whether a medication can cause liver injury.

Viral Hepatitis

Viral causes should be considered in acute hepatocellular injury.

Common Tests

Virus Testing
Hepatitis A HAV IgM
Hepatitis B HBsAg, anti-HBc IgM, anti-HBs
Hepatitis C HCV antibody with reflex viral load
EBV EBV serologies or viral testing when clinically indicated
CMV CMV testing, especially in immunocompromised patients

Autoimmune Hepatitis

Autoimmune hepatitis should be considered in younger patients, especially women, and in patients with personal or family history of autoimmune disease.

Test Association
Anti-smooth muscle antibody Autoimmune hepatitis
ANA Can be positive in autoimmune hepatitis
IgG Often elevated
Board pearl: Anti-smooth muscle antibody is the classic antibody associated with autoimmune hepatitis.

Ischemic Hepatitis and Budd-Chiari

Ischemic hepatitis, also called shock liver, occurs from hepatic hypoperfusion.

Common Settings

  • Severe hypotension
  • Cardiogenic shock
  • Septic shock
  • Hypoxemic respiratory failure
  • Cardiac arrest

Budd-Chiari syndrome is hepatic venous outflow obstruction and can also cause acute liver injury.

Clinical clue: Very high AST/ALT after hypotension or shock strongly suggests ischemic hepatitis.

Alcohol-Associated Hepatitis

Alcohol-associated hepatitis usually has a characteristic AST to ALT pattern.

AST : ALT Ratio > 2 : 1

AST and ALT are usually elevated but typically not into the thousands.

Important: AST or ALT greater than 500 should prompt consideration of another or additional cause beyond alcohol-associated hepatitis.

Maddrey Discriminant Function

The Maddrey discriminant function helps assess severity and whether steroids may be considered.

Maddrey Discriminant Function > 32 = severe alcohol-associated hepatitis

Steroids may be considered in selected patients, although evidence and patient selection remain nuanced.

Hereditary Causes

Wilson Disease

Wilson disease should be considered in younger patients with liver injury, neurologic symptoms, psychiatric symptoms, or Kayser-Fleischer rings.

Test Expected Finding
Serum ceruloplasmin Low
24-hour urine copper Elevated
Eye exam Kayser-Fleischer rings

Hereditary Hemochromatosis

Hemochromatosis is a classic board diagnosis.

Classic Features

  • Diabetes mellitus
  • Bronze or tan skin
  • Arthritis
  • Hypogonadism
  • Liver disease
Memory pattern: Diabetes plus bronze skin plus liver disease should trigger consideration of hemochromatosis.

Malignancy

Malignancy can cause either hepatocellular or cholestatic liver injury depending on the mechanism.

Hepatocellular Carcinoma

  • Consider in cirrhosis or chronic hepatitis B
  • Alpha-fetoprotein may be elevated
  • Diagnosis can often be made with characteristic multiphasic CT or MRI findings
Unique feature: Hepatocellular carcinoma is one of the few cancers that can often be diagnosed radiographically without tissue biopsy when imaging is classic.

Biliary or Pancreatic Malignancy

Pancreatic cancer, cholangiocarcinoma, and other obstructive malignancies can produce a cholestatic pattern with elevated alkaline phosphatase and bilirubin.

AST and ALT in the Thousands

Only certain conditions commonly cause AST and ALT elevations into the thousands.

Cause Notes
Drug-induced liver injury Especially acetaminophen toxicity
Viral hepatitis Acute viral hepatitis can cause marked elevations
Ischemic hepatitis Often very high after hypotension or shock
Wilson disease Can rarely cause severe acute liver injury
High-yield rule: AST or ALT in the thousands is usually not alcohol-associated hepatitis alone.

Cholestatic Injury

A cholestatic pattern is dominated by alkaline phosphatase and bilirubin elevation.

Major Causes

  • Gallstone disease
  • Biliary obstruction
  • Primary sclerosing cholangitis
  • Primary biliary cholangitis
  • Drug-induced liver injury
  • Pancreatic malignancy
  • Cholangiocarcinoma
  • Infiltrative disease

Primary Sclerosing Cholangitis

Primary sclerosing cholangitis is classically associated with ulcerative colitis.

Clue Diagnosis
Middle-aged man with ulcerative colitis and cholestatic labs Primary sclerosing cholangitis
p-ANCA positivity Can be associated

Primary Biliary Cholangitis

Primary biliary cholangitis often presents with cholestatic labs, pruritus, and fatigue.

Clue Diagnosis
Middle-aged woman with itching and jaundice Primary biliary cholangitis
Anti-mitochondrial antibody Classic antibody association
Do not confuse: Anti-smooth muscle antibody is associated with autoimmune hepatitis. Anti-mitochondrial antibody is associated with primary biliary cholangitis.

Terminology

The term “transaminitis” is commonly used but is not ideal. The transaminase enzymes themselves are not inflamed. More precise terms are preferred.

Avoid Prefer
Transaminitis Acute liver injury
Transaminitis Hepatocellular liver injury
Transaminitis Hepatitis when inflammation is suspected
Language pearl: Use “acute liver injury” or “hepatocellular injury” instead of “transaminitis.”

Clinical Pearls

  1. Acute liver failure is altered mental status plus INR greater than 1.5.
  2. Acute liver failure should prompt urgent transplant center consideration.
  3. Stage 1 hepatic encephalopathy can present as sleep-wake disturbance.
  4. Asterixis is typical of stage 2–3 hepatic encephalopathy.
  5. Classify liver injury as hepatocellular, cholestatic, or mixed.
  6. Use the R factor when the pattern is unclear.
  7. Hepatocellular injury is dominated by AST and ALT elevation.
  8. Cholestatic injury is dominated by alkaline phosphatase and bilirubin elevation.
  9. Drug-induced liver injury is one of the most common causes of acute liver injury.
  10. Anti-smooth muscle antibody is associated with autoimmune hepatitis.
  11. Anti-mitochondrial antibody is associated with primary biliary cholangitis.
  12. Primary sclerosing cholangitis is associated with ulcerative colitis.
  13. Alcohol-associated hepatitis usually has AST:ALT greater than 2:1.
  14. AST or ALT greater than 500 should make alcohol-associated hepatitis alone less likely.
  15. AST or ALT in the thousands suggests drug-induced, viral, ischemic, or rarely Wilson disease.
  16. Wilson disease is screened with ceruloplasmin and 24-hour urine copper.
  17. Hemochromatosis can present with diabetes, bronze skin, arthritis, hypogonadism, and liver disease.
  18. HCC can often be diagnosed with characteristic multiphasic CT or MRI findings.
  19. Avoid the term “transaminitis” when possible.

Bottom Line

For undifferentiated liver injury, first determine whether the patient has acute liver failure, defined by altered mental status plus INR greater than 1.5. Then classify the injury pattern as hepatocellular, cholestatic, or mixed using AST/ALT, alkaline phosphatase, bilirubin, and the R factor when needed. Hepatocellular injury suggests causes such as drug-induced liver injury, viral hepatitis, autoimmune hepatitis, ischemic hepatitis, alcohol-associated hepatitis, Wilson disease, hemochromatosis, NASH, or malignancy. Cholestatic injury suggests obstruction, PSC, PBC, malignancy, infiltrative disease, or drug injury. This pattern-based approach makes a very broad differential much easier to manage.

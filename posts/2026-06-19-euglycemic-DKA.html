SGLT2 Inhibitors and Euglycemic DKA: A Practical Guide for Internal Medicine Residents

Added June 19, 2026 • Endocrinology • ~10 min read

A practical explanation of how SGLT2 inhibitors can cause euglycemic diabetic ketoacidosis, why glucose may remain normal, how insulin-glucagon physiology drives ketogenesis, and how to recognize and manage this diagnosis on the wards.

Table of Contents

Overview

SGLT2 inhibitors are increasingly used for type 2 diabetes, heart failure, and chronic kidney disease. Common examples include empagliflozin, dapagliflozin, canagliflozin, and ertugliflozin.

A key safety issue is that these medications can precipitate euglycemic diabetic ketoacidosis, meaning the patient can be in DKA even though the blood glucose is normal or only mildly elevated.

Do not rule out DKA just because the glucose is not very high in a patient taking an SGLT2 inhibitor.

What Is Euglycemic DKA?

Classic DKA usually presents with hyperglycemia, ketosis, and anion gap metabolic acidosis. Euglycemic DKA has the same ketoacidosis physiology, but the glucose is not markedly elevated.

Condition Glucose Ketones Acid-Base Pattern
Classic DKA Usually >250 mg/dL Positive Anion gap metabolic acidosis
Euglycemic DKA Normal or mildly elevated, often <250 mg/dL Positive Anion gap metabolic acidosis
Key point: Euglycemic DKA is still DKA. The defining problem is ketoacidosis, not the glucose number.

Classic DKA Physiology

Step 1: Insulin Deficiency

In classic DKA, there is absolute or relative insulin deficiency. This is common in type 1 diabetes but can also occur in type 2 diabetes during major physiologic stress.

  • Infection
  • Missed insulin
  • Surgery
  • Physiologic stress
  • Reduced oral intake

Step 2: Cells Cannot Use Glucose

Without adequate insulin, glucose remains in the bloodstream and cannot effectively enter cells.

The body sees plenty of glucose in the blood, but the cells behave as if they are starving.

Step 3: Counter-Regulatory Hormones Rise

Because cells cannot access glucose, the body releases counter-regulatory hormones.

  • Glucagon
  • Epinephrine
  • Cortisol
  • Growth hormone

Step 4: The Liver Makes More Glucose

Counter-regulatory hormones stimulate hepatic glucose production.

Process Meaning
Glycogenolysis Breakdown of stored glycogen into glucose
Gluconeogenesis Creation of new glucose from non-carbohydrate substrates

Step 5: Osmotic Diuresis

As glucose rises, the kidney cannot reabsorb all filtered glucose. Glucose spills into the urine, pulling water with it.

  • Polyuria
  • Polydipsia
  • Dehydration
  • Electrolyte loss

Step 6: Lipolysis and Ketogenesis

Insulin deficiency promotes breakdown of triglycerides in adipose tissue.

Triglycerides → Free Fatty Acids + Glycerol

Free fatty acids travel to the liver and undergo beta-oxidation to acetyl-CoA. When acetyl-CoA production exceeds what can enter the Krebs cycle, it is diverted into ketogenesis.

Ketone Bodies

  • Acetone
  • Acetoacetate
  • Beta-hydroxybutyrate
Ketone bodies are acidic. Accumulation of ketones causes the metabolic acidosis in DKA.

What SGLT2 Inhibitors Do

SGLT2 inhibitors block sodium-glucose cotransporter 2 in the proximal tubule of the kidney. This causes glucose to be excreted in the urine.

SGLT2 inhibition → glucosuria → lower plasma glucose

This glucose-lowering effect is beneficial for diabetes and has important heart failure and kidney benefits, but it also explains why DKA can occur with unexpectedly normal glucose levels.

Mechanisms of SGLT2-Associated Euglycemic DKA

Mechanism 1: Glucosuria Keeps Glucose Lower

During DKA physiology, the liver is producing glucose through glycogenolysis and gluconeogenesis. Normally this would cause marked hyperglycemia. But with an SGLT2 inhibitor, much of that glucose is excreted in the urine.

The patient can be severely ketotic and acidotic while the glucose remains only mildly elevated.

Mechanism 2: Lower Insulin Levels

Because plasma glucose is lower, pancreatic beta cells receive less glucose stimulation and secrete less insulin.

Lower glucose → lower insulin secretion → relative insulin deficiency

Relative insulin deficiency removes the normal brake on lipolysis and ketogenesis.

Mechanism 3: Higher Glucagon Levels

SGLT2 inhibitors are associated with increased glucagon levels.

SGLT2 inhibitor → ↓ insulin + ↑ glucagon

This lowers the insulin-to-glucagon ratio and shifts metabolism toward fat breakdown and ketone production.

Mechanism 4: Increased Lipolysis

A low insulin-to-glucagon ratio promotes lipolysis in adipose tissue.

Lipolysis → Free Fatty Acids → Liver → Ketogenesis

Mechanism 5: Increased Renal Ketone Reabsorption

Some literature suggests that SGLT2 inhibitors may increase renal reabsorption of ketones, further increasing the ketone burden.

The combination of glucosuria, relative insulin deficiency, increased glucagon, lipolysis, ketogenesis, and ketone retention explains why SGLT2 inhibitors can cause euglycemic DKA.

Common Triggers

Most cases occur when an SGLT2 inhibitor is combined with a physiologic stressor.

Trigger Why It Matters
Infection Raises counter-regulatory hormones
Surgery Stress response and fasting
Reduced oral intake Promotes starvation physiology
Vomiting or GI losses Dehydration and stress hormones
Low-carbohydrate diet Promotes fat metabolism and ketogenesis
Insulin dose reduction Worsens relative insulin deficiency
Dehydration Worsens ketosis and renal clearance issues

When to Suspect Euglycemic DKA

Suspect euglycemic DKA in any patient taking an SGLT2 inhibitor who has symptoms of DKA, even if the glucose looks reassuring.

Common Symptoms

  • Nausea
  • Vomiting
  • Abdominal pain
  • Malaise
  • Tachypnea
  • Shortness of breath
  • Dehydration
  • Confusion in severe cases
A glucose of 120–220 mg/dL does not exclude DKA in a patient on an SGLT2 inhibitor.

Diagnosis

The diagnostic workup is similar to classic DKA, but clinicians must have a lower threshold to check ketones and acid-base status.

Test Expected Finding
Basic metabolic panel Elevated anion gap, low bicarbonate
VBG or ABG Low pH
Serum beta-hydroxybutyrate Elevated
Urine ketones Positive, though beta-hydroxybutyrate is preferred
Glucose Normal or mildly elevated
Urinalysis Glucosuria and ketonuria

Diagnostic Pattern

SGLT2 inhibitor + anion gap metabolic acidosis + elevated beta-hydroxybutyrate = euglycemic DKA until proven otherwise

Management

Management is similar to classic DKA, but dextrose often needs to be started earlier because glucose is already normal or only mildly elevated.

Step 1: Stop the SGLT2 Inhibitor

Hold the medication immediately.

Step 2: Give IV Fluids

Patients are often volume depleted from glucosuria, vomiting, poor intake, and osmotic diuresis.

Step 3: Start Insulin

Insulin is required to suppress lipolysis and ketogenesis.

Do not withhold insulin just because the glucose is normal.

Step 4: Add Dextrose Early

Because glucose is often normal, dextrose-containing fluids are frequently needed early so insulin can continue safely until the anion gap closes.

The goal is not just to lower glucose. The goal is to stop ketone production and close the anion gap.

Step 5: Monitor Closely

  • Glucose
  • Potassium
  • Bicarbonate
  • Anion gap
  • Beta-hydroxybutyrate
  • Volume status

Prevention

Perioperative Holding

SGLT2 inhibitors should be held before major surgery or prolonged fasting to reduce risk of perioperative euglycemic DKA.

A common teaching point is to hold SGLT2 inhibitors approximately 3 days before surgery, though exact timing may vary by agent and institutional policy.

Sick-Day Rules

Patients should be educated to hold SGLT2 inhibitors during acute illness, poor oral intake, vomiting, dehydration, or severe infection.

Patient Education

  • Do not ignore nausea or vomiting while taking an SGLT2 inhibitor.
  • Seek care for abdominal pain, tachypnea, or inability to eat.
  • DKA can occur even when home glucose readings are not high.
  • Discuss perioperative medication plans before procedures.

High-Yield Pearls

  1. SGLT2 inhibitors cause glucosuria.
  2. Glucosuria keeps glucose relatively normal even during DKA.
  3. Lower glucose leads to lower insulin secretion.
  4. SGLT2 inhibitors increase glucagon and lower the insulin-to-glucagon ratio.
  5. Low insulin-to-glucagon ratio promotes lipolysis and ketogenesis.
  6. Euglycemic DKA is still anion gap metabolic acidosis from ketones.
  7. Always check beta-hydroxybutyrate when symptoms suggest DKA.
  8. Do not rule out DKA because glucose is less than 250.
  9. Treat with fluids, insulin, potassium monitoring, and early dextrose.
  10. Hold SGLT2 inhibitors during acute illness, fasting, and before surgery.
Bottom line: SGLT2 inhibitors can cause DKA with normal or mildly elevated glucose. If the patient has an anion gap metabolic acidosis and elevated beta-hydroxybutyrate, treat as DKA with fluids, insulin, potassium monitoring, and early dextrose.

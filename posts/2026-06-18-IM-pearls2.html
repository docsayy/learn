Electrolytes and Acid-Base Pearls

Hypokalemia With Low pH

When a patient has hypokalemia with acidemia, think about processes that cause potassium loss along with bicarbonate loss or impaired renal acid handling.

Hypokalemia + low pH suggests diarrhea or renal tubular acidosis type 1 or type 2.
  • Diarrhea causes bicarbonate-rich fluid loss.
  • Type 1 and type 2 RTA are associated with low potassium.
  • Type 4 RTA is different because it is usually associated with hyperkalemia.

Hypokalemia With High pH

Hypokalemia with alkalemia usually suggests loss of acid or mineralocorticoid excess.

Hypokalemia + high pH suggests vomiting or hyperaldosteronism.
  • Vomiting causes loss of acid-rich upper GI fluid.
  • Hyperaldosteronism causes renal potassium wasting and metabolic alkalosis.
  • Hypertension is an important clue to hyperaldosteronism.

Hypokalemic Periodic Paralysis

Patients with hypokalemic periodic paralysis can present with very low serum potassium, but much of the abnormality is due to intracellular shifting rather than true total body potassium depletion.

Be cautious with aggressive potassium replacement. As the paralysis resolves, potassium can shift back into the bloodstream and cause rebound hyperkalemia.

Addiction Medicine Pearls

How to Counsel Patients Hesitant About Medication-Assisted Treatment

Patients may worry that buprenorphine or methadone is simply replacing one opioid with another. A helpful counseling point is that medication-assisted treatment is evidence-based harm reduction and saves lives.

Medication-assisted treatment for opioid use disorder reduces mortality risk and should be framed as life-saving treatment, not simply substitution.

Why Slow Methadone Titration After Day 3?

Methadone has a long half-life and can accumulate over time. Early daily titration may seem tolerated, but by day 3 and beyond, a stacking effect can occur.

  • Methadone accumulates because of its long half-life.
  • Rapid daily uptitration can increase overdose and sedation risk.
  • After the first few days, titration should slow down.

Infectious Disease Pearls

Post-Streptococcal Glomerulonephritis

Group A Streptococcus is associated with non-suppurative complications including post-streptococcal glomerulonephritis and rheumatic fever.

  • ASO antibodies are more associated with streptococcal pharyngitis.
  • Anti-DNase B is more associated with streptococcal skin infection.

Toxic Shock Syndrome and Tampons

Tampons are classically taught as a risk factor for toxic shock syndrome, but the strongest historical association was with super-absorbent tampon materials used in the 1970s and 1980s.

Modern tampon regulation has reduced the historical association between tampons and toxic shock syndrome.

MRSA Nares Testing

A negative MRSA nares screen has good negative predictive value for MRSA pneumonia, but it should not be used broadly to rule out MRSA colonization or infection at all skin sites.

A negative MRSA nares test is useful for pneumonia decisions, but not for ruling out MRSA skin colonization in general.

Neurology and Weakness Pearls

Approach to Lower Motor Neuron Weakness

A helpful anatomical approach to lower motor neuron weakness is to localize the problem along the motor pathway.

Anatomic Site Examples
Anterior horn cell Polio, West Nile virus
Peripheral nerve Guillain-Barré syndrome, Lyme disease, toxins, heavy metals, tick paralysis, beriberi
Neuromuscular junction Myasthenia gravis, Lambert-Eaton syndrome, botulism, organophosphate poisoning
Muscle Electrolyte abnormalities, periodic paralysis, myositis, myopathy

Can Spinal Cord Disease Cause Mixed UMN and LMN Signs?

Yes. Spinal cord disease can present with a mixture of upper and lower motor neuron signs, especially depending on timing.

  • Early or hyperacute lesions may show flaccidity or lower motor neuron features.
  • Later, upper motor neuron signs such as hyperreflexia and Babinski may appear.

Hypoglycemia Pearls

Hypoglycemia With Negative Ketones

If a patient has hypoglycemia with negative ketones, think about an insulin-mediated cause.

Pattern Interpretation
Hypoglycemia with low/negative ketones Insulin-mediated hypoglycemia
Hypoglycemia with positive ketones Non-insulin-mediated hypoglycemia

Insulin-Mediated Causes

  • Insulinoma
  • Sulfonylurea use
  • Surreptitious insulin use
Advanced hypoglycemia labs should be drawn while the patient is actively hypoglycemic.

Oncology and CAR-T Pearls

How CAR-T Therapy Works

CAR-T therapy involves removing a patient’s T cells, genetically modifying them to target cancer cells, and reinfusing them into the patient.

  • Patient T cells are collected.
  • The cells are genetically modified to recognize tumor targets.
  • Modified cells are reinfused.
  • Pre-treatment chemotherapy is often used to create space and improve CAR-T expansion.

ICANS and the ICE Score

Immune effector cell-associated neurotoxicity syndrome, or ICANS, is a neurologic complication of CAR-T and some immune therapies.

The ICE score is used to help assess and grade ICANS.

Grade 3 Cytokine Release Syndrome

Cytokine release syndrome, or CRS, is another important complication of CAR-T therapy.

Hypotension despite fluids or FiO₂ requirement greater than 40% suggests at least grade 3 CRS and should prompt ICU-level concern.
  • Higher-dose steroids may be required.
  • ICU consultation should be considered.
  • Institution-specific CAR-T toxicity protocols should be followed.

Vasculitis and Rheumatology Pearls

Why Get a Renal Biopsy in Suspected ANCA Vasculitis?

Even if ANCA serologies are positive in rapidly progressive glomerulonephritis, renal biopsy remains important because it helps determine prognosis and reversibility.

  • Active inflammation may respond to immunosuppression.
  • Extensive fibrosis and scarring suggest irreversible damage.
  • Biopsy helps weigh risks and benefits of prolonged immunosuppression.

Adult-Onset Refractory Asthma

Extremely difficult-to-control adult-onset asthma should raise concern for eosinophilic granulomatosis with polyangiitis, or EGPA, especially if accompanied by sinus disease, neuropathy, or eosinophilia.

Small Vessel Vasculitis Treatment

Treatment generally involves immunosuppression.

  • High-dose steroids
  • Rituximab or cyclophosphamide depending on severity and context
  • Specialist involvement with rheumatology and nephrology when renal disease is present

PR3 vs MPO Vasculitis

PR3-positive disease is associated with more severe disease, increased mortality, and higher relapse rates compared with MPO-positive small vessel vasculitis.

EGPA Pearls

  • EGPA can occur without peripheral eosinophilia.
  • EGPA is more commonly associated with MPO/p-ANCA.
  • Only about 30–40% of EGPA cases are MPO positive.
  • EGPA is strongly associated with mononeuritis multiplex.

Mononeuritis Multiplex

Mononeuritis multiplex refers to involvement of multiple individual nerves in non-contiguous distributions.

Think about vasculitis when a patient has asymmetric neuropathies affecting separate nerves in different regions.

Pulmonary Nodules and Tuberculosis Pearls

Patterns of Multiple Pulmonary Nodules

Pattern Clue Common Differential
Centrilobular Airway-centered Infection, aspiration, inflammatory airway disease
Random Hematogenous spread Metastases, miliary infection, fungal infection
Perilymphatic Nodules abut pleura or fissures Sarcoidosis, lymphangitic malignancy

Quantiferon Gold in Active TB

Quantiferon Gold has limited utility in suspected active tuberculosis because sensitivity is not high enough to rule out active disease.

A negative Quantiferon Gold does not rule out active TB.
  • Useful for latent TB screening.
  • Less reliable for active TB evaluation.
  • Active TB workup requires microbiologic testing.

AFB Smears, MTB PCR, and Culture

Test Key Point
AFB smear Fast and cheap, but only moderately sensitive
MTB PCR Faster molecular confirmation when positive
AFB culture Most sensitive and provides resistance data, but takes weeks

Cirrhosis and Ascites Pearls

Midodrine in Cirrhosis With Hypotension and Hyponatremia

Midodrine may be considered in selected cirrhotic patients with hypotension, refractory ascites, or hyponatremia, though evidence is mixed.

Portal Hypertensive Gastropathy

Portal hypertension itself can cause gastropathy through congestion and hyperemia of the stomach.

Acid is not the main driver of portal hypertensive gastropathy, so PPIs are not routinely helpful unless there is another indication.

ACE Inhibitors and ARBs in Cirrhosis With Ascites

ACE inhibitors and ARBs should generally be discontinued once cirrhotic patients develop ascites because of concern for renal hypoperfusion and hepatorenal physiology.

Frequent Large-Volume Paracentesis

A cirrhotic patient needing more than 8 liters removed every 2 weeks may have dietary sodium non-adherence or inadequate diuretic optimization contributing to rapid fluid reaccumulation.

Brown Ascites Fluid

If ascites fluid is brown, consider sending bilirubin to evaluate for a biliary leak.

  • Gallbladder perforation
  • Bowel perforation
  • Biliary leak
  • Consider amylase if pancreatic source or bowel perforation is possible

Ascites Cultures in Blood Culture Bottles

Ascitic fluid cultures should be inoculated into blood culture bottles at the bedside when possible because this improves culture yield.

In suspected SBP, send cell count with differential and culture ascites fluid in blood culture bottles.

Untreated Hepatitis C and Positive Autoimmune Labs

Untreated hepatitis C can cause positive autoimmune serologies and mimic autoimmune disease.

Interpret autoimmune serologies carefully in patients with untreated hepatitis C.

Other Useful Pearls

Meniere Disease

Episodic vertigo with sensorineural hearing loss, especially affecting low-pitched sounds, suggests Meniere disease.

  • Initial treatment includes lifestyle modification.
  • Thiazide diuretics may be used if lifestyle changes are insufficient.

Clonidine Taper

Clonidine should be tapered to reduce the risk of rebound hypertension.

In monitored inpatient settings, clonidine can sometimes be tapered by about 50% every 2–3 days, but patients on high doses may require a slower approach.

Discharging With Foley and Wound Care

Patients being discharged with both Foley catheter care and wound care needs often benefit from home health nursing support.

Take-Home Pearls

  • Hypokalemia with acidosis suggests diarrhea or type 1/type 2 RTA.
  • Hypokalemia with alkalosis suggests vomiting or hyperaldosteronism.
  • Medication-assisted treatment for opioid use disorder saves lives.
  • Slow methadone titration after day 3 because of accumulation.
  • Negative ketones during hypoglycemia suggest an insulin-mediated process.
  • Renal biopsy helps determine prognosis in ANCA-associated RPGN.
  • PR3-positive vasculitis tends to relapse more and behave more severely than MPO-positive disease.
  • EGPA may be ANCA-negative and may present with refractory adult-onset asthma or mononeuritis multiplex.
  • Negative Quantiferon does not rule out active TB.
  • Ascites cultures should be sent in blood culture bottles.
  • Stop ACE inhibitors and ARBs in cirrhosis once ascites develops unless there is a compelling reason and specialist guidance.

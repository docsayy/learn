Bioterrorism and High-Consequence Biological Threats

Added July 15, 2026 • Infectious Disease • ~62 min read

Clinicians may be the first people to recognize an intentional biological event. The initial presentation may resemble routine pneumonia, gastroenteritis, sepsis, rash, meningitis, or neuromuscular disease. Effective response depends on recognizing an unusual clinical or epidemiologic pattern, stabilizing the patient, immediately applying syndrome-appropriate infection control, notifying infection prevention and public health, warning the laboratory before specimens are processed, initiating time-sensitive treatment or post-exposure prophylaxis, and coordinating the response through established emergency systems. This clinically focused guide intentionally omits information about producing, modifying, or disseminating biological agents.

Clinical Overview

A biological emergency may initially appear to be an ordinary outbreak. Patients might present individually to different emergency departments, urgent-care centers, primary-care practices, or hospitals before anyone recognizes that their illnesses are related.

The clinician’s responsibility is not to determine criminal intent. The clinician must:

  1. Recognize an unusual clinical or epidemiologic pattern.
  2. Stabilize the patient.
  3. Protect healthcare personnel and other patients.
  4. Notify infection prevention, the laboratory, and public health.
  5. Begin time-sensitive treatment or prophylaxis.
  6. Preserve diagnostic opportunities.
Recognize → Isolate → Stabilize → Notify → Test Safely → Treat

Biological Events May Result From:

  • Intentional release
  • Naturally occurring outbreak
  • Laboratory accident
  • Occupational exposure
  • Contaminated food, medication, or consumer product
  • Imported high-consequence infection
The first clinical actions are often the same regardless of whether the event is intentional, accidental, or naturally occurring.

Definitions and Scope

Bioterrorism

Bioterrorism is the intentional use of microorganisms or biologically derived toxins to cause illness, death, fear, social disruption, or economic damage.

Biological Threat

A biological threat is broader and may include:

  • Intentional biological attacks
  • Emerging infectious diseases
  • High-consequence imported infections
  • Laboratory accidents
  • Contamination events

High-Consequence Infectious Disease

A high-consequence infectious disease generally has one or more of the following:

  • High mortality
  • Potential for healthcare transmission
  • Limited treatment or vaccine availability
  • Need for specialized isolation
  • Major public-health implications

Clinical Focus

This article focuses on medical recognition, infection control, diagnosis, treatment, and public-health response. It does not discuss methods for growing, concentrating, modifying, or disseminating biological agents.

Why Clinicians Matter

Clinicians May Detect the First Signal

Electronic surveillance may identify an outbreak, but bedside clinicians may first notice:

  • An unusually severe presentation
  • A rare diagnosis in an unexpected location
  • Several patients with the same unusual syndrome
  • An unexplained pattern among coworkers or event attendees
  • An organism behaving unlike its typical epidemiology

Early Recognition Changes Outcomes

  • Botulinum antitoxin is most effective when given early.
  • Anthrax antitoxin and combination therapy may be lifesaving.
  • Pneumonic plague requires prompt treatment and droplet precautions.
  • Smallpox vaccination is most protective when administered soon after exposure.
  • Viral hemorrhagic fever isolation protects healthcare personnel.
  • Post-exposure prophylaxis may prevent disease among contacts.

The Clinician Does Not Need Proof of Intent

A credible clinical suspicion is enough to activate infection-prevention and public-health consultation.

Historically listed Category B threats include:

  • Glanders

Category C has described emerging pathogens that might have potential for major population impact.

The A, B, and C framework is useful for teaching but is historical and not exhaustive. Actual response priorities depend on the current event, transmissibility, clinical severity, available countermeasures, and public-health guidance.

Recognizing a Suspicious Biological Event

Epidemiologic Warning Signs

  • An abrupt cluster of similar severe illness
  • Many patients from the same location or event
  • A disease occurring outside its expected geography
  • A disease occurring outside its usual season
  • An unusually high attack rate
  • Simultaneous illness in people and animals
  • Unexplained illness among healthcare or laboratory workers
  • Multiple cases without expected person-to-person links
  • An unusual age distribution

Clinical Warning Signs

  • Severe pneumonia with mediastinal widening or pleural effusions
  • Several patients with symmetric descending paralysis
  • A febrile vesiculopustular rash with lesions in the same stage
  • Rapidly progressive hemorrhagic or shock syndrome
  • Severe pneumonia with hemoptysis in a cluster
  • Painless black eschar with extensive edema
  • Unexplained sepsis caused by a rare organism
  • Unusual antibiotic-resistance pattern

Laboratory Warning Signs

  • Unexpected nonhemolytic gram-positive rods
  • Possible safety-pin staining gram-negative organisms
  • Fastidious organisms with laboratory aerosol risk
  • Unexpected identification of a select agent
  • Several similar isolates from epidemiologically linked patients
Do not wait for a second or third case before notifying public health when a single patient has a credible high-consequence syndrome.

Not Every Unusual Cluster Is Intentional

More Common Explanations Include:

  • Natural outbreak
  • Foodborne contamination
  • Medication-associated outbreak
  • Occupational exposure
  • Imported disease
  • Laboratory accident
  • Shared environmental exposure

Avoid Premature Conclusions

Calling an event terrorism without evidence may:

  • Create panic
  • Stigmatize patients or communities
  • Disrupt the investigation
  • Damage public trust
  • Distract from immediate clinical care
Report the unusual disease pattern. Allow public-health and law-enforcement partners to determine whether intentional activity occurred.

Immediate Clinical Response

First Actions

  1. Move the patient away from common waiting areas.
  2. Apply syndrome-appropriate precautions.
  3. Assess airway, breathing, circulation, and mental status.
  4. Notify infection prevention and the supervising clinician.
  5. Contact local or state public health.
  6. Notify the clinical laboratory before sending specimens.
  7. Obtain essential diagnostic specimens safely.
  8. Begin treatment without waiting for definitive confirmation when delay is dangerous.
ABCs + Isolation + Notification + Time-Critical Countermeasures

Document:

  • Symptom onset
  • Potential exposure
  • Locations visited
  • People accompanying the patient
  • Healthcare personnel who entered before precautions
  • Specimens obtained
  • Therapies administered

Stabilization

Airway and Breathing

Prepare early for respiratory failure with:

  • Botulism
  • Inhalation anthrax
  • Pneumonic plague
  • Viral hemorrhagic fever
  • Severe toxin exposure

Circulation

  • Establish reliable vascular access using trained personnel.
  • Use balanced crystalloid when appropriate.
  • Begin vasopressors for persistent shock.
  • Monitor lactate, urine output, and peripheral perfusion.

Minimize Unnecessary Procedures

When a transmissible high-consequence disease is possible:

  • Limit phlebotomy.
  • Avoid nonessential aerosol-generating procedures.
  • Avoid unnecessary transport.
  • Use point-of-care testing only when approved by institutional protocols.

Do Not Delay Lifesaving Care

Infection control should make resuscitation safer—not prevent clinically necessary airway management, surgery, drainage, or critical care.

Isolate and Notify

Who to Notify

  • Hospital infection prevention
  • Emergency-department or ICU leadership
  • Clinical microbiology laboratory
  • Local or state health department
  • Occupational health
  • Hospital emergency management

Additional Notification May Include:

  • Poison control for toxin or chemical exposures
  • CDC through the health department
  • Emergency medical services leadership
  • Specialized treatment centers
  • Institutional security

Do Not:

  • Send the patient through public hallways without planning.
  • Send specimens to the laboratory without warning.
  • Ask unprotected staff to obtain repeated samples.
  • Release unverified information publicly.

Public-Health Coordination

Public Health Can Assist With:

  • Case-definition assessment
  • Diagnostic testing
  • Specimen transport
  • Contact identification
  • Post-exposure prophylaxis
  • Antitoxin or antiviral access
  • Strategic National Stockpile coordination
  • Outbreak investigation
  • Risk communication

Do Not Wait for Laboratory Confirmation

Immediate public-health consultation is particularly important for suspected:

  • Anthrax
  • Botulism
  • Pneumonic plague
  • Smallpox
  • Tularemia
  • Viral hemorrhagic fever
  • Ricin or another unusual toxin exposure

Hospital Incident Command

Activation May Be Needed When:

  • Several linked patients arrive.
  • A high-consequence diagnosis is suspected.
  • Specialized PPE or isolation is required.
  • Staff exposures have occurred.
  • Countermeasure distribution is needed.
  • Media interest is substantial.
  • Routine hospital operations may be disrupted.

Incident Command Coordinates:

  • Clinical operations
  • Infection control
  • Staffing
  • Supplies and PPE
  • Laboratory operations
  • Security
  • Communications
  • Public-health liaison
Individual clinicians should not independently manage hospital-wide messaging, employee prophylaxis, or public notification.

Risk Communication

Communicate What Is Known

  • What syndrome is suspected
  • What precautions are required
  • What remains uncertain
  • What actions are being taken
  • When additional information will be available

Avoid:

  • Speculation
  • Absolute reassurance before investigation
  • Stigmatizing language
  • Sharing protected patient information
  • Uncoordinated social-media posts

Address the Worried Well

Clear communication may reduce unnecessary emergency visits, panic purchasing, inappropriate antibiotic use, and demand for unindicated testing.

Infection-Control Principles

Transmission Determines Precautions

Potential biological agents differ substantially:

  • Anthrax is generally not transmitted from person to person.
  • Botulism is not transmitted from person to person.
  • Tularemia is rarely transmitted between people.
  • Pneumonic plague can spread through respiratory droplets.
  • Smallpox requires airborne and contact precautions.
  • Viral hemorrhagic fevers spread primarily through blood and body fluids.

When the Agent Is Unknown

Use precautions appropriate to the presenting syndrome and possible route until:

  • The agent is identified.
  • Transmission has been clarified.
  • Public health provides updated guidance.

Precaution Summary

Threat Healthcare Precautions Important Duration
Anthrax Standard; Contact if uncontained cutaneous drainage; decontamination precautions with visible spore contamination Based on drainage and decontamination status
Botulism Standard Routine duration
Pneumonic plague Standard plus Droplet Until 48 hours of effective therapy

Always follow current pathogen-specific CDC and institutional guidance. Precautions may change as an event is characterized.

Personal Protective Equipment

PPE Selection Depends On:

  • Route of transmission
  • Risk of splash or body-fluid exposure
  • Whether the patient is visibly contaminated
  • Whether an aerosol-generating procedure is required
  • Current public-health guidance

PPE Systems Must Include:

  • Training
  • Observed donning and doffing when required
  • A designated doffing area
  • Waste-management planning
  • Occupational-health monitoring

PPE Is Not Enough

PPE must be combined with hand hygiene, environmental control, sharps safety, limited personnel, and safe work practices.

Aerosol-Generating Procedures

Potential Procedures

  • Endotracheal intubation
  • Noninvasive ventilation
  • Open suctioning
  • Bronchoscopy
  • Cardiopulmonary resuscitation
  • Nebulized treatment in selected circumstances

When Required:

  • Use an airborne-infection isolation room when available.
  • Limit personnel.
  • Use appropriate respiratory and eye protection.
  • Use the most experienced operator.
  • Use closed systems when possible.
  • Plan equipment disposal or decontamination.

Healthcare Contact Tracking

Identify:

  • Staff who entered before precautions
  • Staff involved in aerosol-generating procedures
  • Persons with mucous-membrane exposure
  • Persons with percutaneous exposure
  • Other patients or visitors with close contact
  • Laboratory personnel who handled specimens

Document:

  • Exposure date and duration
  • PPE used
  • Type of contact
  • Symptoms
  • Need for prophylaxis
  • Monitoring plan

Occupational Health Role

Occupational health should coordinate:

  • Exposure assessment
  • Medication distribution
  • Vaccination
  • Daily symptom monitoring
  • Work restrictions
  • Confidential documentation

Laboratory Safety

Why Advance Notification Matters

Some organisms pose major aerosol or laboratory-acquisition risk during:

  • Culture manipulation
  • Centrifugation
  • MALDI-TOF preparation
  • Automated identification
  • Biochemical testing
  • Opening leaking containers

Before Sending Specimens

  • Call the microbiology laboratory.
  • Discuss the suspected agent.
  • Use approved containers.
  • Limit specimens to those that are clinically necessary.
  • Coordinate transport with public health.
Do not write only “rule out bioterrorism” on a routine specimen and send it through the pneumatic-tube system.

Laboratory Response Network

The Laboratory Response Network for Biological Threats links:

  • Hospital and commercial sentinel laboratories
  • State and local public-health laboratories
  • Federal reference laboratories
  • Military, veterinary, food, and environmental laboratories

Functions

  • Rapid detection
  • Confirmatory testing
  • Secure result communication
  • Standardized testing algorithms
  • Coordination during public-health emergencies

Clinical Implication

Most clinicians access the Laboratory Response Network through their hospital laboratory and local or state health department rather than contacting a distant reference laboratory independently.

Specimen Collection

General Principles

  • Collect before treatment when doing so does not delay lifesaving therapy.
  • Collect the correct specimen for the clinical syndrome.
  • Use leakproof primary and secondary containment.
  • Coordinate labeling and transport.
  • Minimize sharps and unnecessary tubes.

Potential Specimens

  • Blood cultures
  • Serum and plasma
  • Lesion swabs or tissue
  • Respiratory specimens
  • Pleural fluid
  • Stool or gastric contents
  • CSF when safe and indicated

Do Not:

  • Attempt environmental sampling yourself.
  • Transport suspicious material in personal vehicles.
  • Open suspicious packages.
  • Perform unnecessary bedside manipulation.

Suspicious Laboratory Isolates

Immediate Actions

  • Stop unnecessary manipulation.
  • Secure the specimen or isolate.
  • Notify laboratory leadership and biosafety personnel.
  • Notify public health.
  • Identify potentially exposed laboratory personnel.
  • Arrange confirmatory testing through the LRN.

Examples of High-Risk Organisms

  • Bacillus anthracis
  • Francisella tularensis
  • Brucella species
  • Burkholderia pseudomallei
  • Burkholderia mallei
  • Yersinia pestis
Many laboratory exposures occur before the organism is recognized. A careful travel, occupational, animal, and epidemiologic history helps the laboratory choose safer procedures.

Patient Decontamination

When Decontamination May Be Needed

  • Visible powder or particulate contamination
  • Known external toxin contamination
  • Contaminated clothing
  • Environmental exposure before hospital arrival

Basic Principles

  • Prevent contamination of the emergency department.
  • Remove outer clothing carefully.
  • Place clothing in designated containers.
  • Wash exposed skin with soap and water.
  • Irrigate exposed eyes with water or saline.
  • Avoid aggressive scrubbing that damages skin.

Anthrax Spores

After suspected spore contact:

  • Use soap and water for hand and skin cleansing.
  • Alcohol-based hand rubs are not reliably active against spores.
  • Do not apply concentrated bleach directly to skin.

Internal Exposure

External decontamination does not treat inhaled, ingested, or injected disease. Medical evaluation and prophylaxis remain necessary.

Suspicious Powder or Material

Do Not:

  • Shake or smell it.
  • Clean it with a vacuum.
  • Sweep or brush it.
  • Carry it through the hospital.
  • Send it to the clinical laboratory.
  • Apply household chemicals indiscriminately.

Actions

  • Move away from the material.
  • Prevent others from entering the area.
  • Notify institutional security and emergency management.
  • Contact public-health and emergency-response authorities.
  • Follow decontamination instructions.

Clinical Evaluation

Assess:

  • Direct skin or mucous-membrane contact
  • Possible inhalation
  • Symptoms
  • Need for anthrax PEP or other countermeasures

Incubation Period Helps

The interval between exposure and symptom onset can:

  • Support or weaken a diagnosis
  • Identify contacts still within a prophylaxis window
  • Distinguish infection from immediate chemical exposure
  • Guide duration of monitoring

Severe Pneumonia Cluster

Consider:

  • Inhalation anthrax
  • Pneumonic plague
  • Pneumonic tularemia
  • Q fever
  • Melioidosis
  • Glanders
  • Legionella
  • Influenza or another respiratory virus
  • Chemical inhalational injury

Clues Favoring Anthrax

  • Marked mediastinal adenopathy or widening
  • Large pleural effusions
  • Severe systemic illness with limited early pulmonary findings
  • Hemorrhagic mediastinitis

Clues Favoring Plague

  • Rapidly progressive pneumonia
  • Hemoptysis
  • Bloody sputum
  • Shock and DIC
  • Close-contact transmission

Descending Paralysis Cluster

Botulism Pattern

  • Alert mental status
  • Cranial-nerve palsies
  • Blurred vision or diplopia
  • Ptosis
  • Dysarthria and dysphagia
  • Symmetric descending weakness
  • Respiratory failure
  • Usually preserved sensation

Cluster Questions

  • Did patients eat the same food?
  • Did they receive injections from the same source?
  • Do they share a wound or drug-use exposure?
  • Did symptoms begin within a similar interval?
Do not wait for respiratory failure or laboratory confirmation before requesting botulinum antitoxin.

Fever and Vesiculopustular Rash Cluster

Consider:

  • Smallpox
  • Mpox
  • Varicella
  • Disseminated HSV or zoster
  • Disseminated bacterial infection
  • Drug eruption

Smallpox Clues

  • High fever preceding the rash
  • Deep, firm lesions
  • Centrifugal distribution
  • Prominent face and extremity involvement
  • Palms and soles involved
  • Lesions in the same stage within a body region

Action

Airborne + Contact Precautions → Infection Prevention → Public Health

Hemorrhagic or Shock Syndrome

Consider:

  • Viral hemorrhagic fever
  • Severe malaria
  • Dengue
  • Leptospirosis
  • Meningococcemia
  • Rickettsial disease
  • Severe sepsis with DIC
  • Acute leukemia
  • Toxin or anticoagulant exposure

Epidemiology Is Essential

Viral hemorrhagic fever becomes credible when compatible illness is combined with:

  • Travel to an active outbreak area
  • Healthcare exposure
  • Funeral or body-fluid exposure
  • Contact with a confirmed case
  • Specific animal or laboratory exposure
Bleeding is not required for viral hemorrhagic fever, and many patients never develop dramatic hemorrhage.

Gastrointestinal Cluster

Potential Causes

  • Routine foodborne outbreak
  • Salmonella
  • Shigella
  • Shiga toxin-producing E. coli
  • Cholera
  • Botulinum toxin
  • Ricin or another toxin
  • Anthrax after ingestion

Important Clues

  • Neurologic findings suggest botulism.
  • Profuse watery diarrhea suggests cholera or toxin-mediated disease.
  • Bloody diarrhea suggests invasive infection or Shiga toxin.
  • Severe vomiting and diarrhea with multiorgan failure may suggest toxin exposure.
  • Oropharyngeal ulcers or neck edema may occur with gastrointestinal anthrax.

Encephalitis Cluster

Consider:

  • Arboviral encephalitis
  • HSV encephalitis
  • West Nile virus
  • Eastern equine encephalitis
  • Venezuelan equine encephalitis
  • Japanese encephalitis after travel
  • Toxin exposure

Evaluation

  • Airway and seizure stabilization
  • Neuroimaging
  • CSF studies when safe
  • Blood cultures
  • HSV PCR and empiric acyclovir when appropriate
  • Public-health arboviral testing

Anthrax

Anthrax is caused by Bacillus anthracis, a spore-forming gram-positive bacterium. Naturally occurring disease may follow contact with infected animals or contaminated animal products. An intentional exposure could initially be unrecognized.

Major Forms

  • Cutaneous
  • Inhalation
  • Gastrointestinal or oropharyngeal
  • Injection
  • Meningeal or systemic disease

Key Clinical Principle

Any form of anthrax can become systemic and can be complicated by hemorrhagic meningitis.

Anthrax Syndromes

Form Typical Findings
Cutaneous Painless papule progressing to vesicle and black eschar with extensive edema
Inhalation Biphasic systemic illness, mediastinal widening, adenopathy, pleural effusions, shock
Gastrointestinal Severe abdominal pain, vomiting, bloody diarrhea, ascites, sepsis
Oropharyngeal Oral or pharyngeal lesion, cervical edema, lymphadenopathy, dysphagia

Cutaneous Anthrax

Evolution

  1. Pruritic papule
  2. Vesicle or bulla
  3. Ulceration
  4. Painless black eschar
  5. Prominent surrounding edema

Clinical Clues

  • Lesion is often painless.
  • Edema may appear disproportionate.
  • Regional lymphadenopathy may occur.
  • Fever suggests systemic involvement.

Differential Diagnosis

  • Brown recluse bite
  • Ecthyma gangrenosum
  • Orf
  • Tularemia
  • Rickettsial eschar
  • Necrotic bacterial infection

Inhalation Anthrax

Early Phase

  • Fever
  • Fatigue
  • Malaise
  • Nonproductive cough
  • Chest discomfort
  • Nausea or abdominal symptoms

Progressive Phase

  • Severe dyspnea
  • Hypoxemia
  • Shock
  • Altered mental status
  • Respiratory failure

Imaging Findings

  • Mediastinal widening
  • Mediastinal or hilar adenopathy
  • Pleural effusions
  • Hemorrhagic mediastinitis
  • Variable pulmonary infiltrates
Large pleural effusions and mediastinal abnormalities are more characteristic than a routine lobar-pneumonia pattern.

Gastrointestinal and Oropharyngeal Anthrax

Gastrointestinal Features

  • Severe abdominal pain
  • Nausea and vomiting
  • Hematemesis
  • Bloody diarrhea
  • Ascites
  • Sepsis

Oropharyngeal Features

  • Oral or pharyngeal ulcer
  • Neck swelling
  • Cervical lymphadenopathy
  • Dysphagia
  • Airway compromise

Evaluation

  • Blood cultures
  • Stool and lesion testing
  • Ascitic-fluid testing when present
  • CT abdomen and pelvis
  • Airway assessment

Injection Anthrax

Potential Presentation

  • Severe soft-tissue edema
  • Deep pain
  • Necrosis
  • Compartment syndrome
  • Septic shock
  • Minimal or absent classic black eschar

Management

  • Immediate surgical evaluation
  • Systemic anthrax therapy
  • Assessment for meningitis
  • Antitoxin when indicated
  • Aggressive critical care

Do Not Confuse With Routine Injection Infection

The differential includes:

  • Clostridial myonecrosis
  • Necrotizing streptococcal infection
  • Polymicrobial injection-site infection
  • Compartment syndrome

Anthrax Diagnosis

Potential Specimens

  • Blood cultures
  • Lesion swab beneath the eschar
  • Vesicular fluid
  • Skin biopsy
  • Pleural fluid
  • Respiratory specimens
  • CSF
  • Ascitic fluid or stool

Diagnostic Methods

  • Culture
  • PCR
  • Immunohistochemistry
  • Serology
  • Toxin testing through specialized laboratories

Meningitis Assessment

Evaluate all patients with systemic anthrax for:

  • Severe headache
  • Altered mental status
  • Meningeal signs
  • Focal neurologic findings
  • Seizure
Notify the laboratory before submitting specimens. Do not allow routine manipulation of a suspected anthrax isolate without biosafety review.

Anthrax Treatment

Localized Cutaneous Disease Without Systemic Findings

Potential oral agents include:

  • Ciprofloxacin 500 mg orally every 12 hours
  • Doxycycline 100 mg orally every 12 hours
  • Levofloxacin 750 mg orally every 24 hours

Duration depends on whether disease is naturally acquired or associated with possible aerosol exposure.

Systemic Anthrax

Systemic disease requires combination therapy. A practical initial approach generally includes:

  • One or more bactericidal drugs
  • A protein-synthesis inhibitor with potential toxin-suppressing activity
  • Drugs with CNS penetration when meningitis is possible

Example Meningitis-Capable Framework

Meropenem + Ciprofloxacin + Doxycycline or Linezolid

Final selection should be coordinated with CDC and public health and adjusted for:

  • Susceptibility
  • Meningitis probability
  • Pregnancy
  • Age
  • Renal and hepatic function
  • Drug availability

Supportive and Procedural Care

  • Drain significant pleural effusions continuously when needed.
  • Manage cerebral edema and intracranial hemorrhage.
  • Provide vasopressors and ventilation.
  • Obtain early critical-care and infectious-disease consultation.

Anthrax Antitoxin

Available Countermeasures Include:

  • Raxibacumab
  • Obiltoxaximab
  • Anthrax immune globulin intravenous

Use

Antitoxin is considered for systemic anthrax because antibiotics kill bacteria but do not neutralize toxin already circulating in the body.

Antibiotics + Antitoxin + Source-Specific Supportive Care

Access

  • Contact public health immediately.
  • Antitoxins are available through federal emergency systems and the Strategic National Stockpile.
  • Do not wait for late deterioration before requesting consultation.

Anthrax Post-Exposure Prophylaxis

After a Credible Aerosol Exposure

Post-exposure prophylaxis generally includes:

  • Up to 60 days of an active oral antimicrobial
  • Plus post-exposure anthrax vaccination for eligible exposed persons

Potential Antimicrobials

  • Ciprofloxacin
  • Doxycycline
  • Levofloxacin

Vaccines for Eligible Adults

  • Anthrax Vaccine Adsorbed on a public-health-directed post-exposure schedule
  • Adjuvanted anthrax vaccine on its approved post-exposure schedule

Why Prolonged Antibiotics?

Inhaled spores may remain dormant before germinating, so short prophylactic courses are inadequate after aerosol exposure.

Public-Health Direction Is Essential

Do not prescribe a few days of doxycycline for a suspected anthrax exposure without immediate public-health coordination.

Anthrax Infection Control

Person-to-Person Transmission

  • Inhalation anthrax is not transmitted between people.
  • Gastrointestinal anthrax is not transmitted between people.
  • Transmission from cutaneous drainage is extremely rare.

Precautions

  • Standard Precautions for most patients
  • Contact Precautions when cutaneous drainage cannot be contained
  • Special decontamination precautions for visible spore contamination

Hand Hygiene After Spore Contact

  • Use soap and water.
  • Alcohol hand rub alone is not reliable against spores.

Botulism

Botulism is caused by neurotoxin that blocks acetylcholine release at the neuromuscular junction. Forms include:

  • Foodborne botulism
  • Wound botulism
  • Infant botulism
  • Adult intestinal colonization
  • Iatrogenic botulism
  • Inhalational exposure in exceptional circumstances

Core Clinical Pattern

Cranial Neuropathy → Symmetric Descending Paralysis → Respiratory Failure

Botulism Recognition

Early Findings

  • Diplopia
  • Blurred vision
  • Ptosis
  • Fixed or poorly reactive pupils
  • Dry mouth
  • Dysarthria
  • Dysphonia
  • Dysphagia

Progression

  • Neck weakness
  • Upper-extremity weakness
  • Respiratory-muscle weakness
  • Lower-extremity weakness

Characteristic Features

  • Symmetric weakness
  • Descending pattern
  • Usually normal sensation
  • Usually alert mental status
  • No fever unless another process is present
  • Constipation or ileus may occur

Respiratory Monitoring

  • Serial forced vital capacity
  • Negative inspiratory force
  • Swallow and secretion assessment
  • Continuous respiratory observation

Botulism Diagnosis

Clinical Diagnosis Comes First

Laboratory confirmation may take time and should not delay antitoxin.

Potential Specimens

  • Serum
  • Stool
  • Enema specimen
  • Gastric contents
  • Wound tissue or culture
  • Suspected food

Before Antitoxin

Collect serum before antitoxin when this can be accomplished immediately without delaying treatment.

Electrodiagnostic Testing

Repetitive nerve stimulation or electromyography may support the diagnosis but is not required before treatment.

Botulism Treatment

Immediate Action

Call the State Health Department → Request Antitoxin → Treat Immediately

Antitoxin

  • Heptavalent botulism antitoxin is used for most noninfant cases.
  • BabyBIG is used for infant botulism in the United States.
  • Antitoxin neutralizes circulating toxin.
  • Antitoxin does not reverse paralysis already established.

Supportive Care

  • Early airway management
  • Mechanical ventilation
  • Enteral nutrition
  • Aspiration prevention
  • DVT prophylaxis
  • Pressure-injury prevention
  • Physical rehabilitation

Recovery

Recovery may require weeks to months because new neuromuscular connections must develop.

Do not wait for laboratory confirmation or transfer to a tertiary center before arranging antitoxin.

Wound Botulism

Risk Factors

  • Injection-drug use
  • Black-tar heroin exposure
  • Contaminated traumatic wound
  • Rare surgical or injection-associated exposure

Management

  • Administer antitoxin.
  • Perform surgical wound evaluation and debridement.
  • Obtain anaerobic cultures.
  • Use penicillin or metronidazole when clinically indicated.

Medication Caution

Drugs that impair neuromuscular transmission may worsen weakness, including:

  • Aminoglycosides
  • Magnesium
  • Selected neuromuscular-blocking agents

Botulism Infection Control

Transmission

  • Botulism is not transmitted from person to person.

Precautions

  • Standard Precautions
  • Routine respiratory isolation is not required

Contacts

  • Household contacts do not require antitoxin merely because they cared for the patient.
  • People who ate the same implicated food require urgent public-health assessment.
  • Remove suspected food from use without manipulating it unnecessarily.

Plague

Plague is caused by Yersinia pestis. Naturally occurring disease is associated with infected fleas, rodents, and some domestic animals. Pneumonic disease may spread through close respiratory-droplet exposure.

Forms

  • Bubonic
  • Septicemic
  • Pneumonic
  • Pharyngeal
  • Meningeal

Plague Syndromes

  • Fever
  • Chills
  • Weakness
  • Very painful swollen lymph node or bubo

Septicemic Plague

  • Sepsis and shock
  • DIC
  • Purpura
  • Digital or tissue ischemia
  • Abdominal symptoms

Pneumonic Plague

  • Fever
  • Cough
  • Dyspnea
  • Chest pain
  • Bloody sputum or hemoptysis
  • Rapid shock

Pneumonic Plague

Progression

Pneumonic plague can progress rapidly from nonspecific febrile illness to:

  • Severe pneumonia
  • Respiratory failure
  • Hemoptysis
  • Circulatory collapse
  • DIC

Transmission

  • Requires close exposure to infectious respiratory droplets.
  • Risk is greatest with advanced illness and copious bloody sputum.
  • Appropriate antibiotic therapy rapidly decreases infectiousness.

Potential Specimens

  • Blood cultures
  • Sputum
  • Tracheal aspirate
  • Bubo aspirate

  • Gram stain
  • Culture

  • Serology

Warn the laboratory before submitting specimens because manipulation of Y. pestis may expose laboratory personnel.

Plague meningitis requires a regimen with reliable CNS penetration and immediate expert consultation.

Smallpox

Smallpox is caused by variola virus. Naturally occurring smallpox has been eradicated. A confirmed case anywhere would constitute an international public-health emergency.

Incubation

  • Approximately 7–19 days
  • Average approximately 12 days

Transmission

  • Prolonged face-to-face respiratory exposure
  • Direct contact with lesions or scabs
  • Contaminated materials
  • Rare airborne transmission

Smallpox Recognition

Prodrome

  • High fever
  • Severe malaise
  • Headache
  • Back pain
  • Vomiting

Rash

  • Begins after the febrile prodrome
  • Often starts in the mouth and face
  • Spreads centrifugally
  • Prominently involves face and extremities
  • May involve palms and soles
  • Progresses from macules to papules, vesicles, pustules, and scabs
  • Lesions in a body region are generally in the same stage

Lesion Quality

  • Deep seated
  • Firm
  • Round and well circumscribed
  • May be umbilicated

Smallpox vs Varicella

Feature Smallpox Varicella
Prodrome Severe fever before rash Mild or absent in children
Distribution Centrifugal Central or truncal
Palms and soles Often involved Usually spared
Lesion stage Same stage in an area Successive crops in different stages
Lesion quality Deep and firm Superficial and delicate
Do not rely on photographs alone. Immediately involve public health for any credible smallpox-compatible illness.

Immediate Smallpox Response

Actions

  1. Place the patient in an airborne-infection isolation room.
  2. Use Standard, Contact, and Airborne Precautions.
  3. Notify infection prevention and public health immediately.
  4. Restrict room entry.
  5. Use vaccinated personnel when directed and available.
  6. Coordinate all testing through public health.
  7. Begin contact identification.

Do Not:

  • Send lesion samples through routine laboratory workflows.
  • Transport the patient without advance planning.
  • Allow visitors without public-health approval.
  • Wait for additional cases.

Smallpox Post-Exposure Vaccination

Timing

Within 3 Days: May Prevent or Substantially Reduce Disease
Within 4–7 Days: May Reduce Disease Severity

Public-Health Response

An outbreak response would likely use:

  • Rapid contact identification
  • Post-exposure vaccination
  • Ring vaccination
  • Symptom monitoring
  • Isolation of cases

Routine Vaccination

  • Routine smallpox vaccination is not recommended for the general public.
  • Selected occupational groups handling orthopoxviruses may receive vaccination.

Risk-Benefit During an Outbreak

Conditions that normally make vaccination risky may become relative rather than absolute contraindications when the risk of smallpox is substantial.

Smallpox Treatment

Medical Countermeasures

  • Tecovirimat is FDA approved for smallpox.
  • Brincidofovir is FDA approved for smallpox.
  • Cidofovir has laboratory and animal activity but is not FDA approved for smallpox.

Evidence Limitation

Because naturally occurring smallpox has been eradicated, these antivirals have not been proven effective in humans who are ill with smallpox. Recommendations during an outbreak would be directed by CDC and public health.

Supportive Care

  • Fluid and electrolyte management
  • Skin and wound care
  • Secondary bacterial-infection treatment
  • Respiratory support
  • Eye evaluation when involved
  • Pain management

Precautions

  • Standard
  • Contact
  • Airborne

Continue Until Every Scab Has Separated and Healthy Skin Remains

Contaminated Materials

Common Clinical Scenarios

Case 1: Painless Black Eschar

A patient presents with a painless forearm lesion, black eschar, and extensive surrounding edema after handling animal hides.

Consider cutaneous anthrax. Cover the lesion, obtain appropriately coordinated lesion and blood testing, notify the laboratory and public health, and begin active therapy.

Case 2: Mediastinal Widening and Shock

Several previously healthy office workers develop fever, dyspnea, pleural effusions, and mediastinal widening.

Consider inhalation anthrax. Begin combination systemic therapy, assess for meningitis, request antitoxin, and activate public-health response.

Case 3: Descending Paralysis

Three people who attended the same gathering develop diplopia, dysphagia, ptosis, and progressive descending weakness.

Call the health department immediately and request botulinum antitoxin. Do not wait for laboratory confirmation.

Case 4: Pneumonia With Bloody Sputum

A patient develops rapidly progressive pneumonia, hemoptysis, shock, and DIC after close contact with a sick animal.

Use Droplet Precautions and begin plague-active antibiotics immediately while obtaining blood and respiratory cultures.

Case 5: Fever Followed by Synchronous Pustules

A patient has severe fever followed by deep pustular lesions involving the face, extremities, palms, and soles.

Immediately use Airborne and Contact Precautions, notify infection prevention and public health, and do not send routine lesion testing without coordination.

Case 6: Pneumonia After Landscaping

A patient develops fever and pneumonia after mowing over dead animals.

Consider pneumonic tularemia. Warn the laboratory, obtain appropriate specimens, and use a current first-line tularemia regimen.

Case 7: Laboratory Exposure

A laboratory worker manipulated a culture later suspected to be F. tularensis outside a biosafety cabinet.

Stop further manipulation, notify biosafety and public health, identify all exposed personnel, and initiate exposure-specific prophylaxis when recommended.

Case 8: Fever After Funeral Exposure

A traveler develops fever, vomiting, diarrhea, and weakness after direct body-fluid contact during a funeral in an active VHF outbreak region.

Isolate immediately using current VHF precautions and contact infection prevention and public health before routine testing.

Case 9: Severe GI Illness After Suspicious Exposure

Several people develop abrupt vomiting, diarrhea, hypotension, and organ injury after a shared suspicious exposure.

Consider both infectious and toxin-mediated causes. Decontaminate when indicated, provide aggressive supportive care, and coordinate testing through public health and poison control.

Case 10: One Negative Test

Initial testing for a suspected high-consequence disease is negative, but the clinical and epidemiologic pattern remains strongly compatible.

Maintain appropriate precautions and public-health coordination until the diagnosis and transmission risk have been adequately resolved.

Case 11: Staff Entered Without PPE

Several staff members provided close care before pneumonic plague was considered.

Create an exposure list immediately. Public health and occupational health should determine who needs seven-day prophylaxis and symptom monitoring.

Case 12: Asymptomatic Person Requests Antibiotics

A person who watched a news report about a suspected event requests ciprofloxacin but has no defined exposure.

Do not provide unindicated prophylaxis. Determine whether the person meets the public-health exposure definition and provide clear monitoring instructions.

Common Pitfalls

  • Waiting for several cases before reporting a highly unusual syndrome
  • Assuming a biological event must be intentional
  • Announcing terrorism before evidence exists
  • Leaving a potentially contagious patient in a common waiting area
  • Using the same precautions for every biological agent
  • Failing to use Droplet Precautions for pneumonic plague
  • Failing to use Airborne and Contact Precautions for possible smallpox
  • Assuming anthrax is transmitted person to person
  • Failing to wash with soap and water after anthrax-spore contact
  • Applying concentrated bleach directly to skin
  • Sending suspicious specimens through a pneumatic tube
  • Failing to notify the laboratory before culture processing
  • Continuing to manipulate a suspicious isolate
  • Collecting environmental samples without authorization
  • Opening, smelling, shaking, or cleaning suspicious powder
  • Delaying botulinum antitoxin for laboratory confirmation
  • Waiting for hypoxemia before intubating a patient with progressive botulism
  • Giving antitoxin without preparing for prolonged ventilatory support
  • Using monotherapy for severe systemic anthrax
  • Failing to assess systemic anthrax for meningitis
  • Failing to request anthrax antitoxin early
  • Using a short antibiotic course after aerosol anthrax exposure
  • Treating severe pneumonic or septicemic plague with an inactive routine pneumonia regimen
  • Stopping plague Droplet Precautions before 48 hours of effective therapy
  • Failing to vaccinate smallpox contacts promptly
  • Assuming smallpox lesions occur in successive crops like varicella
  • Sending possible smallpox specimens through routine workflows
  • Using doxycycline monotherapy for severe delayed tularemia
  • Failing to recognize laboratory aerosol risk from tularemia or brucellosis
  • Assuming dramatic bleeding is required for VHF
  • Performing unnecessary phlebotomy or aerosol procedures in possible VHF
  • Failing to test safely for malaria in a febrile traveler
  • Ignoring ordinary sepsis, malaria, pneumonia, or drug toxicity
  • Providing prophylactic antibiotics to people without a defined exposure
  • Failing to identify exposed healthcare personnel
  • Allowing uncoordinated public or social-media communication
  • Neglecting staff mental health and occupational support

Board and ID Consult Pearls

  • Clinicians may be the first people to recognize a biological emergency.
  • A suspicious event is usually recognized from an unusual syndrome plus an epidemiologic pattern.
  • The clinician does not need to determine whether an event was intentional before notifying public health.
  • Recognize, isolate, stabilize, notify, test safely, and treat.
  • Notify the clinical laboratory before sending specimens from a suspected select-agent infection.
  • The Laboratory Response Network connects sentinel, public-health, and federal laboratories.
  • Do not manipulate or clinically process suspicious environmental materials.
  • Anthrax, botulism, and tularemia generally do not spread person to person.
  • Pneumonic plague requires Standard and Droplet Precautions.
  • Smallpox requires Standard, Contact, and Airborne Precautions.
  • VHF care requires specialized barrier precautions and meticulous PPE processes.
  • Cutaneous anthrax classically produces a painless black eschar with prominent edema.
  • Inhalation anthrax often produces mediastinal widening, adenopathy, and pleural effusions.
  • All systemic anthrax should prompt assessment for meningitis.
  • Systemic anthrax requires combination antimicrobial therapy.
  • Anthrax antitoxin is added for systemic illness and does not replace antibiotics.
  • Anthrax aerosol-exposure prophylaxis requires prolonged antibiotics plus vaccination when indicated.
  • Botulism produces cranial neuropathies followed by symmetric descending paralysis.
  • Mental status and sensation are usually preserved in botulism.
  • Request botulinum antitoxin immediately and do not wait for laboratory confirmation.
  • Botulinum antitoxin prevents progression but does not reverse established paralysis.
  • Pneumonic plague may progress rapidly to hemoptysis, shock, and DIC.
  • Pneumonic and septicemic plague require immediate plague-active therapy.
  • Severe or intentionally acquired plague should initially receive two active antimicrobial classes.
  • Plague treatment is generally continued for 10–14 days.
  • Close unprotected pneumonic-plague contacts may need seven days of prophylaxis.
  • Smallpox fever generally precedes the rash.
  • Smallpox lesions are deep, centrifugal, and synchronous within a body region.
  • Smallpox post-exposure vaccination is most effective within three days.
  • Vaccination within four to seven days may reduce smallpox severity.
  • Smallpox patients remain isolated until all scabs have separated.
  • Tularemia may be ulceroglandular, glandular, oculoglandular, oropharyngeal, pneumonic, or typhoidal.
  • Ciprofloxacin, levofloxacin, gentamicin, and doxycycline are first-line tularemia options under the 2025 CDC guidance.
  • Doxycycline treatment for tularemia is longer than fluoroquinolone or gentamicin therapy.
  • Severe or delayed tularemia should not receive doxycycline monotherapy.
  • Viral hemorrhagic fever suspicion requires both compatible illness and credible epidemiologic exposure.
  • Bleeding is not required for viral hemorrhagic fever.
  • Malaria remains an urgent alternative diagnosis in febrile travelers.
  • Ricin is a noncontagious toxin with no specific antidote.
  • Biological emergency prophylaxis should be distributed according to public-health exposure definitions.
  • Protecting healthcare personnel preserves the ability to care for the entire community.

Bottom Line

Bioterrorism and high-consequence biological events are uncommon, but delayed recognition can cause preventable deaths and healthcare transmission. Clinicians should suspect a biological event when an unusual disease, severe clinical syndrome, unexpected epidemiology, or cluster of similar patients cannot be explained by routine patterns.

The immediate approach is to stabilize the patient, remove the patient from common areas, apply syndrome-appropriate precautions, notify infection prevention and public health, warn the laboratory before specimens are processed, and begin time-sensitive treatment. The clinician does not need to prove intentional release before activating the response.

The major Category A threats are anthrax, botulism, plague, smallpox, tularemia, and selected viral hemorrhagic fevers. Anthrax may cause a painless black eschar, mediastinal disease, pleural effusions, gastrointestinal disease, or hemorrhagic meningitis. Systemic anthrax requires combination antibiotics, assessment for meningitis, and consideration of antitoxin. Aerosol-exposure prophylaxis requires prolonged antibiotics and vaccination under public-health direction.

Botulism causes cranial neuropathies followed by symmetric descending paralysis and respiratory failure. Antitoxin should be requested immediately through public health without waiting for laboratory confirmation. Pneumonic plague requires Droplet Precautions and prompt plague-active antibiotics. Close unprotected contacts may require seven days of prophylaxis.

Smallpox causes a severe febrile prodrome followed by deep, centrifugal lesions that are generally in the same stage within a body region. Possible smallpox requires immediate Standard, Contact, and Airborne Precautions. Post-exposure vaccination is most beneficial within three days and may still reduce severity when given within four to seven days.

Tularemia is a major laboratory hazard and may present with ulceroglandular, pneumonic, or typhoidal disease. Current CDC guidance includes ciprofloxacin, levofloxacin, gentamicin, and doxycycline as first-line options, with treatment selected according to severity and timing. Viral hemorrhagic fevers require credible epidemiologic exposure, immediate specialized isolation, safe testing, aggressive supportive care, and public-health coordination.

The most important practical rule is simple: recognize the unusual pattern, protect the healthcare environment, notify the appropriate systems early, preserve diagnostic safety, and administer effective treatment or prophylaxis before preventable deterioration occurs.

Pleural Fluid Interpretation and Drainage Decision Calculator

Added July 13, 2026 • Pulmonary • Adult bedside diagnostic helper

Classify a pleural effusion with Light’s criteria, check for a diuretic-associated pseudo-exudate, assess whether a parapneumonic effusion needs drainage, and interpret cell differential, chylothorax, hemothorax, tuberculosis, malignancy, pancreatic disease, heart failure, and urinothorax clues.

Clinical safety notice This tool supports clinical interpretation; it does not decide whether thoracentesis or chest-tube placement is technically safe. Use thoracic ultrasound, evaluate bleeding risk and respiratory stability, confirm specimen handling, and involve pulmonary, interventional radiology, thoracic surgery, or another appropriate specialist when needed.

How to Use the Result

Step 1: Classify With Light’s Criteria

Exudate if any one criterion is met
  • Pleural protein ÷ serum protein >0.50
  • Pleural LDH ÷ serum LDH >0.60
  • Pleural LDH > two-thirds of the serum laboratory LDH upper limit of normal

Light’s criteria favor sensitivity: they are designed not to miss exudates. Heart-failure and hepatic-hydrothorax effusions may become concentrated during diuresis and meet an exudative cutoff despite a transudative mechanism.

Step 2: Check for a Pseudo-Exudate

Serum–pleural albumin gradient >1.2 g/dL supports a transudative mechanism
Serum–pleural protein gradient >3.1 g/dL also supports a transudative mechanism

Use these gradients when Light’s criteria indicate an exudate but the clinical picture strongly favors heart failure, cirrhosis, or another transudative process—especially after diuresis. They do not override an incompatible clinical picture.

Step 3: Determine Whether a Parapneumonic Effusion Needs Drainage

Pleural finding Interpretation Typical action
Frank pus or positive Gram stain/culture Empyema / established pleural infection Antibiotics plus drainage when technically feasible
pH ≤7.20 High probability of complicated parapneumonic effusion or infection Insert an intercostal drain if accessible fluid makes this safe
pH >7.20 and <7.40 Intermediate probability Consider drainage when LDH >900 U/L and clinical/imaging features support infection
pH ≥7.40 Low probability based on pH No immediate drain based on chemistry alone
pH unavailable and glucose <60 mg/dL Supports complicated infection in the appropriate syndrome Use with imaging, clinical course, LDH, and microbiology
Pleural pH is highly vulnerable to collection error. Collect nonpurulent fluid anaerobically in a heparinized blood-gas syringe, expel air, avoid contamination with local anesthetic, and analyze promptly. Frank pus does not need a pH measurement to establish drainage-level concern.

Etiology-Specific Interpretation

Finding Important possibilities Nuance
Neutrophil-predominant exudate Parapneumonic effusion, pulmonary embolism, early tuberculosis Cell differential narrows the differential but is not diagnostic
Lymphocyte-predominant exudate Malignancy, tuberculosis, lymphoma, chronic inflammatory disease, chylothorax Use cytology, imaging, ADA, microbiology, biopsy, and exposure history
Eosinophils ≥10% Air or blood in pleural space, drugs, parasites, fungi, malignancy Does not exclude malignancy
Pleural hematocrit ≥50% of serum hematocrit Hemothorax Trauma, surgery, vascular lesion, malignancy, or anticoagulation may be responsible
Triglycerides ≥110 mg/dL or chylomicrons present Chylothorax Chylomicrons are definitive; fluid may not appear milky
Triglycerides 50–109 mg/dL Indeterminate for chylothorax Send chylomicrons/lipoprotein electrophoresis
Cholesterol >200 mg/dL with low triglycerides Pseudochylothorax Often associated with chronic rheumatoid or tuberculous pleuritis
ADA around or above 40 U/L in a lymphocytic exudate Supports tuberculous pleuritis in the right epidemiologic context Not diagnostic; empyema, lymphoma, and other inflammatory disease can elevate ADA
Pleural amylase above serum ULN or pleural/serum ratio >1 Pancreatic disease, esophageal rupture, malignancy Clinical context and isoenzyme testing may be required
Pleural/serum creatinine ratio >1 Urinothorax Usually a transudate with low pH; evaluate urinary obstruction or trauma
Very low glucose Pleural infection, rheumatoid pleuritis, malignancy, tuberculosis Interpret with pH, LDH, imaging, and clinical syndrome

Specimen and Follow-Up Checklist

Routine Diagnostic Thoracentesis

  • Protein and LDH with paired serum studies
  • Cell count and differential
  • Glucose
  • pH when pleural infection is possible
  • Gram stain and culture when infection is possible
  • Cytology when malignancy is possible

When Infection Is Suspected

  • Send fluid in a sterile container and inoculate aerobic and anaerobic blood-culture bottles at the bedside when possible.
  • Do not rely on a negative culture to exclude infection, particularly after antibiotics.
  • Use ultrasound to evaluate septations, accessible fluid, and drain position.
  • If drainage stops while residual infected fluid remains, reassess catheter position, intrapleural therapy, and thoracic-surgery involvement.

When Malignancy Is Suspected

  • Send approximately 25–50 mL for cytology when enough fluid is available.
  • Request smear and cell-block preparation.
  • A positive cytology is diagnostic, but a negative result does not exclude malignancy.
  • Consider image-guided or thoracoscopic pleural biopsy when cytology is nondiagnostic and suspicion remains high.
  • Send flow cytometry when lymphoma is possible.

When Tuberculosis Is Suspected

  • Interpret ADA according to local prevalence and pretest probability.
  • Send mycobacterial culture and evaluate sputum for pulmonary tuberculosis when appropriate.
  • Pleural tissue for histology and culture generally provides better diagnostic yield than pleural fluid alone.

Common Pitfalls

  • Calling an effusion transudative when only one negative Light criterion was available
  • Using pleural-protein concentration alone instead of paired ratios
  • Ignoring the serum LDH laboratory upper limit
  • Assuming every diuresed heart-failure effusion is a true exudate
  • Allowing lidocaine, air, or delayed analysis to distort pleural pH
  • Waiting for culture positivity before draining frank pus
  • Treating pH as a stand-alone decision without imaging and clinical context
  • Calling ADA ≥40 diagnostic of tuberculosis
  • Assuming a negative cytology excludes malignant pleural disease
  • Using right- or left-sided location alone to assign an etiology
  • Assuming milky fluid is always chylous or that nonmilky fluid cannot be chylous
  • Failing to compare pleural hematocrit with serum hematocrit
  • Sending only a superficial or tiny cytology sample when a larger sample is available

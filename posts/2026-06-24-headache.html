Headache: Evaluation, Red Flags, Imaging, and Management

Added June 24, 2026 • Hospital Medicine • ~14 min read

Headache is one of the most common neurologic complaints encountered in medicine. While most headaches are benign primary headache disorders, clinicians must rapidly identify the small number of patients with dangerous secondary causes such as subarachnoid hemorrhage, meningitis, giant cell arteritis, elevated intracranial pressure, or intracranial mass lesions. This guide provides a practical bedside approach to evaluation, imaging decisions, red flags, and management.

Classifying Headaches

The first step in headache evaluation is determining whether the headache is likely primary or secondary.

Category Examples
Primary Headache Migraine, tension headache, cluster headache
Secondary Headache Subarachnoid hemorrhage, meningitis, giant cell arteritis, intracranial mass, elevated intracranial pressure
Most headaches are primary headache disorders, but every patient must be screened for dangerous secondary causes.

Red Flags (SNOOP10)

The SNOOP10 framework is a useful way to identify dangerous headaches.

  • S – Systemic symptoms (fever, weight loss)
  • S – Secondary risk factors (cancer, HIV, immunosuppression)
  • N – Neurologic deficits
  • O – Older age (>50 years)
  • O – Sudden onset (thunderclap)
  • P – Pattern change or progressive headache
  • Positional headache
  • Papilledema
  • Precipitated by exertion or Valsalva
  • Pregnancy or postpartum state
  • Painful eye with autonomic symptoms
  • Post-traumatic headache
  • Pathology of immune system
A sudden thunderclap headache should be considered subarachnoid hemorrhage until proven otherwise.

When Imaging Is Needed

Clinical Scenario Preferred Study
Thunderclap headache Non-contrast CT head
Focal neurologic deficit MRI brain
Papilledema MRI brain
Suspected mass lesion MRI brain
Suspected meningitis LP ± CT first
Typical migraine with normal exam No imaging needed
Routine imaging is not recommended for patients with classic migraine and a normal neurologic examination.

Subarachnoid Hemorrhage

Classic Presentation

  • Sudden thunderclap headache
  • "Worst headache of my life"
  • Peak intensity within seconds
  • Nausea and vomiting
  • Meningismus
  • Loss of consciousness

Evaluation

  1. Non-contrast CT head immediately
  2. If CT negative and suspicion remains high → lumbar puncture
  3. CTA to identify aneurysm
Never dismiss a true thunderclap headache as a migraine without appropriate evaluation.

Giant Cell Arteritis

Any patient older than 50 years with a new headache should be evaluated for giant cell arteritis.

Classic Findings

  • New headache
  • Jaw claudication
  • Visual symptoms
  • Scalp tenderness
  • Elevated ESR
  • Elevated CRP
If giant cell arteritis is suspected, start corticosteroids immediately to prevent irreversible blindness.

Diagnosis

  • ESR
  • CRP
  • Temporal artery ultrasound
  • Temporal artery biopsy

Meningitis

Classic Triad

  • Fever
  • Headache
  • Neck stiffness

The full triad is present in only a minority of patients.

Additional Findings

  • Photophobia
  • Altered mental status
  • Nausea and vomiting
  • Petechial rash
Do not delay antibiotics in suspected bacterial meningitis while waiting for lumbar puncture.

Migraine

Typical Features

  • Unilateral
  • Throbbing or pulsatile quality
  • Moderate to severe intensity
  • Worsened by activity
  • Nausea and vomiting
  • Photophobia
  • Phonophobia

Migraine Aura

  • Visual disturbances
  • Scintillating scotomas
  • Sensory symptoms
  • Speech changes

Acute Treatment

  • NSAIDs
  • Triptans
  • Metoclopramide
  • Prochlorperazine
  • IV fluids

Preventive Therapy

  • Topiramate
  • Propranolol
  • Metoprolol
  • Amitriptyline
  • CGRP inhibitors

Tension Headache

Tension headache is the most common primary headache disorder.

Classic Features

  • Bilateral
  • Band-like pressure
  • Tightness around the head
  • Mild to moderate intensity
  • No significant nausea
  • Not worsened by activity

Treatment

  • NSAIDs
  • Stress reduction
  • Sleep optimization
  • Exercise

Cluster Headache

Cluster headache is among the most painful conditions encountered in neurology.

Classic Features

  • Severe unilateral orbital pain
  • Short attacks (15–180 minutes)
  • Occurs in clusters over weeks
  • Lacrimation
  • Conjunctival injection
  • Nasal congestion
  • Restlessness

Acute Treatment

  • 100% oxygen
  • Subcutaneous sumatriptan

Prevention

  • Verapamil
  • Neurology referral
Migraine patients want a dark quiet room. Cluster headache patients pace the room because of severe pain.

Other Secondary Headaches

Idiopathic Intracranial Hypertension

  • Young obese women
  • Papilledema
  • Transient visual obscurations
  • Pulsatile tinnitus

Brain Tumor

  • Progressive headache
  • Morning headaches
  • Neurologic deficits
  • Seizures

Cerebral Venous Sinus Thrombosis

  • Pregnancy
  • Postpartum state
  • Hypercoagulable disorders
  • Papilledema

Medication Approach

Medication Role
NSAIDs First-line for many primary headaches
Triptans Migraine-specific treatment
Antiemetics Migraine adjunct
Steroids Giant cell arteritis
Oxygen Cluster headache
Medication overuse headache should be considered in patients using headache medications frequently.

Follow-Up and Reassessment

  • Worsening headache frequency
  • Progressive severity
  • New neurologic symptoms
  • Failure of treatment
  • Development of red flags
A headache diagnosis should be revisited whenever the clinical pattern changes significantly.

Clinical Pearls

  • Always screen for red flags before diagnosing migraine.
  • Thunderclap headache is subarachnoid hemorrhage until proven otherwise.
  • New headache after age 50 should trigger evaluation for giant cell arteritis.
  • Jaw claudication is highly suggestive of giant cell arteritis.
  • Do not delay antibiotics in bacterial meningitis.
  • Typical migraine with a normal neurologic exam does not require routine imaging.
  • Cluster headaches respond dramatically to oxygen.
  • Migraine patients lie still; cluster headache patients are restless.
  • Medication overuse can cause chronic headache.
  • Papilledema warrants urgent evaluation for elevated intracranial pressure.
  • Visual symptoms in giant cell arteritis are a medical emergency.
  • Subarachnoid hemorrhage can present with a normal neurologic examination initially.

Bottom Line

The most important step in headache evaluation is identifying dangerous secondary causes. Screen every patient for thunderclap onset, neurologic deficits, fever, papilledema, age over 50 with a new headache, and symptoms of giant cell arteritis. Most headaches are primary disorders such as migraine, tension headache, or cluster headache, but recognizing the small number of life-threatening headaches is the key skill that prevents catastrophic outcomes.

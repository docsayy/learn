HIV Infection: Diagnosis, Initial Evaluation, and Management

Added July 13, 2026 • Infectious Disease • ~20 min read

A high-yield internal medicine review of HIV infection covering diagnostic testing, baseline evaluation, CD4 and viral load interpretation, initial antiretroviral therapy, opportunistic infection prophylaxis, inpatient management, immune reconstitution inflammatory syndrome, and board-style clinical pearls.

Clinical Overview

HIV infection is a chronic viral illness characterized by progressive impairment of cell-mediated immunity. Modern antiretroviral therapy can achieve durable viral suppression, restore immune function, prevent opportunistic infections, and eliminate sexual transmission when viral suppression is maintained.

For the internal medicine resident, HIV management revolves around four questions:

CD4 Count • HIV Viral Load • ART Status • Opportunistic Infection Risk
  • How immunosuppressed is the patient?
  • Is the virus suppressed?
  • Is the patient receiving a complete and effective ART regimen?
  • Does the patient have symptoms or CD4 thresholds suggesting an opportunistic infection?
Resident Pearl: Do not manage HIV by CD4 count alone. CD4 reflects immune function, while viral load reflects active viral replication and response to treatment.

Key Definitions

Term Definition Clinical Importance
HIV infection Infection with human immunodeficiency virus Requires lifelong antiretroviral therapy
AIDS CD4 count <200 cells/µL or an AIDS-defining illness Signals advanced immunosuppression
Virologic suppression HIV RNA below the assay detection threshold Primary goal of ART
Viral blip Isolated low-level detectable HIV RNA followed by resuppression Usually not treatment failure
Virologic failure Persistent inability to achieve or maintain suppression Requires adherence, interaction, and resistance evaluation
IRIS Inflammatory worsening after immune recovery with ART May unmask or worsen an opportunistic infection

HIV Screening

Routine opt-out HIV screening should be incorporated into general medical care. Repeat testing is appropriate when ongoing exposure risk is present.

Who Should Be Tested?

  • All adolescents and adults at least once
  • Patients with ongoing sexual or injection-related risk
  • All pregnant patients early in pregnancy
  • Patients with another sexually transmitted infection
  • Patients with tuberculosis, hepatitis B, or hepatitis C
  • Patients with unexplained weight loss, lymphadenopathy, cytopenias, or recurrent infections
  • Patients with an AIDS-defining or unusual opportunistic infection
HIV testing should be offered whenever the diagnosis would change the differential, treatment plan, or infection-control counseling.

HIV Diagnostic Algorithm

Step Test Interpretation
1 Fourth-generation HIV-1/2 antigen-antibody assay Detects p24 antigen and HIV-1/2 antibodies
2 HIV-1/HIV-2 antibody differentiation assay Confirms infection and differentiates HIV-1 from HIV-2
3 HIV-1 RNA nucleic acid testing Used when differentiation testing is negative or indeterminate, or acute infection is suspected

How to Interpret Common Results

Result Interpretation Next Step
Fourth-generation negative No laboratory evidence of HIV Order HIV RNA if acute infection is strongly suspected
Fourth-generation positive, differentiation positive Confirmed HIV infection Baseline evaluation and ART
Fourth-generation positive, differentiation negative Possible acute HIV or false-positive screen Order HIV-1 RNA
Fourth-generation negative, HIV RNA positive Acute HIV infection Start ART promptly
Board Pearl: A reactive fourth-generation test followed by a negative differentiation assay requires HIV RNA testing.

Acute HIV Infection

Acute HIV often resembles infectious mononucleosis, influenza, or another nonspecific viral syndrome. Symptoms typically occur several weeks after exposure.

Common Manifestations

  • Fever
  • Pharyngitis
  • Diffuse maculopapular rash
  • Lymphadenopathy
  • Myalgias and arthralgias
  • Diarrhea
  • Oral or genital ulcers
  • Aseptic meningitis

Diagnostic Clue

Compatible Syndrome + Recent Exposure + Negative Antibody Test = Check HIV RNA

ART should be initiated promptly in acute HIV because viral burden is high, transmission risk is elevated, and early therapy preserves immune function.

Baseline Evaluation After Diagnosis

The baseline evaluation should determine the degree of immune suppression, identify coinfections, assess organ function, detect transmitted resistance, and identify contraindications to specific ART regimens.

Test Purpose
CD4 count and percentage Stages immune function and guides OI prophylaxis
HIV viral load Measures baseline viral replication
Genotypic resistance testing Detects transmitted resistance
CBC with differential Evaluates anemia, leukopenia, and thrombocytopenia
Comprehensive metabolic panel Assesses kidney and liver function
Urinalysis and urine protein Evaluates renal disease and possible HIVAN
Hepatitis B serologies Guides HBV-active ART selection
Hepatitis C antibody with reflex RNA Identifies active HCV coinfection
Hepatitis A immunity Determines vaccination need
TB screening Detects latent or active tuberculosis risk
Toxoplasma IgG Determines need for prophylaxis at CD4 <100
Syphilis and site-specific STI testing Identifies common coinfections
Pregnancy testing when applicable Guides regimen and perinatal management
HLA-B*57:01 Required before abacavir
G6PD level Required before dapsone or primaquine
Lipid panel and A1c Establishes cardiometabolic baseline
Do not delay ART solely while waiting for every baseline result. Send resistance testing first, then use a high-barrier regimen when rapid treatment is appropriate.

CD4 Count Guide

CD4 Count Clinical Associations Important Actions
>500 cells/µL Near-normal cellular immunity Routine HIV management
<350 cells/µL Increased TB, zoster, bacterial pneumonia, and HPV disease Assess vaccines and latent TB
<200 cells/µL PCP and esophageal candidiasis Start PCP prophylaxis
<100 cells/µL Toxoplasmosis, cryptococcosis, PML Toxoplasma prophylaxis if IgG positive; evaluate for cryptococcal disease when indicated
<50 cells/µL CMV retinitis, disseminated MAC, primary CNS lymphoma Ask about vision symptoms and assess need for MAC prophylaxis
Classic Board Thresholds: CD4 <200 = PCP. CD4 <100 = toxoplasmosis and cryptococcus. CD4 <50 = CMV and MAC.

Important CD4 Nuance

CD4 count may transiently fall during acute illness, sepsis, corticosteroid exposure, or physiologic stress. Use the clinical context and CD4 percentage when a result seems discordant.

HIV Viral Load

HIV viral load is the best marker of treatment response. The goal of ART is durable virologic suppression.

Monitoring Strategy

  • Obtain before ART initiation
  • Repeat after starting or changing therapy
  • Continue every several weeks until suppressed
  • Once stable, monitor every 3–6 months

Persistent Viremia: Think About

  • Missed doses
  • Medication access
  • Drug-drug interactions
  • Drug-food interactions
  • Malabsorption
  • Incorrect dosing
  • Resistance
  • Incomplete regimen
The most common cause of virologic failure is nonadherence, not resistance.

When to Start ART

ART is recommended for every patient with HIV regardless of CD4 count. For most patients, treatment should begin immediately or as soon as possible.

Start ART Promptly

  • Newly diagnosed HIV without a major untreated CNS infection
  • Acute HIV
  • Pregnancy
  • Hepatitis B coinfection using an HBV-active regimen
  • Most opportunistic infections after OI treatment has begun

Important Timing Exceptions

Condition Recommended ART Timing Reason
Cryptococcal meningitis Usually delay 4–6 weeks Early ART increases severe CNS IRIS and mortality
TB meningitis Delay initially High risk of neurologic IRIS
Non-CNS tuberculosis with CD4 <50 Start within approximately 2 weeks Reduces mortality despite increased IRIS risk
Non-CNS tuberculosis with CD4 ≥50 Start within approximately 8 weeks Balances benefit and IRIS risk
PCP Start within 2 weeks Early ART improves outcomes
Most Tested Exception: Do not start ART immediately in cryptococcal meningitis.

Initial ART Regimens

Most patients should receive an integrase strand transfer inhibitor combined with two nucleoside reverse transcriptase inhibitors.

Regimen Advantages Important Limitations
Bictegravir/TAF/FTC (Emtricitabine) Single tablet, high resistance barrier, well tolerated Check renal function and polyvalent cation use
Dolutegravir + TAF/FTC High resistance barrier, flexible regimen Cation interaction; monitor weight and metabolic effects
Dolutegravir + TDF/FTC Effective and HBV active Avoid or use caution in CKD and osteoporosis
Dolutegravir/lamivudine Two-drug single-tablet option Not for HBV coinfection, very high viral load, unknown resistance, or rapid start before key labs
Boosted darunavir + two NRTIs Very high resistance barrier Many drug interactions and metabolic effects

TAF versus TDF

Feature TAF (Tenofovir Alafenamide) TDF (Tenofovir Disoproxil Fumarate)
Renal toxicity Lower Higher proximal tubular toxicity risk
Bone effects Less bone loss Greater reduction in bone mineral density
Lipid effects May raise lipids more More lipid-lowering effect
Weight May be associated with more weight gain Less weight gain
HBV activity Active Active

When a Two-Drug Regimen Is Not Appropriate

  • Hepatitis B coinfection
  • Known lamivudine resistance
  • Very high baseline viral load
  • Suspected opportunistic infection requiring immediate treatment decisions
  • Rapid ART start before HBV and resistance results are available

ART Classes and Major Toxicities

Class Examples High-Yield Toxicities and Pearls
INSTIs (Integrase strand transfer inhibitors) Bictegravir, dolutegravir, raltegravir, cabotegravir Preferred first-line class; cation interactions; possible weight gain
NRTIs (Nucleoside/Nucleotide Reverse Transcriptase Inhibitors) Tenofovir, emtricitabine, lamivudine, abacavir Tenofovir is HBV active; abacavir requires HLA-B*57:01
NNRTIs (Non-nucleoside reverse transcriptase inhibitors) Efavirenz, rilpivirine, doravirine Resistance and interaction concerns; efavirenz causes neuropsychiatric effects
Protease inhibitors Darunavir, atazanavir CYP interactions, dyslipidemia, insulin resistance; high resistance barrier
CCR5 antagonist Maraviroc Requires tropism testing
Fusion inhibitor Enfuvirtide Injection-site reactions
Capsid inhibitor Lenacapavir Used for multidrug-resistant HIV under specialist management

Classic Drug Toxicities

Drug Classic Toxicity
Abacavir Hypersensitivity reaction; avoid if HLA-B*57:01 positive
TDF Proximal tubular injury and bone mineral loss
Zidovudine Macrocytic anemia and myopathy
Efavirenz Vivid dreams, mood changes, CNS effects
Atazanavir Indirect hyperbilirubinemia and nephrolithiasis
Boosted protease inhibitors Metabolic effects and major CYP3A interactions

Important Drug Interactions

Polyvalent Cations

Calcium, iron, magnesium, aluminum, antacids, and multivitamins can reduce INSTI absorption. Separate administration according to the specific product instructions.

Rifamycins

Rifampin strongly induces drug metabolism and interacts with many ART agents. TB and HIV treatment should be coordinated with infectious disease and pharmacy.

Acid Suppression

  • Rilpivirine requires gastric acidity
  • Atazanavir absorption is reduced by acid suppression
  • Review PPI and H2-blocker use carefully

Boosted Protease Inhibitors

Ritonavir- and cobicistat-boosted regimens can interact with:

  • Statins
  • Direct oral anticoagulants
  • Antiarrhythmics
  • Antiepileptic medications
  • Antipsychotics
  • Inhaled and systemic corticosteroids
Avoid simvastatin and lovastatin with boosted protease inhibitors because of severe toxicity risk.

Opportunistic Infection Prophylaxis

Indication Prevented Infection Preferred Regimen Discontinuation
CD4 <200 cells/µL or oropharyngeal candidiasis PCP TMP-SMX DS 1 tablet daily CD4 >200 for at least 3 months on suppressive ART
CD4 <100 and Toxoplasma IgG positive Toxoplasma encephalitis TMP-SMX DS 1 tablet daily CD4 >200 for at least 3 months on suppressive ART
CD4 <50 and no immediate effective ART Disseminated MAC Azithromycin 1200 mg weekly After effective ART and immune recovery
Latent TB infection Active tuberculosis Interaction-adjusted latent TB regimen After completion of therapy

PCP Prophylaxis Alternatives

  • Dapsone after confirming normal G6PD
  • Atovaquone
  • Aerosolized pentamidine
TMP-SMX is preferred because it prevents PCP and toxoplasmosis and provides additional protection against some bacterial infections.

Important MAC Nuance

Primary MAC prophylaxis is generally not required if effective ART will be started immediately. Before starting azithromycin prophylaxis, exclude active disseminated MAC if compatible symptoms are present.

Vaccinations in HIV

Vaccine Recommendation High-Yield Note
Influenza Annually Use inactivated or recombinant vaccine
COVID-19 Per current immunocompromised schedule Advanced HIV may require additional doses
Pneumococcal PCV20/21 alone or PCV15 followed by PPSV23 Follow current adult immunization guidance
Hepatitis B Vaccinate if nonimmune Confirm post-vaccination anti-HBs response
Hepatitis A Vaccinate when indicated and nonimmune Especially important with chronic liver disease
HPV Routine through age 26; shared decision-making through age 45 Use a 3-dose series in immunocompromised patients
Tdap/Td Tdap once, then Td or Tdap every 10 years Follow routine adult schedule
Recombinant zoster Recommended for adults with HIV Non-live vaccine
MMR If nonimmune and CD4 ≥200 Live vaccine; avoid with severe immunosuppression
Varicella If nonimmune and CD4 ≥200 Live vaccine; avoid with severe immunosuppression
Live Vaccine Rule: MMR and varicella are generally avoided when CD4 count is below 200 cells/µL.

Approach to the Hospitalized Patient with HIV

Admission Checklist

  • Confirm the exact ART regimen and last dose
  • Determine adherence and access barriers
  • Review the most recent CD4 count and viral load
  • Check whether OI prophylaxis is indicated
  • Screen for headache, dyspnea, diarrhea, weight loss, visual symptoms, and neurologic deficits
  • Review renal and hepatic dosing
  • Check all medication interactions
  • Avoid incomplete ART regimens
  • Continue established ART unless there is a specific contraindication

Do Not Stop ART Casually

Abrupt interruption may cause viral rebound, resistance, HBV flare in coinfected patients, and progression of immune dysfunction.

If the patient cannot take oral medication, consult infectious disease and pharmacy rather than administering only part of the regimen.

When to Obtain Infectious Disease Consultation

  • New AIDS diagnosis with an opportunistic infection
  • Suspected cryptococcal or TB meningitis
  • Virologic failure or resistance
  • Complex drug interactions
  • Pregnancy
  • Severe IRIS
  • Multidrug-resistant HIV

Fever in Advanced HIV

Clinical Pattern Major Differential Initial Evaluation
Fever + headache Cryptococcus, TB meningitis, toxoplasmosis, CNS lymphoma Neuro exam, serum CrAg, imaging when indicated, LP with opening pressure
Fever + hypoxemia PCP, bacterial pneumonia, TB, fungal pneumonia Chest imaging, beta-D-glucan, sputum testing, ABG if severe
Fever + chronic diarrhea Cryptosporidium, CMV, MAC, bacterial or parasitic disease Stool studies, imaging, endoscopy when needed
Fever + visual symptoms CMV retinitis Urgent dilated ophthalmologic examination
Fever + weight loss + cytopenias Disseminated MAC, TB, histoplasmosis, lymphoma Blood cultures including AFB, imaging, tissue diagnosis when possible
A low CD4 count broadens the differential but does not eliminate common diagnoses such as bacterial pneumonia, urinary infection, endocarditis, or medication fever.

Immune Reconstitution Inflammatory Syndrome

IRIS is an inflammatory syndrome occurring after ART initiation as recovering immunity responds to an existing pathogen or antigen burden.

Types of IRIS

Type Definition Example
Paradoxical IRIS Previously diagnosed infection worsens after ART Worsening cryptococcal symptoms despite antifungal therapy
Unmasking IRIS Previously occult infection becomes clinically apparent after ART New TB lymphadenitis after ART initiation

Common Associations

  • Tuberculosis
  • Cryptococcosis
  • MAC
  • CMV
  • PML
  • Hepatitis B and hepatitis C

Management

  • Exclude treatment failure, resistance, nonadherence, and a new infection
  • Continue ART in most cases
  • Continue or optimize treatment of the underlying infection
  • Use NSAIDs for mild inflammatory symptoms when appropriate
  • Use corticosteroids for severe IRIS after excluding uncontrolled infection
  • Manage CNS IRIS with specialist input
Stopping ART is rarely necessary and is reserved for life-threatening IRIS or situations where continued therapy creates greater immediate harm.

Approach to Virologic Failure

Stepwise Evaluation

  1. Repeat HIV viral load to confirm persistence
  2. Assess missed doses and refill history
  3. Review food requirements
  4. Review antacids, supplements, and drug interactions
  5. Confirm renal and hepatic dosing
  6. Obtain resistance testing while the patient is taking the failing regimen or soon after discontinuation
  7. Construct a new complete regimen using at least two fully active agents when possible
Do Not Add One Active Drug to a Failing Regimen

Adding a single active medication to a failing regimen promotes resistance to the new agent. Regimen redesign should be done with an HIV specialist.

Important Coinfections

Hepatitis B

  • Use a regimen containing TAF or TDF plus FTC or lamivudine
  • Do not use lamivudine as the only HBV-active drug
  • Do not stop HBV-active ART without a plan because severe hepatitis flare may occur

Hepatitis C

  • Confirm active infection with HCV RNA
  • Treat with direct-acting antivirals
  • Check drug interactions with ART
  • Assess fibrosis and hepatocellular carcinoma risk

Tuberculosis

  • Always consider active TB before treating latent TB
  • Rifamycins interact with many ART agents
  • ART timing depends on CD4 count and CNS involvement
  • TB-IRIS is common when ART is started with advanced immunosuppression

Long-Term HIV Care

Routine Monitoring

Item Monitoring Approach
HIV viral load After initiation or regimen change, then every 3–6 months once stable
CD4 count More frequently when low; less often after sustained suppression and immune recovery
CBC and CMP Based on regimen, organ function, and comorbidities
Lipids and A1c Baseline and periodically
STI screening Based on sexual practices and risk
Renal assessment Especially with tenofovir or proteinuria
Bone health Assess risk factors; consider DEXA when indicated
Cancer screening Age- and risk-based, including cervical and anal screening when appropriate

Common Noninfectious Complications

  • Accelerated cardiovascular disease
  • Chronic kidney disease and HIV-associated nephropathy
  • Bone mineral loss
  • Metabolic syndrome and weight gain
  • Neurocognitive impairment
  • Depression and substance use disorders
  • HPV-associated malignancy

HIV-Associated Nephropathy

HIVAN classically causes proteinuria, renal dysfunction, enlarged echogenic kidneys, and collapsing focal segmental glomerulosclerosis.

Management

  • Start effective ART
  • Use ACE inhibitor or ARB when appropriate
  • Control blood pressure
  • Exclude medication toxicity and other renal disease

Common Pitfalls

  • Starting ART immediately in cryptococcal meningitis
  • Forgetting PCP prophylaxis when CD4 is below 200
  • Forgetting toxoplasma prophylaxis when CD4 is below 100 and IgG is positive
  • Automatically giving MAC prophylaxis despite immediate effective ART
  • Using dolutegravir/lamivudine in HBV coinfection
  • Prescribing abacavir without HLA-B*57:01 testing
  • Missing cation interactions with integrase inhibitors
  • Stopping HBV-active ART abruptly
  • Calling a single low viral load result treatment failure
  • Administering only part of an ART regimen
  • Missing CMV retinitis in a patient with floaters or vision loss
  • Assuming every fever in AIDS is an opportunistic infection

Clinical Algorithm for Newly Diagnosed HIV

  1. Confirm HIV using the standard laboratory algorithm
  2. Assess for acute illness and symptoms of opportunistic infection
  3. Obtain CD4 count, viral load, genotype, CBC, CMP, hepatitis serologies, TB testing, STI testing, and pregnancy testing when applicable
  4. Look for CNS symptoms before immediate ART initiation
  5. Start a high-barrier INSTI-based regimen in most patients
  6. Provide HBV-active therapy when HBV coinfection is present
  7. Begin OI prophylaxis according to CD4 thresholds
  8. Update vaccinations
  9. Repeat viral load after ART initiation
  10. Address adherence, insurance, pharmacy access, transportation, mental health, and substance use

Board & Inpatient Pearls

  • Fourth-generation testing detects p24 antigen and HIV antibodies
  • Positive screen + negative differentiation assay → HIV RNA
  • AIDS = CD4 <200 or AIDS-defining illness
  • ART is recommended for every patient with HIV
  • Cryptococcal meningitis is the major exception to immediate ART
  • CD4 <200 → PCP prophylaxis
  • CD4 <100 + Toxoplasma IgG positive → toxoplasma prophylaxis
  • CD4 <50 → think CMV and disseminated MAC
  • TMP-SMX prevents both PCP and toxoplasmosis
  • Abacavir requires HLA-B*57:01 testing
  • Tenofovir + FTC or lamivudine provides HBV activity
  • Integrase inhibitors interact with polyvalent cations
  • Rifampin interacts with many ART agents
  • Continue ART during most hospital admissions
  • Do not add one active drug to a failing regimen
  • Undetectable viral load prevents sexual transmission: U=U

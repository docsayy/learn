HIV/AIDS: Diagnosis, Initial Evaluation & Management

Added July 6, 2026 • Infectious Disease • ~22 min read

This is a PGY-3 medicine review of HIV/AIDS focused on what matters on rounds, overnight admissions, clinic follow-up, and board exams: how to diagnose HIV, what labs to order, when to start ART, which opportunistic infections to prevent, and what not to miss in hospitalized patients with advanced AIDS.

Big Picture

HIV is now a chronic manageable disease when diagnosed early and treated with effective antiretroviral therapy. For internal medicine, the key tasks are:

  • Diagnose HIV correctly.
  • Stage immune status using CD4 count.
  • Assess viral replication using HIV viral load.
  • Start ART promptly unless a specific opportunistic infection requires delay.
  • Prevent opportunistic infections based on CD4 count.
  • Recognize complications of advanced AIDS and immune reconstitution.
PGY-3 framing: Every HIV patient should be approached with 3 questions:
1. What is the CD4 count?
2. What is the viral load?
3. Are they on ART and taking it reliably?

Definitions

Term Meaning Clinical Importance
HIV infection Chronic infection with human immunodeficiency virus Can be asymptomatic for years but causes progressive immune dysfunction without ART.
AIDS CD4 <200 cells/µL or AIDS-defining illness High risk for opportunistic infections and malignancy.
CD4 count Marker of immune function Guides OI risk and prophylaxis.
HIV viral load Amount of circulating HIV RNA Measures disease activity and ART response.
Virologic suppression Usually HIV RNA below assay detection Goal of ART and prevents transmission: U=U.

Screening & Diagnosis

Who should be screened?

  • All adults and adolescents at least once using opt-out screening.
  • Repeat screening for patients with ongoing risk.
  • All pregnant patients early in pregnancy.
  • Patients with STI, TB, hepatitis B, hepatitis C, unexplained cytopenias, weight loss, recurrent infections, or opportunistic infections.

HIV diagnostic algorithm

Step Test Interpretation
1 Fourth-generation HIV-1/2 antigen-antibody immunoassay Detects p24 antigen and HIV antibodies. Best first screening test.
2 HIV-1/HIV-2 antibody differentiation assay Confirms and differentiates HIV-1 from HIV-2.
3 HIV-1 RNA nucleic acid test Used when acute HIV is suspected or differentiation assay is negative/indeterminate after positive screen.
Board pearl: Positive fourth-generation test plus negative differentiation assay means possible acute HIV. Next step is HIV RNA testing.

When to suspect acute HIV

  • Fever
  • Pharyngitis
  • Diffuse rash
  • Lymphadenopathy
  • Myalgias
  • Aseptic meningitis
  • Recent high-risk exposure
  • Negative or indeterminate antibody testing with high suspicion

Baseline Evaluation After New HIV Diagnosis

The first visit should answer: how advanced is the disease, is ART safe to start, are there coinfections, and does the patient need opportunistic infection prophylaxis?

Initial labs

Test Why It Matters
CD4 count Stages immune suppression; determines OI prophylaxis.
HIV viral load Baseline disease activity; used to monitor response to ART.
HIV genotype resistance testing Detects transmitted resistance; obtain before or at ART start.
CBC with differential Baseline anemia, leukopenia, thrombocytopenia; medication toxicity monitoring.
CMP Renal and liver function before ART and prophylaxis.
Hepatitis B serologies HBsAg, anti-HBs, anti-HBc; determines need for HBV-active ART.
Hepatitis C antibody with reflex RNA Common coinfection; affects liver management.
HAV IgG Vaccinate if nonimmune and risk factors/chronic liver disease.
TB screening IGRA or TST; HIV increases TB risk.
Toxoplasma IgG If positive and CD4 <100, needs toxoplasma prophylaxis.
Serum cryptococcal antigen Consider when CD4 <100, especially if symptoms or high-prevalence setting.
STI testing Syphilis, gonorrhea, chlamydia, trichomonas when relevant.
Pregnancy test For patients who could become pregnant; affects regimen selection.
G6PD If considering dapsone or primaquine.
HLA-B*57:01 Required before abacavir.
Lipid panel and A1c Baseline cardiometabolic risk.
Urinalysis Baseline renal disease; proteinuria may suggest HIVAN or tenofovir concerns.
Do not delay ART just because every baseline lab is not back. Resistance testing should be sent, but ART can usually be started before results return if using a high-barrier regimen.

CD4 Count Guide

CD4 count predicts the risk of opportunistic infections and determines prophylaxis.

CD4 Count Think About Prophylaxis
>500 Near-normal immune function No OI prophylaxis
<350 TB, zoster, bacterial pneumonia, HPV-related disease No routine CD4-based prophylaxis, but screen for TB/STIs
<200 Pneumocystis jirovecii pneumonia, esophageal candidiasis TMP-SMX for PCP prophylaxis
<100 Toxoplasmosis, cryptococcosis, PML TMP-SMX if Toxo IgG positive; consider serum CrAg screening
<50 CMV retinitis, disseminated MAC, CNS lymphoma MAC prophylaxis only if not starting effective ART promptly or remains viremic
Classic board association: CD4 <200 = PCP. CD4 <100 = toxoplasma and cryptococcus. CD4 <50 = MAC and CMV.

HIV Viral Load

HIV viral load is used to assess disease activity and response to ART.

How to use viral load

  • Obtain at baseline.
  • Repeat about 2–4 weeks after starting or changing ART.
  • Then monitor every 4–8 weeks until suppressed.
  • Once stable and suppressed, monitor every 3–6 months depending on adherence and stability.

Expected response

  • Viral load should fall significantly within weeks of ART.
  • Goal is durable virologic suppression.
  • Persistent viremia suggests nonadherence, resistance, drug interaction, malabsorption, or wrong regimen.
Do not call ART failure after one small viral blip. Repeat testing and assess adherence, missed doses, and interactions.

Approach to a Newly Diagnosed HIV Patient

Initial approach

  1. Confirm diagnosis using the HIV testing algorithm.
  2. Assess symptoms suggesting opportunistic infection: fever, headache, cough, dyspnea, diarrhea, weight loss, visual symptoms, focal neurologic deficits.
  3. Order baseline labs including CD4, viral load, genotype, CBC, CMP, hepatitis serologies, STI testing, TB testing, and pregnancy test if applicable.
  4. Screen for OIs based on symptoms and CD4 count.
  5. Start ART promptly unless a specific infection requires delay.
  6. Start OI prophylaxis if CD4 threshold is met.
  7. Arrange close follow-up within 1–2 weeks if newly started on ART.

Symptoms that should slow down same-day ART

Symptom Concern Why It Matters
Severe headache, photophobia, altered mental status Cryptococcal meningitis or TB meningitis ART may need to be delayed, especially in cryptococcal meningitis.
Focal neurologic deficit or seizure Toxoplasmosis, CNS lymphoma, PML Needs urgent imaging and diagnosis.
Severe dyspnea or hypoxemia PCP, TB, bacterial pneumonia Needs acute treatment; ART timing depends on diagnosis.
Visual floaters or vision loss CMV retinitis Urgent ophthalmology evaluation.
Persistent fever and weight loss TB, MAC, lymphoma, disseminated fungal infection Workup before assuming uncomplicated HIV.

When to Start ART

For most patients, ART should be started immediately or as soon as possible after diagnosis, regardless of CD4 count.

Start ART promptly in most situations

  • New HIV diagnosis without severe untreated OI
  • Pregnancy
  • Acute HIV infection
  • HBV coinfection, using HBV-active ART
  • Most opportunistic infections after treatment is started

Important ART timing exceptions

Condition ART Timing Reason
Cryptococcal meningitis Delay ART about 4–6 weeks after antifungal therapy Early ART increases risk of severe CNS IRIS and mortality.
TB meningitis Delay ART initially, often several weeks High risk of CNS IRIS.
Other TB disease Usually start ART within 2 weeks if CD4 <50; by 8 weeks if CD4 higher Balances mortality benefit and IRIS risk.
PCP Start ART within 2 weeks of PCP diagnosis Early ART improves outcomes.
Most tested exception: Cryptococcal meningitis — do not start ART immediately.

Initial ART Regimens

Most initial regimens use an integrase strand transfer inhibitor plus two nucleoside reverse transcriptase inhibitors.

Preferred initial regimens for most patients

Regimen Components Best Use Important Cautions
Bictegravir/TAF/FTC INSTI + 2 NRTIs Excellent single-tablet first-line option Not for severe renal dysfunction depending on formulation limits; check interactions with polyvalent cations.
Dolutegravir + TAF/FTC INSTI + 2 NRTIs High barrier to resistance Separate from iron, calcium, magnesium, aluminum.
Dolutegravir + TDF/FTC INSTI + 2 NRTIs Good when TDF appropriate Avoid or use caution in CKD or osteoporosis risk.
Dolutegravir/lamivudine Two-drug regimen Select patients with low complexity Do not use if HBV coinfection, high viral load, unknown resistance, or need for rapid start before labs.

Backbone choice: TAF vs TDF

Feature TAF TDF
Renal safety Better More renal tubular toxicity risk
Bone safety Better More bone mineral density loss
Lipids/weight May increase lipids/weight more More lipid-lowering effect
HBV activity Yes Yes

When to avoid certain regimens

Situation Avoid / Be Careful Reason
HBV coinfection Avoid dolutegravir/lamivudine alone Needs two HBV-active agents, usually TAF/TDF + FTC/3TC.
Unknown genotype and high concern for resistance Avoid low-barrier regimens Use high-barrier INSTI or boosted darunavir-based regimen.
Severe CKD Avoid TDF Renal toxicity and dosing limitations.
Abacavir consideration Do not use without HLA-B*57:01 testing Risk of severe hypersensitivity reaction.
Polyvalent cations Separate from INSTIs Calcium, iron, magnesium, aluminum reduce INSTI absorption.

ART Pearls for Medicine Residents

Major ART classes

Class Examples Key Toxicities / Pearls
INSTIs Bictegravir, dolutegravir, raltegravir, cabotegravir First-line class. Watch weight gain and cation interactions.
NRTIs Tenofovir, emtricitabine, lamivudine, abacavir Backbone drugs. Tenofovir covers HBV; abacavir needs HLA-B*57:01.
NNRTIs Efavirenz, rilpivirine, doravirine More interactions/resistance issues; efavirenz causes CNS effects.
Protease inhibitors Darunavir, atazanavir Often boosted; many CYP interactions; high resistance barrier.
Entry/fusion/capsid agents Maraviroc, enfuvirtide, lenacapavir Usually specialist-managed for resistant HIV.

Common interactions

  • INSTIs: separate from calcium, iron, magnesium, aluminum, multivitamins.
  • Boosted protease inhibitors: many CYP3A interactions; check statins, DOACs, antiarrhythmics, anticonvulsants.
  • Rifampin: major interaction with many ART drugs; coordinate with ID/HIV specialist.
  • Acid suppression: affects rilpivirine and atazanavir absorption.
  • HBV coinfection: stopping tenofovir/emtricitabine/lamivudine can cause HBV flare.
Medication reconciliation matters: HIV patients are high risk for drug interactions, especially with rifamycins, antiepileptics, PPIs, statins, anticoagulants, and supplements.

Opportunistic Infection Prophylaxis

Indication Prevent Preferred Prophylaxis When to Stop
CD4 <200 or oropharyngeal candidiasis PCP TMP-SMX DS 1 tab daily CD4 >200 for at least 3 months on ART
CD4 <100 and Toxoplasma IgG positive Toxoplasma encephalitis TMP-SMX DS 1 tab daily CD4 >200 for at least 3 months on ART
CD4 <50 and not starting effective ART promptly Disseminated MAC Azithromycin 1200 mg weekly Once on effective ART with immune recovery
Positive latent TB testing Active TB Latent TB regimen based on interactions After completing LTBI therapy

PCP prophylaxis alternatives

  • Dapsone if G6PD normal
  • Atovaquone
  • Aerosolized pentamidine
Best prophylaxis drug: TMP-SMX covers PCP and toxoplasmosis and also reduces some bacterial infections.

Vaccinations in HIV

Vaccine Recommendation Pearl
Influenza Annually Use inactivated vaccine.
COVID-19 Per current guidance Advanced HIV may need enhanced schedule.
Pneumococcal PCV20 once, or PCV15 followed by PPSV23 Important due to increased pneumococcal risk.
Hepatitis B Vaccinate if nonimmune Check post-vaccine antibody response.
Hepatitis A If risk factors, chronic liver disease, or nonimmune with indication Especially important with liver disease or MSM risk.
HPV Through age 26; consider shared decision-making to 45 Prevents HPV-related malignancy.
Tdap/Td Tdap once, then Td/Tdap every 10 years Routine adult care.
MMR If nonimmune and CD4 >200 Live vaccine; avoid if CD4 <200.
Varicella If nonimmune and CD4 >200 Live vaccine; avoid if CD4 <200.
Recombinant zoster Recommended for eligible adults Non-live vaccine.
Live vaccine rule: Avoid live vaccines when CD4 <200 cells/µL.

Approach to the Hospitalized Patient with HIV

Hospitalized patients may present with known HIV, newly diagnosed HIV, uncontrolled viremia, ART interruption, or an opportunistic infection.

Admission checklist

  • Confirm current ART regimen and last dose.
  • Check adherence and barriers.
  • Check CD4 count if unknown or outdated.
  • Check HIV viral load if unknown, not suppressed, or concern for failure.
  • Review OI prophylaxis needs.
  • Screen for OI symptoms: headache, cough, dyspnea, diarrhea, fever, weight loss, visual symptoms, neurologic deficits.
  • Review drug interactions before starting antibiotics, anticonvulsants, anticoagulants, statins, or TB therapy.
  • Do not stop ART casually.
  • Consult ID for advanced AIDS, OI, ART failure, major interactions, pregnancy, or resistance.

When to continue ART inpatient

  • Most patients already taking ART should continue it.
  • Confirm dosing and renal adjustment.
  • Avoid partial regimens; incomplete ART can drive resistance.
  • If unable to take PO, discuss with ID/pharmacy rather than holding indefinitely.

When to be cautious with ART initiation inpatient

  • Suspected cryptococcal meningitis
  • Suspected TB meningitis
  • Severe undiagnosed CNS process
  • Concern for adherence barriers that make immediate outpatient continuation unlikely
  • Major drug interaction requiring regimen selection help
Inpatient pearl: Starting ART is usually good. Starting ART blindly in an undiagnosed CNS opportunistic infection can be dangerous.

Approach to Fever in AIDS

Fever in AIDS should be evaluated based on CD4 count, symptoms, geography, exposures, and ART status.

Clinical Pattern Think About Initial Workup
Fever + headache Cryptococcus, TB meningitis, toxoplasmosis, CNS lymphoma Neuro exam, serum CrAg, brain imaging if indicated, LP with opening pressure.
Fever + dyspnea/hypoxemia PCP, bacterial pneumonia, TB, fungal pneumonia CXR/CT chest, LDH, beta-D-glucan, sputum studies, ABG if severe.
Fever + chronic diarrhea Cryptosporidium, CMV colitis, MAC, bacterial/parasitic infection Stool studies, AFB if indicated, colonoscopy if severe or persistent.
Fever + visual symptoms CMV retinitis Urgent ophthalmology exam.
Fever + weight loss + anemia Disseminated MAC, TB, lymphoma, histoplasmosis Blood cultures including AFB, imaging, ferritin, LDH, biopsy if nodes/mass.

Immune Reconstitution Inflammatory Syndrome

IRIS is clinical worsening caused by recovering immune response after ART initiation. It is most common in patients with low CD4 counts and high pathogen burden.

Two forms

Type Meaning Example
Paradoxical IRIS Known OI worsens after ART despite appropriate therapy Cryptococcal meningitis symptoms worsen after ART.
Unmasking IRIS Previously occult infection becomes clinically apparent after ART TB lymphadenitis appears after ART.

Common IRIS-associated infections

  • TB
  • Cryptococcal meningitis
  • MAC
  • CMV
  • Progressive multifocal leukoencephalopathy
  • Hepatitis B or C flare

Management

  • Rule out treatment failure, nonadherence, resistance, and new infection.
  • Continue ART in most cases.
  • Treat the underlying opportunistic infection.
  • Use NSAIDs for mild symptoms if appropriate.
  • Use corticosteroids for severe IRIS, especially with CNS disease, airway compromise, or severe inflammatory complications.
  • Consult ID for severe or CNS IRIS.
Exception: Cryptococcal meningitis is the classic scenario where ART is delayed to reduce severe IRIS risk.

Long-Term HIV Care

Routine monitoring

Item Frequency / Timing
HIV viral load After ART start/change, then until suppressed, then every 3–6 months.
CD4 count More often if low; less often once stable and suppressed with high CD4.
CBC/CMP Baseline and periodically depending on regimen and comorbidities.
Lipids/A1c Baseline and periodically for cardiovascular risk.
STI screening Based on risk and exposure.
Hepatitis B/C monitoring Based on serology and coinfection status.
Cancer screening Age/risk appropriate; include cervical and anal cancer screening when indicated.

Comorbidities to remember

  • Cardiovascular disease risk is increased in HIV.
  • Chronic kidney disease can be from HIVAN, tenofovir toxicity, diabetes, hypertension, or other causes.
  • Hepatitis B coinfection requires HBV-active ART.
  • Hepatitis C should be treated.
  • Depression, substance use, housing instability, and adherence barriers directly affect outcomes.
  • HPV-related malignancy risk is increased.

Malignancy screening pearls

  • Cervical cancer screening is essential for patients with a cervix.
  • Anal cancer screening is considered for higher-risk groups depending on local practice and available high-resolution anoscopy.
  • Screen for liver cancer in cirrhosis or chronic HBV per standard recommendations.
  • Kaposi sarcoma, lymphoma, and cervical cancer are AIDS-defining malignancies.

Common Pitfalls

  • Forgetting CD4-based prophylaxis when CD4 is low.
  • Starting ART immediately in cryptococcal meningitis instead of delaying.
  • Using dolutegravir/lamivudine in HBV coinfection without adequate HBV coverage.
  • Not checking HLA-B*57:01 before abacavir.
  • Missing INSTI-cation interactions with iron, calcium, magnesium, aluminum, or multivitamins.
  • Stopping HBV-active ART and causing hepatitis B flare.
  • Calling viral blips treatment failure without repeat testing.
  • Holding ART inpatient without a plan.
  • Not asking about visual symptoms in CD4 <50 patients.
  • Not considering HIV in unexplained thrombocytopenia, weight loss, lymphadenopathy, or recurrent infections.

Board Pearls

1. Fourth-generation HIV test detects p24 antigen and HIV antibodies.
2. Positive screening test with negative differentiation assay requires HIV RNA testing.
3. Start ART immediately in most patients regardless of CD4 count.
4. Delay ART in cryptococcal meningitis, usually about 4–6 weeks.
5. CD4 <200 requires PCP prophylaxis.
6. CD4 <100 plus positive Toxoplasma IgG requires toxoplasma prophylaxis.
7. CD4 <50 raises concern for CMV retinitis and disseminated MAC.
8. TMP-SMX is preferred for PCP prophylaxis and also covers toxoplasmosis.
9. Abacavir requires HLA-B*57:01 testing before use.
10. Tenofovir plus emtricitabine or lamivudine treats both HIV and HBV.
11. Rifampin has major interactions with ART.
12. Live vaccines are generally avoided when CD4 <200.

Clinical Algorithm

New HIV diagnosis

  1. Confirm HIV diagnosis with standard testing algorithm.
  2. Assess symptoms for opportunistic infection, especially headache, dyspnea, fever, diarrhea, visual symptoms, and focal neurologic deficits.
  3. Order baseline labs: CD4, viral load, genotype, CBC, CMP, hepatitis serologies, STI testing, TB screening, pregnancy test if applicable.
  4. Start ART promptly unless concern for cryptococcal meningitis, TB meningitis, or severe undiagnosed CNS OI.
  5. Choose regimen: usually bictegravir/TAF/FTC or dolutegravir plus TAF/FTC or TDF/FTC.
  6. Start OI prophylaxis based on CD4 count.
  7. Vaccinate after reviewing CD4 count and live vaccine safety.
  8. Follow viral load until suppressed.
  9. Reassess adherence and interactions at every visit.

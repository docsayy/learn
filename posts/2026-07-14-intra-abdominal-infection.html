Gastrointestinal and Intra-Abdominal Infections: The Complete Advanced Resident Guide

Added July 14, 2026 • Infectious Disease • ~52 min read

Gastrointestinal and intra-abdominal infections range from self-limited infectious diarrhea to perforated viscus, diffuse peritonitis, biliary sepsis, spontaneous bacterial peritonitis, postoperative anastomotic leak, liver abscess, and infected pancreatic necrosis. Successful management depends on recognizing the anatomic source, identifying sepsis and organ dysfunction, obtaining meaningful cultures, selecting appropriate empiric therapy, and achieving timely procedural or surgical source control.

Clinical Overview

An intra-abdominal infection may remain confined to a hollow viscus or extend into the peritoneal cavity, retroperitoneum, solid organs, biliary system, or postoperative spaces.

The Major Clinical Questions

  1. What is the anatomic source?
  2. Is infection confined or complicated by perforation, abscess, necrosis, or peritonitis?
  3. Is the patient septic or in shock?
  4. Does the patient need immediate operative, endoscopic, or percutaneous source control?
  5. Which organisms are likely from this anatomic site?
  6. Is the infection community acquired or healthcare associated?
  7. Are resistant gram-negative organisms, Enterococcus, MRSA, or Candida plausible?
  8. Can antimicrobial therapy be narrowed or stopped after source control?
Antibiotics Treat Microorganisms; Source Control Treats the Infected Anatomy

Time-Critical Emergencies

  • Perforated viscus with diffuse peritonitis
  • Acute cholangitis with organ dysfunction
  • Ischemic or necrotic bowel
  • Anastomotic leak with sepsis
  • Toxic megacolon
  • Intra-abdominal compartment syndrome
  • Ruptured liver or splenic abscess
  • Neutropenic enterocolitis with perforation or uncontrolled bleeding
  • Infected pancreatic necrosis with progressive organ failure
Do not allow temporary improvement after fluids and antibiotics to delay definitive source control in a patient with perforation, obstruction, infected necrosis, or an undrained abscess.

Classification of Intra-Abdominal Infection

Uncomplicated Infection

Infection or inflammation is confined to the organ of origin without extension into the peritoneal cavity or another normally sterile abdominal space.

Examples

  • Early uncomplicated appendicitis
  • Uncomplicated diverticulitis
  • Acute cholecystitis without perforation

Complicated Intra-Abdominal Infection

A complicated intra-abdominal infection extends beyond the organ of origin into the peritoneal space or another sterile abdominal compartment and may cause peritonitis or abscess formation.

Examples

  • Perforated appendicitis
  • Diverticular abscess
  • Perforated peptic ulcer
  • Anastomotic leak
  • Secondary peritonitis
  • Infected pancreatic necrosis
  • Postoperative intra-abdominal abscess

Community-Acquired Infection

Community-acquired infection develops outside the healthcare environment without major exposure selecting resistant hospital flora.

Healthcare-Associated Infection

Healthcare-associated infection is more likely to involve resistant organisms after:

  • Recent hospitalization
  • Recent abdominal surgery
  • Recent broad-spectrum antibiotics
  • Residence in a facility with substantial healthcare exposure
  • Prolonged hospitalization
  • Known colonization with resistant organisms

Primary, Secondary, and Tertiary Peritonitis

Type Mechanism Typical Example
Primary Infection without a surgically treatable perforated source Spontaneous bacterial peritonitis
Secondary Contamination from perforation, ischemia, leak, or inflamed organ Perforated diverticulitis
Tertiary Persistent or recurrent peritoneal infection after attempted source control Postoperative infection with resistant or low-virulence organisms

Microbiology by Anatomic Source

Source Common Organisms
Stomach and proximal small bowel Streptococci, oral flora, gram-negative bacilli in selected patients, Candida in high-risk perforation
Distal small bowel Enterobacterales, streptococci, increasing anaerobic burden
Colon Enterobacterales, Bacteroides fragilis group, other anaerobes, streptococci
Biliary tract E. coli, Klebsiella, Enterobacter, Enterococcus; anaerobes in selected severe or altered-anatomy disease
Community appendicitis Enterobacterales and anaerobes
Postoperative infection Resistant gram-negative bacilli, Enterococcus, staphylococci, Candida
Spontaneous bacterial peritonitis Enterobacterales, streptococci, enterococci; resistance varies with healthcare exposure
Pyogenic liver abscess Enterobacterales, streptococci, anaerobes, Enterococcus; occasionally monomicrobial hypervirulent Klebsiella
Infected pancreatic necrosis Enteric gram-negative bacilli, gram-positive organisms, anaerobes; Candida in selected patients

Polymicrobial Infection

Colonic perforation and postoperative leaks are commonly polymicrobial. Empiric therapy generally needs reliable activity against:

  • Enterobacterales
  • Streptococci
  • Obligate anaerobes

Organisms Do Not All Require Equal Attention

The significance of an organism depends on:

  • Whether the specimen came from a normally sterile site
  • Whether the sample came from a newly placed or chronic drain
  • Whether source control has occurred
  • The patient’s severity and immune status
  • Whether the organism is a plausible pathogen or likely colonizer

Initial Approach

Step 1: Identify Sepsis and Shock

  • Hypotension
  • Elevated lactate
  • Acute kidney injury
  • Altered mental status
  • Hypoxemia
  • Coagulopathy
  • Oliguria

Step 2: Perform a Focused Abdominal Examination

  • Location of maximal tenderness
  • Guarding or rigidity
  • Rebound tenderness
  • Distention
  • Bowel sounds
  • Hernias
  • Surgical wounds and drains
  • Rectal or pelvic examination when indicated

Step 3: Obtain Initial Laboratory Studies

  • CBC
  • Electrolytes and renal function
  • Liver tests
  • Lipase when pancreatitis is possible
  • Lactate in significant illness
  • Coagulation studies when a procedure is likely
  • Pregnancy testing when relevant
  • Blood cultures when indicated

Step 4: Obtain Appropriate Imaging

Select imaging based on the suspected source rather than ordering a generic test without a clinical question.

Step 5: Start Antibiotics When Infection Is Likely

Administer prompt antibiotics in:

  • Sepsis or shock
  • Perforation
  • Diffuse peritonitis
  • Cholangitis
  • Complicated appendicitis or diverticulitis
  • Postoperative leak
  • SBP
  • Neutropenic enterocolitis

Step 6: Contact the Procedural Team

  • General surgery
  • Gastroenterology or advanced endoscopy
  • Interventional radiology
  • Hepatobiliary surgery
  • Colorectal surgery
  • Critical care
In shock from an intra-abdominal source, resuscitation, antibiotics, and source-control planning should occur in parallel.

Source Control

What Source Control Means

  • Repair or resection of perforated bowel
  • Appendectomy
  • Drainage of an abscess
  • ERCP biliary decompression
  • Cholecystectomy or gallbladder drainage
  • Debridement of infected necrosis
  • Removal of infected foreign material
  • Control of an anastomotic leak

Timing

Sepsis or Shock + Controllable Abdominal Source → Source Control as Soon as Feasible

The appropriate timing depends on:

  • Hemodynamic stability
  • Presence of perforation or ischemia
  • Anatomic accessibility
  • Need for stabilization before anesthesia
  • Availability of surgical, endoscopic, or interventional expertise

Percutaneous vs Operative Drainage

Percutaneous drainage may be appropriate for a well-localized accessible abscess in a patient without generalized peritonitis or another reason for surgery.

When Antibiotics Alone May Be Reasonable

  • Small, well-contained abscess
  • Uncomplicated diverticulitis in selected patients
  • Selected uncomplicated appendicitis
  • No obstruction, necrosis, fistula, or diffuse peritonitis
  • Reliable clinical follow-up

Indicators of Inadequate Source Control

  • Persistent fever
  • Continued vasopressor requirement
  • Rising WBC or lactate
  • Persistent ileus
  • Increasing abdominal pain
  • Ongoing bacteremia
  • Failure to improve after several days of active therapy
  • Persistent high-output enteric drainage
Persistent infection after several days of active antibiotics should prompt reassessment of anatomy and source control—not automatic extension or broadening of antibiotics.

Cultures and Microbiologic Evaluation

Blood Cultures

Obtain blood cultures before antibiotics when feasible in patients with:

  • Septic shock
  • Organ dysfunction
  • Cholangitis
  • Immunocompromise
  • Healthcare-associated infection
  • Concern for resistant organisms
  • Endovascular or metastatic infection concern

Intra-Abdominal Cultures

Obtain cultures during source-control procedures in complicated infections when the result could influence therapy.

Preferred Specimens

  • Aspirated pus
  • Fresh peritoneal fluid
  • Deep tissue
  • Gallbladder or biliary fluid
  • Abscess material

Send for:

  • Aerobic bacterial culture
  • Anaerobic culture
  • Gram stain when useful
  • Fungal culture in selected high-risk patients

Drain Cultures

Cultures from a drain that has been in place for more than approximately 24 hours frequently reflect colonization and may lead to unnecessary broad-spectrum treatment.

Uncomplicated Appendicitis

Routine intra-abdominal cultures are generally unnecessary during appendectomy for uncomplicated appendicitis.

How to Improve Yield

  • Obtain specimens before antibiotics when this does not delay urgent care.
  • Send fluid rather than a superficial swab.
  • Use appropriate anaerobic transport.
  • Inoculate appropriate fluid into culture bottles according to institutional protocol.

Imaging

CT Abdomen and Pelvis

Contrast-enhanced CT is the primary imaging test for many adult intra-abdominal infections, including:

  • Diverticulitis
  • Complicated appendicitis
  • Abscess
  • Perforation
  • Anastomotic leak
  • Infected pancreatic necrosis
  • Secondary peritonitis

Ultrasound

Ultrasound is especially useful for:

  • Acute cholecystitis
  • Biliary dilation
  • Appendicitis in children and pregnancy
  • Ascites and paracentesis guidance
  • Superficial or hepatic collections

MRI

  • Pregnancy when ultrasound is nondiagnostic
  • MRCP for noninvasive biliary imaging
  • Selected liver or pancreatic lesions
  • Patients in whom radiation should be avoided

HIDA Scan

A hepatobiliary scan may help diagnose acute cholecystitis when ultrasound is equivocal and clinical suspicion remains significant.

Imaging in Pregnancy

Ultrasound First → MRI if Nondiagnostic → CT if Necessary for Maternal Care

Do Not Delay Surgery for Unnecessary Imaging

A patient with diffuse peritonitis, shock, or clear perforation may require immediate operative management rather than serial diagnostic studies.

Empiric Antibiotic Selection

Core Coverage

Most complicated infections arising from distal small bowel or colon require coverage for:

  • Enterobacterales
  • Streptococci
  • Anaerobes

Common Community-Acquired Regimens

Regimen Typical Role
Ceftriaxone plus metronidazole Common community-acquired complicated infection without Pseudomonas risk
Amoxicillin-clavulanate Selected stable oral treatment when local susceptibility is adequate
Ertapenem Once-daily broad therapy without Pseudomonas or Enterococcus activity
Ciprofloxacin or levofloxacin plus metronidazole Only when susceptibility is likely and fluoroquinolone risks are acceptable

Severe or Healthcare-Associated Infection

Regimen Typical Role
Piperacillin-tazobactam Broad gram-negative, anaerobic, and selected enterococcal coverage
Cefepime plus metronidazole Antipseudomonal gram-negative coverage plus anaerobic therapy
Meropenem Severe infection with ESBL or substantial resistant gram-negative risk
Aztreonam plus metronidazole plus gram-positive coverage Selected severe beta-lactam allergy situations

Example Adult Doses

  • Ceftriaxone 2 g IV every 24 hours
  • Metronidazole 500 mg IV or orally every 8 hours
  • Cefepime 2 g IV every 8 hours with renal adjustment
  • Piperacillin-tazobactam 4.5 g IV every 6 hours or by an institutional extended-infusion protocol
  • Meropenem 1 g IV every 8 hours with renal adjustment
  • Ertapenem 1 g IV every 24 hours
Doses must be adjusted for renal function, obesity, critical illness, dialysis, allergy, local susceptibility, and infusion strategy.

Anaerobic Coverage

Metronidazole is unnecessary when the selected beta-lactam already provides reliable anaerobic activity, such as:

  • Piperacillin-tazobactam
  • Meropenem
  • Ertapenem
Avoid duplicate anaerobic therapy such as piperacillin-tazobactam plus metronidazole unless a distinct indication exists.

Risk Assessment for Resistant Gram-Negative Organisms

Risk Factors

  • Previous ESBL or carbapenem-resistant organism
  • Recent broad-spectrum antibiotics
  • Recent hospitalization
  • Prolonged ICU stay
  • Recent abdominal surgery
  • Recurrent biliary instrumentation
  • Long-term care exposure
  • Known regional or unit-level resistance

ESBL-Producing Enterobacterales

A carbapenem is generally preferred for severe infection caused by an ESBL-producing organism outside the urinary tract.

Do Not Use Pseudomonas Coverage Automatically

Routine antipseudomonal treatment is unnecessary in many stable community-acquired infections.

De-Escalation

Culture Results + Clinical Response + Source Control → Narrow the Regimen

De-escalate promptly when:

  • Pseudomonas is not isolated
  • ESBL risk is disproven
  • Enterococcus or MRSA coverage is unnecessary
  • Source control is adequate

When to Cover Enterococcus

Routine Community-Acquired Infection

Routine empiric enterococcal coverage is usually unnecessary in lower-risk community-acquired infection.

Consider Coverage With:

  • Healthcare-associated infection
  • Postoperative infection
  • Previous cephalosporin exposure
  • Immunocompromise
  • Severe valvular or intravascular prosthetic material risk
  • Known prior Enterococcus colonization or infection
  • Persistent infection despite appropriate non-enterococcal therapy

Common Active Agents

  • Ampicillin for susceptible E. faecalis
  • Piperacillin-tazobactam
  • Vancomycin
  • Linezolid or daptomycin for selected vancomycin-resistant isolates

Species Matters

E. faecium is substantially more likely than E. faecalis to be resistant to ampicillin and vancomycin.

When to Cover MRSA

Routine empiric MRSA coverage is not required for most community-acquired intra-abdominal infections.

Consider Vancomycin With:

  • Known MRSA colonization or previous infection
  • Healthcare-associated postoperative infection
  • Abdominal wall or surgical-site infection suggesting MRSA
  • Concurrent MRSA bacteremia
  • Severe infection with substantial institutional MRSA risk

Stop Coverage When Unsupported

Discontinue empiric vancomycin when cultures and the clinical syndrome do not support MRSA or resistant enterococcal infection.

Intra-Abdominal Candidiasis

Risk Factors

  • Recent abdominal surgery
  • Recurrent gastrointestinal perforation
  • Anastomotic leak
  • Necrotizing pancreatitis
  • Prolonged broad-spectrum antibiotics
  • Parenteral nutrition
  • Critical illness
  • Persistent sepsis despite antibacterial therapy

Meaningful Specimens

Candida recovered from:

  • Operative specimens
  • Percutaneously aspirated sterile collections
  • Newly placed drains sampling a sterile space

is more clinically meaningful than Candida recovered from a chronic drain.

Empiric Antifungal Therapy

Consider empiric therapy in a severely ill patient with intra-abdominal infection and strong Candida risk factors, particularly after anastomotic leak, recurrent perforation, or necrotizing pancreatitis.

Preferred Initial Therapy in Critical Illness

  • An echinocandin

Fluconazole

Fluconazole may be appropriate for a stable patient with:

  • No recent azole exposure
  • Low likelihood of azole-resistant species
  • A susceptible isolate

Source Control

Antifungal Therapy + Drainage or Debridement

Duration

Duration depends on:

  • Adequacy of source control
  • Clinical response
  • Species and susceptibility
  • Presence of candidemia
  • Residual collections

Antibiotic Duration After Source Control

Complicated Intra-Abdominal Infection

Approximately 4 Days After Adequate Source Control

Short-course treatment is appropriate for many patients after effective operative or percutaneous source control.

Do Not Continue Until Every Marker Normalizes

Fever, ileus, leukocytosis, and inflammatory markers may resolve gradually. Prolonging therapy until every abnormality normalizes increases:

  • Drug toxicity
  • Clostridioides difficile infection
  • Selection of resistant organisms
  • Fungal superinfection

Situations Requiring Individualization

  • Inadequate or impossible source control
  • Persistent bacteremia
  • Endocarditis
  • Infected pancreatic necrosis
  • Liver or splenic abscess
  • Severe immunocompromise
  • Unusual organisms
  • Ongoing intestinal leak

Persistent Illness After Four to Seven Days

Reimage or reassess the source. Do not simply extend broad-spectrum antibiotics without investigating an undrained abscess, leak, obstruction, ischemia, or infected device.

Oral Step-Down Therapy

Requirements

  • Hemodynamic stability
  • Improving clinical condition
  • Adequate source control
  • Functional gastrointestinal absorption
  • Susceptible organisms or predictable microbiology
  • A defined short treatment endpoint

Potential Options

  • Amoxicillin-clavulanate
  • Ciprofloxacin plus metronidazole when susceptibility and patient-specific safety permit
  • Levofloxacin plus metronidazole when appropriate
  • Other culture-directed oral regimens

Fluoroquinolone Caution

  • Resistance may be substantial.
  • QT prolongation
  • Tendinopathy
  • Peripheral neuropathy
  • Dysglycemia
  • Aortic and CNS adverse effects
Do not place a PICC solely because the patient initially had a complicated abdominal infection. Many patients can stop therapy or complete a brief oral course after source control.

Infectious Diarrhea

Clinical Categories

  • Acute watery diarrhea
  • Inflammatory or bloody diarrhea
  • Persistent diarrhea lasting at least 14 days
  • Healthcare-associated diarrhea
  • Traveler’s diarrhea
  • Diarrhea in an immunocompromised host

Initial Priorities

  • Assess hydration and hemodynamics.
  • Review stool frequency and blood.
  • Review travel, food, water, animal, and outbreak exposure.
  • Review recent antibiotics and healthcare exposure.
  • Assess immune status.
  • Determine whether stool testing is needed.

Supportive Care

Oral Rehydration Is the Foundation of Most Acute Diarrhea Treatment
  • Use oral rehydration solution when feasible.
  • Use IV isotonic fluid for shock, severe dehydration, or inability to drink.
  • Replace potassium, magnesium, and bicarbonate losses.
  • Continue age-appropriate or regular nutrition as tolerated.

Inflammatory Features

  • Fever
  • Bloody stool
  • Severe abdominal pain
  • Tenesmus
  • Fecal inflammatory markers

When to Order Stool Testing

Test When There Is:

  • Bloody diarrhea
  • Fever with significant diarrhea
  • Severe abdominal pain
  • Sepsis
  • Immunocompromise
  • Persistent diarrhea
  • Outbreak concern
  • High-risk food or water exposure
  • Public-health or occupational implications

Potential Pathogens to Evaluate

  • Salmonella
  • Shigella
  • Campylobacter
  • Yersinia
  • Shiga toxin-producing E. coli
  • Clostridioides difficile when appropriate
  • Vibrio with compatible seafood or water exposure

Blood Cultures

Obtain blood cultures with:

  • Sepsis
  • Suspected enteric fever
  • Immunocompromise
  • High fever with systemic toxicity
  • Concern for invasive Salmonella or another bacteremic pathogen

Persistent Diarrhea

When diarrhea lasts at least 14 days, consider:

  • Giardia
  • Cryptosporidium
  • Cyclospora
  • Cystoisospora
  • Entamoeba histolytica
  • Microsporidia in advanced immune suppression
  • Noninfectious inflammatory or malabsorptive disease

Multiplex Gastrointestinal PCR Panels

Advantages

  • Rapid detection
  • Broad pathogen coverage
  • Useful in severe disease or immunocompromise
  • May identify pathogens not included in routine culture

Limitations

  • May detect colonization or prolonged shedding
  • May detect more than one organism
  • Does not always provide susceptibility testing
  • Can lead to treatment of clinically irrelevant organisms
  • Cost

Culture Confirmation

Culture may still be required for:

  • Antimicrobial susceptibility
  • Outbreak investigation
  • Public-health reporting
  • Serotyping
A multiplex result should be interpreted with the symptom pattern and exposure history. Detection does not always equal causation.

Empiric Treatment of Infectious Diarrhea

Most Watery Diarrhea

Routine empiric antibiotics are not indicated for most acute watery diarrhea without international travel, sepsis, or major immune compromise.

Bloody Diarrhea

Avoid routine empiric antibiotics in an immunocompetent adult while results are pending, except in selected circumstances such as:

  • Severe dysentery strongly suggestive of Shigella
  • Recent international travel with high fever or sepsis
  • Severe illness in an immunocompromised patient
  • Suspected enteric fever

Potential Empiric Agents

  • Azithromycin
  • Ciprofloxacin when local resistance and travel history support it
  • Ceftriaxone for severe invasive illness or enteric fever

Antimotility Agents

Avoid loperamide when there is:

  • Bloody diarrhea
  • High fever
  • Suspected STEC
  • Toxic colitis
  • Severe inflammatory diarrhea

Shiga Toxin-Producing E. coli

Clinical Clues

  • Severe abdominal cramping
  • Bloody diarrhea
  • Little or no fever
  • Undercooked beef
  • Leafy greens or contaminated produce
  • Unpasteurized products
  • Farm or animal exposure
  • Outbreak setting

Major Complication

Shiga Toxin Infection → Hemolytic Uremic Syndrome Risk

Avoid:

  • Antibiotics
  • Loperamide and other antimotility agents

These interventions may increase the risk of hemolytic uremic syndrome in STEC infection.

Monitor For:

  • Falling hemoglobin
  • Thrombocytopenia
  • Rising creatinine
  • Hemolysis
  • Neurologic symptoms

Supportive Care

  • Adequate hydration
  • Renal and hematologic monitoring
  • Early nephrology involvement when HUS develops

Nontyphoidal Salmonella

Typical Disease

  • Fever
  • Diarrhea
  • Abdominal cramps
  • Usually self-limited gastroenteritis

Most Healthy Adults

Do not routinely treat uncomplicated gastroenteritis because antibiotics may not shorten illness and can prolong carriage.

Treat Higher-Risk Patients

  • Severe disease
  • Bacteremia
  • Advanced age with atherosclerotic vascular disease
  • Immunocompromise
  • Hemoglobinopathy
  • Prosthetic vascular material
  • Selected prosthetic-joint or endovascular risk

Potential Agents

  • Ceftriaxone
  • Azithromycin
  • Fluoroquinolone when susceptible

Metastatic Infection

Evaluate persistent bacteremia for:

  • Endovascular infection
  • Mycotic aneurysm
  • Osteomyelitis
  • Septic arthritis
  • Visceral abscess

Shigella

Clinical Features

  • Fever
  • Abdominal cramps
  • Tenesmus
  • Frequent small-volume stools
  • Blood or mucus

Transmission

  • Very low infectious dose
  • Person-to-person spread
  • Childcare outbreaks
  • Sexual transmission
  • Food and water exposure

Who to Treat

  • Severe disease
  • Immunocompromised patient
  • Bacteremia
  • Public-health or transmission-control indication

Potential Treatment

  • Azithromycin
  • Ceftriaxone
  • Ciprofloxacin when susceptible
Shigella antimicrobial resistance is increasingly common. Obtain culture and susceptibility testing when treatment is planned or symptoms persist.

Campylobacter Enteritis

Exposure

  • Undercooked poultry
  • Unpasteurized milk
  • Contaminated water
  • Animal exposure

Clinical Features

  • Fever
  • Severe abdominal pain
  • Watery or bloody diarrhea
  • May mimic appendicitis

Treatment

Most cases are self-limited. Treat:

  • Severe disease
  • Prolonged symptoms
  • Pregnancy
  • Immunocompromise
  • Extraintestinal infection

Preferred Agent

  • Azithromycin

Postinfectious Complications

  • Guillain–Barré syndrome
  • Reactive arthritis
  • Postinfectious irritable bowel syndrome

Yersinia Enterocolitica

Clinical Features

  • Fever
  • Diarrhea
  • Terminal ileitis
  • Mesenteric adenitis
  • Right-lower-quadrant pain mimicking appendicitis

Exposure

  • Undercooked pork
  • Chitterlings
  • Contaminated food or water

Severe Systemic Disease Risk

  • Iron overload
  • Deferoxamine therapy
  • Chronic liver disease
  • Immunocompromise

Treatment

Uncomplicated enteritis is usually supportive. Treat severe or invasive disease with susceptibility-guided therapy.

Vibrio Infections

Vibrio Parahaemolyticus

  • Raw or undercooked seafood
  • Watery diarrhea
  • Usually supportive treatment

Vibrio Cholerae

  • Profuse watery diarrhea
  • Rapid dehydration
  • Travel or outbreak exposure
  • Rehydration is the primary treatment
  • Antibiotics may shorten severe disease

Vibrio Vulnificus

Risk Factors

  • Cirrhosis
  • Iron overload
  • Immunocompromise
  • Raw oyster exposure
  • Seawater wound exposure

Clinical Syndromes

  • Rapid septicemia
  • Hemorrhagic bullae
  • Necrotizing soft-tissue infection
  • High mortality

Treatment

  • Immediate active antibiotics
  • Commonly doxycycline plus a third-generation cephalosporin
  • Emergency surgical debridement for necrotizing infection

Enteric Fever

Organisms

  • Salmonella Typhi
  • Salmonella Paratyphi

Clinical Features

  • Persistent fever
  • Headache
  • Abdominal symptoms
  • Constipation or diarrhea
  • Relative bradycardia may occur
  • Hepatosplenomegaly
  • Rose spots
  • Encephalopathy in severe disease

Diagnosis

  • Blood cultures
  • Stool cultures
  • Bone-marrow culture in selected difficult cases
  • Susceptibility testing

Treatment

Selection depends heavily on travel geography and resistance:

  • Ceftriaxone
  • Azithromycin
  • Carbapenem for selected extensively resistant severe disease

Complications

  • Intestinal bleeding
  • Ileal perforation
  • Encephalopathy
  • Relapse
  • Chronic gallbladder carriage

Public Health

Notify public health and follow occupational restrictions for food handlers, healthcare personnel, and childcare workers according to jurisdictional guidance.

Selected Protozoal Infections

Giardiasis

  • Foul-smelling greasy diarrhea
  • Bloating
  • Weight loss
  • Untreated water or daycare exposure
  • Treat with tinidazole, metronidazole, or another active agent

Cryptosporidiosis

  • Watery diarrhea
  • Swimming-pool or water exposure
  • Severe prolonged disease in advanced HIV
  • Supportive care and immune restoration are central
  • Nitazoxanide may benefit immunocompetent patients

Cyclospora

  • Prolonged relapsing watery diarrhea
  • Fresh produce outbreaks
  • TMP-SMX is preferred treatment

Entamoeba Histolytica

  • Bloody diarrhea
  • Colitis
  • Liver abscess
Tissue-Active Agent Followed by a Luminal Agent

Metronidazole or tinidazole must be followed by a luminal agent such as paromomycin because tissue-active treatment alone does not eradicate intraluminal cysts.

Acute Appendicitis

Clinical Features

  • Periumbilical pain migrating to the right lower quadrant
  • Anorexia
  • Nausea or vomiting
  • Fever
  • Localized tenderness
  • Guarding with perforation

Imaging

  • CT is highly accurate in nonpregnant adults.
  • Ultrasound is commonly first line in children and pregnancy.
  • MRI is useful in pregnancy after nondiagnostic ultrasound.

Appendectomy

Laparoscopic appendectomy remains definitive treatment for most patients.

Preoperative Antibiotics

  • Give a single preoperative dose active against enteric gram-negative organisms and anaerobes.
  • Administer before incision.

Uncomplicated Appendicitis

After successful appendectomy for uncomplicated disease:

No Postoperative Antibiotics

Antibiotics-First Management

Nonoperative treatment may be discussed with selected stable adults who have imaging-confirmed uncomplicated appendicitis.

Important Counseling

  • Risk of treatment failure
  • Risk of recurrence
  • Need for later appendectomy
  • Need for close follow-up
  • Greater complication risk with an appendicolith

Appendicolith

An appendicolith increases the risk of failure and complications during antibiotics-first management.

Perforated Appendicitis

  • Appendectomy or drainage/source control
  • Broad gram-negative and anaerobic therapy
  • Short postoperative course after adequate source control

Appendiceal Abscess or Phlegmon

Potential management includes:

  • Antibiotics
  • Percutaneous drainage when accessible
  • Early surgery in selected patients and experienced centers
  • Interval evaluation for malignancy in older adults or atypical disease

Acute Diverticulitis

Uncomplicated Diverticulitis

Inflammation remains localized without:

  • Abscess
  • Fistula
  • Obstruction
  • Free perforation
  • Diffuse peritonitis

Complicated Diverticulitis

  • Abscess
  • Perforation
  • Fistula
  • Obstruction
  • Diffuse peritonitis

Diagnosis

CT is particularly important:

  • During a first or uncertain episode
  • When severe disease is suspected
  • When the patient is immunocompromised
  • When symptoms fail to improve
  • Before procedural planning

Antibiotics Are Selective in Uncomplicated Disease

Mild CT-confirmed uncomplicated diverticulitis may be managed without antibiotics in a stable immunocompetent patient with reliable follow-up.

Use Antibiotics With:

  • Immunocompromise
  • Systemic inflammatory response
  • Sepsis
  • Refractory symptoms
  • Vomiting or inability to maintain intake
  • Marked leukocytosis or inflammatory markers
  • Fluid collection
  • Long inflamed colonic segment
  • Significant comorbidity or frailty

Outpatient Options

  • Amoxicillin-clavulanate
  • A fluoroquinolone plus metronidazole only when appropriate

Diverticular Abscess

  • Small abscesses may respond to antibiotics alone.
  • Larger abscesses generally require percutaneous drainage when feasible.
  • Clinical deterioration or inability to drain may require surgery.

Diffuse Peritonitis

Free perforation with diffuse peritonitis requires emergency surgical evaluation and broad antimicrobial therapy.

Colonoscopy After Diverticulitis

Colonic evaluation is generally appropriate after:

  • Complicated diverticulitis
  • A first episode when colorectal cancer has not recently been excluded
  • Atypical imaging or persistent symptoms

Colonoscopy is usually delayed until acute inflammation resolves, commonly about six to eight weeks.

Elective Surgery

Do not base elective colectomy solely on the number of previous episodes. Individualize according to:

  • Complications
  • Persistent symptoms
  • Immune status
  • Quality of life
  • Operative risk
  • Patient preference

Acute Cholecystitis

Clinical Features

  • Right-upper-quadrant pain
  • Fever
  • Nausea or vomiting
  • Murphy sign
  • Leukocytosis

Ultrasound Findings

  • Gallstones
  • Gallbladder-wall thickening
  • Pericholecystic fluid
  • Sonographic Murphy sign
  • Gallbladder distention

Source Control

Early Laparoscopic Cholecystectomy When Feasible

Antibiotic Coverage

Empiric therapy generally targets enteric gram-negative bacilli. Broaden for:

  • Severe sepsis
  • Healthcare-associated infection
  • Biliary instrumentation
  • Immunocompromise
  • Known resistant organisms

Anaerobic Coverage

Anaerobic coverage is most relevant with:

  • Biliary-enteric anastomosis
  • Emphysematous or gangrenous disease
  • Perforation
  • Severe polymicrobial infection

Postoperative Antibiotics

After uncomplicated cholecystectomy with complete source control, prolonged postoperative antibiotics are generally unnecessary.

Percutaneous Cholecystostomy

Consider gallbladder drainage in a critically ill or high-risk patient who cannot safely undergo early surgery.

Cholecystostomy is often a bridge rather than definitive management. Reassess candidacy for cholecystectomy after stabilization.

Acute Cholangitis

Pathophysiology

Cholangitis results from infection within an obstructed biliary system.

Common Causes of Obstruction

  • Choledocholithiasis
  • Malignant stricture
  • Benign stricture
  • Blocked biliary stent
  • Primary sclerosing cholangitis
  • Postoperative anatomy

Charcot Triad

Fever + Right-Upper-Quadrant Pain + Jaundice

Reynolds Pentad

Charcot Triad + Hypotension + Altered Mental Status
Neither Charcot triad nor Reynolds pentad is sufficiently sensitive to exclude cholangitis. The full pentad generally represents advanced disease.

Initial Management

  • Blood cultures
  • IV antibiotics
  • Hemodynamic resuscitation
  • Ultrasound for dilation or stones
  • MRCP or EUS in selected stable cases
  • Urgent biliary drainage when indicated

Definitive Source Control

ERCP With Biliary Decompression

Urgent Drainage Is Particularly Important With:

  • Organ dysfunction
  • Shock
  • Persistent obstruction
  • Moderate or severe cholangitis
  • Failure to improve with initial therapy

Antibiotics Do Not Relieve Obstruction

A patient may transiently improve with antibiotics while infected bile remains obstructed. Definitive decompression is the central intervention.

Treatment Duration

After successful biliary drainage, a total course of approximately four to seven days is commonly recommended. Shorter courses may be reasonable in selected rapidly improving patients according to organism, bacteremia, and local guidance.

Gram-Positive Bacteremia

Longer treatment or additional evaluation may be needed for:

  • Enterococcus bacteremia
  • Staphylococcus aureus bacteremia
  • Persistent bacteremia
  • Endocarditis concern

Infected Pancreatic Necrosis

Sterile Necrosis

Do not give prophylactic antibiotics for sterile pancreatic necrosis.

Antibiotics do not prevent infected necrosis and increase resistant organisms and fungal complications.

When to Suspect Infection

  • Clinical deterioration after initial stabilization
  • Persistent or recurrent fever
  • Sepsis after approximately one to two weeks
  • Gas within a necrotic collection
  • Bacteremia without another source
  • Progressive organ failure

Antibiotics

Use agents with activity against enteric organisms and adequate penetration into necrotic tissue, such as:

  • Carbapenem
  • Selected cephalosporin plus metronidazole
  • Fluoroquinolone plus metronidazole when susceptibility and safety permit

Fine-Needle Aspiration

Routine diagnostic aspiration is often unnecessary when the clinical and imaging syndrome strongly suggests infected necrosis.

Step-Up Approach

Antibiotics → Percutaneous or Endoscopic Drainage → Minimally Invasive Necrosectomy if Needed

Timing of Intervention

When the patient is stable, delay invasive intervention for approximately four to six weeks to allow the collection to become walled off.

Do Not Delay With:

  • Uncontrolled sepsis
  • Progressive organ failure
  • Abdominal compartment syndrome
  • Perforation
  • Uncontrolled hemorrhage
  • Bowel ischemia

Acute Pancreatitis With Cholangitis

ERCP Within Approximately 24 Hours

Routine urgent ERCP is not indicated for every patient with gallstone pancreatitis without cholangitis or persistent biliary obstruction.

Bowel Perforation

Potential Causes

  • Diverticulitis
  • Appendicitis
  • Peptic ulcer
  • Malignancy
  • Ischemia
  • Inflammatory bowel disease
  • Foreign body
  • Endoscopic or surgical injury

Clinical Features

  • Sudden severe pain
  • Rigid abdomen
  • Diffuse tenderness
  • Shock
  • Free intraperitoneal air
  • Sepsis

Management

  • Immediate surgical evaluation
  • Broad gram-negative and anaerobic therapy
  • Fluid and vasopressor resuscitation
  • Nasogastric decompression when indicated
  • Operative repair, resection, or diversion

Upper-GI Perforation

Delayed treatment, malignancy, acid suppression, critical illness, or repeated surgery increases the likelihood of gram-negative and Candida involvement.

Postoperative and Anastomotic-Leak Infection

Clinical Clues

  • Unexpected tachycardia
  • Persistent ileus
  • New fever
  • Increasing abdominal pain
  • Purulent or enteric drain output
  • Wound drainage
  • Failure to progress after surgery
  • Unexplained organ dysfunction

Imaging

CT with appropriate IV and enteric contrast may identify:

  • Anastomotic leak
  • Abscess
  • Free air or fluid
  • Fistula
  • Ischemia

Microbiology

Postoperative infection is more likely to include:

  • Resistant Enterobacterales
  • Pseudomonas
  • Enterococcus
  • MRSA in selected cases
  • Candida

Management

Broad Initial Coverage + Fresh Deep Cultures + Immediate Source-Control Assessment

Anastomotic Leak

Management may include:

  • Reoperation
  • Percutaneous drainage
  • Endoscopic stenting or vacuum therapy
  • Diversion
  • Bowel rest and nutritional support

Spontaneous Bacterial Peritonitis

SBP is infection of ascitic fluid without an evident surgically treatable intra-abdominal source.

Who Is at Risk?

  • Cirrhosis with ascites
  • Advanced liver dysfunction
  • Low ascitic protein
  • Previous SBP
  • Recent gastrointestinal bleeding
  • Renal dysfunction
  • Hospitalization

Clinical Presentation

  • Fever
  • Abdominal pain or tenderness
  • Encephalopathy
  • Acute kidney injury
  • Hypotension
  • Ileus
  • Leukocytosis
  • Clinical deterioration without localizing symptoms
SBP may be asymptomatic or present only with encephalopathy or renal dysfunction.

Who Needs Diagnostic Paracentesis?

  • Any hospitalized patient with cirrhosis and ascites
  • New-onset ascites
  • Fever or abdominal symptoms
  • Encephalopathy
  • Acute kidney injury
  • Gastrointestinal bleeding
  • Hypotension or unexplained deterioration
Perform paracentesis promptly after hospital presentation. Do not delay solely because of an elevated INR or modest thrombocytopenia in cirrhosis.

Diagnosis of SBP

Diagnostic Threshold

Ascitic PMN Count ≥250 Cells/mm³

Culture Technique

  • Inoculate ascitic fluid directly into aerobic and anaerobic blood-culture bottles at the bedside.
  • Send cell count with differential.
  • Send albumin and total protein when indicated.

Culture-Negative Neutrocytic Ascites

Treat as SBP when PMN count is at least 250 cells/mm³ even if culture is negative.

Monomicrobial Non-Neutrocytic Bacterascites

A positive culture with PMN count below 250 may represent early infection, transient bacterascites, or contamination.

Management

  • Treat symptomatic patients.
  • Repeat paracentesis in selected asymptomatic patients.
  • Interpret the organism and clinical trajectory.

Traumatic Tap

When ascitic fluid contains substantial blood, a correction of approximately one PMN for every 250 RBCs may be considered, but clinical context remains essential.

Treatment of SBP

Community-Acquired SBP

  • Cefotaxime
  • Ceftriaxone

Common Adult Regimens

  • Cefotaxime 2 g IV every 8 hours
  • Ceftriaxone 1–2 g IV every 24 hours

Healthcare-Associated or Nosocomial SBP

Broaden therapy according to:

  • Recent antibiotic exposure
  • Previous resistant organisms
  • Local ESBL prevalence
  • Enterococcal or VRE risk
  • Critical illness

Potential Broader Therapy

  • Piperacillin-tazobactam
  • Carbapenem for high ESBL risk
  • Add vancomycin or another resistant gram-positive agent when indicated

Duration

  • Commonly five to seven days
  • Individualize for bacteremia, resistant organisms, or inadequate response

Repeat Paracentesis

Consider repeat paracentesis after approximately 48 hours when:

  • Clinical response is inadequate
  • Infection is nosocomial
  • Resistant organisms are possible
  • Secondary peritonitis is possible

Expected Response

A decline in ascitic PMNs of less than approximately 25% after 48 hours raises concern for treatment failure, resistance, or secondary peritonitis.

Albumin in SBP

Regimen

Albumin 1.5 g/kg on Day 1 + 1 g/kg on Day 3

Purpose

  • Reduce renal failure
  • Reduce hepatorenal syndrome
  • Improve survival in higher-risk patients

Patients With Strongest Evidence of Benefit

  • Serum creatinine above approximately 1 mg/dL
  • BUN above approximately 30 mg/dL
  • Total bilirubin above approximately 4 mg/dL
  • Significant renal or circulatory dysfunction
Many institutions administer albumin broadly for confirmed SBP. Consider volume status and risk of pulmonary edema while following hepatology guidance.

SBP Prophylaxis

Secondary Prophylaxis

A previous episode of SBP creates a high recurrence risk. Long-term prophylaxis is generally recommended until:

  • Transplantation
  • Resolution of ascites
  • Death
  • A major contraindication develops

Potential Agents

  • Ciprofloxacin
  • Trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole
  • Other locally recommended alternatives

Acute Gastrointestinal Bleeding

Patients with cirrhosis and acute upper-GI bleeding should receive short-course antibiotic prophylaxis, commonly ceftriaxone for up to seven days.

Primary Prophylaxis

Guidelines support considering prophylaxis in selected patients with:

  • Ascitic protein below 1.5 g/dL
  • Plus advanced liver failure, renal dysfunction, or severe hyponatremia

Important Stewardship Nuance

Primary prophylaxis should not be prescribed merely because ascitic protein is low. Long-term antibiotics select resistant organisms and increase adverse events. Apply current hepatology guidance and individual risk assessment.

Secondary Bacterial Peritonitis

Secondary peritonitis results from a perforated, ischemic, inflamed, or leaking abdominal source.

Clues Against Simple SBP

  • Multiple organisms in ascitic culture
  • Very high PMN count
  • Localized severe tenderness
  • Peritoneal signs
  • Failure to improve after appropriate SBP therapy
  • Free air
  • Abscess or inflamed organ on imaging

Runyon-Type Laboratory Clues

Secondary peritonitis is more likely when at least two are present:

  • Ascitic total protein above 1 g/dL
  • Ascitic glucose below 50 mg/dL
  • Ascitic LDH above the serum upper limit of normal

Management

Broad Antibiotics + Urgent CT + Surgical or Procedural Source Control

Do Not Misclassify

Treating a bowel perforation as SBP with ceftriaxone alone can delay life-saving surgery and inadequate anaerobic coverage.

Peritoneal Dialysis-Associated Peritonitis

Clinical Features

  • Cloudy dialysis effluent
  • Abdominal pain
  • Fever
  • Exit-site or tunnel infection

Diagnosis

Diagnosis is supported by at least two of:

  • Compatible clinical features
  • Effluent WBC above 100 cells/µL after an adequate dwell, with more than 50% neutrophils
  • Positive dialysate culture

Specimen Collection

  • Send effluent cell count and differential.
  • Inoculate appropriate blood-culture bottles.
  • Culture the exit site when infection is present.

Empiric Treatment

Provide intraperitoneal coverage for:

  • Gram-positive organisms
  • Gram-negative organisms including Pseudomonas according to local protocol

Catheter Removal

Consider or perform catheter removal with:

  • Refractory peritonitis
  • Relapsing infection
  • Fungal peritonitis
  • Selected mycobacterial infection
  • Severe tunnel infection

Pyogenic Liver Abscess

Sources

  • Biliary infection
  • Portal spread from diverticulitis or appendicitis
  • Hematogenous spread
  • Trauma
  • Postoperative infection
  • Cryptogenic infection

Clinical Features

  • Fever
  • Right-upper-quadrant pain
  • Weight loss
  • Elevated alkaline phosphatase
  • Leukocytosis
  • Bacteremia

Diagnosis

  • Contrast CT or ultrasound
  • Blood cultures
  • Percutaneous aspiration or drainage cultures

Management

Drainage + Gram-Negative and Anaerobic Antibiotics

Common Empiric Therapy

  • Ceftriaxone plus metronidazole
  • Piperacillin-tazobactam
  • Carbapenem when resistant-organism risk is substantial

Duration

Commonly several weeks, depending on:

  • Adequacy of drainage
  • Abscess size and number
  • Organism
  • Bacteremia
  • Clinical and radiographic response

Hypervirulent Klebsiella Syndrome

Consider with:

  • Monomicrobial Klebsiella liver abscess
  • Diabetes
  • Community onset
  • East or Southeast Asian epidemiology, although cases occur worldwide
  • Eye or CNS symptoms
Klebsiella liver abscess may cause metastatic endophthalmitis or CNS infection. Obtain urgent ophthalmologic evaluation for visual symptoms.

Amoebic Liver Abscess

Cause

  • Entamoeba histolytica

Clinical Features

  • Fever
  • Right-upper-quadrant pain
  • Travel or residence in an endemic region
  • Diarrhea may be absent
  • Usually a solitary right-lobe lesion

Diagnosis

  • Serology
  • Stool antigen or PCR when intestinal infection is present
  • Imaging
  • Clinical response to therapy

Treatment

Metronidazole or Tinidazole → Then a Luminal Agent

Drainage Is Usually Unnecessary

Consider aspiration or drainage with:

  • Diagnostic uncertainty
  • Failure to improve within several days
  • Large left-lobe abscess
  • Impending rupture
  • Need to exclude pyogenic abscess
Do not omit the luminal agent after metronidazole or tinidazole.

Splenic Abscess

Risk Factors

  • Endocarditis
  • Bacteremia
  • Immunocompromise
  • Trauma
  • Splenic infarction
  • Hemoglobinopathy

Clinical Features

  • Fever
  • Left-upper-quadrant pain
  • Persistent bacteremia
  • Left pleural effusion

Management

  • Blood cultures
  • Broad antibiotics
  • Percutaneous drainage in selected unilocular accessible abscesses
  • Splenectomy when drainage is not feasible or disease is extensive
  • Evaluation for endocarditis

After Splenectomy

Provide appropriate:

  • Pneumococcal vaccination
  • Meningococcal vaccination
  • Haemophilus influenzae type b vaccination
  • Postsplenectomy infection education

Pylephlebitis

Pylephlebitis is septic thrombosis of the portal venous system associated with an intra-abdominal infection.

Sources

  • Diverticulitis
  • Appendicitis
  • Cholangitis
  • Pancreatitis
  • Inflammatory bowel disease
  • Intra-abdominal abscess

Clinical Features

  • Persistent fever
  • Abdominal pain
  • Bacteremia
  • Portal-vein thrombosis
  • Liver abscesses

Diagnosis

  • Contrast CT
  • Blood cultures
  • Evaluation of the primary abdominal source

Treatment

  • Broad antibiotics covering enteric gram-negative organisms and anaerobes
  • Source control
  • Often four to six weeks of therapy

Anticoagulation

Anticoagulation is individualized and may be considered with:

  • Progressive thrombosis
  • Extension into mesenteric veins
  • Persistent fever despite active therapy
  • Underlying thrombophilia

Neutropenic Enterocolitis

Neutropenic enterocolitis, or typhlitis, is inflammatory and infectious bowel injury occurring during profound neutropenia, commonly involving the cecum and ascending colon.

Classic Features

Neutropenia + Fever + Right-Lower-Quadrant Pain + Bowel-Wall Thickening

Risk Factors

  • Acute leukemia
  • Cytotoxic chemotherapy
  • Prolonged severe neutropenia
  • Mucosal injury

Diagnosis

  • CT abdomen and pelvis
  • Blood cultures
  • Stool testing when diarrhea is present
  • Evaluation for C. difficile

Empiric Treatment

Provide broad therapy active against:

  • Pseudomonas
  • Enterobacterales
  • Anaerobes
  • Selected resistant gram-positive organisms when indicated

Potential Regimens

  • Piperacillin-tazobactam
  • Meropenem
  • Cefepime plus metronidazole

Supportive Care

  • Bowel rest when severe
  • IV fluid and electrolyte support
  • Blood-product support
  • Management of neutropenia
  • Avoid unnecessary colonoscopy

Surgical Indications

  • Perforation
  • Uncontrolled bleeding
  • Necrosis
  • Clinical deterioration despite medical therapy
  • Another surgical abdominal process
Neutropenic patients may lack leukocytosis or peritoneal signs despite advanced bowel injury.

Suspected Treatment Failure

Common Causes

  • Missed source
  • Inadequate drainage
  • Ongoing anastomotic leak
  • Ischemic or necrotic bowel
  • Obstruction
  • Resistant pathogen
  • Incorrect antibiotic dose
  • Intra-abdominal Candida
  • Noninfectious mimic

Reassessment

  1. Repeat the complete abdominal examination.
  2. Review operative and procedural findings.
  3. Review all cultures and susceptibilities.
  4. Confirm antibiotic dose and administration.
  5. Repeat blood cultures if bacteremia persists.
  6. Obtain targeted repeat imaging.
  7. Reconsult surgery, GI, or interventional radiology.
  8. Consider ischemia, malignancy, inflammatory disease, or drug toxicity.

Do Not Treat the CT Alone

Residual postoperative fluid and inflammatory changes may persist after successful treatment. Correlate imaging with:

  • Symptoms
  • Hemodynamics
  • Laboratory trends
  • Drain output
  • Ability to eat and recover bowel function
Escalating from cefepime to meropenem without addressing a leaking anastomosis will not cure the infection.

Daily Inpatient Checklist

  • What is the exact anatomic source?
  • Is the infection uncomplicated or complicated?
  • Has source control occurred?
  • Is additional drainage or surgery needed?
  • Were meaningful blood or intra-abdominal cultures obtained?
  • Is the empiric regimen appropriate for the source?
  • Does the patient truly need Pseudomonas coverage?
  • Does the patient truly need Enterococcus, MRSA, or Candida coverage?
  • Can therapy be narrowed?
  • Is duplicate anaerobic therapy being given?
  • Are renal function and antibiotic doses appropriate?
  • Is the abdomen improving?
  • Are bowel function and oral intake returning?
  • Is bacteremia clearing?
  • What is the source-control date?
  • What is the antibiotic stop date?
  • Can IV therapy stop or transition to oral therapy?
  • Does failure require repeat imaging rather than more antibiotics?

How to Present on ID Rounds

  • Anatomic diagnosis
  • Community vs healthcare-associated infection
  • Sepsis severity
  • Imaging findings
  • Source-control procedure and date
  • Blood and intra-abdominal cultures
  • Current antibiotic, dose, and start date
  • Resistance risks
  • Clinical response
  • Planned duration and stop date
Avoid saying only, “The patient has an abdominal infection and is on Zosyn.” State the source, whether perforation or abscess exists, whether source control is complete, what cultures show, and when therapy can stop.

Common Clinical Scenarios

Case 1: Perforated Appendicitis After Successful Appendectomy

A stable patient undergoes appendectomy with washout and complete source control.

Use a short postoperative course, commonly about three to five days, rather than continuing antibiotics until the WBC fully normalizes.

Case 2: Mild CT-Confirmed Uncomplicated Diverticulitis

An immunocompetent patient has mild symptoms, no systemic inflammation, no abscess, and reliable follow-up.

Supportive treatment without antibiotics is reasonable.

Case 3: Diverticular Abscess

CT demonstrates a large pelvic abscess associated with sigmoid diverticulitis.

Use antibiotics and obtain percutaneous drainage when technically feasible. Antibiotics alone may not provide adequate source control.

Case 4: Cholangitis With Hypotension

A patient has jaundice, fever, hypotension, and a dilated common bile duct.

Begin antibiotics and resuscitation immediately, but arrange urgent biliary decompression. Antibiotics alone do not relieve the obstruction.

Case 5: Persistent Fever After Abdominal Surgery

A postoperative patient has tachycardia, ileus, and increasing abdominal pain despite broad antibiotics.

Suspect an anastomotic leak or undrained abscess. Obtain urgent imaging and surgical reassessment rather than simply broadening antibiotics.

Case 6: Cirrhosis With Confusion and AKI

A patient with ascites has no abdominal pain but develops encephalopathy and rising creatinine.

Perform prompt diagnostic paracentesis. SBP may present without fever or tenderness.

Case 7: Ascitic PMN 640 With Negative Culture

The patient has cirrhosis and culture-negative ascites with 640 PMNs/mm³.

Treat as SBP. A negative culture does not override the diagnostic PMN count.

Case 8: Polymicrobial Ascitic Culture

Ascitic fluid grows E. coli, Enterococcus, and anaerobes, with severe focal abdominal tenderness.

Suspect secondary peritonitis. Obtain urgent CT and surgical evaluation.

Case 9: Pancreatic Necrosis on Day 3

A patient with severe pancreatitis has necrosis but no gas, bacteremia, or new clinical deterioration.

Do not give prophylactic antibiotics for presumed sterile necrosis.

Case 10: Pancreatic Necrosis With Sepsis on Day 14

The patient deteriorates with fever, bacteremia, and gas inside a necrotic collection.

Treat infected necrosis and plan a step-up drainage strategy. If stable enough, delay definitive necrosectomy until the collection becomes walled off.

Case 11: Bloody Diarrhea After Undercooked Beef

A patient has severe cramps, bloody stool, and minimal fever.

Test for Shiga toxin. Avoid antibiotics and antimotility agents because of HUS risk.

Case 12: Positive GI PCR for Multiple Organisms

A multiplex panel detects enteropathogenic E. coli and norovirus in a patient with mild self-limited diarrhea.

Do not automatically treat every detected organism. Interpret the result with the syndrome and expected clinical significance.

Case 13: Liver Abscess With Visual Changes

A patient with diabetes and monomicrobial Klebsiella liver abscess reports blurred vision.

Arrange urgent ophthalmologic evaluation for metastatic endophthalmitis.

Case 14: Neutropenia With Right-Lower-Quadrant Pain

A patient receiving leukemia chemotherapy has fever, profound neutropenia, and cecal-wall thickening.

Treat neutropenic enterocolitis with broad antipseudomonal and anaerobic therapy, bowel support, and close surgical monitoring.

Common Pitfalls

  • Delaying source control while repeatedly changing antibiotics
  • Failing to recognize postoperative anastomotic leak
  • Using antibiotics alone for obstructed cholangitis
  • Continuing antibiotics for weeks after adequate source control
  • Waiting for WBC normalization before stopping therapy
  • Failing to obtain fresh deep cultures during drainage
  • Treating chronic drain colonization
  • Using superficial swabs instead of aspirated fluid or tissue
  • Giving routine Pseudomonas coverage for low-risk community infection
  • Giving routine vancomycin for every abdominal infection
  • Giving routine antifungal therapy without Candida risk
  • Missing Candida after recurrent perforation or anastomotic leak
  • Giving piperacillin-tazobactam plus unnecessary metronidazole
  • Failing to renal-adjust cefepime or other agents
  • Using a fluoroquinolone without reviewing resistance and adverse effects
  • Giving postoperative antibiotics after uncomplicated appendectomy
  • Treating every uncomplicated diverticulitis episode with antibiotics
  • Using antibiotics-first appendicitis management without discussing recurrence
  • Ignoring the higher failure risk from an appendicolith
  • Delaying paracentesis in hospitalized cirrhosis
  • Ruling out SBP because abdominal pain is absent
  • Requiring a positive ascitic culture to treat SBP
  • Mistaking secondary peritonitis for SBP
  • Failing to give albumin in high-risk SBP
  • Prescribing primary SBP prophylaxis based only on low ascitic protein
  • Giving prophylactic antibiotics for sterile pancreatic necrosis
  • Intervening too early on stable pancreatic necrosis without allowing wall formation
  • Giving antibiotics for suspected STEC diarrhea
  • Using loperamide with bloody inflammatory diarrhea
  • Treating every multiplex GI-panel detection
  • Failing to culture Shigella when resistance information is needed
  • Omitting a luminal agent after treatment of amoebiasis

Board and ID Consult Pearls

  • Complicated intra-abdominal infection extends beyond the organ of origin into a sterile abdominal space.
  • Source control is the central treatment of perforation, abscess, obstruction, leak, and infected necrosis.
  • CT is the primary imaging test for many adult complicated abdominal infections.
  • Ultrasound is first line for acute cholecystitis.
  • Fresh aspirated fluid or deep tissue is superior to a chronic-drain culture.
  • Community colonic infection requires gram-negative and anaerobic coverage.
  • Ceftriaxone plus metronidazole is a common community-acquired regimen.
  • Piperacillin-tazobactam and carbapenems already provide anaerobic activity.
  • Do not add metronidazole routinely to piperacillin-tazobactam.
  • Routine Enterococcus coverage is unnecessary in low-risk community infection.
  • Routine MRSA coverage is unnecessary in most abdominal infections.
  • Consider Candida with anastomotic leak, recurrent perforation, recent surgery, or necrotizing pancreatitis.
  • After adequate source control, approximately four days of antibiotics is sufficient for many complicated infections.
  • Persistent illness should trigger reassessment of source control.
  • Uncomplicated appendicitis after appendectomy does not require postoperative antibiotics.
  • Selected uncomplicated appendicitis can be managed with antibiotics, but recurrence and failure must be discussed.
  • An appendicolith increases failure risk during nonoperative appendicitis treatment.
  • Selected immunocompetent patients with mild uncomplicated diverticulitis do not require antibiotics.
  • A larger diverticular abscess generally requires percutaneous drainage when feasible.
  • Early laparoscopic cholecystectomy is preferred for most operable acute cholecystitis.
  • Acute cholangitis is infection plus biliary obstruction.
  • Moderate or severe cholangitis requires urgent biliary drainage.
  • Antibiotics do not replace ERCP in obstructed cholangitis.
  • Do not give prophylactic antibiotics for sterile pancreatic necrosis.
  • Stable infected necrosis is managed with a delayed step-up approach when possible.
  • Hospitalized patients with cirrhosis and ascites need prompt diagnostic paracentesis.
  • An ascitic PMN count of at least 250 cells/mm³ diagnoses SBP.
  • Culture-negative neutrocytic ascites is treated as SBP.
  • Bedside inoculation into blood-culture bottles improves ascitic culture yield.
  • Albumin in SBP is given at 1.5 g/kg on day 1 and 1 g/kg on day 3.
  • Polymicrobial ascites suggests secondary peritonitis.
  • Secondary peritonitis requires imaging and source control.
  • Do not give antibiotics or antimotility agents for suspected STEC.
  • Most uncomplicated nontyphoidal Salmonella gastroenteritis does not require antibiotics.
  • Azithromycin is preferred for Campylobacter when treatment is needed.
  • Shigella treatment should be susceptibility guided because resistance is increasing.
  • Amoebic liver abscess treatment requires a tissue-active drug followed by a luminal agent.
  • Pyogenic liver abscess generally requires drainage plus prolonged therapy.
  • Neutropenic enterocolitis requires antipseudomonal and anaerobic coverage.

Bottom Line

Gastrointestinal and intra-abdominal infections must be approached anatomically. Determine whether disease remains confined to the organ of origin or has progressed to perforation, peritonitis, abscess, obstruction, ischemia, necrosis, or postoperative leak. In unstable patients, antimicrobial therapy, resuscitation, imaging, and source-control planning should occur simultaneously.

Empiric therapy should match the source and resistance risk. Community-acquired colonic infection usually requires Enterobacterales, streptococcal, and anaerobic coverage. Pseudomonas, Enterococcus, MRSA, and Candida coverage should be added selectively rather than automatically. Obtain fresh deep cultures during drainage or surgery and narrow therapy when results return.

Adequate source control permits short antibiotic courses. Approximately four days is sufficient for many complicated intra-abdominal infections after definitive source control. Persistent fever, leukocytosis, ileus, or shock should trigger reassessment for an undrained collection, ischemia, obstruction, or ongoing leak rather than indefinite broad-spectrum therapy.

Important syndrome-specific principles include selective antibiotic use in uncomplicated diverticulitis, urgent biliary decompression for cholangitis, no prophylactic antibiotics for sterile pancreatic necrosis, prompt paracentesis for hospitalized patients with cirrhosis and ascites, albumin plus antibiotics for high-risk SBP, and avoidance of antibiotics and antimotility drugs in suspected Shiga toxin-producing E. coli infection.

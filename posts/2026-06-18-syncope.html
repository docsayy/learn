Syncope: A Practical Guide for Internal Medicine Residents

Added June 18, 2026 • Cardiology • ~12 min read

A practical framework for evaluating syncope, including vasovagal syncope, orthostatic hypotension, cardiogenic syncope, red flags, orthostatic vital signs, telemetry, EKG interpretation, and admission decisions.

Definition of Syncope

Syncope is a transient loss of consciousness caused by temporary cerebral hypoperfusion followed by spontaneous and rapid recovery.

Rapid recovery is one of the most important features of syncope. Prolonged confusion or a prolonged post-ictal state suggests an alternative diagnosis such as seizure.

Major Causes of Syncope

Most cases of syncope fall into three major categories:

Cause Approximate Frequency
Reflex Syncope 65%
Orthostatic Syncope 15%
Cardiogenic Syncope 10%
Neurologic / Mimics 10%
The most important diagnosis to exclude is cardiogenic syncope because it carries the highest risk of sudden death.

Reflex Syncope

Reflex syncope is the most common cause of syncope.

Types

  • Vasovagal syncope
  • Situational syncope
  • Carotid sinus hypersensitivity

Vasovagal Syncope

Patients typically describe a clear prodrome before losing consciousness.

Typical Prodrome

  • Flushing
  • Warmth
  • Diaphoresis
  • Nausea
  • Lightheadedness
  • Visual dimming

Common Triggers

  • Standing for prolonged periods
  • Pain
  • Emotional stress
  • Blood draws
  • Medical procedures
A clear prodrome is reassuring and strongly suggests vasovagal syncope rather than an arrhythmic cause.

Situational Syncope

Situational syncope is essentially a vasovagal response triggered by specific activities.

  • Micturition (urination)
  • Defecation
  • Coughing
  • Sneezing
  • Swallowing

Carotid Sinus Hypersensitivity

Excessive stimulation of the carotid sinus increases vagal tone and can lead to syncope.

Classic Board Question

  • Elderly man
  • Tight collar or necktie
  • Sudden syncope after turning head

Orthostatic Syncope

Orthostatic syncope results from impaired blood pressure compensation when standing.

Common Causes

  • Dehydration
  • Poor oral intake
  • Vomiting
  • Diarrhea
  • Over-diuresis
  • Blood loss

Autonomic Dysfunction Causes

  • Advanced age
  • Diabetes mellitus
  • Parkinson disease
  • Autonomic neuropathy

Typical Symptoms

  • Lightheadedness when standing
  • Dizziness after rising from bed
  • Presyncope with position changes

Orthostatic Vital Signs

How to Measure

  1. Patient lies supine for 5 minutes.
  2. Measure blood pressure and pulse.
  3. Patient stands.
  4. Repeat measurements after 1 minute.

Positive Orthostatic Hypotension

Finding Definition
Systolic BP Drop ≥ 20 mmHg
Diastolic BP Drop ≥ 10 mmHg
Heart Rate Increase ≥ 30 bpm suggests POTS
Every patient admitted with syncope should have orthostatic vital signs unless a clear alternative diagnosis already exists.

Cardiogenic Syncope

Cardiogenic syncope is the most dangerous cause of syncope because it may represent life-threatening arrhythmias or structural heart disease.

Common Causes

  • Ventricular tachycardia
  • Bradyarrhythmias
  • High-grade AV block
  • Severe aortic stenosis
  • Hypertrophic cardiomyopathy
  • Pulmonary embolism

High-Risk Features

The following features should immediately raise concern for cardiogenic syncope.
  • No prodrome
  • Sudden loss of consciousness
  • Exertional syncope
  • Syncope while supine
  • Chest pain
  • Palpitations
  • Increasing frequency of episodes
  • Known structural heart disease
  • Family history of sudden cardiac death

Family History Pearl

Ask whether any relatives died suddenly or drowned unexpectedly at a young age.

Inherited arrhythmia syndromes may present this way.

Evaluation & Workup

Tests Most Patients Need

  • Orthostatic vital signs
  • 12-lead ECG
  • Telemetry monitoring
  • Echocardiogram (selected patients)

Telemetry Is Important Because

  • Arrhythmias may be intermittent
  • Baseline ECG may be normal
  • Monitoring can identify dangerous rhythm disturbances

San Francisco Syncope Rule

Patients with any of the following features are generally considered higher risk and often require admission.

Risk Factor Mnemonic: CHESS
Congestive heart failure history C
Hematocrit < 30% H
Abnormal ECG E
Shortness of breath S
Systolic BP < 90 mmHg S
CHESS is a useful board-style mnemonic for remembering high-risk syncope patients.

Neurologic Causes and Syncope Mimics

Seizure

Seizure is the most important mimic of syncope.

Syncope Seizure
Rapid recovery Post-ictal confusion
Brief myoclonic jerks possible Prolonged convulsions
Jerks after LOC Jerks begin with LOC
< 15 seconds Often > 15 seconds
Brief myoclonic jerks do not automatically mean the patient had a seizure.

Other Mimics

  • Subclavian steal syndrome
  • Vertebrobasilar insufficiency
  • Psychogenic pseudosyncope

High-Yield Pearls

  • Reflex syncope is the most common cause of syncope.
  • Cardiogenic syncope is the most dangerous cause.
  • A prodrome strongly suggests vasovagal syncope.
  • Exertional syncope should always trigger concern for cardiac disease.
  • Supine syncope is abnormal and concerning.
  • Always obtain orthostatic vital signs.
  • Orthostatic hypotension = SBP drop ≥20 or DBP drop ≥10.
  • Telemetry and ECG are high-yield tests.
  • Routine CT head is usually not indicated without neurologic findings.
  • Rapid recovery favors syncope; prolonged confusion favors seizure.
  • Remember the CHESS criteria for admission decisions.

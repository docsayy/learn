Lymphoma vs Leukemia: A Practical Resident Guide

Added June 20, 2026 • Hematology Oncology • ~15 min read

Lymphoma and leukemia are among the most intimidating topics for trainees because of the large number of diagnoses, translocations, staging systems, and treatment regimens. This article provides a practical framework for understanding the major disease categories, common board associations, diagnostic approaches, and high-yield clinical pearls.

Lymphoma vs Leukemia

The easiest way to differentiate lymphoma from leukemia is by where the disease predominantly resides.

Disease Primary Location Concept
Lymphoma Lymph nodes and lymphatic tissues Solid malignancy
Leukemia Peripheral blood and bone marrow Liquid malignancy
A useful analogy is that lymphoma is like having lemons (localized masses), whereas leukemia is like lemonade (circulating throughout the bloodstream).

There can be overlap. Some lymphomas involve bone marrow and blood, while some leukemias involve lymph nodes. However, this framework remains useful for understanding the diseases.

Hodgkin Lymphoma

Hodgkin lymphoma generally has a better prognosis than non-Hodgkin lymphoma and follows a predictable pattern of spread.

Classic Features

  • Localized lymph node involvement
  • Often begins in cervical lymph nodes
  • Contiguous spread
  • Reed-Sternberg cells
  • Association with EBV
  • Bimodal age distribution

Board Buzzwords

  • Pruritus after hot shower
  • Pain in lymph nodes after alcohol ingestion
  • Reed-Sternberg cells
Characteristic Hodgkin Lymphoma
Spread Pattern Contiguous
Classic Cell Reed-Sternberg Cell
EBV Association Yes
Typical Treatment ABVD

Non-Hodgkin Lymphoma

Non-Hodgkin lymphoma is more heterogeneous and generally has a less predictable spread pattern.

  • Non-contiguous spread
  • May involve multiple nodal regions
  • Often categorized into B-cell and T-cell lymphomas
Most lymphomas encountered clinically and on board examinations are B-cell lymphomas.

Major B-Cell Lymphomas

Category Examples
Indolent Follicular lymphoma, Marginal zone lymphoma
Aggressive Diffuse Large B Cell Lymphoma (DLBCL), Mantle Cell Lymphoma
Very Aggressive Burkitt lymphoma, Lymphoblastic lymphoma

Follicular Lymphoma

  • Indolent course
  • Often managed with watchful waiting
  • T(14;18)
  • BCL2 overexpression

Follicular lymphoma may transform into diffuse large B-cell lymphoma.

Diffuse Large B Cell Lymphoma (DLBCL)

  • Most common lymphoma overall
  • Aggressive but potentially curable
  • Frequently treated with R-CHOP-based regimens

Burkitt Lymphoma

Burkitt lymphoma is one of the fastest-growing human tumors.

Feature Burkitt Lymphoma
Typical Patient Children and young adults
Presentation Jaw mass or abdominal mass
Association EBV
Translocation T(8;14)
Gene MYC
Pathology Starry sky appearance
Burkitt lymphoma can double in size within 24 hours and requires urgent treatment.

Diagnosis and Staging of Lymphoma

Excisional Biopsy

Fine needle aspiration is not sufficient for lymphoma diagnosis.

The preferred diagnostic test is an excisional lymph node biopsy because it preserves lymph node architecture.

Avoid Steroids Before Biopsy

Steroids may partially treat lymphoma and obscure the diagnosis before tissue is obtained.

Ann Arbor Staging

Lymphoma staging uses the Ann Arbor system.

Crossing the diaphragm automatically makes the disease at least Stage III.
Finding Stage Implication
One side of diaphragm only Stage I-II
Both sides of diaphragm Stage III or higher

Acute Lymphoblastic Leukemia (ALL)

  • Most common leukemia in children
  • Can occur in adults
  • Often presents with fatigue, infections, bleeding
If the board question involves a child with leukemia, ALL is usually the answer.

Acute Myeloid Leukemia (AML)

  • Most common acute leukemia in adults
  • Usually presents around age 60
  • May arise from myelodysplastic syndrome

Common Treatments

  • 7+3 induction therapy
  • HiDAC consolidation
  • Venetoclax-based regimens in selected patients

Acute Promyelocytic Leukemia (APML)

APML is the leukemia you must recognize quickly because treatment dramatically improves outcomes.

Classic Findings

  • DIC
  • Bleeding and clotting simultaneously
  • Auer rods
  • PML-RARA fusion
  • T(15;17)
If APML is suspected, start ATRA immediately while awaiting confirmation.
Feature APML
Translocation T(15;17)
Gene PML-RARA
Complication DIC
Treatment ATRA + Arsenic

Chronic Leukemias

The major chronic leukemias are CLL and CML.

CLL and SLL

CLL is the most common leukemia in older adults.

  • Usually diagnosed around age 70
  • Lymphocytosis >5,000 for at least 3 months
  • Flow cytometry confirms clonality
  • Smudge cells on peripheral smear

Richter Transformation

Approximately 5% of patients transform into diffuse large B-cell lymphoma.

Richter transformation carries a poor prognosis and is the major feared complication of CLL.

SLL

Small lymphocytic lymphoma is essentially the same disease as CLL but predominantly involves lymph nodes rather than blood.

Treatment

  • Observation in many patients
  • Treatment when symptomatic or progressive

Chronic Myeloid Leukemia (CML)

CML is driven by the Philadelphia chromosome.

Feature CML
Translocation T(9;22)
Fusion Protein BCR-ABL
Finding Very high WBC count
Treatment Tyrosine kinase inhibitors

Disease Phases

  • Chronic phase
  • Accelerated phase
  • Blast phase

Common treatments include:

  • Imatinib
  • Dasatinib
  • Ponatinib
  • Other tyrosine kinase inhibitors

Leukostasis

Leukostasis occurs when extremely elevated white blood cell counts impair microvascular blood flow.

Symptoms

  • Headache
  • Confusion
  • Visual changes
  • Dyspnea
  • Neurologic deficits
Myeloid leukemias carry a much higher risk of leukostasis than lymphoid leukemias because myeloid blasts are larger and stickier.

High-Yield Pearls

  • Lymphoma = lymph nodes; leukemia = blood and bone marrow.
  • Hodgkin lymphoma has Reed-Sternberg cells and contiguous spread.
  • Non-Hodgkin lymphoma spreads non-contiguously.
  • DLBCL is the most common lymphoma.
  • Follicular lymphoma carries T(14;18) and BCL2 overexpression.
  • Burkitt lymphoma carries T(8;14) and MYC activation.
  • Burkitt lymphoma has a starry sky appearance.
  • Excisional biopsy is the diagnostic gold standard.
  • Never rely on FNA alone for lymphoma diagnosis.
  • Avoid steroids before biopsy if possible.
  • Crossing the diaphragm means at least Stage III lymphoma.
  • Acute leukemia requires >20% blasts.
  • ALL is the most common leukemia in children.
  • AML is the most common acute leukemia in adults.
  • APML is associated with DIC and T(15;17).
  • Start ATRA immediately if APML is suspected.
  • CLL shows lymphocytosis and smudge cells.
  • CML is associated with the Philadelphia chromosome.
  • BCR-ABL is treated with tyrosine kinase inhibitors.
  • Myeloid leukemias carry the highest risk of leukostasis.

Bottom Line

Start by determining whether the disease is primarily in lymph nodes (lymphoma) or blood and bone marrow (leukemia). For lymphoma, distinguish Hodgkin from non-Hodgkin disease and remember that excisional biopsy is required for diagnosis. For leukemia, separate acute from chronic disease based on blast percentage and clinical presentation. The highest-yield diagnoses to recognize quickly are Burkitt lymphoma, APML, CLL with Richter transformation, and CML with the Philadelphia chromosome.
